1. PLANT CLASSIFICATION & STRUCTURE
-------------------------------------
Plants are multicellular, photosynthetic eukaryotes. Kingdom Plantae.
~400,000 known species.

Major Plant Groups:
- Non-vascular (Bryophytes) : Mosses, liverworts, hornworts. No true roots/stems.
                               Depend on water for reproduction.
- Vascular, seedless        : Ferns, club mosses, horsetails. Have xylem/phloem.
- Gymnosperms               : Seed plants; naked seeds (no fruit). Conifers, cycads,
                               ginkgo. Pollinated by wind.
- Angiosperms               : Flowering plants; seeds enclosed in fruit. Most
                               diverse plant group (~300,000 species).
  - Monocots                : 1 seed leaf; parallel leaf veins; fibrous roots.
                               Examples: grasses, lilies, corn, wheat.
  - Dicots (Eudicots)       : 2 seed leaves; net-like veins; taproot. Examples:
                               roses, oaks, beans, tomatoes.

1.1 PLANT ORGANS
-----------------
Roots:
- Anchor plant; absorb water and minerals (via root hairs); store nutrients.
- Taproot (dicots): single main root.
- Fibrous roots (monocots): many small roots.

Stems:
- Support leaves and flowers; transport water/nutrients.
- Nodes: points where leaves attach. Internodes: stem sections between nodes.
- Modified stems: rhizomes (ginger), stolons (strawberry), tubers (potato), bulbs (onion).

Leaves:
- Primary site of photosynthesis.
- Blade (lamina): flat surface for light capture.
- Petiole: stalk attaching leaf to stem.
- Stomata: pores in epidermis for gas exchange; flanked by guard cells.
- Mesophyll: photosynthetic cells inside leaf.
  Palisade mesophyll (upper): tightly packed; maximum light absorption.
  Spongy mesophyll (lower): loosely packed; gas exchange spaces.

Flowers: Reproductive organs.
- Sepals (calyx)    : Protect flower bud.
- Petals (corolla)  : Attract pollinators.
- Stamens           : Male organs (anther + filament); produce pollen.
- Carpels/Pistil    : Female organs (stigma + style + ovary); contains ovules.

2. PLANT TISSUE SYSTEMS
------------------------
Three tissue systems run throughout roots, stems, and leaves:

Dermal Tissue:
- Epidermis        : Outer protective layer; often covered by waxy cuticle.
- Guard cells      : Control stomatal opening/closing (K+ flux regulates turgor).
- Root hairs       : Increase surface area for absorption.
- Trichomes        : Epidermal hairs; reduce water loss; deter herbivores.

Vascular Tissue:
- Xylem            : Transports water and minerals UPWARD (unidirectional).
                     Cells: tracheids and vessel elements (dead at maturity; lignified).
                     Mechanism: cohesion-tension theory (transpiration pull).
- Phloem           : Transports sugars (sucrose) bidirectionally (source to sink).
                     Cells: sieve tube elements (alive; no nucleus) + companion cells.
                     Mechanism: pressure-flow hypothesis.

Ground Tissue:
- Parenchyma       : Most abundant; thin walls; photosynthesis, storage.
- Collenchyma      : Unevenly thickened walls; flexible support (celery strings).
- Sclerenchyma     : Thick, lignified walls; rigid support. Fibres and sclereids.

3. TRANSPORT IN PLANTS
-----------------------
Water Movement:
- Osmosis          : Water enters root hair cells from soil (high water potential).
- Apoplast pathway : Water moves through cell walls/spaces (outside cell membranes).
- Symplast pathway : Water moves through cytoplasm via plasmodesmata.
- Casparian strip  : Suberin band in endodermis forces water into symplast;
                     controls mineral entry to vascular cylinder.
- Transpiration    : Evaporation of water from leaves drives upward movement.
- Transpiration pull + Cohesion (water molecules stick together) + Adhesion
  (water sticks to xylem walls) = cohesion-tension mechanism.

Stomatal regulation:
  Light → H+ pumped out of guard cells (H+-ATPase) → K+ influx → osmotic
  pressure rises → guard cells swell → stomata open.
  ABA (abscisic acid) triggers stomata closure under drought stress.

Phloem Loading (Source to Sink):
  Source: Photosynthetic leaves load sucrose into phloem (active transport).
  Sink: Growing regions, roots, fruits unload sucrose.
  High osmotic pressure at source → water enters → hydrostatic pressure
  drives bulk flow of phloem sap toward sink.

4. PLANT HORMONES
------------------
Hormone        | Produced In             | Key Effects
---------------|-------------------------|---------------------------------------------
Auxin (IAA)    | Shoot apical meristem   | Cell elongation; phototropism; apical dominance
Cytokinins     | Roots                   | Cell division; delays senescence
Gibberellins   | Young leaves, seeds     | Stem elongation; seed germination; fruit growth
Abscisic Acid  | Leaves (drought stress) | Stomatal closure; seed dormancy
Ethylene       | Ripening fruit, nodes   | Fruit ripening; leaf/fruit abscission
Brassinosteroids| Young tissues          | Cell elongation; pollen tube growth
Jasmonates     | Damaged tissue          | Defence responses; wound signalling
Salicylic Acid | Systemically           | Systemic acquired resistance (SAR) to pathogens

Phototropism: Auxin redistributes to shaded side of shoot → greater elongation
→ shoot bends toward light.
Gravitropism: Auxin accumulates on lower side of horizontal root → inhibits
root elongation on lower side → root curves downward.

5. ADVANCED PHOTOSYNTHESIS
---------------------------
Light reactions (Thylakoid membrane):
  Photosystem II (P680): H2O oxidised → O2 + H+ + e-. Electrons passed to PQ.
  Cytochrome b6f complex: Pumps H+ into thylakoid lumen (contributes to PMF).
  Photosystem I (P700): Electrons re-energised; transferred via ferredoxin
  to FNR → NADP+ reduced to NADPH.
  ATP synthase (CF0-CF1): Proton gradient drives ATP synthesis (3H+ per ATP).

Calvin Cycle — 3 stages:
  Carbon Fixation  : CO2 + RuBP (5C) → 2x 3-phosphoglycerate (3C) [RuBisCO enzyme]
  Reduction        : 3-PGA → G3P using ATP + NADPH
  Regeneration     : G3P → RuBP using ATP (3 CO2 fixed per cycle turn)
  Net: 3CO2 → 1 G3P (used to build glucose, fatty acids, amino acids).

C4 Photosynthesis:
  Adaptation to hot, dry climates. CO2 first fixed in mesophyll cells by PEP
  carboxylase (higher CO2 affinity than RuBisCO) → Oxaloacetate → Malate →
  transported to bundle sheath cells → decarboxylated → CO2 released locally
  → concentrates CO2 around RuBisCO → suppresses photorespiration.
  Examples: Maize, sugarcane, sorghum.

CAM (Crassulacean Acid Metabolism):
  Adaptation to extreme aridity. Stomata open ONLY at night (reduces water loss).
  CO2 fixed by PEP carboxylase at night → stored as malate in vacuoles.
  During day: malate decarboxylated → CO2 feeds Calvin cycle with stomata closed.
  Examples: Cacti, agaves, pineapples, orchids.

6. PLANT DEFENCE & SIGNALLING
-------------------------------
Physical Defences:
  Thorns, spines, trichomes, thick bark, silica deposits, latex canals.

Chemical Defences:
- Terpenes        : Repel herbivores; toxic to insects (pyrethrin from chrysanthemum).
- Alkaloids       : Nicotine, caffeine, morphine, quinine — disrupt animal neurology.
- Phenolics/Tannins: Reduce palatability; inhibit digestion; astringent.
- Glucosinolates  : In Brassica; hydrolysed by myrosinase → toxic isothiocyanates.
- Protease inhibitors: Induced by wounding; impair herbivore digestion.

Systemic Acquired Resistance (SAR):
  Pathogen attack → salicylic acid (SA) synthesis → NPR1 activation →
  PR (pathogen-related) gene expression → broad-spectrum disease resistance.
  "Immunisation" of distal uninvaded tissues.

Jasmonate signalling (herbivory response):
  Herbivore feeding → wound signal (electrical + hydraulic) → jasmonate synthesis
  (COI1-JAZ pathway) → protease inhibitor production → systemic defence.
  Volatiles (green leaf volatiles) warn neighbouring plants.

Auxin polar transport:
  PIN proteins (efflux carriers) localised asymmetrically → directional auxin flow.
  PIN localisation regulated by endocytosis (clathrin-mediated) and phosphorylation
  by PINOID kinase. Basis for phyllotaxis (leaf/flower arrangement patterns).

[AGRICULTURAL LINK] Understanding plant biology drives crop improvement:
  - Green Revolution: Semi-dwarf wheat/rice varieties (Rht/sd1 mutations in
    gibberellin signalling) → reduced lodging + increased yield.
  - GMO crops: Bt cotton/corn (Bacillus thuringiensis toxin); herbicide-resistant
    soybean (EPSPS modification); Golden Rice (beta-carotene biosynthesis genes).
  - CRISPR in plants: Knockout of mlo gene → powdery mildew resistance in wheat.
