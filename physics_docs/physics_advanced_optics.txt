ADVANCED OPTICS - COMPLETE GUIDE
==================================
Wave Optics, Interference, Diffraction, Polarization, Lasers, Fiber Optics

PART 1: NATURE OF LIGHT

DUAL NATURE OF LIGHT:
Light behaves as both wave and particle.
Wave nature: interference, diffraction, polarization.
Particle nature: photoelectric effect, Compton scattering.
Wave-particle duality resolved by quantum mechanics: photon is a quantum of electromagnetic field.

ELECTROMAGNETIC WAVE:
Light is a transverse EM wave. E and B fields perpendicular to each other and to direction of propagation.
Speed in vacuum: c = 3 × 10⁸ m/s.
In medium: v = c/n (n = refractive index).
Wavelength: λ = λ₀/n (frequency unchanged, wavelength decreases in denser medium).
Energy of photon: E = hf = hc/λ.
Momentum of photon: p = h/λ = E/c.

HUYGENS' PRINCIPLE (detailed):
Every point on a wavefront acts as a secondary source of spherical wavelets.
New wavefront = envelope of all secondary wavelets after time t.
Explains: reflection, refraction, diffraction.
Backward waves don't exist (Huygens' assumption, explained by interference of secondary wavelets).

PART 2: INTERFERENCE

CONDITIONS FOR INTERFERENCE:
1. Coherent sources: constant phase difference (same frequency, fixed phase relationship).
2. Nearly equal amplitudes (for good contrast).
3. Sources close together (path difference < coherence length).
4. Monochromatic light (single wavelength for clear fringes).

COHERENCE:
Temporal coherence: correlation of wave with itself at different times. Related to bandwidth.
Coherence length: Lc = λ²/Δλ. Laser: very long. White light: ~1 μm.
Spatial coherence: correlation across wavefront. Determines source size for interference.

YOUNG'S DOUBLE SLIT EXPERIMENT (detailed):
Two slits separated by d, screen at distance D.
Path difference: Δ = d sin θ ≈ yd/D (for small θ).
Constructive interference (bright): Δ = nλ, n = 0, ±1, ±2, ...
Destructive interference (dark): Δ = (n + ½)λ.
Fringe width: β = λD/d.
Intensity: I = 4I₀cos²(δ/2), where δ = 2πΔ/λ = phase difference.
Maximum intensity: 4I₀ (constructive). Minimum: 0 (destructive).
Angular fringe width: θ = λ/d.

Effect of different parameters:
Increasing D: fringes wider (β ∝ D).
Increasing d: fringes narrower (β ∝ 1/d).
Increasing λ: fringes wider (β ∝ λ).
White light: central fringe white, colored fringes on either side (different λ gives different β).
One slit covered: single slit diffraction pattern.
One slit covered with glass of thickness t, refractive index n: fringe shift = (n-1)t/λ × β.

LLOYD'S MIRROR:
One real source + one virtual image source (in mirror).
Phase shift of π on reflection from denser medium.
Central fringe is DARK (not bright) due to this phase shift.

THIN FILM INTERFERENCE:
Light reflects from top and bottom surfaces of thin film.
Path difference = 2nt cos r (n = refractive index, t = thickness, r = angle of refraction).
Phase change: π at rarer→denser reflection. No change at denser→rarer.
For reflected light from film in air:
Minima (destructive for reflected): 2nt cos r = mλ.
Maxima (constructive): 2nt cos r = (m + ½)λ.
Applications: anti-reflection coatings (λ/4 thickness), soap bubbles, oil films.

Anti-reflection coating: n_coating = √(n_glass). Thickness = λ/4n. Destructive interference of reflected light.
High-reflection coating: constructive interference of reflected light.

NEWTON'S RINGS:
Plano-convex lens on flat glass surface. Air wedge between them.
Circular fringes due to varying air gap thickness.
Radius of mth dark ring: rₘ = √(mλR) (R = radius of curvature of lens).
Radius of mth bright ring: rₘ = √((m+½)λR).
Center: dark (destructive, phase change at one surface).
Used to find wavelength or radius of curvature.

MICHELSON INTERFEROMETER:
Beam splitter divides light into two paths (arms), reflects back, recombines.
Path difference = 2(L₁ - L₂) where L₁, L₂ are arm lengths.
Moving mirror by λ/2 shifts fringes by one fringe.
Applications:
- Measure wavelengths very precisely.
- Measure refractive indices.
- Detect gravitational waves (LIGO uses this principle, but km-scale!).
- Verify constancy of speed of light (Michelson-Morley experiment, 1887).

PART 3: DIFFRACTION

DIFFRACTION: bending of waves around obstacles or through openings.
Significant when: slit width ≈ wavelength.
Fresnel diffraction: source/screen at finite distance (near-field).
Fraunhofer diffraction: source/screen effectively at infinity (far-field, parallel rays). Simpler math.

SINGLE SLIT DIFFRACTION (Fraunhofer):
Slit width a.
Condition for minima: a sin θ = mλ, m = ±1, ±2, ...
Width of central maximum: 2λ/a (angular), 2λD/a (linear on screen).
Central maximum is twice as wide as secondary maxima.
Intensity: I = I₀ [sin(α)/α]² where α = πa sinθ/λ.
Secondary maxima at: tan α = α (approximately at α = 3π/2, 5π/2, ...).
Intensity of secondary maxima: I₁ ≈ 4.7% I₀, I₂ ≈ 1.7% I₀.

DOUBLE SLIT DIFFRACTION:
Combination of single slit diffraction envelope × double slit interference pattern.
I = I₀ [sin(α)/α]² × cos²(β)
α = πa sinθ/λ (single slit), β = πd sinθ/λ (double slit).
Missing orders: when interference maximum coincides with diffraction minimum.
n×(λ/d) = m×(λ/a) → n/m = d/a. If d/a = integer, those orders missing.

DIFFRACTION GRATING:
N slits, each of width a, separated by d (grating spacing).
Principal maxima: d sin θ = mλ (m = grating order).
Resolving power: R = λ/Δλ = mN (m = order, N = total slits illuminated).
Dispersion: dθ/dλ = m/(d cos θ).
For good resolution: use higher order m or more slits N.
Used in spectroscopy to measure wavelengths precisely.

RESOLVING POWER:
Rayleigh criterion: two sources just resolved when central maximum of one falls on first minimum of other.
For circular aperture: θ_min = 1.22λ/D (D = diameter).
For telescope: minimum resolvable angle = 1.22λ/D.
For microscope: minimum resolvable distance = 0.61λ/n sin α (where n sin α = numerical aperture).
Higher resolution: shorter λ or larger aperture.
Oil immersion microscope: increases n → improves resolution.
Electron microscope: much shorter λ than light → much better resolution.

X-RAY DIFFRACTION:
Bragg's law: 2d sin θ = nλ (d = interplanar spacing).
X-rays diffract off crystal planes (d ~ 0.1-0.3 nm, similar to X-ray wavelength).
Used to determine crystal structure (DNA double helix discovered this way).
Powder diffraction: many randomly oriented crystals → rings instead of spots.

PART 4: POLARIZATION

TRANSVERSE WAVES CAN BE POLARIZED. Longitudinal waves (sound) cannot.
Unpolarized light: E field vibrates in all transverse directions randomly.
Plane polarized (linearly polarized): E vibrates in one plane only.
Circularly polarized: E rotates in a circle (equal amplitudes, 90° phase difference).
Elliptically polarized: E traces an ellipse (general case).

METHODS OF POLARIZATION:

1. Polaroid (Selective absorption):
Contains long chain molecules aligned. Absorbs one component of E.
Transmission axis: direction of E that passes through.
Absorbs E perpendicular to transmission axis.

Malus's Law: I = I₀ cos²θ (θ = angle between polarizer and analyzer).
Unpolarized → polarizer: I = I₀/2.

2. Reflection (Brewster's Law):
At Brewster's angle θ_B: reflected ray is completely plane polarized (perpendicular to plane of incidence).
tan θ_B = n₂/n₁ = n (for air to medium).
At θ_B: reflected and refracted rays are perpendicular to each other.
θ_B + θ_r = 90°.
Water: θ_B ≈ 53°. Glass: θ_B ≈ 57°.
Application: polaroid sunglasses reduce glare (reflected light is polarized horizontally).

3. Refraction (birefringence):
Birefringent crystals (calcite, quartz): different refractive indices for different polarizations.
Ordinary ray (o-ray): follows Snell's law, speed same in all directions.
Extraordinary ray (e-ray): speed varies with direction.
Double refraction: incident ray splits into two rays.
Wave plate (retarder): birefringent material of specific thickness.
Quarter wave plate: retardation of λ/4 → converts linear to circular polarization (and vice versa).
Half wave plate: retardation of λ/2 → rotates plane of polarization by 2θ.

4. Scattering:
Scattered light is polarized. Sky light is partially polarized (Rayleigh scattering).
At 90° to sun: maximum polarization.

OPTICAL ACTIVITY:
Some materials rotate plane of polarization (optically active).
Dextrorotatory (+): rotates clockwise (as viewed facing light).
Levorotatory (−): rotates anticlockwise.
Specific rotation [α] = α/(l × c) (l = path length in dm, c = concentration in g/mL).
Each enantiomer rotates equally but in opposite direction.
Saccharimeter: measures optical rotation → determines sugar concentration.

PART 5: LASERS

LASER: Light Amplification by Stimulated Emission of Radiation.

PRINCIPLES:
Stimulated emission: photon hitting excited atom → two identical photons (same frequency, phase, direction).
Population inversion: more atoms in excited state than ground state (non-equilibrium condition needed for laser action).
Optical cavity (resonator): mirrors at ends → light bounces back and forth → amplified.

THREE-LEVEL vs FOUR-LEVEL LASER:
Three-level (ruby): ground state as lower laser level. Harder to achieve population inversion (must excite > 50% of atoms).
Four-level (Nd:YAG, He-Ne): lower laser level is different from ground state → empties quickly → easier inversion.

PROPERTIES OF LASER LIGHT:
Monochromatic: single wavelength (narrow bandwidth).
Coherent: all photons in phase (temporal and spatial coherence).
Collimated: very low divergence (parallel beam).
High intensity: power concentrated in narrow beam.

TYPES OF LASERS:
Gas lasers: He-Ne (632.8 nm, red), Ar⁺ (488, 514 nm), CO₂ (10.6 μm, IR, high power).
Solid state: Ruby (694 nm), Nd:YAG (1064 nm), Ti:Sapphire (tunable 650-1100 nm).
Semiconductor diode lasers: compact, efficient, used in CD/DVD, fiber optics, laser pointers.
Excimer lasers: UV, pulsed. ArF (193 nm), KrF (248 nm). Eye surgery (LASIK).
Dye lasers: tunable wavelength (organic dye dissolved in solvent).
Free electron lasers: tunable, very high power.

APPLICATIONS OF LASERS:
Medical: LASIK eye surgery, cancer treatment (photodynamic therapy), laser surgery, dental.
Industrial: cutting, welding, engraving, 3D printing (SLA).
Communications: fiber optic communications (semiconductor lasers).
Research: spectroscopy, interferometry, trapping atoms, fusion research.
Military: rangefinders, target designators.
Consumer: CD/DVD/Blu-ray, barcode scanners, laser printers, pointers.

PART 6: FIBER OPTICS

PRINCIPLE: Total Internal Reflection (TIR).
Light guided along fiber by repeated TIR.
Core (high n) surrounded by cladding (lower n).
Critical angle: sin θ_c = n_clad/n_core.
Acceptance angle: θ_a = √(n_core² - n_clad²). Numerical aperture (NA).

TYPES OF OPTICAL FIBER:
Single mode: very thin core (~8 μm), one ray path, no modal dispersion. Long distance, high bandwidth.
Multimode step-index: wider core (~50-100 μm), many ray paths, modal dispersion limits bandwidth.
Multimode graded-index: refractive index decreases from center → rays travel curved paths → reduces modal dispersion.

FIBER OPTIC COMMUNICATION:
Transmitter: laser or LED converts electrical signal to light.
Fiber: guides light over long distances (attenuation ~0.2 dB/km at 1550 nm).
Repeater/amplifier: EDFA (erbium-doped fiber amplifier) amplifies without converting to electrical.
Receiver: photodetector converts light back to electrical signal.
Wavelength Division Multiplexing (WDM): multiple wavelengths in one fiber → multiplies bandwidth.
Applications: internet backbone, telephone, cable TV, medical (endoscopes), sensors.

DISPERSION IN FIBERS:
Modal dispersion: different modes travel at different speeds. Eliminated in single mode fiber.
Chromatic dispersion: different wavelengths travel at different speeds.
Material dispersion: refractive index varies with wavelength.
Waveguide dispersion: propagation constant varies with wavelength.
Zero dispersion wavelength: 1310 nm (standard single mode fiber). 1550 nm with dispersion-shifted fiber.

PART 7: PHOTONICS AND MODERN OPTICS

NONLINEAR OPTICS:
At high intensities (lasers), medium responds nonlinearly.
Second harmonic generation (SHG): 2ω output. Doubles frequency (halves wavelength). Green laser pointers from IR diode.
Sum/difference frequency generation.
Optical parametric oscillation: one photon → two photons.
Self-phase modulation, solitons in fibers.

HOLOGRAPHY:
Records both amplitude AND phase information using interference.
Reference beam + object beam → interference pattern on film.
Reconstruction: illuminate with reference beam → 3D image appears.
Dennis Gabor (Nobel 1971).
Applications: security holograms (credit cards), data storage, displays, interferometry.

OPTICAL COHERENCE TOMOGRAPHY (OCT):
Medical imaging using low-coherence interferometry.
Cross-sectional images of tissue (like ultrasound but with light).
Resolution ~10 μm. Used in ophthalmology (retinal imaging), cardiology.

PHOTONIC CRYSTALS:
Periodic dielectric structures with photonic bandgap (wavelengths that cannot propagate).
Analog of electronic bandgap in semiconductors.
Applications: low-loss waveguides, LEDs, lasers, sensors.

METAMATERIALS:
Engineered structures with properties not found in nature.
Negative refractive index → perfect lenses (beyond diffraction limit), cloaking.
Still largely experimental.

IMPORTANT FORMULAS SUMMARY:
Young's double slit: β = λD/d, Δ = yd/D.
Single slit minima: a sin θ = mλ.
Diffraction grating: d sin θ = mλ.
Resolving power grating: R = mN.
Rayleigh criterion: θ = 1.22λ/D.
Bragg's law: 2d sin θ = nλ.
Malus's law: I = I₀cos²θ.
Brewster's law: tan θ_B = n.
Newton's rings (dark): rₘ = √(mλR).
Thin film (bright, reflected): 2nt = (m+½)λ.
