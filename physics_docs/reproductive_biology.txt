1. MODES OF REPRODUCTION
--------------------------
Asexual Reproduction:
  One parent; offspring genetically identical (clones). Rapid; no mate required.
  - Binary fission    : Bacteria split into two equal cells.
  - Budding           : Offspring grows from parent body (yeast, Hydra).
  - Fragmentation     : Body parts regenerate into whole organisms (planaria, starfish).
  - Parthenogenesis   : Egg develops without fertilisation (aphids, Komodo dragons,
                        some sharks and snakes — facultative parthenogenesis).
  - Vegetative prop.  : Plants via runners (strawberry), tubers (potato), bulbs (onion).
  - Sporulation       : Fungi, mosses, ferns produce haploid spores.

Sexual Reproduction:
  Two parents; genetic recombination; offspring genetically diverse.
  Advantages: Increases variation → better adaptation to changing environments;
  removes harmful mutations (Muller's ratchet); Red Queen hypothesis — sexual
  reproduction is an evolutionary arms race with parasites.
  Disadvantages: Costly (two-fold cost of males); requires finding mate; dilutes
  genome by half each generation.

1.1 HUMAN MALE REPRODUCTIVE SYSTEM
-------------------------------------
Organs: Testes, epididymis, vas deferens, seminal vesicles, prostate, urethra, penis.
Testes: Produce sperm (spermatogenesis) and testosterone (Leydig cells).
  Temperature: Sperm production requires ~2–3°C below core body temp → external testes.
Spermatogenesis:
  Spermatogonia → Primary spermatocyte (meiosis I) → Secondary spermatocyte
  (meiosis II) → Spermatid → Spermatozoon (mature sperm).
  Duration: ~74 days. Sertoli cells support and nourish developing sperm.
  Production: ~300 million sperm/day.

Sperm structure: Head (nucleus + acrosome), midpiece (mitochondria for ATP),
tail (flagellum for motility).

1.2 HUMAN FEMALE REPRODUCTIVE SYSTEM
---------------------------------------
Organs: Ovaries, fallopian tubes, uterus, cervix, vagina.
Oogenesis: Begins before birth — primary oocytes arrested in prophase I until puberty.
  One oocyte completes meiosis I per cycle; meiosis II only completed if fertilised.
  At birth: ~1–2 million primary oocytes. At puberty: ~400,000. Lifetime ovulations: ~400.

Menstrual Cycle (~28 days):
  Follicular phase (days 1–14): FSH → follicle growth → oestrogen rises →
  LH surge (day 13) → ovulation (day 14).
  Luteal phase (days 14–28): Corpus luteum → progesterone + oestrogen →
  endometrium thickens. If no fertilisation → corpus luteum degenerates →
  hormone drop → menstruation.

2. FERTILISATION & EARLY EMBRYOGENESIS
----------------------------------------
Fertilisation Events:
  Capacitation: Sperm undergo changes in female reproductive tract → become
  hyperactivated + able to acrosome react.
  Chemotaxis: Oocyte releases chemoattractants (progesterone) → guides sperm.
  Zona binding: Sperm ZP3 receptor binds zona pellucida glycoprotein ZP3.
  Acrosome reaction: Lysosomal enzymes released → digest zona pellucida.
  Cortical reaction: Ca2+ wave releases cortical granules → zona hardening
  (fast block: membrane potential change; slow block: zona hardening) → prevents polyspermy.
  Pronuclei formation: Sperm and egg nuclei each form pronuclei → fuse → 2N zygote.

Preimplantation development:
  Day 1–2: Cleavage divisions (totipotent blastomeres).
  Day 3–4: Morula (16 cells); compaction (tight junctions + gap junctions form).
  Day 4–5: Blastocyst; cavitation forms blastocoel; ICM (epiblast + hypoblast) + trophoblast.
  Day 6–10: Implantation in uterine wall; trophoblast invades decidua.
  Week 2: Bilaminar disc (epiblast + hypoblast). Yolk sac + amniotic cavity form.
  Week 3: Gastrulation → trilaminar disc.

Placenta Development:
  Trophoblast differentiates into cytotrophoblast and syncytiotrophoblast.
  Syncytiotrophoblast invades maternal spiral arteries → remodels them (loss of
  smooth muscle → low-resistance, high-flow vessels).
  Failure of spiral artery remodelling → pre-eclampsia.
  Placental functions: Gas/nutrient/waste exchange; hormone production (hCG, oestrogen,
  progesterone); immune tolerance (expresses HLA-G to prevent NK cell attack).

3. HORMONAL CONTROL OF REPRODUCTION
--------------------------------------
HPG Axis (Hypothalamic-Pituitary-Gonadal):
  GnRH (pulsatile, hypothalamus) → FSH + LH (anterior pituitary) → gonads.
  Testosterone/oestrogen → negative feedback on hypothalamus and pituitary.
  Exception: Pre-ovulatory oestrogen surge → positive feedback → LH surge.

Hormone roles in males:
  FSH → Sertoli cells → supports spermatogenesis; produces inhibin B (FSH feedback).
  LH → Leydig cells → testosterone synthesis.
  Testosterone → male secondary sex characteristics; muscle mass; libido;
  converted to DHT (5-alpha reductase) → prostate, hair follicles;
  converted to oestradiol (aromatase) → bone density; male.

Hormone roles in females:
  FSH → follicle growth; oestrogen production.
  LH → ovulation trigger; corpus luteum formation; progesterone production.
  Oestrogen → endometrial proliferation; female secondary sex characteristics;
  bone density; positive feedback on LH surge.
  Progesterone → endometrial secretion; maintains pregnancy; inhibits uterine
  contractions; raises basal body temperature.
  hCG → maintains corpus luteum in early pregnancy; basis of pregnancy test.

4. CONTRACEPTION & ASSISTED REPRODUCTION
------------------------------------------
Contraception Methods:
Method                 | Mechanism                          | Efficacy (typical use)
-----------------------|------------------------------------|----------------------
Combined OCP           | Inhibits ovulation (oestrogen/prog)| ~91%
Progestogen-only pill  | Cervical mucus thickening + ovul.  | ~91%
IUD (copper)           | Toxic to sperm; prevents implant.  | >99%
IUS (Mirena)           | Progesterone; thin endometrium     | >99%
Condom                 | Barrier; STI protection            | ~85%
Vasectomy/tubal ligation| Permanent; blocks gamete transport| >99%
Emergency contraception| Delays/prevents ovulation          | ~85–89%

Assisted Reproductive Technologies (ART):
  IUI (Intrauterine Insemination): Washed sperm placed directly in uterus.
  IVF (In Vitro Fertilisation): Ovarian hyperstimulation (FSH injections) →
  egg retrieval → fertilisation in lab → embryo transfer to uterus.
  ICSI (Intracytoplasmic Sperm Injection): Single sperm injected into oocyte.
  Preimplantation Genetic Testing (PGT): Biopsy of 5–8 cell blastomere or TE
  cells → genetic analysis before transfer. Screens for aneuploidy (PGT-A) or
  single gene disorders (PGT-M).
  Cryopreservation: Embryo/oocyte vitrification (ultra-rapid freezing) → storage.
  Success rates: ~40–50% per cycle for women <35 years; declines with age.

5. MEIOSIS IN DEPTH
--------------------
Meiosis I (Reductional division):
  Prophase I (longest): Homologous chromosomes synapse (synapsis) → bivalents.
    Synaptonemal complex (SC): Protein scaffold holding homologs together.
    Recombination nodules mark crossover sites.
    Leptotene → Zygotene → Pachytene (crossovers occur) → Diplotene → Diakinesis.
  Metaphase I: Bivalents align at metaphase plate; kinetochores of homologs
    face opposite poles (amphitelic orientation).
  Anaphase I: Homologs separated (not sister chromatids); chiasma resolution.
  Telophase I/Cytokinesis I: Two haploid cells (each with sister chromatid pairs).

Meiosis II (Equational division):
  Resembles mitosis; sister chromatids separated. Four haploid cells produced.

Recombination (Crossing Over):
  SPO11 enzyme creates double-strand breaks → 5' resection → single-strand invasion
  of homolog (strand exchange mediated by RAD51, DMC1) → Holliday junction →
  resolution → crossover or non-crossover.
  At least 1 crossover per chromosome arm required for proper segregation.
  ~2–3 crossovers per human chromosome per meiosis.

Non-Disjunction & Aneuploidy:
  Failure of chromosomes to separate properly → extra or missing chromosomes.
  Trisomy 21 (Down syndrome): Extra chromosome 21; maternal non-disjunction in
  meiosis I (age-dependent → older mothers at higher risk).
  Trisomy 18 (Edwards), Trisomy 13 (Patau): Usually fatal.
  Sex chromosome aneuploidies: Klinefelter (XXY), Turner (45,X), XYY, XXX.

6. EPIGENETIC REPROGRAMMING IN REPRODUCTION
---------------------------------------------
Global epigenetic reprogramming occurs at two stages:
  1. Fertilisation → Early embryo: Genome-wide demethylation (except imprinted
     loci and some retrotransposons). Paternal genome rapidly demethylated
     (active, TET-mediated). Maternal genome passively demethylated during cleavage.
     De novo methylation restored after implantation by DNMT3A/3B.
  2. Primordial Germ Cells (PGCs): Near-complete demethylation (including
     imprinted loci) → sex-specific re-imprinting in germline.

Transgenerational Epigenetic Inheritance:
  Some epigenetic marks escape reprogramming → transmitted to offspring.
  Examples: Agouti mouse (maternal diet affects coat colour via methylation);
  Dutch Hunger Winter — grandchildren of famine-exposed women showed altered
  IGF2 methylation; effects on metabolism and disease risk.
  Mechanisms: Small RNAs (piRNAs, siRNAs), retained histone modifications,
  partially methylated regions escaping reprogramming.

[CLINICAL LINK] Recurrent Pregnancy Loss (RPL): Affects ~1–2% of couples.
Causes: Chromosomal abnormalities (~50%); uterine anomalies; thrombophilias
(Factor V Leiden, antiphospholipid syndrome); endocrine (thyroid, PCOS);
immunological (NK cell dysfunction); unexplained (~50%).
Pre-eclampsia: Defective spiral artery remodelling → placental ischaemia →
sFlt1 + sEng release → systemic endothelial dysfunction → hypertension + proteinuria.
Treatment: Aspirin prophylaxis (from 12 weeks); antihypertensives; delivery.
