1. ECOLOGY — LEVELS OF ORGANIZATION
--------------------------------------
Ecology is the study of how organisms interact with each other and their environment.

Levels of Ecological Organization:
  Individual → Population → Community → Ecosystem → Biome → Biosphere

- Individual    : A single organism and its adaptations to the environment.
- Population    : All individuals of the same species in an area.
- Community     : All populations of different species in an area.
- Ecosystem     : A community plus its non-living (abiotic) environment.
- Biome         : Large regions with similar climate and characteristic biota.
- Biosphere     : All ecosystems on Earth; the global sum of all life.

2. EVOLUTION — CORE CONCEPTS
------------------------------
Evolution is change in the heritable characteristics of a population over time.
Charles Darwin (1859, "On the Origin of Species") proposed Natural Selection.

Darwin's Four Postulates:
  1. Variation: Individuals in a population differ in their traits.
  2. Heritability: Traits are passed from parents to offspring.
  3. Differential Survival: Some traits improve survival and reproduction.
  4. Selection: Individuals with favourable traits reproduce more successfully.

Key Terms:
- Fitness          : An organism's reproductive success relative to others.
- Adaptation       : An inherited trait that increases fitness in an environment.
- Natural Selection : The mechanism driving evolution; "survival of the fittest."
- Speciation       : The formation of new species from existing ones.

3. POPULATION ECOLOGY
----------------------
Population dynamics describe how and why population sizes change over time.

Exponential Growth (unlimited resources):
  dN/dt = rN
  N = population size; r = intrinsic growth rate; t = time
  Produces a J-shaped curve. Possible only when resources are unlimited.

Logistic Growth (limited resources):
  dN/dt = rN × (K - N) / K
  K = carrying capacity (maximum sustainable population size).
  Produces an S-shaped (sigmoidal) curve. Population stabilises at K.

Factors limiting population growth:
  Density-dependent : Competition, predation, disease, starvation (intensify
                      as population grows).
  Density-independent: Natural disasters, climate change, habitat destruction
                       (affect population regardless of size).

Life History Strategies:
  r-selected species: High reproduction, small body size, short lifespan,
                      little parental care. Example: mice, insects.
  K-selected species: Low reproduction, large body size, long lifespan,
                      high parental investment. Example: elephants, humans.

4. COMMUNITY ECOLOGY
---------------------
4.1 Trophic Levels & Energy Flow:
  Producers (autotrophs) → Primary Consumers → Secondary Consumers →
  Tertiary Consumers → Decomposers

  10% Rule: Only ~10% of energy is transferred between trophic levels;
            90% lost as heat and metabolic activity.

4.2 Food Webs:
  Complex networks of feeding relationships within a community. Keystone species
  have disproportionately large effects on ecosystem structure relative to their
  biomass (e.g., sea otters controlling sea urchin populations → kelp forests).

4.3 Ecological Succession:
  Primary Succession: Colonisation of bare rock/land with no prior soil.
    Pioneer species (e.g., lichens, mosses) break down rock → soil formation.
  Secondary Succession: Recovery after disturbance; soil remains.
    Faster than primary; mid-succession shrubs → climax community (stable forest).

5. BIOGEOCHEMICAL CYCLES
--------------------------
Carbon Cycle:
  CO2 fixed by photosynthesis → stored in organic molecules → released by
  respiration, decomposition, combustion. Ocean is major carbon sink.
  Human impact: Fossil fuel burning raises atmospheric CO2 → climate change.

Nitrogen Cycle:
  N2 (unusable) → Nitrogen fixation (Rhizobium bacteria) → NH3/NH4+ →
  Nitrification (NH4+ → NO2- → NO3-) → Plant uptake → Decomposition →
  Denitrification (NO3- → N2, back to atmosphere).

Water Cycle:
  Evaporation + Transpiration (evapotranspiration) → Condensation →
  Precipitation → Runoff/Infiltration → back to ocean/groundwater.

Phosphorus Cycle:
  No atmospheric phase. Weathering of rocks releases PO4³- → absorbed by plants
  → consumed by animals → returned via decomposition.

5. MECHANISMS OF EVOLUTION
----------------------------
Beyond natural selection, other forces drive evolution:

Genetic Drift      : Random change in allele frequencies; most impactful in
                     small populations.
  - Founder Effect : Small group establishes new population (e.g., Amish
                     community with high Ellis-van Creveld syndrome).
  - Bottleneck Effect: Severe population reduction; random alleles survive
                     (e.g., cheetahs have very low genetic diversity).

Gene Flow          : Migration of individuals carrying alleles between
                     populations; increases genetic diversity.

Mutation           : Ultimate source of all new genetic variation.

Sexual Selection   : Traits that improve mating success (not just survival)
                     are selected for. Example: peacock tail feathers.

6. POPULATION GENETICS & HARDY-WEINBERG EQUILIBRIUM
-----------------------------------------------------
The Hardy-Weinberg (H-W) principle describes a non-evolving population.
Allele frequencies remain constant generation-to-generation IF:
  1. No mutation
  2. No gene flow
  3. Very large population (no genetic drift)
  4. Random mating (panmixia)
  5. No natural selection

H-W Equations:
  p + q = 1   (allele frequencies; p = dominant A, q = recessive a)
  p² + 2pq + q² = 1   (genotype frequencies)
  p² = AA frequency; 2pq = Aa frequency; q² = aa frequency

Deviation from H-W indicates that evolution is occurring.
Used to calculate carrier frequencies in genetic counselling.
Example: If q² (affected) = 1/10,000, then q = 1/100; carrier freq (2pq) ≈ 1/50.

7. SPECIATION
--------------
Speciation = formation of new species that are reproductively isolated.

Allopatric Speciation (most common):
  Geographic barrier (mountain, river, ocean) separates populations →
  populations evolve independently → reproductive isolation → new species.
  Example: Darwin's finches (Galapagos Islands); squirrels either side of Grand Canyon.

Sympatric Speciation:
  New species arise within the same geographic range without physical separation.
  Mechanisms: polyploidy (common in plants — e.g., wheat is hexaploid);
  ecological niche divergence; sexual selection.

Reproductive Isolating Mechanisms:
  Pre-zygotic barriers (prevent mating/fertilisation):
    - Habitat isolation   : Different habitats; rarely encounter each other.
    - Temporal isolation  : Different breeding seasons.
    - Behavioural isolation: Different mating calls, dances, or signals.
    - Mechanical isolation : Incompatible reproductive structures.
    - Gametic isolation   : Gametes cannot fertilise each other.
  Post-zygotic barriers (hybrid is unfit):
    - Hybrid inviability  : Hybrid embryo fails to develop.
    - Hybrid sterility    : Hybrid is sterile (e.g., mule = horse x donkey).
    - Hybrid breakdown    : F1 fertile but F2 offspring inviable/sterile.

8. PHYLOGENETICS & SYSTEMATICS
--------------------------------
Phylogenetics reconstructs evolutionary relationships.

Key concepts:
- Cladogram/Phylogenetic tree: Diagram showing evolutionary relationships.
- Clade (monophyletic group): Ancestor + ALL its descendants.
- Synapomorphy: Shared derived character that defines a clade.
- Homology: Same structure derived from common ancestor (bat wing, human arm).
- Analogy (homoplasy): Similar function but different evolutionary origin
  (bird wing, insect wing — convergent evolution).

Tree of Life — Three Domains:
  Bacteria → Archaea → Eukarya (Plants, Fungi, Animals, Protists)
  Eukarya and Archaea are sister groups (more closely related to each other).

Molecular Clocks:
  Neutral mutations accumulate at roughly constant rates. By comparing sequences,
  scientists estimate divergence times between lineages.
  Example: Human-chimpanzee divergence ~6–7 million years ago.

9. CONSERVATION BIOLOGY & CLIMATE CHANGE
-----------------------------------------
Biodiversity Metrics:
  Alpha diversity  : Species richness within a habitat.
  Beta diversity   : Difference in species composition between habitats.
  Gamma diversity  : Total diversity across a landscape.

Threats to Biodiversity (HIPPO):
  H - Habitat destruction (leading cause)
  I - Invasive species
  P - Pollution
  P - Population growth (human)
  O - Overhunting/Overfishing

Conservation Strategies:
- Protected areas (national parks, marine reserves)
- Habitat corridors connecting fragmented landscapes
- Captive breeding and reintroduction programmes
- Ex-situ conservation (seed banks, zoos)
- Biodiversity hotspots prioritisation (e.g., tropical Andes, Madagascar)

Climate Change Impacts on Ecosystems:
- Phenological shifts: Earlier flowering, migration timing mismatches.
- Range shifts: Species moving poleward or to higher altitudes.
- Coral bleaching: Ocean warming → symbiotic algae (zooxanthellae) expelled.
- Ocean acidification: Rising CO2 → H2CO3 → lower pH → affects calcification
  in molluscs, corals, echinoderms.
- Trophic cascades disrupted by asynchronous seasonal timing.
