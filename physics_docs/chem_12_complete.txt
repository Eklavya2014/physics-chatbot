CHAPTER 1: SOLUTIONS

TYPES OF SOLUTIONS: solid-solid (alloys), liquid-liquid, gas-liquid.
Solubility: maximum amount of solute that dissolves at given temperature.
Henry's law: solubility of gas ∝ partial pressure. p = K_H × x

COLLIGATIVE PROPERTIES (depend on number, not nature, of solute particles):

1. Relative lowering of vapour pressure:
(P° - P)/P° = X_solute = n/(N+n) (Raoult's law)

2. Elevation of boiling point:
ΔT_b = K_b × m (K_b = ebullioscopic constant, m = molality)

3. Depression of freezing point:
ΔT_f = K_f × m (K_f = cryoscopic constant)
K_f for water = 1.86 K·kg/mol
Used to determine molar mass: M = (1000 × K_f × w)/(ΔT_f × W)

4. Osmotic pressure:
π = MRT (M = molarity, R = gas constant, T = temperature)
Osmosis: flow of solvent from dilute to concentrated solution through semipermeable membrane.
Isotonic: same osmotic pressure. Hypertonic: higher concentration. Hypotonic: lower.

VAN'T HOFF FACTOR (i):
For electrolytes: i > 1 (dissociation). For non-electrolytes: i = 1.
For association: i < 1.
Modified colligative properties: ΔT_b = i × K_b × m

CHAPTER 2: ELECTROCHEMISTRY

ELECTROCHEMICAL CELLS:
Galvanic cell: converts chemical energy to electrical energy.
Electrolytic cell: converts electrical energy to chemical energy.

GALVANIC CELL (Daniell Cell):
Zn | Zn²⁺ || Cu²⁺ | Cu
Anode (-): oxidation occurs. Zn → Zn²⁺ + 2e⁻
Cathode (+): reduction occurs. Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Cu
Cell potential: E_cell = E_cathode - E_anode
E°_cell = E°_cathode - E°_anode = 1.10 V (for Daniell cell)
Salt bridge: maintains electrical neutrality by allowing ion flow.

STANDARD ELECTRODE POTENTIAL:
Measured relative to Standard Hydrogen Electrode (SHE) = 0.00 V.
More positive E°: better oxidizing agent. More negative E°: better reducing agent.
E°(Cu²⁺/Cu) = +0.34 V, E°(Zn²⁺/Zn) = -0.76 V

NERNST EQUATION:
E = E° - (RT/nF) ln Q = E° - (0.0592/n) log Q (at 25°C)
n = number of electrons, Q = reaction quotient.
At equilibrium: E = 0, Q = K → log K = nE°/0.0592

GIBBS ENERGY: ΔG° = -nFE° (F = Faraday = 96485 C/mol)
ΔG = -nFE

CONDUCTANCE:
Resistance: R = ρ × l/A (ρ = resistivity)
Conductance: G = 1/R (Siemens, S)
Conductivity: κ = 1/ρ = G × l/A (S/m)
Molar conductivity: Λ_m = κ/C (C = concentration in mol/m³)

Kohlrausch's law: Λ°_m = ν₊λ°₊ + ν₋λ°₋
(limiting molar conductivity = sum of ionic conductivities)
Strong electrolytes: Λ_m = Λ°_m - b√C (Debye-Hückel-Onsager equation)

FARADAY'S LAWS OF ELECTROLYSIS:
1st law: mass deposited ∝ charge passed. m = ZIt = ZQ
2nd law: mass ∝ equivalent weight. m₁/m₂ = E₁/E₂
Electrochemical equivalent Z = M/(nF)

BATTERIES:
Primary (non-rechargeable): Dry cell (Leclanché), Mercury cell.
Secondary (rechargeable): Lead storage battery, Nickel-cadmium, Lithium ion.
Lead-acid battery: Pb + PbO₂ + 2H₂SO₄ → 2PbSO₄ + 2H₂O, E = 2V per cell (12V car battery = 6 cells).
Fuel cell: H₂ and O₂ combine to give water and electricity continuously.

CORROSION:
Electrochemical process. Fe acts as anode, C (impurity) as cathode.
Fe → Fe²⁺ + 2e⁻ (anode). O₂ + H₂O + 4e⁻ → 4OH⁻ (cathode).
Fe²⁺ + 2OH⁻ → Fe(OH)₂ → Fe₂O₃·xH₂O (rust).
Prevention: galvanizing, cathodic protection, paint, alloying.

CHAPTER 3: CHEMICAL KINETICS

RATE OF REACTION: change in concentration per unit time.
Rate = -d[A]/dt = +d[B]/dt
Instantaneous rate: slope of concentration-time curve at that point.

RATE LAW: Rate = k[A]^m[B]^n
k = rate constant (depends on T, not concentration).
m, n = order with respect to each reactant (determined experimentally).
Overall order = m + n.

ZERO ORDER: Rate = k, [A] = [A]₀ - kt, t₁/₂ = [A]₀/2k
FIRST ORDER: Rate = k[A], [A] = [A]₀ e^(-kt), t₁/₂ = 0.693/k (independent of concentration!)
SECOND ORDER: Rate = k[A]², 1/[A] = 1/[A]₀ + kt, t₁/₂ = 1/(k[A]₀)

INTEGRATED RATE LAWS:
First order: log[A] = log[A]₀ - kt/2.303. Graph of log[A] vs t is straight line with slope -k/2.303.
Radioactive decay follows first-order kinetics.

ACTIVATION ENERGY (Eₐ):
Minimum energy required for reaction to occur.
Transition state theory: reactants pass through high-energy transition state.
Arrhenius equation: k = A e^(-Eₐ/RT)
ln k = ln A - Eₐ/RT
log(k₂/k₁) = (Eₐ/2.303R)(1/T₁ - 1/T₂)
A = pre-exponential/frequency factor.

Effect of temperature: 10°C rise roughly doubles rate (rule of thumb).

COLLISION THEORY:
Rate = Z × f × p (Z = collision frequency, f = fraction with E > Eₐ, p = steric factor)
f = e^(-Eₐ/RT)

CATALYSIS:
Catalyst: increases reaction rate by providing alternative pathway with lower Eₐ.
Does NOT change equilibrium position or ΔG.
Homogeneous: same phase as reactants (H₂SO₄ in esterification).
Heterogeneous: different phase (Pt in hydrogenation).
Enzyme: biological catalyst (highly specific, very efficient).
Inhibitor: decreases reaction rate (anti-catalyst).

CHAPTER 4: ORGANIC CHEMISTRY

NOMENCLATURE (IUPAC):
1. Find longest carbon chain (parent chain).
2. Number from end giving lowest locants to substituents.
3. Name substituents as prefixes.
4. Use suffixes for functional groups.

ISOMERISM:
Structural: same formula, different connectivity.
- Chain isomerism: different carbon skeletons.
- Position isomerism: different position of substituent/bond.
- Functional group isomerism: different functional groups.
- Metamerism: different alkyl groups on same functional group.

Stereoisomerism: same connectivity, different spatial arrangement.
- Geometrical (cis-trans): restricted rotation (double bonds, rings).
- Optical: non-superimposable mirror images (chiral carbon).

REACTIONS:

SUBSTITUTION:
Nucleophilic (SN1, SN2): at sp3 carbon.
SN2: one step, inversion of configuration, bimolecular. Favored by primary alkyl halides.
SN1: two steps, racemization, unimolecular. Favored by tertiary (stable carbocation).
Electrophilic (EAS): on benzene ring.
Free radical: alkanes with halogens in UV light.

ADDITION:
Electrophilic addition (EAS): to alkenes and alkynes.
Markovnikov's rule: H adds to carbon with more H (less substituted).
Anti-Markovnikov (peroxide effect): H adds to more substituted carbon (free radical mechanism).
Nucleophilic addition: to carbonyl compounds (aldehydes, ketones).

ELIMINATION:
E1, E2: opposite of substitution.
Saytzeff's rule: major product has more substituted double bond.

NAMED REACTIONS:
Cannizzaro reaction: aldehyde without α-H with strong base → acid + alcohol.
Aldol condensation: aldehyde/ketone with dilute base → β-hydroxy carbonyl.
Clemmensen reduction: C=O → CH₂ with Zn-Hg/HCl.
Wolf-Kishner reduction: C=O → CH₂ with NH₂NH₂/KOH.
Fries rearrangement: phenyl ester → hydroxyaryl ketone.
Beckmann rearrangement: oxime → amide.
Baeyer's test: pink KMnO₄ decolorized by alkenes/alkynes.
Tollens' test (silver mirror): aldehydes reduce Ag(NH₃)₂⁺ → Ag mirror.
Fehling's solution: blue → red precipitate (Cu₂O) with aliphatic aldehydes.

CHAPTER 5: COORDINATION COMPOUNDS

WERNER'S THEORY:
Primary valency (ionizable): shown by oxidation state.
Secondary valency (coordinate): coordination number, shown in square brackets.

NOMENCLATURE:
Cation named first, then anion.
Ligands named before metal. Anionic ligands end in -o, neutral ligands use common names.
Oxidation state of metal in Roman numerals.
Example: [Co(NH₃)₆]³⁺ = hexaamminecobalt(III) ion.

CRYSTAL FIELD THEORY:
d-orbitals split in ligand field.
Octahedral: t₂g (lower, 3 orbitals) and e_g (higher, 2 orbitals). Splitting = Δₒ.
Strong field ligands: large Δₒ, low spin. Weak field ligands: small Δₒ, high spin.
Spectrochemical series: I⁻ < Br⁻ < Cl⁻ < F⁻ < OH⁻ < H₂O < NH₃ < en < CN⁻ < CO

ISOMERISM IN COORDINATION COMPOUNDS:
Structural: ionization, linkage, coordination, solvate isomers.
Stereoisomerism: geometrical (cis-trans), optical (Δ, Λ for octahedral).

STABILITY CONSTANT: K_stability = [complex]/[metal][ligand]^n
Chelate effect: bidentate/polydentate ligands form more stable complexes (entropy factor).
EDTA: hexadentate ligand, forms very stable complexes with many metal ions.

CHAPTER 6: BIOMOLECULES

CARBOHYDRATES (Cx(H₂O)y):
Monosaccharides: glucose (C₆H₁₂O₆), fructose, galactose. Cannot be hydrolyzed further.
Glucose structure: open chain (Fischer), cyclic (Haworth, α and β forms).
Disaccharides: maltose (glucose+glucose), sucrose (glucose+fructose), lactose (glucose+galactose).
Polysaccharides: starch (storage in plants), glycogen (storage in animals), cellulose (structural in plants).
Reducing sugars: have free aldehyde/ketone group, reduce Tollens'/Fehling's. Sucrose is non-reducing.

PROTEINS:
Made of amino acids connected by peptide bonds (-CO-NH-).
20 standard amino acids. Essential amino acids: must obtain from diet.
Primary structure: sequence of amino acids.
Secondary: α-helix, β-pleated sheet (H-bonds).
Tertiary: overall 3D folding (disulfide, H-bonds, hydrophobic).
Quaternary: multiple polypeptide chains.
Denaturation: loss of 3D structure (by heat, pH, chemicals).
Enzymes: protein catalysts, highly specific (lock-and-key model).

NUCLEIC ACIDS:
DNA: deoxyribose sugar, A-T and G-C base pairs, double helix, carries genetic info.
RNA: ribose sugar, single strand, carries genetic info from DNA to ribosome.
Nucleotide = sugar + phosphate + nitrogenous base.
Purines: adenine (A), guanine (G). Pyrimidines: thymine (T), cytosine (C), uracil (U).

LIPIDS:
Triglycerides: glycerol + 3 fatty acids. Saturated (no double bonds), unsaturated (double bonds).
Saponification: triglyceride + NaOH → soap (sodium salt of fatty acid) + glycerol.
Phospholipids: cell membrane structure (bilayer).
Steroids: cholesterol, hormones (testosterone, estrogen, cortisol).

VITAMINS:
Fat soluble: A, D, E, K (stored in body).
Water soluble: B-complex, C (not stored, must be regularly consumed).
Vitamin A: vision. Vitamin C: antioxidant. Vitamin D: calcium absorption. Vitamin K: blood clotting.
