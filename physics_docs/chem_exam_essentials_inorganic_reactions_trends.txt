CHEMISTRY EXAM ESSENTIALS: INORGANIC REACTIONS, TRENDS, AND IDENTIFICATION

Use this file for quick revision of p-block, d-block, metallurgy, coordination basics, qualitative analysis, and very common board/entrance exam traps.

--------------------------------------------------
1. PERIODIC TRENDS YOU MUST REMEMBER
--------------------------------------------------
Across a period:
- Atomic radius decreases
- Ionization enthalpy generally increases
- Electron gain enthalpy usually becomes more negative
- Metallic character decreases
- Non-metallic character increases

Down a group:
- Atomic radius increases
- Ionization enthalpy decreases
- Metallic character increases
- Shielding increases

Important exceptions:
- Be > B in ionization enthalpy
- N > O in ionization enthalpy
Reason:
- Be has filled 2s
- N has half-filled 2p
- O has paired electron repulsion

Electronegativity:
- F is highest
- Cs/Fr among lowest
- Noble gases usually not assigned standard values in basic tables

--------------------------------------------------
2. CHEMICAL BONDING FACTS FREQUENTLY ASKED
--------------------------------------------------
Ionic bond favored by:
- Low ionization enthalpy of cation-forming atom
- High electron affinity/electron gain tendency of anion-forming atom
- High lattice enthalpy

Covalent bond:
- Formed by sharing of electrons

Coordinate bond:
- Shared pair donated by one atom only
Examples:
- NH4+
- H3O+
- BF3.NH3

Fajan's rule:
More covalent character if:
- Cation is small and highly charged
- Anion is large and highly charged

Shapes to revise:
- BeCl2 linear
- BF3 trigonal planar
- CH4 tetrahedral
- NH3 trigonal pyramidal
- H2O bent
- PCl5 trigonal bipyramidal
- SF6 octahedral
- SF4 seesaw
- ClF3 T-shaped
- XeF4 square planar

Hybridization quick list:
- sp: linear
- sp2: trigonal planar
- sp3: tetrahedral
- sp3d: trigonal bipyramidal
- sp3d2: octahedral

Common mistake:
- Shape depends on lone pairs also, not hybridization alone.

--------------------------------------------------
3. s-BLOCK AND p-BLOCK HIGH-YIELD POINTS
--------------------------------------------------
Hydrogen:
- Shows similarity with alkali metals and halogens
- Isotopes: protium, deuterium, tritium

Alkali metals:
- Very reactive
- Stored under kerosene
- React vigorously with water
General reaction:
2M + 2H2O -> 2MOH + H2

Alkaline earth metals:
- Less reactive than alkali metals
- Be shows anomalous behavior
- Mg reacts slowly with cold water but readily with steam

Diagonal relationship:
- Li with Mg
- Be with Al

Important compounds:
NaOH:
- Strong base
- Made by chlor-alkali process

Na2CO3.10H2O:
- Washing soda

NaHCO3:
- Baking soda
- On heating:
2NaHCO3 -> Na2CO3 + CO2 + H2O

CaOCl2:
- Bleaching powder
- Used as bleaching and disinfecting agent

CaCO3:
- Limestone/marble/chalk
On heating:
CaCO3 -> CaO + CO2

Plaster of Paris:
- CaSO4.1/2H2O

Gypsum:
- CaSO4.2H2O

--------------------------------------------------
4. IMPORTANT p-BLOCK TRENDS AND REACTIONS
--------------------------------------------------
Boron family:
- B is non-metal, Al is metal
- BF3 is electron deficient Lewis acid
- Boric acid B(OH)3 is weak monobasic Lewis acid, not a proton donor in the usual sense

Carbon family:
- Catenation: C highest
- Inert pair effect increases down the group
- Pb shows +2 more stable than +4
- Sn shows both +2 and +4

Nitrogen family:
- NH3 is basic due to lone pair
- Basic strength of hydrides generally decreases down group
- N2 is very stable due to triple bond

Oxygen family:
- H2O liquid due to hydrogen bonding
- H2S is gas and weaker hydrogen bonding

Halogens:
- F2 strongest oxidizing agent
- Bond dissociation enthalpy exception: F-F is weaker than expected due to lone pair repulsion
- HF is weak acid; HCl, HBr, HI are strong acids in water
- Reducing character of hydrogen halides: HF < HCl < HBr < HI

Interhalogen compounds:
- More reactive than halogens except fluorine
Examples:
- ClF3
- BrF5
- IF7

Noble gases:
- Xe forms compounds with F and O
Examples:
- XeF2
- XeF4
- XeF6

--------------------------------------------------
5. METALLURGY ESSENTIALS
--------------------------------------------------
Concentration methods:
- Hydraulic washing: based on density difference
- Magnetic separation: magnetic ore and non-magnetic impurities
- Froth flotation: sulphide ores
- Leaching: suitable solvent dissolves ore

Common roasting/calcination:
Roasting:
- Heating sulphide ore in excess air
Example:
2ZnS + 3O2 -> 2ZnO + 2SO2

Calcination:
- Heating carbonate/hydrated ore in limited or no air
Example:
ZnCO3 -> ZnO + CO2

Reduction:
- Metal oxide reduced by carbon, CO, Al, etc.

Thermite reaction:
Fe2O3 + 2Al -> Al2O3 + 2Fe + heat

Flux:
- Added to remove gangue

Slag:
- Fusible product formed from gangue + flux

Examples:
- SiO2 acidic gangue removed by basic flux CaO
- CaO + SiO2 -> CaSiO3

Most important ores:
- Haematite: Fe2O3
- Magnetite: Fe3O4
- Bauxite: Al2O3.xH2O
- Zinc blende: ZnS
- Calamine: ZnCO3
- Cinnabar: HgS
- Copper pyrites: CuFeS2

Common mistake:
- Bauxite is ore of aluminium, not Al metal itself.
- Cryolite Na3AlF6 is used in extraction of Al to lower melting point and improve conductivity.

--------------------------------------------------
6. d-BLOCK AND TRANSITION ELEMENTS
--------------------------------------------------
Transition elements show:
- Variable oxidation states
- Colored ions
- Complex formation
- Catalytic behavior
- Paramagnetism

Why variable oxidation states?
- (n-1)d and ns electrons have similar energies

Examples of oxidation states:
- Fe: +2, +3
- Mn: +2 to +7
- Cr: +2, +3, +6

Color of ions:
- Due to d-d transitions
- d0 and d10 ions usually colorless
Examples:
- Sc3+ colorless
- Zn2+ colorless
- Cu2+ colored

Magnetic behavior:
- More unpaired electrons -> more paramagnetic

Important oxidizing/reducing species:
- KMnO4 strong oxidizing agent
- K2Cr2O7 strong oxidizing agent
- Fe2+ acts as reducing agent
- Sn2+ acts as reducing agent

7. COORDINATION COMPOUNDS QUICK REVISION
--------------------------------------------------
Terms:
- Central metal atom/ion
- Ligand
- Coordination number
- Oxidation state
- Coordination sphere

Ligands:
- Unidentate: NH3, H2O, Cl-
- Bidentate: en, C2O4^2-
- Ambidentate: NO2-, SCN-
- Chelating ligands form rings and increase stability

Important formulas:
- [Co(NH3)6]Cl3
- K4[Fe(CN)6]
- K3[Fe(CN)6]
- [Cu(NH3)4]SO4

Oxidation state examples:
- In [Fe(CN)6]4-, Fe = +2
- In [Fe(CN)6]3-, Fe = +3

Isomerism:
- Ionization isomerism
- Hydrate isomerism
- Linkage isomerism
- Geometrical isomerism
- Optical isomerism

Werner theory idea:
- Primary valency = oxidation state
- Secondary valency = coordination number

VBT quick idea:
- Inner orbital complex usually low spin
- Outer orbital complex usually high spin

Common mistake:
- Do not confuse oxidation state with coordination number.
Example:
- In [Co(NH3)6]Cl3, oxidation state of Co = +3, coordination number = 6

8. QUALITATIVE INORGANIC ANALYSIS SNAPSHOT
--------------------------------------------------
Colored ions:
- Cu2+ blue
- Fe2+ pale green
- Fe3+ yellow/brown
- Ni2+ green
- MnO4- purple
- Cr2O7^2- orange

Gas identification:
- H2: burns with pop sound
- O2: rekindles glowing splint
- CO2: turns lime water milky
- NH3: pungent, turns red litmus blue
- H2S: rotten egg smell, blackens lead acetate paper
- SO2: pungent, decolorizes acidified KMnO4

Flame colors:
- Na: golden yellow
- K: lilac
- Ca: brick red
- Ba: apple green
- Cu: blue-green

Precipitate reminders:
- AgCl white, soluble in NH3
- AgBr pale yellow, partly soluble in NH3
- AgI yellow, insoluble in NH3
- BaSO4 white, insoluble in acids
- Cu(OH)2 blue
- Fe(OH)3 reddish brown
- Fe(OH)2 dirty green

9. IMPORTANT OXIDIZING AND REDUCING AGENTS
--------------------------------------------------
Strong oxidizing agents:
- KMnO4
- K2Cr2O7
- Concentrated HNO3
- O3
- Halogens, especially F2 and Cl2

Common reducing agents:
- H2
- CO
- C
- SO2
- SnCl2
- FeSO4
- I-

Remember:
- Oxidation = loss of electrons / increase in oxidation number
- Reduction = gain of electrons / decrease in oxidation number

--------------------------------------------------
11. TOP COMMON MISTAKES
--------------------------------------------------
- HF is weak acid, not strong like HCl/HBr/HI in water.
- NH4OH is better viewed as aqueous ammonia; do not overthink it as a stable isolated compound.
- Oxidation number and valency are not always identical.
- Be and Al show diagonal relationship-type anomalies; do not apply group trends blindly.
- d0 and d10 ions are generally colorless.
- F is strongest oxidizing halogen, but HF is weak acid.
- In coordination compounds, ligands inside bracket affect coordination number; counter ions outside do not.
- Roasting is mainly for sulphide ores, calcination mainly for carbonate/hydrated ores.
