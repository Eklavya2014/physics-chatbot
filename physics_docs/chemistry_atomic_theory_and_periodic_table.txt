1. HISTORY OF ATOMIC THEORY
-----------------------------
Democritus (~400 BCE): Proposed matter consists of indivisible particles called "atomos."
John Dalton (1803): Atomic theory — elements made of atoms; atoms of the same element are identical; compounds are combinations of atoms in fixed ratios.
J.J. Thomson (1897): Discovered electrons; "plum pudding" model — positive sphere with embedded electrons.
Ernest Rutherford (1911): Gold foil experiment → nuclear model — tiny dense positive nucleus surrounded by mostly empty space with orbiting electrons.
Niels Bohr (1913): Electrons orbit nucleus in discrete energy levels (shells); electrons absorb/emit photons when changing orbits.
Erwin Schrödinger (1926): Quantum mechanical model — electrons exist in probability clouds (orbitals), not fixed orbits.

1.1 SUBATOMIC PARTICLES
------------------------
Particle   | Symbol | Charge  | Mass (amu) | Location
-----------|--------|---------|------------|------------------
Proton     | p+     | +1      | 1.0073     | Nucleus
Neutron    | n0     | 0       | 1.0087     | Nucleus
Electron   | e-     | -1      | 0.000549   | Orbitals around nucleus

Atomic Number (Z): Number of protons in nucleus. Defines the element.
Mass Number (A): Protons + Neutrons. A = Z + N.
Atomic Mass: Weighted average of all isotopes by natural abundance.
Isotopes: Same Z, different N (different mass numbers). Example: Carbon-12, Carbon-13, Carbon-14.
Ions: Atoms that have gained (anion, negative) or lost (cation, positive) electrons.
  Na → Na+ (lost 1 electron). Cl → Cl- (gained 1 electron).

1.2 ELECTRONIC CONFIGURATION
------------------------------
Electrons occupy shells and subshells:
  Shells: n = 1, 2, 3, 4 ... (K, L, M, N ...)
  Subshells: s (2e max), p (6e max), d (10e max), f (14e max)
  Orbitals: s=1, p=3, d=5, f=7 orbitals each hold 2e (opposite spins)

Aufbau Principle: Fill lowest energy subshells first.
  Fill order: 1s → 2s → 2p → 3s → 3p → 4s → 3d → 4p → 5s → 4d → 5p → 6s → 4f → 5d → 6p → 7s → 5f → 6d → 7p

Pauli Exclusion Principle: No two electrons in an atom can have the same four quantum numbers. Each orbital holds max 2 electrons with opposite spins.
Hund's Rule: Fill orbitals singly before pairing. Electrons in singly occupied orbitals have same spin.

Example Configurations:
  H  (Z=1):  1s¹
  C  (Z=6):  1s² 2s² 2p²
  Na (Z=11): 1s² 2s² 2p⁶ 3s¹  (or [Ne] 3s¹)
  Fe (Z=26): [Ar] 3d⁶ 4s²
  Cu (Z=29): [Ar] 3d¹⁰ 4s¹  (exception — full d is extra stable)
  Cr (Z=24): [Ar] 3d⁵ 4s¹   (exception — half-filled d is extra stable)

Valence Electrons: Electrons in the outermost shell. Determine chemical behaviour.
  Group 1: 1 valence e-. Group 2: 2 valence e-. Group 17: 7 valence e-. Group 18: 8 valence e-.

2. THE PERIODIC TABLE — ORGANISATION & TRENDS
-----------------------------------------------
The modern periodic table (Dmitri Mendeleev, 1869; modern form by Henry Moseley, 1913) organises 118 elements by increasing atomic number into 18 groups and 7 periods.

Structure:
  Periods (rows): 7 periods. Period number = number of electron shells.
  Groups (columns): 18 groups. Group number often = valence electrons (for main groups).
  s-block: Groups 1–2 + He (filling s orbitals).
  p-block: Groups 13–18 (filling p orbitals).
  d-block (transition metals): Groups 3–12 (filling d orbitals).
  f-block (lanthanides + actinides): Elements 57–71 and 89–103 (filling f orbitals).

2.1 PERIODIC TRENDS
--------------------
Atomic Radius:
  Across period (L→R): Decreases. More protons → greater nuclear charge → electrons pulled closer.
  Down group: Increases. More electron shells → outer electrons further from nucleus.
  Largest: Cs (francium is unstable). Smallest (non-noble): F.

Ionic Radius:
  Cations (M+, M2+): Smaller than parent atom (fewer electrons, same protons).
  Anions (X-, X2-): Larger than parent atom (more electrons, same protons).
  Fe2+ > Fe3+ (more electrons, same protons).

Ionisation Energy (IE):
  Energy to remove one electron from gaseous atom. Always endothermic (+ve).
  First IE: Highest → lowest: He > Ne > F > O (O exception: half-filled 2p in N is extra stable).
  Trend: Increases across period; decreases down group.
  Successive IEs: Large jump when removing electron from new inner shell.

Electron Affinity (EA):
  Energy change when gaseous atom gains one electron.
  Most negative (highest affinity): Cl (-348 kJ/mol) > F (-328 kJ/mol).
    F exception: smaller atom → inter-electron repulsion in small 2p orbital.

Electronegativity (Pauling scale):
  Ability of atom to attract bonding electrons.
  Trend: Increases across period; decreases down group.
  Most electronegative: F (4.0) > O (3.5) > N/Cl (3.0) > Br (2.8).
  Least electronegative: Fr (0.7), Cs (0.79), Rb (0.82).
  Difference >1.7: Ionic bond. 0.4–1.7: Polar covalent. <0.4: Non-polar covalent.

Metallic Character:
  Increases down group; decreases across period (left to right).
  Metals: Good conductors, malleable, ductile, lustrous, lose electrons (reduce).
  Non-metals: Poor conductors, brittle, dull, gain electrons (oxidise).
  Metalloids (semi-metals): B, Si, Ge, As, Sb, Te, Po, At — intermediate properties.

2.2 VALENCY & OXIDATION STATES
--------------------------------
Valency: Number of bonds an element can form. Related to valence electrons.
  Common valencies: H=1; O=2; N=3; C=4; S=2,4,6; Cl=1 (common), 3,5,7.
  Transition metals: Variable valency (e.g., Fe: 2,3; Cu: 1,2; Mn: 2,3,4,6,7).

Oxidation State (Oxidation Number):
  Hypothetical charge if all bonds were ionic.
  Rules:
    1. Element in free state = 0 (O2, Fe, Na).
    2. Monatomic ion = charge (Na+ = +1, Cl- = -1).
    3. O = -2 (except peroxides O22- = -1; OF2 where O = +2).
    4. H = +1 (except metal hydrides where H = -1).
    5. F = always -1.
    6. Sum of oxidation states in neutral compound = 0.
    7. Sum of oxidation states in polyatomic ion = ion charge.

Common Oxidation States by Group:
  Group 1 : +1 only (Li, Na, K, Rb, Cs, Fr)
  Group 2 : +2 only (Be, Mg, Ca, Sr, Ba, Ra)
  Group 13: +3 (Al, Ga, In); +1,+3 (Tl, B)
  Group 14: +4,-4; also +2 for Pb, Sn, Ge
  Group 15: -3, +3, +5 (N, P, As, Sb, Bi)
  Group 16: -2, +4, +6 (S, Se, Te)
  Group 17: -1, +1, +3, +5, +7 (Cl, Br, I)

3. QUANTUM NUMBERS & ORBITAL THEORY
--------------------------------------
Four Quantum Numbers describe each electron completely:
  n (Principal): Shell number; n = 1,2,3... Energy and size of orbital.
  l (Angular Momentum): Subshell shape; l = 0 to n-1. (0=s, 1=p, 2=d, 3=f).
  ml (Magnetic): Orbital orientation; ml = -l to +l. Number of orbitals in subshell.
  ms (Spin): Electron spin; +½ or -½.

Orbital Shapes:
  s orbitals: Spherical. One orbital per subshell.
  p orbitals: Dumbbell-shaped; three orbitals (px, py, pz); perpendicular to each other.
  d orbitals: Complex shapes; five orbitals (dxy, dxz, dyz, dx²-y², dz²).
  f orbitals: Very complex; seven orbitals.

Nodal Planes/Surfaces:
  s: 0 angular nodes, n-1 radial nodes.
  p: 1 angular node (nodal plane through nucleus).
  d: 2 angular nodes.

Effective Nuclear Charge (Zeff):
  Zeff = Z - S (shielding constant). Inner electrons shield outer electrons from full nuclear charge.
  Slater's Rules: Define shielding contributions from different shell electrons.
  Zeff increases across period → explains increase in IE, EA, electronegativity.

Relativistic Effects:
  For heavy elements (Z > 60): Electrons in 1s orbital approach speed of light → relativistic mass increase → orbital contraction → s and p orbitals contract + stabilise.
  Consequences: Gold (Au) appears yellow (relativistic effect on d→s transitions absorbs blue light). Mercury (Hg) is liquid at room temp (contracted 6s² → weak bonding). Platinum group catalytic activity. Lead's +2 preference (inert pair effect — 6s² stabilised by relativistic contraction).

5. NUCLEAR CHEMISTRY
---------------------
Radioactive Decay Types:
  Alpha (α): Emission of ⁴₂He nucleus. Penetration: Low (stopped by paper). Range in air: ~5 cm.
    Decay: ᴬZ X → ᴬ⁻⁴(Z-2) Y + ⁴₂He. A decreases by 4; Z decreases by 2.
  Beta minus (β-): Neutron → proton + e- + antineutrino. Z increases by 1; A unchanged.
    Penetration: Medium (stopped by 5 mm Al).
  Beta plus (β+): Proton → neutron + positron + neutrino. Z decreases by 1; A unchanged.
  Gamma (γ): High-energy electromagnetic radiation emitted with α or β decay. No mass/charge change. Most penetrating (requires thick lead/concrete).
  Electron Capture: Inner electron captured by nucleus; proton → neutron; Z decreases by 1.
  Neutron Emission: Rare; unstable nucleus emits neutron.

Half-Life (t½): Time for half of radioactive nuclei to decay.
  N(t) = N₀ × (½)^(t/t½) = N₀ × e^(-λt)   where λ = decay constant = ln2/t½.

Nuclear Fission: Heavy nucleus splits into lighter nuclei + energy + neutrons.
  ²³⁵U + n → ²³⁶U* → ⁹²Kr + ¹⁴¹Ba + 3n + energy (~200 MeV).
  Chain reaction: Released neutrons trigger further fissions. Critical mass required.

Nuclear Fusion: Light nuclei combine → heavier nucleus + energy.
  ²H + ³H → ⁴He + n + 17.6 MeV. Powers the sun; hydrogen bombs; target of fusion reactors (ITER).

Binding Energy: Energy needed to completely separate all nucleons in nucleus.
  E = Δm × c² (Einstein). Peak binding energy per nucleon: ⁵⁶Fe (most stable nucleus).
  Elements lighter than Fe: Fusion releases energy. Elements heavier than Fe: Fission releases energy.

Mass Defect: Actual nuclear mass < sum of individual proton + neutron masses. Difference converted to binding energy (E = mc²).

[APPLICATIONS] Radiocarbon dating (¹⁴C, t½ = 5,730 years); ²³⁸U/²⁰⁶Pb dating (t½ = 4.47 billion years); Medical imaging (PET uses β+ emitters: ¹⁸F-FDG); Radiotherapy (γ + β-); Nuclear power (²³⁵U fission).
