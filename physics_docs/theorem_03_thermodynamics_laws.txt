LAWS OF THERMODYNAMICS AND STATISTICAL MECHANICS
==================================================

THE FOUR LAWS OF THERMODYNAMICS

ZEROTH LAW OF THERMODYNAMICS:
Statement: If system A is in thermal equilibrium with system C, and system B is in thermal equilibrium with system C, then A and B are in thermal equilibrium with each other.
Significance: This defines temperature as a meaningful physical quantity. It allows us to use thermometers — if thermometer reads the same for two objects, they are at the same temperature.
This law was recognized AFTER the first and second laws were established, hence called "zeroth."

FIRST LAW OF THERMODYNAMICS (Conservation of Energy):
Statement: Energy cannot be created or destroyed, only converted from one form to another.
Mathematical form: dU = δQ - δW
ΔU = Q - W
U = internal energy of system
Q = heat added TO system (positive when heat flows in)
W = work done BY system (positive when system expands)
For infinitesimal process: dU = TdS - PdV + μdN

Different processes:
Isothermal (constant T): ΔU = 0, Q = W (for ideal gas)
Adiabatic (Q = 0): ΔU = -W
Isochoric (constant V): W = 0, ΔU = Q = nCvΔT
Isobaric (constant P): W = PΔV, Q = nCpΔT

Work done by gas: W = ∫PdV
For isothermal expansion of ideal gas: W = nRT ln(V₂/V₁)
For adiabatic: W = (P₁V₁ - P₂V₂)/(γ-1)

SECOND LAW OF THERMODYNAMICS:
This is the most profound law in physics — it introduces the arrow of time.

Kelvin-Planck statement: It is impossible to construct a heat engine that operates in a cycle and converts ALL heat input into work with no other effect. (Efficiency < 100% always)

Clausius statement: It is impossible for heat to flow spontaneously from a colder to a hotter body without work being done.

Entropy statement: The entropy of an isolated system never decreases. ΔS ≥ 0
For reversible process: ΔS = 0
For irreversible process: ΔS > 0
The universe always evolves toward states of higher entropy.

ENTROPY:
Thermodynamic definition: dS = δQ_rev/T
Boltzmann definition: S = k_B ln Ω (Ω = number of microstates)
This connects macroscopic thermodynamics to microscopic statistical mechanics.
Units: J/K

Why entropy increases: There are vastly more disordered states than ordered states. Random processes naturally evolve toward more probable (higher entropy) states.
Example: Gas expands to fill container — there are vastly more ways to distribute molecules throughout volume than to have them all in one corner.

CARNOT'S THEOREM:
The most efficient heat engine operating between temperatures T_H (hot) and T_C (cold) is the Carnot engine.
Carnot efficiency: η_Carnot = 1 - T_C/T_H
No real engine can exceed Carnot efficiency (with same temperature reservoirs).
Carnot cycle: isothermal expansion → adiabatic expansion → isothermal compression → adiabatic compression.

For Carnot engine:
Q_H/T_H = Q_C/T_C (heat exchanged proportional to temperature)
W = Q_H - Q_C
η = W/Q_H = 1 - Q_C/Q_H = 1 - T_C/T_H

Coefficient of Performance (refrigerator):
COP = Q_C/W = T_C/(T_H - T_C) (Carnot)

THIRD LAW OF THERMODYNAMICS (Nernst Theorem):
Statement: As temperature approaches absolute zero, the entropy of a perfect crystal approaches zero.
lim(T→0) S = 0
Consequence: It is impossible to reach absolute zero temperature in a finite number of steps.
At T = 0, all thermal motion ceases, only zero-point quantum motion remains.
The third law sets the absolute scale of entropy.

THERMODYNAMIC POTENTIALS:
Four potentials, each most useful in different conditions:

Internal energy: U = TS - PV + μN
dU = TdS - PdV + μdN
Natural variables: S, V, N
Use when: isolated system (fixed S, V)

Helmholtz free energy: F = U - TS
dF = -SdT - PdV + μdN
Natural variables: T, V, N
Use when: constant T, V (isothermal, fixed volume)
F = minimum at equilibrium for constant T, V process.
Maximum work = -ΔF

Gibbs free energy: G = U - TS + PV = H - TS
dG = -SdT + VdP + μdN
Natural variables: T, P, N
Use when: constant T, P (most laboratory conditions!)
G = minimum at equilibrium for constant T, P process.
ΔG < 0: spontaneous process; ΔG = 0: equilibrium; ΔG > 0: non-spontaneous

Enthalpy: H = U + PV
dH = TdS + VdP + μdN
Natural variables: S, P
Heat of reaction at constant pressure = ΔH

Chemical potential: μ = (∂G/∂N)_{T,P} = G/N (for pure substance)
Equilibrium condition: μ same in all phases.
Phase transitions occur when chemical potentials are equal.

MAXWELL RELATIONS:
From exactness of thermodynamic differentials:
(∂T/∂V)_S = -(∂P/∂S)_V [from dU]
(∂T/∂P)_S = (∂V/∂S)_P [from dH]
(∂S/∂V)_T = (∂P/∂T)_V [from dF] ← most useful
(∂S/∂P)_T = -(∂V/∂T)_P [from dG]

STATISTICAL MECHANICS

FUNDAMENTAL POSTULATE:
For an isolated system in equilibrium, all accessible microstates are equally probable.
Probability of macrostate ∝ number of microstates (Ω) consistent with it.
This links microscopic to macroscopic.

BOLTZMANN DISTRIBUTION:
For system in contact with heat reservoir at temperature T:
P(state i) = e^(-E_i/k_BT) / Z
Z = Σ_i e^(-E_i/k_BT) (partition function — the most important quantity in stat mech)

From partition function, derive everything:
Average energy: ⟨E⟩ = -∂(ln Z)/∂β (β = 1/k_BT)
Helmholtz free energy: F = -k_BT ln Z
Entropy: S = k_B(ln Z + β⟨E⟩)
Pressure: P = k_BT (∂ ln Z/∂V)_T
Heat capacity: C_v = ∂⟨E⟩/∂T

PARTITION FUNCTION FOR IDEAL GAS:
Z_N = (V/λ³)^N / N! where λ = √(h²/2πmk_BT) (thermal de Broglie wavelength)
This gives: PV = Nk_BT (ideal gas law — DERIVED from first principles!)

EQUIPARTITION THEOREM:
Each quadratic degree of freedom contributes ½k_BT to average energy.
Translational: ½k_BT per direction → 3/2 k_BT total (3 directions)
Rotational: ½k_BT per axis of rotation
Vibrational: k_BT per mode (½ KE + ½ PE)

Total energy:
Monatomic gas: U = 3/2 Nk_BT, C_v = 3/2 R
Diatomic (rigid): U = 5/2 Nk_BT, C_v = 5/2 R (3 trans + 2 rot)
Diatomic (vibrating): U = 7/2 Nk_BT, C_v = 7/2 R

Limitation: Equipartition fails at low T (quantum effects freeze out modes).
Einstein and Debye models correct this for solids.

QUANTUM STATISTICS:
Fermions (half-integer spin, e.g., electrons, protons):
f_FD(ε) = 1/(e^((ε-μ)/k_BT) + 1) (Fermi-Dirac distribution)
At T = 0: all states below Fermi energy E_F filled, above empty (sharp cutoff).
E_F = (ℏ²/2m)(3π²n)^(2/3) (n = electron number density)
Explains: electrical conductivity, specific heat of metals, white dwarf stars.

Bosons (integer spin, e.g., photons, helium-4):
f_BE(ε) = 1/(e^((ε-μ)/k_BT) - 1) (Bose-Einstein distribution)
At low T: macroscopic number of particles occupy ground state (Bose-Einstein condensation).
BEC observed in 1995 (Cornell, Wieman, Ketterle — Nobel Prize 2001).
Photons (μ = 0): gives Planck distribution for blackbody radiation.

Classical limit (high T, low density): both reduce to Maxwell-Boltzmann:
f_MB(ε) ∝ e^(-ε/k_BT)
Maxwell-Boltzmann speed distribution: f(v) = 4πn(m/2πk_BT)^(3/2) v² e^(-mv²/2k_BT)
v_mp = √(2k_BT/m), v_avg = √(8k_BT/πm), v_rms = √(3k_BT/m)

PHASE TRANSITIONS:
Order parameter: quantity that is zero in disordered phase, nonzero in ordered phase.
First-order transition: discontinuous order parameter (melting, boiling).
Latent heat L = TΔS at transition.
Second-order (continuous) transition: continuous order parameter, diverging susceptibility.
Examples: ferromagnet (order parameter = magnetization), superconductor (Cooper pair density).
Critical point: above T_c, order parameter → 0 continuously.

Critical exponents (near T_c):
Order parameter: M ∝ (T_c - T)^β
Susceptibility: χ ∝ |T - T_c|^(-γ)
Specific heat: C ∝ |T - T_c|^(-α)
Universality: different systems have same critical exponents if same symmetry class.

FLUCTUATION-DISSIPATION THEOREM:
Response of system to external perturbation equals its spontaneous fluctuations at equilibrium.
Johnson-Nyquist noise: ⟨V²⟩ = 4k_BTR·Δf (thermal noise in resistor)
Einstein relation: D = μk_BT (diffusion constant = mobility × k_BT)

IMPORTANT FORMULAS:
- ΔU = Q - W (first law)
- dS = δQ_rev/T (entropy)
- S = k_B ln Ω (Boltzmann entropy)
- η_Carnot = 1 - T_C/T_H
- F = -k_BT ln Z (Helmholtz free energy)
- Z = Σ e^(-βE_i) (partition function)
- ⟨E⟩ = -∂ ln Z/∂β
- PV = Nk_BT (ideal gas, derived)
- E_F = (ℏ²/2m)(3π²n)^(2/3) (Fermi energy)
