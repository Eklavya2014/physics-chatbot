CHAPTER 1: CHEMICAL REACTIONS AND EQUATIONS

CHEMICAL REACTION:
A process in which substances (reactants) are transformed into new substances (products) with different properties.

Signs of a chemical reaction:
- Change in color
- Change in temperature (heat released or absorbed)
- Evolution of gas (bubbles)
- Formation of precipitate
- Change in smell

CHEMICAL EQUATION:
Represents a chemical reaction using symbols and formulas.
Word equation: Magnesium + Oxygen → Magnesium oxide
Chemical equation: 2Mg + O₂ → 2MgO
Balanced equation: follows law of conservation of mass.

BALANCING EQUATIONS:
Number of atoms of each element must be equal on both sides.
Example: H₂ + O₂ → H₂O (unbalanced)
Balanced: 2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O

TYPES OF CHEMICAL REACTIONS:

1. Combination (Synthesis): A + B → AB
Two or more substances combine to form one product.
Example: CaO + H₂O → Ca(OH)₂ (quicklime + water → slaked lime)
2C + O₂ → 2CO₂

2. Decomposition: AB → A + B
Single substance breaks down into two or more simpler substances.
Types:
- Thermal decomposition: 2Pb(NO₃)₂ → 2PbO + 4NO₂ + O₂ (heat)
- Electrolytic decomposition: 2H₂O → 2H₂ + O₂ (electricity)
- Photo decomposition: 2AgCl → 2Ag + Cl₂ (sunlight) [used in photography]

3. Displacement: A + BC → AC + B
More reactive element displaces less reactive element.
Example: Fe + CuSO₄ → FeSO₄ + Cu (iron displaces copper)
Zn + H₂SO₄ → ZnSO₄ + H₂ (zinc displaces hydrogen)

4. Double Displacement: AB + CD → AD + CB
Exchange of ions between two compounds.
Example: Na₂SO₄ + BaCl₂ → BaSO₄↓ + 2NaCl (white precipitate)
NaOH + HCl → NaCl + H₂O (neutralization)

5. Oxidation-Reduction (Redox):
Oxidation: loss of electrons (or gain of oxygen, loss of hydrogen)
Reduction: gain of electrons (or loss of oxygen, gain of hydrogen)
These always occur together.
Example: CuO + H₂ → Cu + H₂O
CuO is reduced (loses oxygen), H₂ is oxidized (gains oxygen)

CORROSION:
Slow chemical attack of metals by environment.
Iron rusting: 4Fe + 3O₂ + 6H₂O → 4Fe(OH)₃ → 2Fe₂O₃·3H₂O (rust)
Prevention: painting, galvanizing, alloying, oiling.

RANCIDITY:
Oxidation of fats/oils causing bad smell and taste.
Prevention: antioxidants, airtight containers, refrigeration, nitrogen gas packing.

CHAPTER 2: ACIDS, BASES AND SALTS

ACIDS:
Substances that give H⁺ ions (protons) in aqueous solution.
Properties: sour taste, turns blue litmus red, pH < 7, conducts electricity.
Strong acids: HCl, H₂SO₄, HNO₃ (fully dissociate)
Weak acids: CH₃COOH, H₂CO₃, H₃PO₄ (partially dissociate)
Uses of H₂SO₄: car batteries, fertilizers, detergents.
Uses of HNO₃: fertilizers, explosives, dyes.
Uses of HCl: cleaning metals, digestion (stomach acid).

BASES:
Substances that give OH⁻ ions in aqueous solution.
Properties: bitter taste, soapy feel, turns red litmus blue, pH > 7.
Strong bases: NaOH, KOH, Ca(OH)₂
Weak bases: NH₄OH, Mg(OH)₂
Alkali: a base that is soluble in water.

pH SCALE:
Measures hydrogen ion concentration.
pH = -log[H⁺]
pH 0-6: acidic (lower = stronger acid)
pH 7: neutral
pH 8-14: basic (higher = stronger base)
Indicators: litmus, phenolphthalein, methyl orange, universal indicator.

NEUTRALIZATION:
Acid + Base → Salt + Water
HCl + NaOH → NaCl + H₂O
H₂SO₄ + 2NaOH → Na₂SO₄ + 2H₂O

SALTS:
Formed by reaction of acid and base.
NaCl (common salt): used in food, preservation.
NaHCO₃ (baking soda): used in baking, antacid.
Na₂CO₃ (washing soda): used in glass making, cleaning.
CaOCl₂ (bleaching powder): used in disinfection, bleaching.
Plaster of Paris (CaSO₄·½H₂O): used in bandages, sculptures.

IMPORTANT REACTIONS:
NaOH + HCl → NaCl + H₂O
2NaOH + H₂SO₄ → Na₂SO₄ + 2H₂O
Na₂CO₃ + 2HCl → 2NaCl + H₂O + CO₂
NaHCO₃ + HCl → NaCl + H₂O + CO₂

CHAPTER 3: METALS AND NON-METALS

PHYSICAL PROPERTIES OF METALS:
Lustrous, malleable, ductile, good conductors of heat and electricity, high melting points (except Hg, Ga), sonorous.
Exceptions: Na and K are soft metals. Mercury is liquid at room temperature.

PHYSICAL PROPERTIES OF NON-METALS:
Dull (no lustre), brittle (if solid), poor conductors (except graphite), low melting points.
Exception: Diamond (hardest natural substance), Graphite (conducts electricity).

CHEMICAL PROPERTIES OF METALS:
With oxygen: 4Na + O₂ → 2Na₂O (Na burns vigorously)
                2Mg + O₂ → 2MgO (burns brilliantly white)
                4Al + 3O₂ → 2Al₂O₃ (forms protective layer)
With water: 2Na + 2H₂O → 2NaOH + H₂↑ (vigorous, cold water)
            Mg + 2H₂O → Mg(OH)₂ + H₂↑ (with hot water)
            3Fe + 4H₂O → Fe₃O₄ + 4H₂↑ (with steam)
With acids: Zn + H₂SO₄ → ZnSO₄ + H₂↑
            Fe + 2HCl → FeCl₂ + H₂↑

REACTIVITY SERIES (most to least reactive):
K > Na > Ca > Mg > Al > Zn > Fe > Ni > Sn > Pb > H > Cu > Hg > Ag > Au > Pt
Metals above H in series displace H from acids.
More reactive metals displace less reactive from solutions.

EXTRACTION OF METALS:
- Highly reactive (K, Na, Ca, Mg, Al): electrolysis
- Moderately reactive (Zn, Fe, Pb, Cu): reduction with carbon/CO
- Low reactivity (Hg, Ag, Au): found free or heated in air

REFINING: Electrolytic refining for high purity (Cu, Ag, Au).

CORROSION PREVENTION:
Galvanizing: coating iron with zinc.
Alloying: mixing metals (steel, brass, bronze).
Painting, oiling, electroplating.

ALLOYS AND THEIR COMPOSITIONS:
Brass: Cu + Zn
Bronze: Cu + Sn
Solder: Pb + Sn
Stainless steel: Fe + Cr + Ni
Duralumin: Al + Cu + Mg + Mn

CHAPTER 4: CARBON AND ITS COMPOUNDS

CARBON:
Atomic number 6, forms 4 covalent bonds.
Unique properties: catenation (forms chains with itself), tetravalency.

ALLOTROPES OF CARBON:
Diamond: each C bonded to 4 others in tetrahedral arrangement. Hardest substance. Insulator.
Graphite: layers of hexagonal rings. Each C bonded to 3 others. Soft, lubricant. Conductor.
Fullerene (C₆₀): soccer ball shape. Used in nanotechnology.

HYDROCARBONS:
Saturated: alkanes (only single bonds) — CₙH₂ₙ₊₂
Methane (CH₄), Ethane (C₂H₆), Propane (C₃H₈), Butane (C₄H₁₀)
Unsaturated: alkenes (double bond) — CₙH₂ₙ
Ethene (C₂H₄), Propene (C₃H₆)
Alkynes (triple bond) — CₙH₂ₙ₋₂
Ethyne (C₂H₂) (acetylene)

FUNCTIONAL GROUPS:
-OH (hydroxyl): alcohols (methanol CH₃OH, ethanol C₂H₅OH)
-CHO (aldehyde): methanal HCHO, ethanal CH₃CHO
-COOH (carboxyl): acids (ethanoic acid CH₃COOH — vinegar)
-CO- (ketone): propanone CH₃COCH₃ (acetone)
Halogens: chloromethane CH₃Cl

NOMENCLATURE:
Prefix for carbon number: meth(1), eth(2), prop(3), but(4), pent(5), hex(6)
Suffix: -ane (alkane), -ene (alkene), -yne (alkyne), -ol (alcohol), -al (aldehyde), -one (ketone), -oic acid (carboxylic acid)

IMPORTANT REACTIONS:
Combustion: CH₄ + 2O₂ → CO₂ + 2H₂O + heat
Addition: CH₂=CH₂ + H₂ → CH₃-CH₃ (with Ni catalyst)
Substitution: CH₄ + Cl₂ → CH₃Cl + HCl (in sunlight)
Saponification: fat + NaOH → soap + glycerol

SOAPS AND DETERGENTS:
Soap: sodium salt of long-chain fatty acid. Made by saponification.
Micelle formation: hydrophobic tail surrounds oil, hydrophilic head faces water.
Hard water problem: Ca²⁺ and Mg²⁺ react with soap → scum.
Detergents: work in hard water (synthetic, sulphonate group).

CHAPTER 5: PERIODIC CLASSIFICATION

DÖBEREINER'S TRIADS: Groups of 3 elements with middle element's atomic weight = average of other two.
NEWLANDS' OCTAVES: Every 8th element has similar properties.
MENDELEEV'S PERIODIC TABLE: Arranged by atomic mass, left gaps for undiscovered elements.
MODERN PERIODIC TABLE: Arranged by atomic number (Moseley, 1913).
Modern periodic law: Properties of elements are periodic functions of their atomic numbers.

PERIODIC TABLE STRUCTURE:
18 groups (vertical columns) — similar chemical properties.
7 periods (horizontal rows) — same number of electron shells.
4 blocks: s, p, d, f blocks.

PERIODIC TRENDS:
Atomic size: increases down group (more shells), decreases across period (more protons pull electrons in).
Ionization energy: decreases down group, increases across period.
Electronegativity: decreases down group, increases across period (highest: F).
Metallic character: increases down group, decreases across period.
Non-metallic character: decreases down group, increases across period.
Valency: increases from 1 to 4 then decreases to 0 across period.
