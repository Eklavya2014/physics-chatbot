CHAPTER 5: SOURCES OF ENERGY
==============================

WHAT IS A GOOD SOURCE OF ENERGY?
A good source of energy is one that:
- Does a large amount of work per unit volume or mass.
- Is easily accessible.
- Is easy to store and transport.
- Is economical.
- Is safe to use.
- Does not pollute the environment.

TYPES OF ENERGY SOURCES

1. Non-Renewable (Conventional) Sources:
- Fossil fuels: Coal, petroleum, natural gas.
- Nuclear energy.
- Will be exhausted eventually.
- Cannot be replenished in a short time.

2. Renewable (Non-conventional) Sources:
- Solar energy, wind energy, hydro energy, geothermal energy, biomass, ocean energy.
- Can be replenished naturally and are inexhaustible.
- Generally environment-friendly.

FOSSIL FUELS
Coal, petroleum (oil), and natural gas are called fossil fuels because they were formed from the remains of living organisms millions of years ago under heat and pressure.

Problems with fossil fuels:
- They are non-renewable (will run out).
- Burning them causes air pollution (CO₂, SO₂, NO₂).
- CO₂ is a greenhouse gas causing global warming.
- SO₂ and NO₂ cause acid rain.
- Oil spills cause environmental damage.

THERMAL POWER PLANTS
- Burn coal, oil, or gas to produce steam.
- Steam drives a turbine connected to a generator.
- Electrical energy is produced.
- Efficiency is low (about 35-40%) due to heat losses.
- Usually built near coal mines or fuel sources.

HYDROELECTRIC POWER PLANTS
- Use the potential energy of stored water in dams.
- Water flows down from height → drives turbines → generates electricity.
- Renewable, no pollution during operation.
- Dams submerge forests and displace communities.
- Changes aquatic ecosystems.

Example: Bhakra Nangal Dam (India), Three Gorges Dam (China)

BIOMASS
Plant and animal waste used as fuel.

Types:
1. Wood: Traditional fuel. Causes deforestation if not managed.
2. Agricultural waste: Crop residues burned as fuel.
3. Animal dung (Gobar): Used to make biogas.

BIOGAS (Gobar Gas)
Biogas is produced by anaerobic decomposition (without oxygen) of biological waste.

Composition of biogas: Mainly methane (CH₄, 55-70%), CO₂, some H₂, H₂S.

Working of Biogas Plant:
- A mixing tank where dung and water are mixed into slurry.
- The slurry is fed into the digester (underground tank).
- Bacteria decompose the slurry anaerobically, producing biogas.
- Biogas collects in the gas holder (dome) above the digester.
- Gas is piped to homes for cooking/lighting.
- Spent slurry (effluent) is removed as excellent manure.

Advantages of Biogas:
- Clean fuel (no smoke, no ash).
- Renewable energy.
- Slurry is excellent fertilizer.
- Solves the problem of animal waste disposal.
- Biogas burns completely (efficient).

WIND ENERGY
Moving air (wind) has kinetic energy that can be converted to electricity.

Wind Energy Farm:
- Windmills (turbines) are set up in areas with strong, steady winds.
- Minimum wind speed needed: 15 km/h.
- Wind turbines have large blades connected to a generator.
- Many windmills in a farm can generate significant electricity.

Advantages:
- Renewable and non-polluting.
- No fuel cost after installation.

Disadvantages:
- Wind is not always constant (intermittent).
- Requires large areas of land.
- High initial cost.
- Can be noisy and affect bird life.

Best locations: Coastal areas, open plains, hilltops.
India's largest wind energy farm: In Tamil Nadu (Kanyakumari area).

SOLAR ENERGY
Energy obtained from the sun.

Solar Constant: Amount of solar energy reaching the earth's surface = 1.4 kJ/s/m² (1400 W/m²).

Devices using solar energy:
1. Solar Cooker: Uses a glass-topped box with a mirror reflector to concentrate sunlight and cook food.
   - Black painted box absorbs maximum heat.
   - Glass lid creates greenhouse effect.
   - Mirror reflects more sunlight into box.
   - Temperature can reach 100-140°C.
   - Can cook rice, vegetables, dals. Cannot bake bread or fry.

2. Solar Water Heater: Flat plate collectors heat water using sunlight.

3. Solar Cells (Photovoltaic Cells):
   - Convert sunlight directly into electricity.
   - Made of semiconductor material (silicon).
   - A single cell produces about 0.5-1 V.
   - Many cells combined in a solar panel.
   - Used in calculators, satellites, street lights, remote areas.

Advantages of Solar Energy:
- Renewable and inexhaustible.
- No pollution.
- Free source.
- Low maintenance.

Disadvantages:
- Not available at night or on cloudy days.
- Requires large areas for significant power.
- Initial cost is high.
- Cannot be stored easily (needs batteries).

ENERGY FROM THE SEA

1. Tidal Energy:
- The rise and fall of sea water due to gravitational pull of moon creates tides.
- Tidal barrages (dams) across tidal inlets harness this energy.
- Water flows through turbines during high and low tides.
- Limitation: Few sites available with large tidal range.

2. Wave Energy:
- Kinetic energy of ocean surface waves.
- Devices floating on the surface convert wave motion to electricity.
- Still largely experimental.

3. Ocean Thermal Energy (OTEC):
- Uses the temperature difference between warm surface water and cold deep water.
- Needs a temperature difference of at least 20°C.
- Warm water evaporates ammonia → drives turbine → cold water condenses ammonia.

GEOTHERMAL ENERGY
Heat energy from inside the earth (from molten rocks/magma).

- Hot springs and geysers are surface manifestations.
- Steam from geothermal sources can directly drive turbines.
- Geothermal power plants are built in geothermally active regions.
- Available 24/7 (unlike solar/wind).
- Examples: Iceland, New Zealand, some parts of India.

NUCLEAR ENERGY
Energy released during nuclear reactions.

1. Nuclear Fission:
- Heavy nucleus (like Uranium-235, Plutonium-239) splits into smaller nuclei when hit by a neutron.
- Releases enormous energy + more neutrons → chain reaction.
- Used in nuclear power plants and atomic bombs.
- E = mc² (Einstein's equation)

2. Nuclear Fusion:
- Light nuclei (like hydrogen isotopes) combine at very high temperature to form a heavier nucleus.
- Releases even more energy than fission.
- Powers the sun and stars.
- Fusion bomb (hydrogen bomb).
- Controlled fusion not yet achieved (still being researched).

Nuclear Power Plant:
- Nuclear reactor controlled chain reaction.
- Heat produced → steam → turbine → generator.
- Uses enriched uranium as fuel.
- Control rods (cadmium/boron) absorb neutrons to control the reaction.

Advantages of Nuclear Energy:
- Enormous energy from small amount of fuel.
- No greenhouse gas emissions during operation.

Disadvantages:
- Risk of accidents (Chernobyl 1986, Fukushima 2011).
- Radioactive waste is dangerous for thousands of years.
- High setup cost.
- Risk of nuclear weapons proliferation.

ENVIRONMENTAL CONSEQUENCES OF ENERGY USE
- Burning fossil fuels → CO₂ → Global warming.
- SO₂ and NO₂ → Acid rain.
- Nuclear accidents → Radiation hazards.
- Dams → Displacement, ecological disruption.
- Wind farms → Land use, impact on birds.

HOW LONG WILL ENERGY SOURCES LAST?
- Coal: ~200-300 years at current rates.
- Petroleum: ~50-60 years.
- Natural gas: ~100 years.
- Uranium: Several hundred years (with breeder reactors).
- Renewable sources: Indefinitely.

IMPORTANT QUESTIONS AND ANSWERS

Q: What is a solar cell? State its working principle.
A: A solar cell (photovoltaic cell) is a device that converts solar energy directly into electricity. It works on the photovoltaic effect: when light falls on a semiconductor (silicon) p-n junction, it excites electrons, creating a potential difference (voltage) and causing current to flow.

Q: Why is biogas considered an ideal fuel for villages?
A: Biogas is ideal for villages because: (1) raw materials (dung, agricultural waste) are readily available, (2) it is clean-burning with no smoke, (3) the slurry is excellent manure for crops, (4) it is renewable, and (5) it eliminates waste disposal problems.

Q: Why are fossil fuels called non-renewable sources of energy?
A: Fossil fuels formed over millions of years from dead organisms. Once consumed, they cannot be replenished in a human timescale. At current consumption rates, they will be exhausted within a few centuries. Hence they are non-renewable.

Q: What is the difference between nuclear fission and nuclear fusion?
A: In fission, a heavy nucleus splits into smaller ones releasing energy. In fusion, light nuclei combine to form a heavier nucleus releasing even more energy. Fission is used in nuclear reactors; fusion powers the sun. Fusion requires extremely high temperatures (millions of degrees).
