1. WHAT ARE FUNGI?
-------------------
Fungi are eukaryotic heterotrophs that obtain nutrients by external digestion
(secreting enzymes and absorbing breakdown products). Kingdom Fungi; ~150,000
described species but estimated 2–5 million total.

Key Features distinguishing fungi from other kingdoms:
- Cell wall of chitin (vs cellulose in plants; no cell wall in animals).
- Absorptive nutrition (secrete digestive enzymes externally).
- Storage carbohydrate is glycogen (like animals; not starch like plants).
- Ergosterol in cell membranes (vs cholesterol in animals → antifungal target).
- Most are multicellular (hyphae) but some unicellular (yeasts).
- Non-motile (no flagella in most groups).

Fungal Body Plan:
  Hypha (pl. hyphae): Filamentous tubular structure. Tip-growing.
    Septate hyphae   : Cross-walls (septa) divide hyphae into compartments.
    Coenocytic       : No septa; multiple nuclei in continuous cytoplasm (Zygomycetes).
  Mycelium           : Network of hyphae; the main fungal body.
  Fruiting body      : Reproductive structure visible above ground (mushroom).
  Dikaryon (+n + n)  : Two haploid nuclei coexist per cell without fusing.

Major Fungal Groups:
- Chytrids (Chytridiomycota)  : Oldest; aquatic; motile spores; includes
                                  Batrachochytrium dendrobatidis (frog killer).
- Zygomycetes                  : Bread moulds (Rhizopus); coenocytic; sexual via zygospores.
- Ascomycetes (Ascomycota)     : Largest group; sac fungi; sexual spores in ascus.
                                  Includes: yeasts (Saccharomyces), morels, truffles,
                                  Aspergillus, Penicillium, Fusarium, Candida.
- Basidiomycetes (Basidiomycota): Club fungi; sexual spores on basidia.
                                  Includes: mushrooms, puffballs, rusts, smuts.
                                  Includes Cryptococcus, Amanita (death cap).
- Glomeromycetes               : Arbuscular mycorrhizal fungi (AMF); obligate symbiont.

1.1 FUNGAL REPRODUCTION
------------------------
Asexual:
  Mitospores: Conidia (Aspergillus, Penicillium); sporangiospores (Rhizopus); budding (yeast).

Sexual:
  Plasmogamy (cytoplasm fusion) → Karyogamy (nucleus fusion) → Meiosis → Spores.
  Ascomycetes: Ascus contains 8 ascospores (products of meiosis + one mitosis).
  Basidiomycetes: Basidium produces 4 basidiospores externally.
  Zygomycetes: Zygospore (thick-walled resting spore).

2. FUNGAL ECOLOGY
------------------
Decomposers (Saprotrophs):
  Fungi are the primary decomposers of lignocellulosic material (wood, plant litter).
  White rot fungi (Pleurotus, Trametes): Degrade lignin via peroxidases (lignin
  peroxidase, manganese peroxidase) + laccases.
  Brown rot fungi: Degrade cellulose/hemicellulose; leave brown lignin residue.
  Critical for nutrient cycling — especially carbon and nitrogen.

Mycorrhizal Associations:
  ~80–90% of vascular plant species form mycorrhizal symbiosis.
  Ectomycorrhizae (ECM): Hyphal sheath wraps around roots; Hartig net (between cells).
  Trees (oak, pine, birch) + Basidiomycetes. Fungi get sugars; plants get N, P, water.
  Arbuscular mycorrhizae (AM): Hyphae penetrate root cells → arbuscules (exchange organs).
  Glomeromycetes; colonise most crops. Cannot be cultured without host plant.
  Wood Wide Web: ECM network connects forest trees → C and N transfer between trees.
  Mother trees (Suzanne Simard): Large trees support seedlings via fungal network.

Lichens: Mutualistic symbiosis of fungi + photosynthetic partner (algae or cyanobacteria).
  Fungus (mycobiont): Provides structure, protection, water retention.
  Photobiont: Provides organic carbon via photosynthesis.
  Pioneer species: Colonise bare rock; contribute to soil formation.
  Sensitive to air pollution → bioindicators of air quality.

3. MEDICALLY IMPORTANT FUNGI
------------------------------
Candida albicans:
  Normal microbiome (gut, vagina); becomes pathogenic with immunosuppression,
  antibiotic use, or diabetes.
  Dimorphic: Yeast form (commensalism) ↔ Hyphal form (invasive/pathogenic).
  Switching controlled by: Morphogenetic signals (CO2, N-acetylglucosamine, serum).
  Candidaemia: Life-threatening bloodstream infection; mortality ~40%.
  Treatment: Fluconazole (azole); echinocandins (caspofungin — 1st-line for severe);
  amphotericin B (broadest spectrum; most toxic).
  Candida auris: Emerging multidrug-resistant species; nosocomial outbreaks; WHO
  critical priority fungal pathogen.

Aspergillus fumigatus:
  Ubiquitous mould; produces ~1 million conidia/head.
  Invasive Aspergillosis (IA): Life-threatening in neutropenic/immunosuppressed.
  Angioinvasion → haematogenous spread → necrosis. Halo sign on CT.
  Treatment: Voriconazole (first-line); isavuconazole; liposomal amphotericin B.
  Galactomannan antigen: Serum/BAL → diagnosis.
  Azole-resistant Aspergillus: CYP51A mutations → increasing problem globally.

Cryptococcus neoformans/gattii:
  Encapsulated yeast; polysaccharide capsule inhibits phagocytosis.
  Cryptococcal meningitis: Mainly in AIDS (CD4 <100); 100,000 deaths/year globally.
  Diagnosis: India ink (visualise capsule); cryptococcal antigen test.
  Treatment: Induction with liposomal AmB + flucytosine → consolidation fluconazole.

Dermatophytes (Tinea infections):
  Trichophyton, Microsporum, Epidermophyton; digest keratin.
  Tinea pedis (athlete's foot); T. unguium (onychomycosis); T. capitis (scalp);
  T. corporis (ringworm); T. cruris (jock itch).
  Treatment: Topical antifungals (terbinafine, clotrimazole); oral for nail/scalp.

Pneumocystis jirovecii (PCP):
  Atypical fungus (previously classified as protozoan). Cannot be cultured.
  Cause of PCP pneumonia in immunosuppressed (AIDS <200 CD4, transplant, chemotherapy).
  Treatment: Co-trimoxazole (TMP-SMX). Prophylaxis when CD4 <200.

5. FUNGAL MOLECULAR BIOLOGY
-----------------------------
Saccharomyces cerevisiae as a Model Organism:
  First eukaryote genome sequenced (1996); 16 chromosomes; ~6,000 genes.
  Easy genetics: Haploid and diploid forms; knockout libraries (yeast deletion
  collection); two-hybrid system for protein interactions.
  Discoveries using yeast: Cell cycle regulation (Hartwell, Nobel 2001);
  autophagy (Ohsumi, Nobel 2016); protein secretion (Schekman, Nobel 2013);
  RNA splicing; TOR pathway; telomeres.

Pheromone Signalling & Mating:
  Haploid yeast exist in two mating types: MATa and MATalpha.
  MATa secretes a-factor (farnesylated peptide); MATalpha secretes alpha-factor.
  Pheromone binds GPCR → G-protein → MAPK cascade (Ste11 → Ste7 → Fus3/Kss1)
  → Ste12 transcription factor → mating genes → cell cycle arrest (G1) → fusion.
  Archetype for GPCR signalling in eukaryotes.

Chromatin and Gene Regulation in S. cerevisiae:
  SWI/SNF chromatin remodelling complex first characterized in yeast.
  HO endonuclease: Catalyses mating-type switching — model for regulated gene expression.
  Silent chromatin: HML and HMR loci silenced by Sir2 (sirtuin) + Sir3 + Sir4 histone
  deacetylation → telomeric silencing model.
  Sir2 (SIRT1 homolog): NAD+-dependent deacetylase; caloric restriction → increased
  Sir2 activity → lifespan extension in yeast.

6. BIOTECHNOLOGY APPLICATIONS OF FUNGI
----------------------------------------
Antibiotics from Fungi:
  Penicillin (Fleming, 1928): Discovered from Penicillium contamination of Staph plate.
  Penicillium chrysogenum: Industrial penicillin production (deepfermentation).
  Cephalosporins: From Acremonium chrysogenum.
  Griseofulvin: From Penicillium → antifungal.
  Lovastatin: From Aspergillus terreus → cholesterol-lowering drug (first statin).
  Ciclosporin: From Tolypocladium inflatum → immunosuppressant (organ transplant).
  Mycophenolic acid: Penicillium stoloniferum → immunosuppressant (MMF).

Industrial Uses:
  Food: Baker's yeast (S. cerevisiae); beer and wine fermentation; cheese (Penicillium
  roqueforti → Roquefort; P. camemberti → Camembert); soy sauce (Aspergillus oryzae);
  citric acid production (A. niger).
  Biofuels: Yeast fermentation of lignocellulosic biomass → bioethanol.
  Enzymes: Cellulases, proteases, lipases, amylases from Aspergillus/Trichoderma.
  Recombinant proteins: Pichia pastoris (now Komagataella phaffii) → hepatitis B surface
  antigen, insulin, erythropoietin, antibody fragments.

[EMERGING THREAT] Ophiocordyceps (zombie ant fungus):
  Infects carpenter ants → hijacks neural/muscular control → ant bites vegetation
  at precise height/location optimised for sporulation → fruiting body erupts from head.
  Mechanism: Fungal compounds affect serotonin/dopamine signalling; hyphal invasion
  of muscles (not brain); exact mechanism under intense study.
  Batrachochytrium dendrobatidis (Bd): Has caused extinction/decline of >500 amphibian
  species — worst vertebrate disease-driven mass extinction ever recorded.
