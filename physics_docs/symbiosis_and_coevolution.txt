1. SYMBIOSIS
-------------
Symbiosis (Greek: "living together"): A close, persistent, intimate relationship
between two or more different species. All symbiotic relationships fall on a spectrum
from highly beneficial to highly harmful.

Types of Symbiosis:
  Mutualism      : Both species benefit (+/+). Most productive; often highly coevolved.
  Commensalism   : One benefits, other unaffected (+/0). Rare in strict sense.
  Parasitism     : One benefits, other harmed (+/-). Most common symbiosis in nature.
  Competition    : Both harmed (-/-). Not always considered symbiosis.
  Amensalism     : One harmed, other unaffected (0/-). Penicillium kills bacteria.
  Predation      : Consumer kills and eats prey; sometimes considered parasitism.

1.1 MUTUALISM EXAMPLES
------------------------
Obligate mutualism (neither partner can survive without the other):
  - Mycorrhizal fungi + plants: ~450 million years co-evolution. Plant provides C;
    fungus provides P, N, water. Plants without mycorrhizae grow poorly on low-P soils.
  - Rhizobium + legumes: N2 fixation; 12–40% crop nitrogen from symbiosis in well-nodulated legumes.
  - Yucca moth + Yucca plant: Moth pollinates yucca; lays eggs in flower; larvae eat some seeds.
  - Ants + acacia trees: Ants defend tree against herbivores; tree provides food (Beltian bodies)
    + shelter (hollow thorns). Ants prune competing vegetation around tree.
  - Bobtail squid + Aliivibrio fischeri: Bioluminescent bacteria in light organ; squid provides
    nutrients + housing; bacteria produce counter-illumination camouflage.
  - Fig + fig wasp: Each fig species has unique fig wasp pollinator. Wasp lays eggs in fig;
    pollinates flowers; ~80 million years co-evolution.
  - Coral + zooxanthellae: Covered in marine biology document.
  - Termites + gut microbiome: Protists + bacteria in hindgut ferment cellulose → energy.

Facultative mutualism (both benefit but can survive without the other):
  - Cleaner wrasse + client fish: Removes ectoparasites from client. Cleaning stations.
  - Oxpecker + large mammals: Removes ticks from buffalo/rhino (also disputed — feeds on wounds).
  - Cattle egret + large herbivores: Feeds on insects disturbed by grazing animals.

2. COEVOLUTION
---------------
Coevolution: Reciprocal evolutionary changes between two or more species due to
their ecological interaction. Each species acts as a selective pressure on the other.

Coevolutionary Arms Races:
  Host-Parasite: Parasite evolves to exploit host → host evolves resistance →
  parasite evolves counter-resistance (Red Queen hypothesis).
  Predator-Prey: Cheetah speed co-evolves with Thomson's gazelle escape speed.
  Plant-Herbivore: Plants evolve toxins (alkaloids, glucosinolates) → herbivores
    evolve detoxification enzymes → plants evolve more potent toxins.
    Example: Monarch butterfly detoxifies milkweed cardenolides → specialist herbivore.
    Specialist vs generalist herbivores: Specialists tolerate host toxins; generalists avoid.

Diffuse Coevolution:
  Most coevolution is not pairwise — involves multiple species.
  Pollination syndromes: Flower shape, colour, scent, reward type adapted to pollinator guild.
    Bee flowers: Yellow/blue; UV guides; landing platform; scent; open during day.
    Moth flowers: White; strong night scent; deep tube (hawkmoth tongue).
    Bird flowers: Red (birds lack smell sensitivity); copious nectar; sturdy perch/none.
    Bat flowers: Dull; musty smell; large; open at night; robust.

3. ENDOSYMBIOSIS
-----------------
Secondary endosymbiosis: Eukaryote engulfs another eukaryote → multilayered chloroplasts.
  Cryptophytes, diatoms, dinoflagellates, euglenoids: All have secondary plastids.
  Kleptoplasty: Sea slugs (Elysia chlorotica) retain chloroplasts from algae → remain
  photosynthetically active for months; horizontal gene transfer of algal genes into sea slug genome.

Wolbachia:
  Gram-negative alpha-proteobacterium; intracellular endosymbiont of ~60–70% of insect species.
  Maternally inherited (in cytoplasm of eggs). Evolution of selfish element:
  Cytoplasmic incompatibility (CI): Wolbachia-infected males cannot successfully fertilise
  uninfected females → infected females have reproductive advantage → Wolbachia spreads.
  Male killing: Kills male offspring → more daughters (infected) → more Wolbachia spread.
  Feminisation: Converts genetic males into females.
  Parthenogenesis induction: Unfertilised eggs develop into females.
  Obligate mutualism: Some species cannot reproduce without Wolbachia (filarial nematodes;
  some Drosophila populations).
  Biocontrol: Wolbachia reduces dengue/Zika/malaria transmission in mosquitoes.

4. POLLINATION BIOLOGY
------------------------
~87% of flowering plant species are pollinated by animals. Co-evolutionary relationship
between flowers and pollinators is one of the great drivers of angiosperm diversification.

Pollinator Types & Adaptations:
  Honeybees: Most important agricultural pollinator. Communicate flower location (waggle dance).
    Eusocial; colonial; carry pollen in corbiculae (pollen baskets) on hindlegs.
    Colour vision: UV, blue, green (not red). Favourite flowers: Blue, yellow, white.
  Bumblebees: Important wild pollinators. Buzz pollination (sonication): Vibrate thorax at
    resonant frequency of anthers → releases pollen trapped in poricidal anthers (tomato, blueberry).
  Butterflies: Long tongues; prefer clustered flowers; important for milkweed, lantana.
  Hawkmoths: Long proboscis; hover while feeding; night-blooming flowers with strong scent.
  Hummingbirds: New World only; hover; prefer red tubular flowers with copious nectar.
  Bats: Old World fruit bats (Pteropodidae) pollinate >500 plant species including banana, mango, durian.

Decline of Pollinators:
  Colony collapse disorder (CCD) in honeybees: Varroa mite + Nosema + neonicotinoids + habitat loss.
  Wild bee decline: ~40% wild bee species threatened. Loss of wildflowers; pesticides; parasites; climate.
  Economic impact: >€150 billion/year crop production value dependent on pollinators.

5. MOLECULAR COEVOLUTION
--------------------------
Gene-for-Gene Hypothesis (Flor, 1956):
  In plant-pathogen interactions: For each R (resistance) gene in the host, there is a
  corresponding Avr (avirulence) gene in the pathogen.
  Pathogen Avr protein detected by plant R protein (NBS-LRR) → ETI → hypersensitive response.
  Pathogen evolves to lose/modify Avr gene → escape ETI → plant evolves new R gene.
  Arms race at molecular level: Rapidly evolving NBS-LRR gene families; Avr genes under
  strong diversifying selection. Evidenced by patterns of positive selection in both.

Reciprocal Evolution of Immune Receptors:
  MHC polymorphism: Pathogen diversity → strong diversifying selection on MHC genes →
  humans have most diverse MHC of any known species.
  Host antiviral genes (restriction factors) under extreme positive selection:
    TRIM5alpha, APOBEC3G (HIV restriction), MX1 (influenza), tetherin/BST-2.
    Viral antagonists (HIV Vif degrades APOBEC3G) coevolve with host restriction.

Endogenous Retroviruses (ERVs):
  ~8% of human genome consists of retrovirally-derived sequences (vs ~1.5% protein-coding).
  Ancient viral integrations now "domesticated" as host genes:
    Syncytins (ERVW-1): Fusogenic envelope protein → syncytiotrophoblast formation in placenta.
    HARBI1, PGBD5: Transposase-derived genes → DNA repair functions.
    Arc (Activity-Regulated Cytoskeleton): Capsid-like protein → transports mRNA between neurons
    → critical for synaptic plasticity; evolved from Ty3/gypsy retrotransposons.

6. MICROBIOME AS EXTENDED PHENOTYPE
--------------------------------------
The microbiome influences host evolution:
  Hologenome theory (Zilber-Rosenberg & Rosenberg): Host + microbiome = holobiont;
  selection acts on holobiont as a unit. Heritable microbiome → part of extended phenotype.

Host-Microbiome Coevolution Evidence:
  Gut microbiome composition partially heritable (H² ~15–40%); host genetics influence
  microbial composition (e.g., LCT gene → lactose → Bifidobacterium; FUT2 secretor status
  → Bifidobacterium).
  Bifidobacterium infantis + human milk oligosaccharides (HMOs): B. infantis has evolved
  specific transporters to preferentially consume HMOs → advantage in breastfed infants.
  Modern hygiene disrupts coevolution: Loss of ancestral microbiome members (Helicobacter pylori,
  Faecalibacterium prausnitzii) linked to autoimmunity, allergy, IBD (disappearing microbiota hypothesis — Blaser).

[EVOLUTIONARY PERSPECTIVE] The boundary between "organism" and "symbiont" is blurred.
  Mitochondria were once free-living bacteria; chloroplasts were once cyanobacteria;
  ERVs are ancient viruses now integral to genomes; Wolbachia can be essential for host
  reproduction; the gut microbiome influences immunity, metabolism, behaviour, and cognition.
  Life has always been a cooperative endeavour — evolution is not solely about competition
  but equally about the formation of novel partnerships that open new ecological possibilities.
  ~80% of the living world's complexity may be explained by symbiosis as much as by selection.
