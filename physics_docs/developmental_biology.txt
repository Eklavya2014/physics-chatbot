1. FROM FERTILISATION TO ORGANISM
-----------------------------------
Developmental biology studies how a single fertilised cell (zygote) gives rise to
a complex, multicellular organism through regulated cell division, differentiation,
and morphogenesis.

Key Processes:
- Cell division      : Mitosis produces more cells; meiosis produces gametes.
- Cell differentiation: Cells become specialised (e.g., muscle, nerve, blood).
- Morphogenesis      : Cells organise into tissues, organs, and body structures.
- Growth             : Increase in size of the organism.
- Apoptosis          : Programmed cell death sculpts structures (e.g., digits).

1.1 FERTILISATION & EARLY DEVELOPMENT (HUMAN)
-----------------------------------------------
Fertilisation: Sperm penetrates oocyte → acrosome reaction (sperm enzymes digest
zona pellucida) → cortical reaction (prevents polyspermy) → 2N zygote formed.

Cleavage: Rapid mitotic divisions with no growth → morula (16-cell solid ball) →
blastocyst (hollow ball with inner cell mass [ICM] + trophoblast).
  ICM → embryo proper (all three germ layers + extraembryonic membranes).
  Trophoblast → placenta.

Implantation: Blastocyst implants in uterine wall (~6–10 days post-fertilisation).

Gastrulation (~week 3): ICM reorganises into three primary germ layers:
  Ectoderm  → Skin, nervous system, sensory organs.
  Mesoderm  → Muscle, bone, blood, heart, kidneys, gonads.
  Endoderm  → Gut lining, liver, pancreas, lungs, thyroid.

Neurulation: Notochord induces overlying ectoderm to form neural plate →
neural folds rise and fuse → neural tube (→ brain + spinal cord).
Neural crest cells migrate to form: facial bones, peripheral NS, melanocytes,
adrenal medulla.

Organogenesis: (~weeks 3–8): Major organs form. Highly sensitive to teratogens
(alcohol, thalidomide, rubella, valproate) during this period.

1.2 STEM CELLS
---------------
Stem cells are undifferentiated cells that can:
  1. Self-renew: Divide to produce more stem cells.
  2. Differentiate: Give rise to specialised cell types.

Potency Hierarchy:
- Totipotent  : Can form entire organism including extraembryonic membranes.
                Only zygote and first 2–4 blastomeres are totipotent.
- Pluripotent : Can form all cells of embryo proper (3 germ layers) but not
                trophoblast. Embryonic stem cells (ESCs); iPSCs.
- Multipotent : Can form multiple but limited cell types.
                Example: Haematopoietic stem cell → all blood cell types.
- Unipotent   : One cell type only. Example: Spermatogonial stem cells.

2. CELL SIGNALLING IN DEVELOPMENT
-----------------------------------
Development is orchestrated by signalling molecules (morphogens and growth factors)
that act in concentration-dependent gradients to specify cell identity.

2.1 KEY DEVELOPMENTAL SIGNALLING PATHWAYS
-------------------------------------------
Wnt Signalling:
  Wnt OFF: beta-catenin is phosphorylated by destruction complex (APC/Axin/GSK-3)
           → ubiquitinated → proteasomal degradation.
  Wnt ON : Wnt binds Frizzled + LRP5/6 → Dishevelled activated → destruction
           complex inhibited → beta-catenin accumulates → enters nucleus →
           activates TCF/LEF transcription factors → target genes (Cyclin D1, c-Myc).
  Roles: Axis specification, intestinal crypt homeostasis, hair follicle cycling.
  Disease: APC mutations → Wnt overactivation → colorectal cancer.

Notch Signalling:
  Contact-dependent; lateral inhibition. Delta/Jagged ligand on signal cell binds
  Notch receptor on receiving cell → gamma-secretase cleaves NICD (Notch intracellular
  domain) → enters nucleus → activates HES/HEY transcription factors.
  Roles: Neurogenesis, somitogenesis, vascular patterning, T vs B cell fate.

Hedgehog (Hh) Signalling:
  Hh absent: PTCH1 inhibits Smoothened (Smo) → Gli transcription factors cleaved
             to repressor forms.
  Hh present: Hh binds PTCH1 → Smo activated → Gli activator forms → target genes.
  SHH: Left-right asymmetry, limb patterning (anterior-posterior axis), floor plate.
  Disease: PTCH1 mutations → Gorlin syndrome (basal cell carcinomas); Smo mutations
           common in sporadic BCCs.

TGF-beta/BMP Signalling:
  Ligand binds type II receptor → phosphorylates type I receptor → Smad2/3 (TGF-b)
  or Smad1/5/8 (BMP) phosphorylated → complex with Smad4 → nuclear translocation
  → gene regulation.
  BMP4: Epidermal vs neural fate (BMP inhibition by chordin/noggin/follistatin
  → promotes neural tissue; "BMP inhibition = default neural").
  TGF-beta: Epithelial-mesenchymal transition (EMT) in development and cancer.

3. PATTERN FORMATION & BODY AXES
----------------------------------
Anterior-Posterior Axis:
  Drosophila (model system): Bicoid mRNA localised anteriorly → Bicoid protein
  gradient → activates anterior genes (e.g., hunchback) in concentration-dependent
  manner. Posterior: Nanos gradient inhibits Hunchback. Defines head vs tail.

  Hox genes: Highly conserved homeodomain transcription factors that specify
  segment identity along A-P axis. Collinear expression: 3' Hox genes in anterior;
  5' Hox genes in posterior. Found in all bilaterian animals.

Dorsal-Ventral Axis:
  Drosophila: Dorsal protein (NF-kB homolog) gradient; high ventral → neural ectoderm
  vs mesoderm patterning. Tolloid/Sog (BMP regulator) interaction.

Left-Right Asymmetry:
  Nodal cilia at embryonic node beat asymmetrically → leftward fluid flow →
  Nodal signalling on left side only → Lefty (inhibitor) + Pitx2 (transcription factor)
  → heart, stomach, liver adopt asymmetric positions.
  Mutations in cilia genes → Kartagener syndrome (situs inversus, bronchiectasis).

4. SEX DETERMINATION & DIFFERENTIATION
----------------------------------------
Chromosomal sex: XX = female; XY = male.
SRY gene (Y chromosome): Encodes testis-determining factor → activates SOX9 →
Sertoli cells differentiate → AMH (Mullerian Inhibiting Hormone) secreted →
Mullerian ducts (→ uterus, fallopian tubes) regress.
Leydig cells: Testosterone → Wolffian ducts develop → vas deferens, epididymis.
Without SRY: Ovaries develop; Mullerian ducts form uterus/fallopian tubes.

Disorders of Sexual Development (DSDs):
  Congenital Adrenal Hyperplasia (CAH): 21-hydroxylase deficiency → excess androgens
  → virilisation of XX individuals.
  Androgen Insensitivity Syndrome (AIS): XY; non-functional androgen receptor →
  female phenotype.

5. EPIGENETICS IN DEVELOPMENT
-------------------------------
Epigenetics: Heritable changes in gene expression without DNA sequence change.
Critical during development for establishing and maintaining cell identity.

DNA Methylation:
  CpG methylation by DNMT3A/3B (de novo) established in early embryo.
  Maintained by DNMT1 (copies methylation pattern after replication).
  Global demethylation → remethylation occurs twice: at fertilisation and in PGCs.
  Imprinting: Allele-specific methylation established in germline; maintained
  throughout life. ~80 imprinted genes in humans.

Genomic Imprinting:
  Parent-of-origin gene expression. Paternal allele expressed, maternal silenced
  (or vice versa) due to differential germline methylation.
  IGF2 (paternal) / H19 (maternal): Regulated by ICR (imprinting control region)
  CTCF binding when unmethylated (maternal) acts as insulator → H19 expressed.
  Disorders:
    Prader-Willi syndrome: Loss of paternal 15q11-13.
    Angelman syndrome   : Loss of maternal 15q11-13 (UBE3A).
    Beckwith-Wiedemann  : Biallelic IGF2 expression → overgrowth.

Histone Code:
  Polycomb group (PcG) complexes: PRC2 (EZH2) deposits H3K27me3 → gene silencing.
  Trithorax group (TrxG): H3K4me3 → gene activation.
  Bivalent domains: H3K27me3 + H3K4me3 co-occupancy in ESCs → genes poised for
  rapid activation or silencing during differentiation.

6. INDUCED PLURIPOTENT STEM CELLS (iPSCs)
-------------------------------------------
Yamanaka (2006, Nobel Prize 2012):
  Adult somatic cells (fibroblasts) reprogrammed to pluripotency by introducing
  4 transcription factors: OCT4, SOX2, KLF4, c-MYC (Yamanaka factors).
  Mechanism: Remodelling of epigenetic landscape → pluripotency gene network activated.

iPSC Applications:
  - Disease modelling (patient-specific "disease in a dish")
  - Drug screening
  - Cell therapy (autologous; no immune rejection)
  - Organ-on-chip development

Organoids:
  3D self-organising structures derived from stem cells that recapitulate organ
  architecture and function. Types: intestinal, brain (cerebral), liver, kidney,
  lung organoids. Key tool for: drug testing, disease modelling, personalised medicine.
  Cerebral organoids (Lancaster 2013): Model human cortical development; used to
  study microcephaly and SARS-CoV-2 neurotropism.

7. REGENERATION & AGEING
--------------------------
Animal Regeneration:
  Planaria: Remarkable — can regenerate entire body from tiny fragment.
            Neoblasts (pluripotent stem cells) drive regeneration.
  Axolotl:  Regenerates limbs, heart, spinal cord. Dedifferentiation of mature
            cells → blastema (pool of progenitors) → patterned regrowth.
  Mammals:  Limited regeneration. Liver: regenerates ~70% mass loss.
            Heart: Very limited; cardiomyocytes divide at ~1%/year.

Hallmarks of Ageing (Lopez-Otin et al., 2013; updated 2023):
  Genomic instability, Telomere attrition, Epigenetic alterations,
  Loss of proteostasis, Deregulated nutrient sensing (mTOR, AMPK, sirtuins),
  Mitochondrial dysfunction, Cellular senescence, Stem cell exhaustion,
  Altered intercellular communication, Disabled macroautophagy,
  Chronic inflammation, Dysbiosis.

Cellular Senescence:
  Permanent cell cycle arrest in response to DNA damage, oncogene activation,
  or telomere shortening. Senescent cells secrete SASP (Senescence-Associated
  Secretory Phenotype): IL-6, IL-8, MMPs → chronic inflammation.
  Senolytics (dasatinib + quercetin) clear senescent cells → extend healthspan in mice.

[CLINICAL LINK] Congenital malformations affect ~3% of births. Neural tube defects
(spina bifida, anencephaly) result from failed neurulation; folic acid
supplementation reduces risk by ~70% (folate is cofactor for nucleotide synthesis
needed during rapid cell division). Thalidomide (teratogen) inhibits cereblon →
disrupts FGF signalling → limb truncations (phocomelia) in embryos exposed in
weeks 3–8 of development.
