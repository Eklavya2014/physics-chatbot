1. WHAT IS A CELL?
------------------
The cell is the fundamental structural and functional unit of all living
organisms. Every living thing — from a single-celled bacterium to a
trillion-celled human — is composed of cells. Cells carry out all the
processes necessary for life: obtaining energy, growing, reproducing, and
responding to their environment.

Cell Theory (formulated by Schleiden, Schwann, and Virchow, 1830s–1850s):
  1. All living organisms are composed of one or more cells.
  2. The cell is the basic unit of life.
  3. All cells arise from pre-existing cells.

1.1 PROKARYOTIC VS. EUKARYOTIC CELLS
--------------------------------------
Feature              | Prokaryotic                  | Eukaryotic
---------------------|------------------------------|-----------------------------
Nucleus              | Absent (nucleoid region)     | Present (membrane-bound)
Size                 | 1–10 µm                      | 10–100 µm
Organelles           | None (membrane-bound)        | Many (mitochondria, ER, etc.)
DNA                  | Circular, 1 chromosome       | Linear, multiple chromosomes
Examples             | Bacteria, Archaea            | Plants, Animals, Fungi

1.2 KEY CELL ORGANELLES
------------------------
- Cell Membrane     : Phospholipid bilayer; controls what enters/exits the cell.
- Nucleus           : Contains DNA; directs cell activities — the "control center."
- Mitochondria      : Produces ATP via cellular respiration — the "powerhouse."
- Ribosomes         : Sites of protein synthesis; free or attached to ER.
- Rough ER          : Synthesizes and processes proteins.
- Smooth ER         : Produces lipids and detoxifies substances.
- Golgi Apparatus   : Processes and packages proteins for secretion.
- Lysosomes         : Contain digestive enzymes; break down waste materials.
- Chloroplasts      : Found in plant cells; site of photosynthesis.
- Vacuoles          : Storage organelles; large central vacuole in plant cells.
- Cell Wall         : Rigid outer layer in plant/fungal/bacterial cells.

2. CELL MEMBRANE & TRANSPORT
------------------------------
The plasma membrane is described by the Fluid Mosaic Model (Singer & Nicolson,
1972). It is a phospholipid bilayer with embedded proteins, cholesterol, and
glycolipids. Hydrophobic tails face inward; hydrophilic heads face outward.

Transport Mechanisms:
- Passive Diffusion      : Small nonpolar molecules (O2, CO2) move down gradient.
                           No energy required.
- Facilitated Diffusion  : Movement through channel/carrier proteins; still
                           down gradient; no ATP needed.
- Osmosis                : Water moves through semipermeable membrane from low
                           to high solute concentration.
- Active Transport       : Movement AGAINST concentration gradient; requires ATP.
                           Example: Na+/K+ pump (3 Na+ out, 2 K+ in).
- Endocytosis            : Cell engulfs material — phagocytosis (solids),
                           pinocytosis (liquids), receptor-mediated.
- Exocytosis             : Vesicles fuse with membrane to release contents.

3. CELLULAR RESPIRATION
------------------------
Converts glucose + oxygen into ATP, CO2, and water.
Overall equation: C6H12O6 + 6O2 → 6CO2 + 6H2O + 36–38 ATP

Stage                   | Location                    | ATP Yield  | Key Products
------------------------|-----------------------------|-----------|--------------
Glycolysis              | Cytoplasm                   | 2 ATP (net)| 2 Pyruvate, 2 NADH
Pyruvate Oxidation      | Mitochondrial matrix        | 0 ATP      | 2 Acetyl-CoA, 2 NADH
Krebs Cycle             | Mitochondrial matrix        | 2 ATP      | 6 NADH, 2 FADH2
Electron Transport Chain| Inner mitochondrial membrane| 32–34 ATP  | H2O

4. PHOTOSYNTHESIS
-----------------
Converts light energy into chemical energy (glucose). Occurs in chloroplasts.
Overall: 6CO2 + 6H2O + Light → C6H12O6 + 6O2

Two Stages:
- Light-Dependent Reactions (Thylakoid membranes):
    Chlorophyll absorbs light. Water is split (photolysis), releasing O2.
    ATP and NADPH are produced.
- Calvin Cycle / Light-Independent Reactions (Stroma):
    CO2 is fixed using ATP and NADPH to produce G3P, which builds glucose.

5. CELL SIGNALING & SIGNAL TRANSDUCTION
-----------------------------------------
Three core steps: Reception → Transduction → Response.
Ligands bind receptors, triggering intracellular cascades.

Receptor Classes:
- GPCRs (G-Protein Coupled Receptors): Largest receptor family. Activate
  adenylyl cyclase (cAMP pathway) or phospholipase C (IP3/DAG pathway).
- Receptor Tyrosine Kinases (RTKs): Dimerize on ligand binding;
  autophosphorylate; activate Ras-MAPK and PI3K-Akt pathways.
- Ion Channel Receptors: Ligand binding opens/closes ion channels.
  Critical in neurons (e.g., acetylcholine at neuromuscular junction).
- Nuclear Receptors: Steroid/thyroid hormones enter cell and bind intracellular
  receptors that act directly as transcription factors.

6. CELL CYCLE & CHECKPOINTS
-----------------------------
Regulated by cyclin-CDK complexes.

Checkpoint             | Phase     | What is Checked              | Key Regulator
-----------------------|-----------|------------------------------|----------------
G1 Restriction Point   | Late G1   | Cell size, nutrients, growth | Cyclin D/CDK4/6
G2/M Checkpoint        | G2 → M    | DNA replication, damage      | Cyclin B/CDK1
Spindle Assembly (SAC) | M phase   | Kinetochore-spindle attach.  | Mad2, BubR1

7. APOPTOSIS (PROGRAMMED CELL DEATH)
--------------------------------------
Essential for development, homeostasis, and immune function.

Intrinsic Pathway:
  Cellular stress → cytochrome c released from mitochondria → apoptosome
  formation → caspase-9 activation → caspase-3/7 cascade → cell death.
  Regulated by Bcl-2 family: anti-apoptotic (Bcl-2, Bcl-xL) vs.
  pro-apoptotic (Bax, Bak, BH3-only proteins).

Extrinsic Pathway:
  Death ligands (FasL, TNF) bind death receptors → recruit FADD →
  activate caspase-8 → activate effector caspases directly.

[CLINICAL LINK] Cancer arises when cell cycle checkpoints fail. Mutations in
tumor suppressors (TP53, RB1) or oncogenes (RAS, MYC) drive uncontrolled
proliferation. Targeted therapies include imatinib (BCR-ABL inhibitor in CML)
and trastuzumab (HER2-targeting in breast cancer).
