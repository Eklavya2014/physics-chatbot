THERMODYNAMICS AND CHEMICAL EQUILIBRIUM - COMPREHENSIVE GUIDE
==============================================================

PART 1: THERMODYNAMICS

SYSTEM AND SURROUNDINGS:
System: part of universe under study.
Surroundings: rest of universe.
Open system: exchanges both matter and energy with surroundings.
Closed system: exchanges only energy.
Isolated system: no exchange of matter or energy.

STATE FUNCTIONS vs PATH FUNCTIONS:
State functions: depend ONLY on current state, not on how that state was reached.
Examples: T, P, V, U (internal energy), H (enthalpy), S (entropy), G (Gibbs energy).
Path functions: depend on the path taken.
Examples: q (heat), w (work). q and w individually depend on path, but q+w = ΔU (state function).

WORK IN THERMODYNAMICS:
w = work done ON the system = −P_ext × ΔV (expansion work).
For reversible expansion: w_rev = −∫P dV = −nRT ln(V₂/V₁) = nRT ln(P₂/P₁).
For irreversible (constant external pressure): w_irrev = −P_ext ΔV.
|w_rev| > |w_irrev| for expansion. Reversible work is maximum work obtainable.
Electrical work: w_elec = −nFE.

FIRST LAW:
ΔU = q + w (where w = work done ON system, q = heat absorbed by system).
For isolated system: ΔU = 0.
For a cycle: ΔU = 0, so q = −w.
At constant volume: ΔU = qᵥ.
At constant pressure: ΔH = qₚ.

ENTHALPY AND ITS APPLICATIONS:
H = U + PV → ΔH = ΔU + Δ(PV) = ΔU + PΔV (constant P) = ΔU + ΔnᵧRT (ideal gas).
ΔH = qₚ (heat at constant pressure — most lab reactions occur at constant P).

Standard enthalpy of formation (ΔHf°): enthalpy change when 1 mole of substance formed from elements in their standard states at 1 bar, 298 K.
ΔHf° of all elements in standard state = 0 by definition.

Hess's Law: ΔH is independent of pathway.
ΔH°rxn = ΣΔHf°(products) − ΣΔHf°(reactants).

Enthalpy of combustion: heat released when 1 mole burns completely in O₂.
Enthalpy of neutralization: −57.1 kJ/mol (for strong acid + strong base → water).
Enthalpy of atomization: energy to convert 1 mole of substance to gaseous atoms.
Bond dissociation enthalpy: energy to break 1 mole of specific bond in gaseous state.

KIRCHHOFF'S LAW (variation of ΔH with temperature):
ΔHT₂ = ΔHT₁ + ΔCp(T₂ − T₁) [ΔCp = Σcp(products) − Σcp(reactants)]

ENTROPY:
dS = dqrev/T (thermodynamic definition).
S = kB ln Ω (Boltzmann definition, microscopic).

Standard entropy (S°): absolute entropy at 298 K, 1 bar.
Unlike ΔHf°, S° of elements is NOT zero.
S° increases: solid < liquid << gas.
ΔS° = ΣS°(products) − ΣS°(reactants).

Processes that increase entropy:
- Solid → liquid → gas (phase transitions).
- Dissolving.
- Mixing.
- Increase in temperature.
- Reactions that increase moles of gas (Δngas > 0).

GIBBS FREE ENERGY (MOST IMPORTANT IN CHEMISTRY):
G = H − TS → ΔG = ΔH − TΔS (at constant T and P).
ΔG < 0: spontaneous (product-favored).
ΔG = 0: equilibrium.
ΔG > 0: non-spontaneous (reactant-favored).

ΔG = ΔG° + RT ln Q [Q = reaction quotient].
At equilibrium Q = K: ΔG = 0 → ΔG° = −RT ln K.

ΔG° = −RT ln K = −nFE°.
K = exp(−ΔG°/RT).
For large K: ΔG° very negative → reaction strongly favored.
For small K: ΔG° very positive → reaction strongly disfavored.

SPONTANEITY ANALYSIS (Temperature dependence):
ΔG = ΔH − TΔS:
ΔH<0, ΔS>0: ΔG always negative → always spontaneous.
ΔH<0, ΔS<0: ΔG negative at low T → spontaneous only at low T.
ΔH>0, ΔS>0: ΔG negative at high T → spontaneous only at high T.
ΔH>0, ΔS<0: ΔG always positive → never spontaneous.
Crossover temperature: T = ΔH/ΔS (where ΔG = 0, reaction at equilibrium).

HEAT CAPACITY:
Cp = (∂H/∂T)p, Cv = (∂U/∂T)v.
Cp − Cv = R (for ideal gas).
For atoms: Cv = (3/2)R. For linear molecules: Cv = (5/2)R.
Cp(water, liquid) = 75.3 J/mol·K = 4.184 J/g·K.

PART 2: CHEMICAL EQUILIBRIUM (COMPREHENSIVE)

LAW OF MASS ACTION:
For aA + bB ⇌ cC + dD:
Kc = [C]c[D]d / [A]a[B]b (concentration equilibrium constant, dimensionless if standard state).
Kp = (PC)c(PD)d / (PA)a(PB)b (pressure equilibrium constant, using partial pressures in bar).
Kp = Kc(RT)^Δn where Δn = (c+d) − (a+b) = change in moles of gas.

PROPERTIES OF K:
K >> 1: equilibrium favors products (reaction goes nearly to completion).
K << 1: equilibrium favors reactants (very little product).
K = 1: roughly equal amounts at equilibrium.
K is temperature-dependent (given by van't Hoff equation).
K is independent of concentration, pressure, volume, catalyst.
If reaction reversed: K' = 1/K.
If multiplied by n: K' = Kⁿ.
If reactions added: K_total = K₁ × K₂.

REACTION QUOTIENT Q:
Q = same expression as K, but with non-equilibrium concentrations.
Q < K: reaction proceeds forward to reach equilibrium.
Q > K: reaction proceeds backward.
Q = K: at equilibrium.

LE CHATELIER'S PRINCIPLE (DETAILED):
When a system at equilibrium is subjected to a stress, it shifts to partially relieve that stress.

Effect of adding reactant: shifts right (increases products). New equilibrium has less reactant than immediately after addition, but MORE than original equilibrium. K unchanged.
Effect of adding product: shifts left.
Effect of removing product: shifts right (used in industry to drive reactions to completion).

Effect of pressure/volume:
Increase pressure (decrease volume): shifts to side with fewer moles of gas.
Decrease pressure: shifts to side with more moles of gas.
Adding inert gas at constant volume: no effect on equilibrium.
Adding inert gas at constant pressure: increases volume → shifts to side with more gas moles.

Effect of temperature:
Exothermic reaction (ΔH < 0): increasing T decreases K (shifts left).
Endothermic reaction (ΔH > 0): increasing T increases K (shifts right).
Think of heat as a product (exothermic) or reactant (endothermic).

Van't Hoff equation: d(ln K)/dT = ΔH°/RT²
Integrated form: ln(K₂/K₁) = −(ΔH°/R)(1/T₂ − 1/T₁).

Effect of catalyst: NO effect on K or equilibrium position. Only speeds up reaching equilibrium.

ACID-BASE EQUILIBRIA:

Arrhenius acids/bases: H⁺ and OH⁻ in water.
Brønsted-Lowry: proton donor (acid) and proton acceptor (base).
Lewis: electron pair acceptor (acid) and donor (base).

Conjugate acid-base pairs: differ by one H⁺.
Strong acid + strong base → no equilibrium (goes to completion).
Weak acid/base: partial dissociation.

Ka × Kb = Kw (for conjugate acid-base pair at 25°C).
pKa + pKb = pKw = 14.
Strong acid: Ka → ∞, pKa << 0.
Weak acid: Ka small, pKa > 0.
Examples: HF (Ka = 6.8×10⁻⁴, pKa = 3.17), CH₃COOH (Ka = 1.8×10⁻⁵, pKa = 4.74).
NH₃ (Kb = 1.8×10⁻⁵, pKb = 4.74), NH₄⁺ (Ka = 5.6×10⁻¹⁰, pKa = 9.26).

pH calculations:
Strong acid (concentration C): pH = −log C (for C >> Kw^½).
Weak acid (concentration C): [H⁺] = √(Ka·C), pH = ½(pKa − log C).
Polyprotic acids: first dissociation dominates.
Salt of strong acid + strong base (NaCl): pH = 7.
Salt of weak acid + strong base (CH₃COONa): basic. pH = 7 + ½pKa + ½log C.
Salt of strong acid + weak base (NH₄Cl): acidic. pH = 7 − ½pKb − ½log C.

BUFFER SOLUTIONS:
Resist pH changes on addition of acid or base.
Henderson-Hasselbalch: pH = pKa + log([A⁻]/[HA]).
Buffer range: pKa ± 1 (approximately).
Buffer capacity: maximum when [A⁻] = [HA] (pH = pKa).
Adding acid: [HA] increases, [A⁻] decreases → small pH change.
Adding base: [A⁻] increases, [HA] decreases → small pH change.

Blood buffer system: H₂CO₃/HCO₃⁻ (pKa = 6.1) → blood pH ≈ 7.4.
Phosphate buffer: H₂PO₄⁻/HPO₄²⁻ (pKa = 7.2) → important in biology.

SOLUBILITY EQUILIBRIA:
Ksp = solubility product = ion product at saturation.
For AgCl ⇌ Ag⁺ + Cl⁻: Ksp = [Ag⁺][Cl⁻] = 1.8×10⁻¹⁰.
Solubility s: s = [Ag⁺] = [Cl⁻] = √Ksp = √(1.8×10⁻¹⁰) = 1.34×10⁻⁵ mol/L.
For Ag₂CrO₄ ⇌ 2Ag⁺ + CrO₄²⁻: Ksp = (2s)² × s = 4s³.

Common ion effect: adding common ion decreases solubility.
AgCl in 0.1 M NaCl: [Ag⁺] = Ksp/[Cl⁻] = 1.8×10⁻¹⁰/0.1 = 1.8×10⁻⁹ mol/L (much less than 1.34×10⁻⁵).

Precipitation: occurs when Q > Ksp.
Selective precipitation: used to separate ions with different Ksp values.

COMPLEX ION EQUILIBRIA:
Formation constant Kf = stability constant.
[Cu(NH₃)₄]²⁺: Kf = [Cu(NH₃)₄²⁺]/([Cu²⁺][NH₃]⁴) = 1.2×10¹² (very stable).
Complex formation increases solubility: AgCl dissolves in NH₃ solution (forms [Ag(NH₃)₂]⁺).
EDTA: hexadentate ligand, very high Kf with many metal ions. Used in chelation therapy.
