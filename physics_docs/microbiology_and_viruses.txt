1. WHAT IS MICROBIOLOGY?
-------------------------
Microbiology is the study of microorganisms — living things too small to see
with the naked eye. These include bacteria, archaea, fungi, protists, and viruses.
Microorganisms were first observed by Antonie van Leeuwenhoek in the 1670s using
early microscopes.

Major Groups of Microorganisms:
- Bacteria    : Prokaryotes; diverse metabolism; found in every habitat on Earth.
- Archaea     : Prokaryotes; often extremophiles (hot springs, salt lakes, deep sea).
- Fungi       : Eukaryotes; include yeasts, moulds, mushrooms.
- Protists    : Eukaryotes; diverse group; includes algae, amoeba, paramecia.
- Viruses     : Non-cellular; require host cells to replicate; debated as "life."
- Prions      : Misfolded proteins; cause diseases like CJD and BSE.

1.1 BACTERIA — BASIC FEATURES
-------------------------------
Structure:
- Cell wall    : Provides shape and protection. Composition varies.
- Cell membrane: Controls transport.
- Nucleoid     : Region containing circular DNA chromosome.
- Ribosomes    : 70S (smaller than eukaryotic 80S — target for antibiotics).
- Plasmids     : Small circular DNA; carry extra genes (e.g., antibiotic resistance).
- Flagella     : For motility.
- Pili/Fimbriae: Attachment to surfaces; conjugation.
- Capsule      : Polysaccharide layer; protects from phagocytosis.

Gram Staining (developed by Hans Christian Gram, 1884):
  Gram-positive (+): Thick peptidoglycan wall; stains purple.
                     Example: Staphylococcus, Streptococcus, Bacillus.
  Gram-negative (-): Thin peptidoglycan + outer membrane; stains pink/red.
                     Example: E. coli, Salmonella, Pseudomonas, Neisseria.

Bacterial Shapes:
- Coccus       : Spherical (Staphylococcus = clusters; Streptococcus = chains)
- Bacillus     : Rod-shaped (E. coli, Salmonella)
- Spirillum    : Spiral/helical (Helicobacter pylori)
- Vibrio       : Comma-shaped (Vibrio cholerae)

1.2 VIRUSES — BASIC FEATURES
------------------------------
Viruses are non-living infectious agents consisting of:
- Genetic material : DNA or RNA (never both); single- or double-stranded.
- Capsid          : Protein coat surrounding the genetic material.
- Envelope        : Lipid membrane (derived from host cell) — present in some viruses.

Viral Classification by Genome:
  dsDNA  : Herpesviruses, Poxviruses, Adenoviruses
  ssDNA  : Parvoviruses, Circoviruses
  dsRNA  : Reoviruses (Rotavirus)
  +ssRNA : Coronaviruses, Picornaviruses (Poliovirus), Flaviviruses
  -ssRNA : Influenza, Rabies, Ebola, Measles
  ssRNA-RT: Retroviruses (HIV) — reverse transcriptase converts RNA → DNA

2. BACTERIAL REPRODUCTION & GENETICS
--------------------------------------
Binary Fission: Asexual reproduction. Chromosome replicates → cell elongates →
divides into two identical daughter cells. Can occur every 20 minutes (E. coli).

Generation time: Time for population to double. Under optimal conditions,
1 bacterium can become >1 billion in ~10 hours.

Bacterial Gene Transfer (horizontal gene transfer):
- Transformation  : Uptake of free DNA from environment. Basis of natural
                    competence and lab cloning.
- Transduction    : DNA transferred between bacteria via bacteriophage (virus).
  Generalised     : Random bacterial DNA packaged in phage.
  Specialised     : Specific genes near phage integration site transferred.
- Conjugation     : Direct cell-to-cell contact via sex pilus; plasmid transferred.
                    Key mechanism for antibiotic resistance spread.
- Transposons     : "Jumping genes" — mobile genetic elements that move within/
                    between genomes; increase genetic diversity.

3. VIRAL REPLICATION CYCLES
-----------------------------
Lytic Cycle (productive infection):
  Attachment → Penetration → Biosynthesis → Maturation → Release (lysis)
  Host cell is destroyed. Viruses released infect new cells.

Lysogenic Cycle (latent infection):
  Viral DNA integrates into host chromosome as a PROPHAGE.
  Replicates with host; does not immediately lyse cell.
  Stress can trigger switch to lytic cycle (induction).
  Example: Lambda phage; Herpes simplex virus latency.

HIV Replication (Retrovirus):
  1. HIV gp120 binds CD4 receptor + CCR5/CXCR4 co-receptor on T helper cells.
  2. Fusion; HIV RNA genome enters cell.
  3. Reverse transcriptase converts RNA → dsDNA.
  4. Integrase inserts viral DNA into host chromosome (provirus).
  5. Transcription of viral genes; Gag/Pol/Env proteins produced.
  6. Protease cleaves polyproteins → mature virions.
  7. Budding from cell membrane.
  Antiretroviral targets: reverse transcriptase (NRTIs, NNRTIs), integrase
  (INSTIs), protease (PIs), entry (CCR5 antagonists, fusion inhibitors).

4. MICROBIAL ECOLOGY & THE HUMAN MICROBIOME
---------------------------------------------
The human body hosts ~38 trillion microbial cells (comparable to human cells).

Key Microbiome Sites:
- Gut microbiome   : ~1,000 species; dominated by Firmicutes & Bacteroidetes.
                     Functions: digestion, vitamin synthesis (K, B12), immune
                     training, gut-brain axis signalling.
- Skin microbiome  : Staphylococcus epidermidis predominates; protective role.
- Oral microbiome  : >700 species; dysbiosis causes dental caries and periodontitis.
- Vaginal microbiome: Lactobacillus dominant; low pH protects against pathogens.

Dysbiosis: Imbalance of normal microbiome → linked to IBD, obesity, type 2
diabetes, depression, and C. difficile infection.

5. ANTIMICROBIAL RESISTANCE (AMR)
-----------------------------------
AMR is one of the greatest global health threats of the 21st century.
WHO estimates 10 million deaths/year by 2050 if unchecked.

Mechanisms of Antibiotic Resistance:
- Enzymatic inactivation : Beta-lactamases hydrolyse the beta-lactam ring of
                           penicillins/cephalosporins. ESBLs (extended-spectrum
                           beta-lactamases) confer resistance to most beta-lactams.
- Target modification    : MRSA: altered penicillin-binding protein (PBP2a,
                           encoded by mecA) has low affinity for methicillin.
                           VRE: altered D-Ala-D-Ala → D-Ala-D-Lac (vancomycin target).
- Efflux pumps           : Active export of antibiotic from cell (RND family in
                           gram-negatives; broad substrate specificity).
- Reduced permeability   : Loss of OmpF/OmpC porins in gram-negatives prevents
                           antibiotic entry.
- Target bypass          : Alternative metabolic pathway circumvents drug target.

Antibiotic Classes & Targets:
Class                 | Example           | Target
----------------------|-------------------|---------------------------------
Beta-lactams          | Penicillin, Amox  | PBP → cell wall synthesis
Glycopeptides         | Vancomycin        | D-Ala-D-Ala → cell wall
Aminoglycosides       | Gentamicin        | 30S ribosome → mistranslation
Macrolides            | Erythromycin      | 50S ribosome → translocation
Fluoroquinolones      | Ciprofloxacin     | DNA gyrase/topoisomerase IV
Tetracyclines         | Doxycycline       | 30S ribosome → tRNA binding
Rifampicin            | Rifampin          | RNA polymerase beta subunit
Polymyxins            | Colistin          | LPS → cell membrane disruption

6. VIROLOGY — ADVANCED MECHANISMS
------------------------------------
Antigenic Variation:
- Antigenic Drift  : Gradual accumulation of point mutations in surface antigens
                     (haemagglutinin/neuraminidase in influenza). Requires annual
                     vaccine updates.
- Antigenic Shift  : Sudden reassortment of genome segments between two different
                     influenza strains (can only occur in segmented-genome viruses).
                     Produces novel subtypes → pandemic potential (e.g., H1N1 2009).

Viral Pathogenesis Mechanisms:
- Cytopathic effects (CPE): Direct cell killing (lysis, syncytia formation).
- Immune evasion: Downregulation of MHC I (herpesviruses), blocking interferon
  signalling (influenza NS1 protein), latency establishment.
- Oncogenesis: Some viruses integrate and activate proto-oncogenes or inactivate
  tumour suppressors. HPV E6 (degrades p53) and E7 (inactivates Rb) → cervical cancer.
  Hepatitis B/C → hepatocellular carcinoma; EBV → Burkitt lymphoma.

Interferons (IFN) — Innate Antiviral Defence:
  Type I IFN (IFN-alpha/beta): Secreted by infected cells; binds IFNAR on
  neighbouring cells → JAK-STAT signalling → ISGs (interferon-stimulated genes)
  → antiviral state (PKR activation → translational shutdown; RNase L → RNA degradation;
  Mx proteins → inhibit viral RNA synthesis).

[CLINICAL LINK] SARS-CoV-2 exploits ACE2 receptor via spike protein (S1 RBD).
Variants (Alpha, Delta, Omicron) accumulate mutations in spike that increase ACE2
affinity and immune evasion. mRNA vaccines (Pfizer-BioNTech, Moderna) encode the
spike protein, inducing neutralising antibodies and T cell responses.
