1. CHROMOSOMES
---------------
Chromosomes are organised structures of DNA and protein (chromatin) that carry
an organism's genetic information. Humans have 46 chromosomes (23 pairs) in
somatic cells; 23 chromosomes (haploid) in gametes.

Chromosome Numbers:
  Diploid (2n): Two copies of each chromosome (one paternal, one maternal).
  Haploid (n): One copy of each chromosome (gametes).
  Human: 2n = 46 (22 pairs autosomes + 1 pair sex chromosomes: XX female, XY male).
  Interesting extremes: Myrmecia pilosula ant: 2n=2 (fewest). Ophioglossum fern: 2n=1,260 (most).

Chromosome Structure:
  Centromere: Constriction point; spindle fibre attachment (kinetochore); essential for segregation.
    Metacentric: Centromere at middle. Submetacentric: Off-centre. Acrocentric: Near end.
    Telocentric: At end. Acrocentric in humans: chromosomes 13, 14, 15, 21, 22 → NOR regions.
  Telomeres: (TTAGGG)n repeats at chromosome ends; protect from degradation and fusion.
  Short arm = p (petit). Long arm = q.

Chromatin Levels of Compaction:
  Level 1: Naked DNA (2 nm).
  Level 2: Nucleosome (DNA wrapped 1.65x around histone octamer; 11 nm "beads on a string").
    Histone octamer: 2x(H2A, H2B, H3, H4). H1 linker histone seals DNA entry/exit.
  Level 3: 30 nm fibre (solenoid or zigzag arrangement of nucleosomes) — existence debated in vivo.
  Level 4: Loop domains (~300 nm) anchored to nuclear matrix/scaffold.
  Level 5: Rosette structures (~700 nm).
  Level 6: Fully condensed metaphase chromosome (1400 nm).

1.1 KARYOTYPE & CHROMOSOME ABNORMALITIES
------------------------------------------
Karyotype: Complete set of an organism's chromosomes visualised at metaphase.
  G-banding: Giemsa stain → light (AT-rich) and dark (GC-rich) bands; each chromosome has
  unique pattern → identify by band number (e.g., 17q21 = chromosome 17, long arm, band 21).

Numerical abnormalities (aneuploidy):
  Trisomy 21 (Down syndrome): Extra chr 21; intellectual disability; distinct facial features;
    CHD (40%); AML risk; Alzheimer's by 40s (extra APP gene copy).
  Trisomy 18 (Edwards): Severe; usually fatal in first year.
  Trisomy 13 (Patau): Severe; usually fatal in first year.
  Monosomy X (Turner: 45,X): Short stature; gonadal dysgenesis; coarctation of aorta; webbed neck.
  XXY (Klinefelter): Tall; hypogonadism; infertility; gynaecomastia; learning difficulties.
  XYY: Tall; usually normal phenotype; slight increased risk behavioural issues.
  XXX (Triple X): Usually asymptomatic.

Structural abnormalities:
  Deletion: Loss of chromosomal segment (5p- = cri-du-chat; 22q11.2 = DiGeorge).
  Duplication: Extra copy of segment.
  Inversion: Segment reversed within chromosome (paracentric or pericentric).
  Translocation: Segment moved to different chromosome.
    Robertsonian: Fusion of two acrocentric chromosomes (13;14, 14;21). Carrier has 45 chroms.
    Philadelphia chromosome t(9;22): BCR-ABL1 → CML.
    t(15;17): PML-RARalpha → APL.
    t(8;14): MYC-IgH → Burkitt lymphoma.

2. CHROMATIN STRUCTURE & REGULATION
--------------------------------------
Euchromatin vs Heterochromatin:
  Euchromatin: Loosely packed; transcriptionally active; early-replicating;
    enriched H3K4me3 (active promoters), H3K27ac (active enhancers), H3K36me3 (gene bodies).
  Constitutive heterochromatin: Permanently condensed; gene-poor; repetitive DNA
    (satellite sequences, centromeres, pericentromeric regions); H3K9me3 + HP1 (heterochromatin protein 1).
  Facultative heterochromatin: Silenced in specific cell types; H3K27me3 + Polycomb complexes.
    X-chromosome inactivation: One X silenced → Barr body → facultative heterochromatin.

Histone Code Hypothesis:
  Combinations of histone modifications create a "code" read by effector proteins.
  Writers: HATs (acetylation), HMTs (methylation), kinases (phosphorylation), PARPs.
  Erasers: HDACs, KDMs (demethylases), phosphatases.
  Readers: Bromodomains (read Kac), chromodomains (read Kme), PHD fingers, PWWP domains.

Key Modifications:
  H3K4me3 : Active promoters. Written by SETD1A/B, MLL complex. Read by TAF3 (TFIID), BPTF (NURF).
  H3K27me3: Polycomb-silenced genes. Written by PRC2 (EZH2). Read by PRC1 (Cbx proteins).
  H3K9me3 : Constitutive heterochromatin. Written by SETDB1/SUV39H. Read by HP1 (chromodomain).
  H3K27ac : Active enhancers. Written by CBP/p300. Distinguishes active vs poised enhancers.
  H3K36me3: Active gene bodies. Written by SETD2 (coupled to elongating RNAPII CTD).
  H4K20me3: Constitutive heterochromatin. Pericentromeric/telomeric regions.
  H3S10ph : Mitosis marker (written by Aurora B). Also activated gene bodies (MSK1).
  H2AK119ub: Polycomb-silenced (written by PRC1; RNF2/BMI1 subunits).
  H2B K120ub: Active transcription; required for H3K4 and H3K79 methylation (trans-histone crosstalk).

3. NUCLEAR ORGANISATION
-------------------------
The nucleus is not a homogeneous compartment — it has distinct functional zones.

Chromatin Territories:
  Each chromosome occupies a distinct territory in the interphase nucleus.
  Gene-rich chromosomes tend toward nuclear centre; gene-poor toward periphery.
  Active genes loop out from territories toward transcription factories.

Nuclear Lamina & Lamins:
  A-type lamins (Lamin A/C): Inner nuclear membrane; structural support; chromatin attachment.
    Mutations → Laminopathies: Emery-Dreifuss muscular dystrophy, dilated cardiomyopathy,
    Hutchinson-Gilford Progeria Syndrome (HGPS): LMNA G608G mutation → progerin protein →
    nuclear shape abnormalities → accelerated ageing → death ~14 years from CVD.
  B-type lamins (Lamin B1/B2): Required for cell viability; nuclear envelope reformation.
  LADs (Lamina-Associated Domains): Chromatin tethered to lamina; generally repressed.

Nucleolus:
  Site of rRNA transcription (RNA Pol I) and ribosome assembly.
  Located at NOR (Nucleolus Organiser Regions) — rDNA repeats on acrocentric chromosomes 13, 14, 15, 21, 22.
  Three regions: Fibrillar centres (FCs), dense fibrillar component (DFC — rRNA processing), granular component (GC — ribosome assembly).
  Ribosome biogenesis: rDNA → 47S pre-rRNA → processed to 18S + 5.8S + 28S rRNA +
  assembled with ~80 ribosomal proteins → 40S (small) + 60S (large) subunits → exported.

Nuclear Pore Complexes (NPCs):
  ~120 MDa protein complexes; ~1,000 per nucleus; 8-fold symmetry.
  30 different nucleoporins (Nups) arranged in rings and spokes.
  Selective transport: Small molecules (<40 kDa) diffuse freely; larger molecules require
  nuclear localisation signal (NLS) → importins → Ran-GTP cycle.
  Export: Nuclear export signal (NES) → exportins (CRM1/XPO1) → Ran-GTP gradient drives directionality.
  CRM1 inhibitor (selinexor): Approved for r/r myeloma (STORM trial) → blocks nuclear export
  of tumour suppressors → keeps p53, Rb, etc. nuclear.

4. 3D GENOME ORGANISATION
---------------------------
Hi-C Technology: Maps genome-wide chromatin contacts by proximity ligation + sequencing.
Revealed hierarchical organisation:

Compartments (A/B):
  A compartments: Active, gene-rich, early-replicating, accessible chromatin.
  B compartments: Inactive, gene-poor, late-replicating, condensed chromatin.
  Compartments are largely cell-type invariant at large scale but switch locally during differentiation.

TADs (Topologically Associating Domains):
  ~0.1–3 Mb domains of self-interacting chromatin (~2,000 in human genome).
  Boundaries enriched in CTCF binding sites + cohesin; convergent CTCF motifs.
  Within TADs: Enhancer-promoter interactions occur preferentially.
  TAD disruption (boundary deletion or structural variant) → ectopic enhancer-gene contacts → disease.
  CTCF + cohesin: Loop extrusion model → cohesin extrudes chromatin loop until stalled by CTCF.

5. PHASE SEPARATION & CONDENSATES
------------------------------------
Biomolecular Condensates:
  Membraneless organelles formed by liquid-liquid phase separation (LLPS).
  Driven by weak multivalent interactions (IDRs — intrinsically disordered regions,
  pi-pi interactions, cation-pi interactions, hydrogen bonds).
  Properties: Liquid-like; dynamic; rapidly exchange components; concentrate specific molecules.

Examples of Condensates:
  Nucleolus: Largest nuclear condensate; phase-separated from nucleoplasm.
  Transcriptional condensates: Active enhancers + Mediator + RNAPII CTD → concentrate
    TFs and coactivators at super-enhancers → burst transcription.
    BRD4 and Mediator MED1 form condensates at SEs → disrupted by JQ1 (BET bromodomain inhibitor).
  Stress granules: mRNA + RBPs (G3BP1, TIA1) → form under stress → sequester mRNAs from translation.
  Processing bodies (P-bodies): mRNA degradation condensates.
  Heterochromatin: HP1alpha undergoes LLPS → compacts heterochromatin.

Phase Separation in Disease:
  FUS, TDP-43, hnRNPA2 mutations → ALS/FTD: Aggregation-prone; pathological solid condensates.
  GGGGCC repeat expansion in C9orf72: RNA forms G-quadruplexes → sequesters RBPs.
  Cancer: Oncogenic fusion proteins (EWS-FLI1 in Ewing sarcoma) form aberrant condensates
    that dysregulate transcription.

6. TELOMERE BIOLOGY
--------------------
Telomere Structure:
  (TTAGGG)n repeats (varies from ~10 kb in newborns to ~5 kb in elderly).
  Shelterin complex: TRF1, TRF2, POT1, TPP1, TIN2, RAP1 → protect from DNA damage recognition,
  end-joining, and ATM/ATR signalling. T-loop: Telomere folds back → 3' overhang invades duplex DNA.
  TRF2: Maintains t-loop; prevents ATM activation. POT1: Binds single-stranded overhang.

Telomerase:
  Reverse transcriptase (TERT + TERC) → extends telomeres.
  Active in: Stem cells, germline, immune cells, most cancers (~85%).
  Absent in: Most somatic cells → progressive telomere shortening → replicative senescence.

ALT (Alternative Lengthening of Telomeres):
  ~15% of cancers. HDR-based telomere extension. APBs (ALT-associated PML bodies).
  Key features: Heterogeneous telomere lengths; C-circles; increased telomere recombination.
  ATRX/DAXX mutations → loss of histone H3.3 chaperone at telomeres → ALT activation.
  Common in: Glioblastoma, osteosarcoma, liposarcoma.

Telomere Diseases:
  Dyskeratosis congenita: Telomerase component mutations (DKC1, TERT, TERC, NOP10) →
    bone marrow failure, oral leukoplakia, nail dystrophy, pulmonary fibrosis.
  Idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis: Telomere attrition in alveolar epithelial cells.
  Aplastic anaemia: Haematopoietic stem cell exhaustion due to telomere shortening.

[CLINICAL LINK] Chromosome instability (CIN):
  ~85% of solid tumours have CIN (whole-chromosome gains/losses per cell division).
  Causes: Aurora B, MPS1, BUB1 mutations (SAC defects); centrosome amplification;
  cohesion defects; replication stress. CIN drives intratumour heterogeneity and drug resistance.
  PARP inhibitors: Synthetic lethality in BRCA1/2-mutant cancers (DNA repair defect +
  PARP inhibition → replication fork collapse → chromosome breakage → cell death).
