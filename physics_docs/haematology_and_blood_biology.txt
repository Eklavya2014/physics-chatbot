1. BLOOD COMPOSITION & FUNCTION
---------------------------------
Blood is a specialised connective tissue. Volume: ~5 litres in adult.
Composition: ~55% plasma + ~45% formed elements (RBCs, WBCs, platelets).

Plasma (55%): Water (~90%) + proteins (albumin, globulins, fibrinogen, clotting factors)
+ nutrients + hormones + waste products + electrolytes.
  Serum = plasma without clotting factors (after blood clots).
  Albumin: Most abundant plasma protein. Oncotic pressure; drug transport; pH buffer.

Blood Functions:
  Transport: O2 (Hb), CO2, nutrients, hormones, waste, heat.
  Regulation: pH (bicarbonate buffer), temperature, osmotic pressure, fluid balance.
  Protection: WBCs (immunity), platelets/clotting factors (haemostasis).

1.1 RED BLOOD CELLS (ERYTHROCYTES)
------------------------------------
  Count: 4.5–5.5 million/µL (males); 4.0–5.0 million/µL (females).
  Lifespan: ~120 days. Removed by spleen/liver macrophages.
  Structure: Biconcave disc; ~8µm diameter; no nucleus, no mitochondria.
    → Biconcave shape: Maximum surface area for gas exchange; flexible (squeezes through capillaries).
    → No mitochondria: Use anaerobic glycolysis only (prevent O2 consumption).

Haemoglobin (Hb):
  Structure: 4 subunits (2 alpha + 2 beta in HbA); each with haem group (Fe2+).
  O2 binding: Cooperative (sigmoidal curve) — O2 binding increases affinity for next O2.
  Bohr effect: ↑ CO2, ↑ H+, ↑ 2,3-BPG, ↑ temperature → right shift → Hb releases O2.
  CO2 transport: 70% as bicarbonate; 23% as carbaminohaemoglobin; 7% dissolved.
  Normal Hb: Males 130–175 g/L; females 115–160 g/L.
  HbA2: 2 alpha + 2 delta; ~2–3.5%. HbF: 2 alpha + 2 gamma; fetal; higher O2 affinity.

Blood Groups:
  ABO system: Based on surface antigens on RBCs.
    Type A: A antigen + anti-B antibodies.
    Type B: B antigen + anti-A antibodies.
    Type AB: A+B antigens + no antibodies (universal recipient).
    Type O: No antigens + anti-A and anti-B (universal donor).
  Rh system: D antigen. Rh+ (D antigen present) or Rh- (absent).
    Haemolytic disease of newborn (HDN): Rh- mother + Rh+ fetus → anti-D antibodies.
    Prevent: Anti-D immunoglobulin (RhoGAM) given to Rh- mothers.

2. HAEMOSTASIS & COAGULATION
------------------------------
Haemostasis: Stopping bleeding. Three overlapping stages:

Primary Haemostasis (platelet plug):
  Vessel injury → collagen exposed → vWF (von Willebrand factor) bridges collagen
  to platelet GPIb receptor → platelet adhesion → platelet activation (shape change;
  granule release: ADP, TXA2, serotonin) → GPIIb/IIIa activation → fibrinogen bridges
  platelets → platelet aggregation → platelet plug (soft, temporary).

Secondary Haemostasis (coagulation cascade):
  Intrinsic pathway: Contact activation (HMWK, prekallikrein, XII → XI → IX → X).
  Extrinsic pathway: Tissue factor (TF) exposed at injury → TF + VIIa → Xa.
  Common pathway: Xa + Va → prothrombinase → Prothrombin → Thrombin (IIa) →
    Fibrinogen → Fibrin + XIII → cross-linked fibrin clot (stable).
  Thrombin: Key amplifying enzyme; activates V, VIII, XI, XIII, platelets.
  In vivo: Cell-based model. Initiation: TF + VIIa → small thrombin. Amplification:
    Thrombin activates platelets + V + VIII. Propagation: IXa + VIIIa → Xase complex
    → massive thrombin generation.

Anticoagulants:
  Natural: Antithrombin (inhibits thrombin + Xa); Protein C/S system (inactivates Va + VIIIa);
    TFPI (tissue factor pathway inhibitor).
  Drug:
    Heparin (UFH/LMWH): Enhances antithrombin; inhibits Xa (LMWH) + thrombin.
    Warfarin: Inhibits Vit K-dependent factors (II, VII, IX, X, Protein C/S). Monitor INR.
    DOACs: Direct oral anticoagulants. Apixaban/rivaroxaban (Xa inhibitors); dabigatran (IIa).
    No monitoring needed; reversible (andexanet alfa reverses Xa; idarucizumab reverses dabigatran).

Fibrinolysis: Clot dissolution.
  Plasminogen → Plasmin (by tPA, uPA) → cleaves fibrin → clot dissolved.
  Inhibitors: PAI-1 (inhibits tPA); alpha-2-antiplasmin.
  tPA (alteplase): Therapeutic fibrinolytic for stroke/MI/PE (within time window).

3. ANAEMIAS
------------
Anaemia: Hb below normal → reduced O2 carrying capacity.
WHO thresholds: Hb <120 g/L women, <130 g/L men.

Classification by MCV (Mean Corpuscular Volume):
  Microcytic (MCV <80 fL): Small RBCs.
    Iron deficiency anaemia (IDA): Most common worldwide; low ferritin; high TIBC.
    Thalassaemia: Reduced globin chain synthesis (alpha or beta).
    Anaemia of chronic disease: Hepcidin ↑ → iron sequestration.
    Sideroblastic anaemia: Ring sideroblasts in marrow; iron in mitochondria.

  Normocytic (MCV 80–100 fL):
    Haemolytic anaemia: Premature RBC destruction.
    Aplastic anaemia: Bone marrow failure.
    Anaemia of chronic disease (early).
    Acute blood loss.

  Macrocytic (MCV >100 fL): Large RBCs.
    Megaloblastic: B12 or folate deficiency → impaired DNA synthesis → large immature cells.
    Non-megaloblastic: Liver disease; hypothyroidism; alcohol.

4. HAEMOGLOBINOPATHIES
------------------------
Sickle Cell Disease (HbSS):
  Cause: Beta-globin Glu6Val point mutation → HbS.
  Mechanism: Deoxygenated HbS polymerises → rigid fibres → RBC sickling → vaso-occlusion
    + haemolysis.
  Crises: Vaso-occlusive (pain, acute chest, stroke); aplastic (parvovirus B19);
    splenic sequestration; haemolytic.
  Malaria protection: Heterozygotes (HbAS) have survival advantage in malaria-endemic regions
    → explains high frequency of sickle allele in Sub-Saharan Africa (balanced polymorphism).
  Treatment: Hydroxyurea (↑ HbF); voxelotor (Hb polymerisation inhibitor); L-glutamine;
    crizanlizumab (anti-P-selectin); curative: HSCT or gene therapy (Casgevy).

Thalassaemias:
  Alpha-thalassaemia: Deletion of alpha-globin genes on chromosome 16.
    1 gene deleted: Silent. 2: Trait (mild). 3: HbH disease (moderate haemolysis).
    4: Hydrops fetalis (lethal in utero — HbBart).
  Beta-thalassaemia: Mutations in beta-globin gene on chromosome 11.
    Beta-thalassaemia minor (trait): Mild microcytic anaemia; often asymptomatic.
    Beta-thalassaemia major (Cooley's anaemia): Severe; transfusion-dependent.
    Treatment: Regular transfusions + desferrioxamine (iron chelation); HSCT curative;
    Luspatercept (erythroid maturation); betibeglogene (gene therapy — approved 2022).

5. HAEMATOLOGICAL MALIGNANCIES
--------------------------------
Leukaemias:
  AML (Acute Myeloid Leukaemia): Clonal expansion of myeloid blasts (>20% in marrow).
    Driver mutations: FLT3-ITD (~30%), NPM1 (~30%), IDH1/2 (~20%), DNMT3A, CEBPA, TP53.
    Classified by WHO 2022 classification. Specific subtypes: APL (PML-RARalpha t(15;17)
    → treated with ATRA + arsenic trioxide — cure rate >90%).
    Standard treatment: 7+3 (cytarabine 7 days + daunorubicin 3 days) → CR → consolidation
    → allogeneic HSCT for high-risk. Targeted: FLT3 inhibitors (midostaurin, quizartinib);
    IDH inhibitors (enasidenib, ivosidenib); BCL-2 inhibitor (venetoclax + azacitidine).

  ALL (Acute Lymphoblastic Leukaemia): Most common childhood cancer.
    B-ALL (85%): BCR-ABL1+ (Philadelphia chromosome → tyrosine kinase inhibitor);
    ETV6-RUNX1; hyperdiploidy; hypodiploidy; Ph-like ALL.
    T-ALL: NOTCH1 mutations (~60%); HOX gene overexpression.
    Treatment: Multiagent chemotherapy (ALL-BFM protocol); CNS prophylaxis;
    blinatumomab (BiTE); inotuzumab ozogamicin; CAR-T (tisagenlecleucel → up to 80% remission in r/r B-ALL).

Myeloma (Multiple Myeloma):
  Clonal plasma cell malignancy → monoclonal protein (M-protein) → organ damage (CRAB).
  CRAB criteria: HyperCalcaemia, Renal failure, Anaemia, Bone lesions (lytic).
  Pathogenesis: Translocation (11;14), del(17p), del(13), t(4;14) → genetic heterogeneity.
  Treatment: Bortezomib (proteasome inhibitor) + lenalidomide (IMiD) + dexamethasone (VRd).
    Daratumumab (anti-CD38 mAb): Added to triplet → quadruplet therapy → improved PFS.
    BCMA-targeting: Teclistamab (bispecific); ciltacabtagene autoleucel (BCMA CAR-T).
    Still incurable but median survival improving significantly (>5 years for standard risk).

6. HAEMATOPOIETIC STEM CELL TRANSPLANTATION (HSCT)
-----------------------------------------------------
Autologous HSCT: Patient's own stem cells harvested → high-dose chemotherapy →
  cells reinfused. No GvH disease. Used for: Myeloma, lymphoma, POEMS.

Allogeneic HSCT: Donor stem cells (matched sibling, unrelated donor, haplo-identical).
  Conditioning: Myeloablative or reduced-intensity (for older/frailer patients).
  GvHD (Graft-versus-Host Disease):
    Acute GvHD (<100 days): Donor T cells attack host tissue (skin, liver, gut).
    Chronic GvHD: Autoimmune-like; resembles scleroderma, sicca syndrome.
    GvL (Graft-vs-Leukaemia effect): Therapeutic — donor T cells kill residual leukaemia.
    DLI (Donor Lymphocyte Infusion): Boost GvL effect at relapse.
  HLA matching: HLA-A, B, C, DRB1, DQB1 — 10/10 match ideal. Haploidentical (5/10)
  with post-cyclophosphamide now widely used.

[EMERGING] CAR-T in Haematology:
  CD19 CAR-T: Axicabtagene ciloleucel, tisagenlecleucel → DLBCL, ALL, FL.
  BCMA CAR-T: Idecabtagene vicleucel, ciltacabtagene → myeloma.
  CD22, CD20, CD38 — next generation targets. Allogeneic (off-the-shelf) CAR-T in trials.
  Bispecific CAR-T targeting dual antigens → reduce antigen escape.
  Solid tumour CAR-T: Still challenging (immunosuppressive TME; trafficking; antigen heterogeneity).
