CHAPTER 1: LIGHT - REFLECTION AND REFRACTION
=============================================

INTRODUCTION TO LIGHT
Light is a form of energy that enables us to see objects. Light travels in a straight line and has a speed of approximately 3 × 10^8 m/s in vacuum.

REFLECTION OF LIGHT
Reflection is the bouncing back of light when it hits a surface.

Laws of Reflection:
1. The angle of incidence is equal to the angle of reflection. (∠i = ∠r)
2. The incident ray, the normal at the point of incidence, and the reflected ray all lie in the same plane.

TYPES OF REFLECTION
1. Regular Reflection: When light reflects off a smooth, polished surface (like a mirror), it reflects in one direction. This is called specular or regular reflection.
2. Diffuse Reflection: When light reflects off a rough surface, it scatters in many directions. This allows us to see non-luminous objects.

SPHERICAL MIRRORS
A spherical mirror is a mirror whose reflecting surface is a part of a hollow sphere.

Types of Spherical Mirrors:
1. Concave Mirror: The reflecting surface is curved inward (like a cave). Also called converging mirror.
2. Convex Mirror: The reflecting surface is curved outward. Also called diverging mirror.

Important Terms for Spherical Mirrors:
- Pole (P): The midpoint of the reflecting surface of a spherical mirror.
- Centre of Curvature (C): The centre of the sphere of which the mirror is a part.
- Radius of Curvature (R): The radius of the sphere of which the mirror is a part.
- Principal Axis: A straight line passing through the pole and the centre of curvature.
- Principal Focus (F): The point on the principal axis where rays parallel to the axis converge (concave) or appear to diverge from (convex) after reflection.
- Focal Length (f): The distance between the pole and the principal focus.

Relationship: R = 2f (Radius of curvature = 2 × Focal Length)

IMAGE FORMATION BY CONCAVE MIRROR
1. Object at infinity → Image at F, real, inverted, point-sized
2. Object beyond C → Image between F and C, real, inverted, diminished
3. Object at C → Image at C, real, inverted, same size
4. Object between C and F → Image beyond C, real, inverted, enlarged
5. Object at F → Image at infinity
6. Object between F and P → Image behind mirror, virtual, erect, enlarged

Uses of Concave Mirror:
- Shaving mirrors and makeup mirrors
- Dentist's mirror
- Reflectors in torches, headlights, searchlights
- Used in solar furnaces

IMAGE FORMATION BY CONVEX MIRROR
- A convex mirror always forms a virtual, erect, and diminished image regardless of the object position.
- Image is always formed between P and F behind the mirror.

Uses of Convex Mirror:
- Rear-view mirrors in vehicles (provides wider field of view)
- Security mirrors in shops and banks

MIRROR FORMULA
1/v + 1/u = 1/f

Where:
- v = image distance (measured from pole)
- u = object distance (measured from pole)
- f = focal length

Sign Convention (New Cartesian Sign Convention):
- All distances are measured from the pole.
- Distances in the direction of incident light are positive.
- Distances opposite to incident light are negative.
- Heights above principal axis are positive.
- Heights below principal axis are negative.

For concave mirror: f is negative
For convex mirror: f is positive

MAGNIFICATION
m = -v/u = h'/h

Where h' = image height, h = object height
- Positive magnification → erect image
- Negative magnification → inverted image
- |m| > 1 → enlarged image
- |m| < 1 → diminished image

REFRACTION OF LIGHT
Refraction is the bending of light as it passes from one medium to another due to the change in its speed.

Laws of Refraction (Snell's Laws):
1. The incident ray, the refracted ray, and the normal at the point of incidence all lie in the same plane.
2. The ratio of the sine of the angle of incidence to the sine of the angle of refraction is constant for a given pair of media.
   sin i / sin r = constant = n₂₁ (Snell's Law)

REFRACTIVE INDEX
The refractive index of medium 2 with respect to medium 1:
n₂₁ = sin i / sin r = speed of light in medium 1 / speed of light in medium 2
n₂₁ = v₁ / v₂

Absolute Refractive Index: Refractive index with respect to vacuum/air.
n = c/v (where c = speed of light in vacuum, v = speed of light in medium)

Optically denser medium: higher refractive index, light slows down, bends toward normal.
Optically rarer medium: lower refractive index, light speeds up, bends away from normal.

Refractive indices of common materials:
- Air: 1.0003 (≈1)
- Water: 1.33
- Glass: 1.5
- Diamond: 2.42

REFRACTION THROUGH A GLASS SLAB
- When light passes through a glass slab, the emergent ray is parallel to the incident ray but laterally displaced.
- Lateral displacement depends on thickness of slab, angle of incidence, and refractive index.

LENSES
A lens is a transparent medium bounded by two curved surfaces.

Types of Lenses:
1. Convex Lens (Converging Lens): Thicker at the centre than at the edges. Converges light rays.
2. Concave Lens (Diverging Lens): Thinner at the centre than at the edges. Diverges light rays.

Important Terms for Lenses:
- Optical Centre (O): The central point of a lens.
- Principal Axis: The straight line passing through both centres of curvature.
- Principal Focus (F): The point where parallel rays converge (convex) or appear to diverge from (concave) after refraction.
- Focal Length (f): Distance between optical centre and principal focus.

IMAGE FORMATION BY CONVEX LENS
1. Object at infinity → Image at F₂, real, inverted, point-sized
2. Object beyond 2F₁ → Image between F₂ and 2F₂, real, inverted, diminished
3. Object at 2F₁ → Image at 2F₂, real, inverted, same size
4. Object between F₁ and 2F₁ → Image beyond 2F₂, real, inverted, enlarged
5. Object at F₁ → Image at infinity
6. Object between F₁ and O → Image on same side, virtual, erect, enlarged

Uses of Convex Lens:
- Magnifying glass
- Spectacles for far-sighted people
- Camera lens, telescope, microscope

IMAGE FORMATION BY CONCAVE LENS
- Always forms virtual, erect, diminished images.
- Image is always between O and F₁ (same side as object).

Uses of Concave Lens:
- Spectacles for near-sighted people
- Peephole in doors

LENS FORMULA
1/v - 1/u = 1/f

MAGNIFICATION FOR LENSES
m = v/u = h'/h

POWER OF A LENS
Power of a lens is the reciprocal of its focal length in meters.
P = 1/f (in meters)
Unit: Dioptre (D)
- Convex lens: positive power
- Concave lens: negative power

When lenses are combined:
P = P₁ + P₂ + P₃ + ...

IMPORTANT QUESTIONS AND ANSWERS

Q: Why does a convex mirror always produce a diminished image?
A: A convex mirror is a diverging mirror. The reflected rays diverge and when extended behind the mirror, they appear to meet at a point between P and F. Since the image is always formed between P and F, it is always smaller than the object.

Q: Why do we prefer a convex mirror as a rear-view mirror?
A: A convex mirror gives a wider field of view and always produces an erect image, making it easier for drivers to see the traffic behind them.

Q: What happens to the focal length of a lens when it is immersed in water?
A: The focal length increases when a lens is immersed in water. This is because the difference in refractive indices between the lens and the surrounding medium decreases, reducing the bending of light.

Q: What is the difference between real and virtual images?
A: A real image is formed by actual convergence of light rays and can be projected on a screen. A virtual image is formed by the apparent divergence of rays and cannot be projected on a screen.

KEY FORMULAS SUMMARY
- Mirror formula: 1/v + 1/u = 1/f
- Magnification (mirror): m = -v/u
- Snell's Law: n₁ sin i = n₂ sin r
- Refractive index: n = c/v
- Lens formula: 1/v - 1/u = 1/f
- Magnification (lens): m = v/u
- Power of lens: P = 1/f(m), unit = Dioptre
