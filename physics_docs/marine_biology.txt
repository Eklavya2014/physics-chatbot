1. THE OCEAN — OVERVIEW
------------------------
The ocean covers 71% of Earth's surface and contains 97% of Earth's water.
Average depth: 3,688 m. Deepest point: Mariana Trench (~11,000 m).
Marine biology is the study of organisms living in the ocean and other saltwater
environments (estuaries, coastal wetlands, coral reefs, deep sea).

Ocean Zones by Depth:
Zone             | Depth Range    | Light         | Key Features
-----------------|----------------|---------------|----------------------------
Epipelagic       | 0–200 m        | Sunlit zone   | Photosynthesis; most life
Mesopelagic      | 200–1,000 m    | Twilight zone | Bioluminescence common
Bathypelagic     | 1,000–4,000 m  | No light      | High pressure; cold
Abyssopelagic    | 4,000–6,000 m  | No light      | Extreme pressure; sparse
Hadopelagic      | >6,000 m       | No light      | Trenches; extremophiles

Ocean Zones by Distance from Shore:
- Intertidal (littoral): Between high and low tide marks. Most dynamic zone.
- Neritic zone: Over continental shelf (<200 m deep). High productivity.
- Oceanic zone: Open ocean beyond continental shelf.
- Benthic zone: Ocean floor (any depth).

1.1 MAJOR MARINE ECOSYSTEMS
-----------------------------
Coral Reefs:
  "Rainforests of the sea." Cover <1% of ocean floor but house ~25% of all marine species.
  Built by coral polyps (cnidarians) that secrete calcium carbonate skeletons.
  Symbiosis: Corals host zooxanthellae (photosynthetic dinoflagellates) in tissues →
  provide up to 90% of coral's energy via photosynthesis.

Kelp Forests:
  Giant kelp (Macrocystis pyrifera) grows up to 45 cm/day; forms underwater forests.
  Habitat for fish, sea otters, invertebrates. Important carbon sink.
  Keystone species: Sea otters control sea urchins → protect kelp.

Estuaries:
  Where rivers meet sea. Highly productive; nursery habitat for many species.
  Salinity varies (brackish water). Examples: Chesapeake Bay, Sundarbans.

Mangroves:
  Tropical coastal forests; salt-tolerant trees. Roots provide nursery habitat;
  buffer coastlines against erosion and storm surge; carbon storage.

Deep Sea Hydrothermal Vents:
  Discovered 1977 (Galapagos Rift). Chemosynthetic ecosystems — no sunlight.
  Bacteria oxidise H2S → energy (chemolithotrophy) → base of food web.
  Unique fauna: Tube worms (Riftia pachyptila), vent crabs, vent fish, Pompeii worms.

1.2 MAJOR MARINE ORGANISMS
----------------------------
Plankton (drifting organisms):
  Phytoplankton : Photosynthetic microorganisms (diatoms, dinoflagellates,
                  cyanobacteria). Produce ~50% of Earth's oxygen.
  Zooplankton   : Small animals (copepods, krill, jellyfish larvae). Base of
                  marine food webs.

Marine Invertebrates: Sponges, cnidarians (jellyfish, corals, anemones),
  echinoderms (sea stars, urchins), molluscs (squid, octopus, clams),
  crustaceans (crabs, shrimp, lobsters).

Marine Vertebrates: Fishes (cartilaginous: sharks, rays; bony: teleosts),
  marine reptiles (sea turtles, sea snakes), seabirds, marine mammals
  (cetaceans, pinnipeds, sirenians, sea otters).

2. MARINE ECOLOGY
------------------
Primary Production in the Ocean:
  Driven by phytoplankton in the photic zone (top ~200 m).
  Limited by: light, nutrients (N, P, Fe), temperature, CO2.
  Iron hypothesis (Martin, 1990): Iron limits primary production in HNLC
  (High Nutrient, Low Chlorophyll) regions (Southern Ocean, equatorial Pacific).
  Iron fertilisation experiments: Adding Fe → phytoplankton bloom → carbon export?

Marine Food Webs:
  Phytoplankton → Zooplankton (copepods, krill) → Small fish (herring, sardines)
  → Large fish (tuna, cod) → Top predators (sharks, seals, dolphins, orcas)
  Microbial loop: Bacteria decompose dissolved organic matter → recycled nutrients.
  Biological pump: Sinking organic particles (marine snow) → carbon to deep ocean.

Upwelling:
  Wind-driven movement of deep, cold, nutrient-rich water to surface.
  Supports massive productivity: Humboldt Current (Peru), California Current.
  El Nino: Weakens upwelling → drastic decline in fish stocks.

3. MARINE ADAPTATIONS
----------------------
Pressure Adaptation (Deep Sea):
  Piezophiles (pressure-loving organisms). Protein adaptations: flexible enzymes,
  increased unsaturated fatty acids in membranes (maintains fluidity under pressure).
  Trimethylamine oxide (TMAO): Accumulates in deep-sea fish; counteracts pressure-
  induced protein destabilisation.

Osmoregulation in Marine Animals:
  Marine bony fish: Hypoosmotic to seawater → lose water → drink seawater constantly;
  excrete excess salt via gills (chloride cells) and produce little, concentrated urine.
  Marine sharks/rays: Osmoconformers; accumulate urea + TMAO to match seawater osmolarity.
  Marine mammals: Obtain water from food; kidneys concentrate urine; skin waterproof.

Thermoregulation:
  Ectotherms (fish, invertebrates): Body temperature = water temperature.
  Some fish (bluefin tuna, great white shark): Partial endothermy via countercurrent
  heat exchange (rete mirabile) → maintain muscles warmer than water.
  Marine mammals (cetaceans, pinnipeds): Endothermic; insulated by thick blubber layer.

Bioluminescence:
  Production of light by living organisms via luciferin + luciferase reaction.
  ~76% of deep-sea species are bioluminescent.
  Functions: Predation (anglerfish lure), counter-illumination camouflage,
  communication (firefly squid), startling predators.
  Dinoflagellates: Blue bioluminescence in surf ("sea sparkle" = Noctiluca, Lingulodinium).

4. CETACEAN BIOLOGY
--------------------
Order Cetacea: Fully aquatic mammals descended from land mammals (~50 Mya).
Sister group to Artiodactyla (hippopotamuses are closest living relatives).

Suborders:
  Mysticeti (baleen whales): Filter feeders. Blue whale (largest animal ever,
  up to 33 m, 173 tonnes). Humpback (breaching, complex songs).
  Odontoceti (toothed whales): Include dolphins, porpoises, sperm whale, orcas.
  Echolocation: Produce clicks → reflect off objects → processed in melon/lower jaw.
  Sperm whale dives to 3,000 m for giant squid; holds breath >90 minutes.

Whale migration: Humpbacks travel up to 25,000 km/year between feeding and breeding grounds.

5. OCEAN CHEMISTRY & CLIMATE
------------------------------
Ocean Acidification:
  Ocean absorbs ~25–30% of anthropogenic CO2 emissions.
  CO2 + H2O → H2CO3 (carbonic acid) → H+ + HCO3- → H+ + CO3 2-
  Since industrial revolution: ocean pH has dropped from 8.2 → 8.1 (~30% more acidic).
  Impacts:
  - Impairs calcification in corals, molluscs, echinoderms, foraminifera
    (aragonite and calcite saturation states decline).
  - Affects fish behaviour: olfaction, predator avoidance (clownfish larvae).
  - Pteropods (sea butterflies): Shell dissolution observed in Southern Ocean.

Ocean Deoxygenation:
  Warmer water holds less oxygen. Oxygen minimum zones (OMZs) expanding.
  Hypoxic zones ("dead zones") near coasts driven by nutrient runoff →
  phytoplankton bloom → decomposition → O2 depletion (Gulf of Mexico, Baltic Sea).

Thermohaline Circulation (Global Ocean Conveyor Belt):
  Driven by density differences (temperature + salinity).
  North Atlantic: Warm surface water cools → becomes dense → sinks (NADW) →
  flows south → upwells in Southern Ocean → returns as surface current.
  Shutdown risk: Freshwater from melting ice sheets → reduces density → slows AMOC.
  Consequences: Cooling of NW Europe; disruption of monsoons; sea level rise on NE US coast.

6. CORAL REEF ECOLOGY & BLEACHING
-----------------------------------
Coral Biology:
  Coral polyp: Cnidarian (phylum); has nematocysts for prey capture.
  Zooxanthellae (Symbiodinium/Cladocopium): Photosynthetic dinoflagellate endosymbionts
  in gastrodermal cells → provide coral with glucose, amino acids, O2 via photosynthesis
  → coral provides CO2, nutrients, protected environment.

Coral Bleaching:
  Mechanism: Thermal stress → oxidative stress in zooxanthellae →
  reactive oxygen species (ROS) damage photosystems → coral expels zooxanthellae
  → coral turns white (bleaches) → starvation if symbiont not restored.
  Threshold: ~1°C above average summer maximum sustained for 4+ weeks.
  Degree Heating Weeks (DHW): Predictive metric used by NOAA Coral Reef Watch.
  Mass bleaching events: 1998, 2010, 2015–2017 (Great Barrier Reef: 50% coral death),
  2022, 2024 (global — 4th mass bleaching event).

Recovery: Possible if thermal stress brief (<8 weeks). Recovery takes 10–15 years.
Assisted evolution strategies: Coral probiotics, selective breeding of heat-tolerant
  symbiont strains (Durusdinium trenchii), CRISPR editing of coral genomes.

7. MARINE CONSERVATION & THREATS
----------------------------------
Threats:
  Overfishing         : ~33% of global fish stocks overexploited (FAO 2022).
                        Bycatch: 40% of global catch is non-target species.
  Plastic Pollution   : 8 million tonnes plastic enters ocean annually.
                        Microplastics (<5mm) in all ocean zones; ingested by filter feeders.
  Habitat Destruction : Trawling damages seafloor; dredging destroys seagrass/coral.
  Invasive Species    : Crown-of-thorns starfish (coral predator); lionfish (Atlantic).
  Climate Change      : Sea level rise, warming, acidification, deoxygenation.
  Noise Pollution     : Ship noise, sonar disrupts cetacean communication/navigation.

Conservation Strategies:
  Marine Protected Areas (MPAs): ~8% of ocean currently protected; target 30x30.
  No-take zones show dramatic recovery of fish biomass (50–150% increase in 5 years).
  Fisheries management: MSC certification, catch quotas, mesh size regulations.
  Whale sanctuaries; International Whaling Commission (IWC) moratorium (1986).
  Blue carbon: Protecting mangroves, seagrasses, salt marshes as carbon sinks.
  Restoration: Coral gardening, oyster reef restoration, seagrass replanting.

[RESEARCH FRONTIER] eDNA (Environmental DNA) sampling:
  Water samples contain DNA shed by organisms → sequencing reveals community
  composition without direct observation. Detects rare/cryptic species.
  Applications: Monitoring invasive species, biodiversity assessments, detecting
  whale shark or great white shark presence without sighting.
  Future: Autonomous underwater vehicles (AUVs) + real-time eDNA sensors.
