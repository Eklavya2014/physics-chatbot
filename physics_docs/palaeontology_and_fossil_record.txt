1. WHAT IS PALAEONTOLOGY?
--------------------------
Palaeontology is the study of ancient life through fossils — preserved remains,
traces, or chemical signatures of organisms from the geological past.

How Fossils Form (Fossilisation/Taphonomy):
  Most organisms are decomposed → rarely preserved. Ideal conditions:
  1. Rapid burial (sediment, volcanic ash, amber, tar, permafrost).
  2. Hard parts (shells, bones, teeth, wood) more commonly preserved.
  3. Mineralisation: Organic matter replaced by minerals (silicification, pyritisation).
  4. Impression/mould fossils: Imprint left in rock; original material gone.
  5. Trace fossils (ichnofossils): Footprints, burrows, coprolites (fossilised dung).
  6. Amber preservation: Organisms trapped in tree resin → exceptional soft tissue preservation.
  7. Permafrost: Woolly mammoths preserved with muscle, hair, stomach contents.

Types of Fossils:
  Body fossils   : Actual remains (bone, shell, wood, teeth).
  Trace fossils  : Evidence of behaviour (tracks, burrows, nests, eggs).
  Chemical fossils (biomarkers): Molecular signatures (sterols, hopanes, porphyrins).
  Compression fossils: Flattened remains with carbonaceous film (plant fossils).
  Lagerstätten   : Exceptional fossil sites with extraordinary preservation.
    Examples: Burgess Shale (Canada, ~508 Mya); Ediacara Hills (Australia, ~560 Mya);
    Messel Pit (Germany, 47 Mya — mammals with fur/stomach contents); Yixian (China — feathered dinosaurs).

2. DATING METHODS
------------------
Relative Dating:
  Law of superposition: Older layers below younger layers (stratigraphy).
  Index fossils: Characteristic species with wide distribution and short time range
  → used to date rock layers. Examples: Trilobites (Palaeozoic), ammonites (Mesozoic).
  Biostratigraphy: Correlating rock layers worldwide using fossil assemblages.

Absolute (Radiometric) Dating:
  Based on radioactive decay of isotopes at known rates (half-life).

Isotope Pair        | Half-life      | Range          | Used For
--------------------|----------------|----------------|-------------------------
Carbon-14/Nitrogen-14 | 5,730 years  | <50,000 years  | Recent fossils, archaeology
Potassium-40/Argon-40 | 1.25 billion yr | >100,000 yr | Volcanic rock, early hominins
Uranium-235/Lead-207  | 703 million yr | Millions-billions | Zircons, ancient rock
Rubidium-87/Strontium-87 | 48.8 billion yr | Billions | Very ancient rocks

U-Pb zircon dating: Most reliable; zircon crystals exclude Pb when forming but
include U → measures accumulated Pb → precise ages from 4.4 Gya.

Paleomagnetism:
  Earth's magnetic field reverses every ~200,000–300,000 years. Recorded in volcanic
  rock as it cools → magnetic stratigraphy; combined with isotopic dating.

3. MASS EXTINCTIONS
--------------------
Big Five Mass Extinctions:
Event              | Date (Mya) | % Species Lost | Cause
-------------------|------------|----------------|--------------------------------
End-Ordovician     | 443        | ~85%           | Glaciation; sea level drop
Late Devonian      | 374        | ~75%           | Multiple pulses; anoxia; asteroid?
End-Permian (P-Tr) | 252        | ~96%           | Siberian Traps volcanism; anoxia; warming
End-Triassic       | 201        | ~80%           | CAMP volcanism; CO2 rise
K-Pg (K-T)         | 66         | ~76%           | Chicxulub asteroid + Deccan Traps volcanism

End-Permian Extinction ("The Great Dying"):
  Most severe extinction in Earth history. Siberian Traps volcanism → massive CO2
  + SO2 + methane release → rapid warming (~10°C) + ocean acidification +
  anoxia + ozone depletion. Recovery took ~10 million years.

K-Pg Extinction:
  Chicxulub asteroid (10–15 km diameter) struck Yucatan Peninsula, Mexico.
  → Immediate: Earthquakes, tsunamis, wildfires, impact winter (soot blocks sun).
  → Months-years: Global cooling, acid rain, photosynthesis collapse.
  → Non-avian dinosaurs, pterosaurs, marine reptiles, ammonites all extinct.
  Evidence: Iridium anomaly (rare on Earth; abundant in asteroids) worldwide at K-Pg boundary.
  Survivors: Birds (avian dinosaurs), crocodilians, turtles, lizards, snakes, mammals.

Sixth Mass Extinction (Anthropocene):
  Current extinction rate: 100–1,000x background rate. Driven by habitat destruction,
  climate change, invasive species, overexploitation, pollution.
  ~1 million species threatened (IPBES 2019).

4. KEY FOSSIL ORGANISMS & EVOLUTIONARY TRANSITIONS
----------------------------------------------------
Cambrian Explosion (~541 Mya):
  Most major animal body plans appear in fossil record within ~20 million years.
  Burgess Shale: Soft-bodied organisms (Pikaia — possible early chordate; Anomalocaris —
  apex predator; Hallucigenia — velvet worm relative; Opabinia — 5 eyes + proboscis).
  Trigger: Rising O2, end of Snowball Earth, ecological cascades.

Fish to Tetrapod Transition:
  Tiktaalik roseae (Daeschler, Shubin, Jenkins 2004): ~375 Mya. "Fishapod."
  Fish-like but with: Neck, ribs, weight-bearing pectoral fins ("proto-limbs"),
  lungs. Found in Devonian deposits, Canadian Arctic. Transitional fossil.
  Tetrapod limb: 5-digit plan (Acanthostega had 8 digits → reduced to 5).

Dinosaur to Bird Transition:
  Archaeopteryx (~150 Mya): Classic transitional fossil; bird features (feathers,
  wishbone) + dinosaur features (teeth, clawed wings, long bony tail).
  Feathered theropods from Yixian Formation, China (Liaoning): Microraptor, Anchiornis,
  Confuciusornis → revealed gradual acquisition of flight-related features.
  Birds are avian dinosaurs (crown clade within Maniraptora → Theropoda → Saurischia).

Whale Evolution:
  ~53 Mya: Pakicetus — terrestrial artiodactyl with dense bones; earliest whale relative.
  Ambulocetus (~49 Mya): Could walk on land and swim.
  Rodhocetus (~47 Mya): Reduced hindlimbs; committed swimmer.
  Basilosaurus (~37 Mya): Fully aquatic; vestigial hindlimbs; serpentine body.
  Modern cetaceans: No hindlimbs externally; retain vestigial pelvic bones.

5. HUMAN EVOLUTION (PALAEOANTHROPOLOGY)
-----------------------------------------
Hominin Phylogeny:
  Family Hominidae: Great apes + humans.
  Tribe Hominini: Humans + chimpanzees + bonobos (split ~6–7 Mya).
  Genus Australopithecus: Bipedal; small brain (~450cc); lived 4–2 Mya.
    A. afarensis: "Lucy" (AL 288-1); 3.2 Mya; Ethiopia. Bipedal but arboreal too.
    A. africanus: Taung Child (Dart, 1924); South Africa; 3–2 Mya.
    A. sediba: Mosaic features; possible Homo ancestor; 1.98 Mya, South Africa.
  Genus Paranthropus: Robust australopiths; large cheek teeth; 2.7–1.2 Mya.
    P. robustus, P. boisei ("Nutcracker Man").
  Genus Homo:
    H. habilis ("Handy Man"): ~2.4 Mya; first stone tools (Oldowan).
    H. erectus: ~1.9 Mya–100 Kya; out of Africa; fire use; Acheulean tools.
    H. heidelbergensis: ~700–200 Kya; Europe/Africa; ancestor of Neanderthals + H. sapiens?
    H. neanderthalensis: ~400–40 Kya; Europe/W Asia; large brain (~1500cc); interbreeding
      with H. sapiens confirmed (1–4% Neanderthal DNA in non-African modern humans).
    H. floresiensis ("Hobbit"): ~100–50 Kya; Flores, Indonesia; ~1m tall; small brain.
    H. denisova: Known from DNA + single finger bone; Siberia; interbreeding documented.
    H. sapiens: ~300 Kya (Morocco, Jebel Irhoud); 70 Kya out-of-Africa expansion.

6. ANCIENT DNA & MOLECULAR PALAEOANTHROPOLOGY
-----------------------------------------------
Ancient DNA (aDNA):
  DNA degrades rapidly → limit ~1 million years in ideal conditions (permafrost).
  Neanderthal genome (Pääbo, 2010 — Nobel Prize 2022): Draft genome from Vindija Cave, Croatia.
  Denisovan genome (2010): Single finger bone + molar → complete genome (exceptional preservation).
  Findings: Interbreeding: Neanderthal → non-Africans (1–4%); Denisovan → Melanesians (3–6%),
  Tibetans (EPAS1/HIF pathway variant → high-altitude adaptation from Denisovans).
  Ancient pathogens: aDNA from ancient plague victims (Yersinia pestis evolution traced
  from Bronze Age to Black Death); medieval leprosy; smallpox from 1600s mummies.

Palaeoproteomics:
  Proteins survive longer than DNA (up to several million years).
  Enamel proteomics: Amelogenin from 1.9 Mya Paranthropus (Welker et al., 2019) →
  sex determination of ancient hominins.
  Collagen from dinosaur bones: T. rex collagen sequences → confirms bird relationship.

[DEEP TIME PERSPECTIVE] The entire human evolutionary lineage represents only the last
~0.15% of Earth's history. Complex multicellular life has existed for only ~600 million
years — less than 15% of Earth's age. Non-avian dinosaurs existed for 165 million years;
modern Homo sapiens have existed for ~300,000 years. The geological record is profoundly
biased: soft-bodied organisms rarely preserve; oceanic crust is destroyed by subduction;
most species that ever existed left no fossil record. Despite this, the fossil record
provides overwhelming evidence for evolution and the deep history of life.
