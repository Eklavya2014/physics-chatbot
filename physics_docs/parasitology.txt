1. WHAT IS PARASITOLOGY?
--------------------------
Parasitology is the study of parasites — organisms that live on or in a host
organism and benefit at the host's expense. Parasitism is one of the most
successful life strategies on Earth; ~50% of all species are parasitic.

Definitions:
- Parasite      : An organism living at the expense of a host (fitness benefit for
                  parasite, harm to host).
- Host          : Organism harbouring the parasite.
  Definitive host   : Where parasite reaches sexual maturity.
  Intermediate host : Parasite undergoes asexual/larval development.
  Reservoir host    : Maintains parasite in nature; source of human infection.
  Paratenic host    : Transport host; parasite doesn't develop further.
- Vector        : Organism (usually arthropod) that transmits parasite between hosts.
- Zoonosis      : Disease transmissible from animals to humans.

Major Groups of Parasites:
- Protozoa      : Single-celled eukaryotes (Plasmodium, Trypanosoma, Toxoplasma, Giardia).
- Helminths     : Multicellular worms. Three groups:
    Nematodes   : Roundworms (Ascaris, hookworm, filaria, pinworm).
    Trematodes  : Flukes (Schistosoma, liver flukes).
    Cestodes    : Tapeworms (Taenia, Echinococcus).
- Ectoparasites : Live on body surface (lice, fleas, ticks, mites, mosquitoes).

2. MALARIA — BIOLOGY & LIFE CYCLE
-----------------------------------
Causative agents: Plasmodium falciparum (most deadly), P. vivax (most widespread),
P. malariae, P. ovale, P. knowlesi.

Life Cycle:
  Mosquito bite → sporozoites injected → liver (hepatocytes) → merozoites
  (exoerythrocytic schizogony; P. vivax/ovale form dormant hypnozoites in liver)
  → red blood cells → ring stage → trophozoite → schizont → merozoites burst
  RBC (erythrocytic schizogony) → fever/chills every 48h (P. falciparum/vivax)
  or 72h (P. malariae) correlate with RBC bursting → some → gametocytes →
  taken up by Anopheles mosquito → sexual reproduction in gut (sporogony) →
  sporozoites in salivary glands → next bite.

P. falciparum pathogenesis:
  Infected RBCs express PfEMP1 (variant surface antigen) → cytoadherence to
  endothelium (via ICAM-1, CD36) + rosetting → microvascular obstruction.
  Cerebral malaria: Sequestration in brain vessels → coma, seizures.
  Severe anaemia: RBC destruction + suppressed erythropoiesis.
  Hypoglycaemia: High parasite metabolic demand + quinine-induced insulin.

Diagnosis: Blood smear microscopy (gold standard); RDT (rapid diagnostic test
detecting HRP2 or pLDH); PCR for species identification.

Treatment:
  Uncomplicated: Artemisinin-based combination therapy (ACT) — artemether-lumefantrine;
  artesunate-amodiaquine.
  Severe: IV artesunate (replaced quinine).
  P. vivax/ovale hypnozoites: Primaquine or tafenoquine (check G6PD status first).
  Prophylaxis: Atovaquone-proguanil; doxycycline; mefloquine.

Resistance: Artemisinin partial resistance (kelch13 mutations) emerging in SE Asia;
  spread to Africa reported. Chloroquine resistance widespread.

3. TRYPANOSOMIASIS
-------------------
African Sleeping Sickness (Trypanosoma brucei):
  Vector: Tsetse fly (Glossina spp.).
  T.b. rhodesiense (E Africa): Acute; months. T.b. gambiense (W/C Africa): Chronic; years.
  Pathogenesis: Bloodstream form (chancre) → lymphatics (lymphadenopathy; Winterbottom's sign)
  → CNS (stage 2: behavioural changes, sleep disruption, coma → death untreated).
  Immune evasion: Antigenic variation of Variant Surface Glycoprotein (VSG);
  switches coat ~every 7 days; parasite always one step ahead of immune response.
  Treatment: Stage 1: Pentamidine (T.b.g.) or suramin. Stage 2: Nifurtimox-eflornithine
  (NECT); fexinidazole (oral; WHO essential medicine).

Chagas Disease (T. cruzi):
  Vector: Triatomine "kissing bugs" (bite near mouth; defecate near wound).
  Acute: Romaña's sign (periorbital oedema); chagoma; fever.
  Chronic: Cardiomyopathy (dilated; arrhythmias; sudden death); megacolon; megaoesophagus.
  Treatment: Benznidazole or nifurtimox (effective in acute; limited in chronic).

4. IMMUNOLOGY OF PARASITISM
-----------------------------
Th2 Response & Helminths:
  Helminths preferentially drive Th2 immunity:
  IL-4 → IgE production; IL-5 → eosinophilia; IL-13 → mucus, smooth muscle contraction.
  IgE + mast cells/basophils → degranulation → toxic to helminths (ADCC).
  Eosinophils: Release major basic protein, eosinophil peroxidase → kill larvae.
  Regulatory response: Helminths also induce Tregs + IL-10 → dampen immune response
  → reduce immunopathology; hygiene hypothesis explains increased allergies in helminth-free populations.

Toxoplasma gondii Manipulation:
  Infects ~1/3 of global population. Definitive host: Cats.
  In rodents: Infects amygdala neurons; reduces fear of cat odours → infected mice
  attracted to cat urine → cats eat mice → Toxoplasma completes sexual cycle.
  In humans: Latent infection; associations with schizophrenia, risk-taking behaviour
  (controversial but documented).

5. MOLECULAR PARASITOLOGY
---------------------------
Plasmodium Invasion Machinery:
  Merozoite invasion of RBCs: MSP1 (merozoite surface protein 1) primes; EBAs
  (erythrocyte binding antigens) and PfRH (reticulocyte homology) ligands bind
  RBC receptors → rhoptry discharge → tight junction forms → moving junction
  powered by MyoA/GACT actomyosin motor → parasite enters PV (parasitophorous vacuole).
  Vaccine targets: PfCSP (circumsporozoite protein) → RTS,S/AS01 (Mosquirix);
  PfRH5 → blocks RBC invasion (Phase 2 trial efficacy ~50%).

Drug Resistance Mechanisms:
  Chloroquine resistance: Mutation in PfCRT (chloroquine resistance transporter)
  → drug effluxed from digestive vacuole.
  Artemisinin partial resistance: Kelch13 (K13) propeller mutations → parasite
  enters dormancy (ring stage) during drug exposure → resumes replication.
  Pyrimethamine resistance: DHFR mutations (dihydrofolate reductase); compound
  resistance in DHPS also (SP resistance).

Helminth Genomics:
  C. elegans: Model nematode; 959 somatic cells; first animal genome sequenced;
  apoptosis, RNAi, and ageing discoveries.
  Parasitic nematode genomes (Ascaris, Brugia, Necator, Trichinella): Reveal
  excretory/secretory proteins → immune evasion; potential drug targets.
  Schistosome genome: Two sexes; females dependent on males for maturation;
  tegument surface proteins (Sm29, Sm-TSP-2) → vaccine candidates.

6. VECTOR BIOLOGY & CONTROL
-----------------------------
Anopheles Mosquito:
  Anopheles gambiae s.l.: Primary African malaria vector.
  Feeding: Female feeds on blood for egg development; rest and digest.
  Olfactory cues: CO2, 1-octen-3-ol, lactic acid attract mosquitoes.
  Malaria vector competence: Oocysts form on midgut; sporozoites migrate to
  salivary glands over 10–14 days (extrinsic incubation period).
  Temperature-sensitive: Warmer temperatures accelerate parasite development
  → climate change expanding malaria range.

Vector Control Strategies:
  ITNs (Insecticide-Treated Nets): Pyrethroid-treated; reduces biting by 70–90%.
  IRS (Indoor Residual Spraying): DDT, pyrethroids; targets resting mosquitoes.
  Larviciding: Bacillus thuringiensis israelensis (Bti) → kills larvae without
  environmental harm.
  Sterile Insect Technique (SIT): Release sterile males → reduced reproduction.
  Wolbachia: Endosymbiotic bacteria in Aedes aegypti mosquitoes → reduces dengue,
  Zika, malaria transmission. World Mosquito Program releases Wolbachia-infected
  mosquitoes; ~77% dengue reduction in Indonesia RCT (2021).
  Gene Drive (CRISPR): Self-propagating genetic modification spreads through
  wild population. Female-specific lethality drives population suppression.
  Cage trials (2018) with An. gambiae showed population crash within 7–11 generations.

[ONE HEALTH LINK] Parasitic diseases at human-animal-environment interface:
  Echinococcus (hydatid cysts): Dog-sheep cycle; affects farmers globally.
  Toxocara (visceral larva migrans): Dogs/cats → soil → children; common in low-income areas.
  Emerging: Baylisascaris (raccoon roundworm); Angiostrongylus cantonensis (rat lungworm)
  spreading due to climate change and exotic pet trade.
