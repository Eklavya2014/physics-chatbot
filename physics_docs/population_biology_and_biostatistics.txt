1. POPULATION BIOLOGY FUNDAMENTALS
------------------------------------
A population is a group of individuals of the same species occupying the same area
at the same time, capable of interbreeding.

Population Characteristics:
  Population size (N)     : Total number of individuals.
  Population density      : Individuals per unit area/volume.
  Dispersion pattern      : Clumped (most common), uniform, random.
  Age structure           : Distribution of individuals across age classes.
  Sex ratio               : Proportion of males to females.
  Birth rate (b)          : Births per individual per time unit.
  Death rate (d)          : Deaths per individual per time unit.
  Immigration (i)         : Individuals moving in.
  Emigration (e)          : Individuals moving out.
  Rate of increase: r = b - d + i - e. If r > 0: growing; r < 0: declining; r = 0: stable.

Survivorship Curves:
  Type I   : Low early mortality; high survival until old age; then rapid death. (Humans, elephants)
  Type II  : Constant mortality rate throughout life. (Birds, squirrels, hydra)
  Type III : Very high early mortality; survivors live long. (Fish, oysters, many plants)

1.1 LIFE TABLES
----------------
Life tables track survival and reproduction through age classes.
  lx : Probability of surviving from birth to age x.
  mx : Average number of female offspring per female at age x.
  Net Reproductive Rate R0 = Σ(lx × mx) = average number of offspring per female.
    R0 > 1: Population growing. R0 = 1: Stable. R0 < 1: Declining.
  Generation time T = Σ(x × lx × mx) / R0.
  Intrinsic rate of increase r ≈ ln(R0) / T.

2. POPULATION GENETICS IN DEPTH
---------------------------------
Genetic Drift in Small Populations:
  Effective population size (Ne): Size of ideal population with same genetic drift.
  Ne < N always (bottlenecks, unequal sex ratio, variance in offspring number).
  Genetic drift rate: ~1/(2Ne) per generation.
  Inbreeding coefficient F: Probability that two alleles are identical by descent.
    F increases as population size decreases → inbreeding depression (homozygosity → deleterious recessive phenotypes).
  Minimum Viable Population (MVP): ~500–5,000 individuals for long-term genetic viability.

Mutation-Selection Balance:
  Deleterious mutations removed by selection but replenished by mutation.
  At equilibrium: q (mutant allele freq) ≈ √(μ/s) for recessive; μ/s for dominant.
  Where μ = mutation rate, s = selection coefficient.
  Explains persistence of rare recessive diseases.

Molecular Evolution:
  Neutral theory (Kimura, 1968): Most molecular variation is neutral, fixed by drift.
  Nearly-neutral theory (Ohta): Slightly deleterious mutations — drift vs selection
    depends on Ne. Small Ne → slightly deleterious mutations can fix by drift.
  Rate of neutral evolution = mutation rate (independent of population size).
  Positive selection: dN/dS > 1 (nonsynonymous > synonymous substitution rate) → adaptive evolution.
  Purifying selection: dN/dS < 1 → functional constraint on protein sequence.
  Tests for selection: Tajima's D; McDonald-Kreitman test; selective sweep detection (iHS, XP-EHH).

3. METAPOPULATION THEORY
--------------------------
Metapopulation: Network of spatially separated local populations connected by dispersal.
Levins Model:
  dp/dt = m × p × (1-p) - e × p
  p = fraction of patches occupied; m = colonisation rate; e = extinction rate.
  Equilibrium p* = 1 - (e/m). Metapopulation persists only if m > e.
  Rescue effect: Immigrants from occupied patches reduce local extinction probability.
  Species-Area Relationship: S = c × A^z (S = species, A = area, z ≈ 0.25–0.35).
    Fragmentation → smaller patches → fewer species → conservation implications.

Island Biogeography (MacArthur & Wilson, 1967):
  Species richness = immigration rate and extinction rate balance.
  Large islands + close to mainland → more species.
  Turnover: Continuous extinction + colonisation even at equilibrium.
  Applied to habitat fragments → minimum patch size for conservation.

4. BIOSTATISTICS — CORE CONCEPTS
-----------------------------------
Descriptive Statistics:
  Mean (x̄): Arithmetic average. Sensitive to outliers.
  Median: Middle value. Robust to outliers.
  Mode: Most frequent value.
  Variance (s²): Average squared deviation from mean.
  Standard deviation (s): √variance. Same units as data.
  Standard error (SE): s/√n. Uncertainty of the mean estimate.
  95% Confidence Interval: x̄ ± 1.96×SE (for large n). Range likely containing true mean.

Probability Distributions:
  Normal (Gaussian): Bell-shaped; symmetric; mean = median = mode.
    68-95-99.7 rule: 68% within 1SD, 95% within 2SD, 99.7% within 3SD.
  Binomial: Discrete; n trials; p probability each. Mean = np; var = np(1-p).
  Poisson: Count data; rare events. Mean = variance = λ.
  t-distribution: Like normal but heavier tails; used for small samples.
  Chi-squared: Sum of squared standard normals; used for categorical data.
  F-distribution: Ratio of variances; used in ANOVA.

Hypothesis Testing:
  Null hypothesis (H0): No effect/difference. Alternative (H1): Effect exists.
  P-value: Probability of data as extreme or more extreme if H0 true.
    p < 0.05: Reject H0 (conventional threshold). Not probability H0 is true.
  Type I error (alpha): Reject true H0. False positive. α = 0.05.
  Type II error (beta): Fail to reject false H0. False negative. β = 0.20 (typical).
  Power: 1 - β = probability of detecting true effect. Increases with: larger n, larger effect, lower variability.
  Multiple testing correction: Bonferroni (α/n tests); FDR (Benjamini-Hochberg).

Key Statistical Tests:
  t-test (independent, paired): Compare means; normally distributed; two groups.
  Mann-Whitney U: Non-parametric equivalent of independent t-test.
  ANOVA: Compare means across 3+ groups. Post-hoc: Tukey, Bonferroni.
  Chi-squared test: Association between categorical variables. Expected vs observed.
  Fisher's exact test: Small sample categorical data.
  Pearson correlation (r): Linear association between two continuous variables (-1 to +1).
  Spearman (rho): Rank-based; non-parametric correlation.
  Simple/Multiple linear regression: Predict continuous outcome from predictor(s).
  Logistic regression: Predict binary outcome; outputs odds ratios.
  Kaplan-Meier: Survival analysis; time to event.
  Cox proportional hazards: Regression for survival data; hazard ratios.

5. QUANTITATIVE GENETICS
--------------------------
Heritability:
  H² (broad): Proportion of phenotypic variance due to all genetic variance.
    H² = VG / VP = (VA + VD + VI) / (VA + VD + VI + VE)
  h² (narrow): Proportion due to additive genetic variance only.
    h² = VA / VP. Predicts response to selection.
  Response to selection: R = h² × S (S = selection differential).
  Examples: h² for height ~0.8; intelligence ~0.5–0.8; personality traits ~0.3–0.5.

QTL (Quantitative Trait Loci) Mapping:
  Localise genomic regions contributing to continuous traits.
  Linkage mapping: F2 or backcross progeny; ANOVA of trait vs marker genotype.
  GWAS: Association of SNPs with trait in unrelated individuals.
  Polygenic risk scores (PRS): Weighted sum of risk alleles across many loci.
    PRS for height explains ~25% variance; for coronary artery disease ~10%.
  Mendelian randomisation: Use genetic variants as instrumental variables to
    establish causal effects of exposures (like BMI) on outcomes (like CHD).

6. CONSERVATION GENETICS
--------------------------
Genetic Diversity Metrics:
  Ho (observed heterozygosity): Proportion of heterozygous individuals.
  He (expected heterozygosity): Expected under HWE = 1 - Σpi².
  Allelic richness: Number of alleles per locus.
  Fst (fixation index): Genetic differentiation between populations (0=none; 1=complete).
    Fst = (HT - HS) / HT. High Fst → strong population structure → limited gene flow.

Genetic Erosion:
  Small isolated populations → genetic drift → loss of alleles → reduced adaptive potential.
  Inbreeding depression: Recessive deleterious alleles expressed → reduced fitness.
  Outbreeding depression: Crossing highly diverged populations → disruption of local adaptations.
  Genetic rescue: Deliberate introduction of new individuals → restore genetic diversity.
    Florida panther: Genetic rescue with 8 Texas pumas (1995) → dramatic demographic recovery.

Ancient DNA in Conservation:
  Historical DNA from museum specimens reveals past genetic diversity.
  Can quantify genetic erosion since human impact began.
  Thylacine (Tasmanian tiger): Genome reconstructed → de-extinction program (Colossal Biosciences).
  Woolly mammoth: Genome 95%+ complete; cold-adaptation genes identified; Colossal program
    aims to produce cold-adapted Asian elephant × mammoth hybrid (Mammophant) by 2028.

[STATISTICAL NOTE] Replication crisis in biology:
  Many published findings fail to replicate (e.g., Open Science Collaboration 2015: only 36%
  of psychology studies replicated). In biology/medicine: NEJM 2011 showed <25% of landmark
  cancer biology papers replicated (Begley & Ellis).
  Causes: Small sample sizes; p-hacking; HARKing (hypothesising after results known);
  publication bias; flexible data analysis; lack of pre-registration.
  Solutions: Pre-registration; larger samples; open data; reproducible analysis pipelines;
  registered reports; multilab replications; Bayesian statistics.
