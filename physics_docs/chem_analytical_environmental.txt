ANALYTICAL CHEMISTRY - COMPREHENSIVE GUIDE
==========================================

PART 1: QUALITATIVE ANALYSIS

CATION ANALYSIS (Classical Scheme):
Group I (Precipitated by HCl): Ag⁺, Pb²⁺, Hg₂²⁺.
Precipitate: AgCl (white), PbCl₂ (white, soluble in hot water), Hg₂Cl₂ (white, turns black with NH₃).
Confirmatory: AgCl + NH₃ → [Ag(NH₃)₂]⁺ (soluble); re-precipitate with HNO₃.
PbCl₂ + H₂SO₄ → PbSO₄ (white precipitate) or yellow with K₂CrO₄.

Group II (Precipitated by H₂S in acidic medium, HCl + H₂S):
IIA: HgS (black), PbS (black), Bi₂S₃ (black), CuS (black), CdS (yellow).
IIB: As₂S₃ (yellow), Sb₂S₃ (orange), SnS₂ (yellow).
Separate IIA (insoluble in yellow ammonium sulfide) from IIB (soluble).

Group III (Precipitated by NH₃/NH₄Cl buffer + H₂S or NH₃):
IIIA: Fe(OH)₃ (red-brown), Al(OH)₃ (white), Cr(OH)₃ (green) — precipitated by NH₃/NH₄Cl.
IIIB: NiS, CoS, MnS, ZnS — precipitated by H₂S in alkaline medium.

Group IV (Precipitated by (NH₄)₂CO₃ in NH₃/NH₄⁺ buffer): Ba²⁺, Sr²⁺, Ca²⁺.
BaCO₃ (white), SrCO₃ (white), CaCO₃ (white).
Confirm: Ba²⁺ → yellow BaCrO₄ with K₂CrO₄ in acetic acid.
Sr²⁺ → white SrSO₄. Ca²⁺ → white CaC₂O₄ (oxalate).
Flame: Ba (apple green), Sr (crimson), Ca (brick red).

Group V (Remain in solution): Mg²⁺, Na⁺, K⁺, NH₄⁺.
Mg²⁺: white Mg(OH)₂ with NaOH; white MgNH₄PO₄ with Na₂HPO₄/NH₃.
Na⁺: yellow flame; white NaZn(UO₂)₃(CH₃COO)₉ with zinc uranyl acetate.
K⁺: lilac flame; white K₂PtCl₆ with chloroplatinic acid; white or yellow KClO₄.
NH₄⁺: Nessler's reagent → brown; NH₃ gas (turns moist red litmus blue).

ANION ANALYSIS:
Dilute H₂SO₄ tests:
CO₃²⁻, HCO₃⁻: CO₂ gas (turns lime water milky).
SO₃²⁻: SO₂ gas (turns acidified K₂Cr₂O₇ green).
NO₂⁻: brown NO₂ gas + reddish fumes.
S²⁻: H₂S gas (rotten egg smell, blackens Pb acetate paper).
Cl⁻, Br⁻, I⁻: gases in hot concentrated H₂SO₄.

Concentrated H₂SO₄ tests:
Cl⁻: HCl gas (fumes in air, white fumes with NH₃).
Br⁻: HBr + Br₂ (orange-brown fumes).
I⁻: HI + I₂ (violet fumes).
NO₃⁻: ring test with FeSO₄.

Ring test for NO₃⁻: FeSO₄ added to NO₃⁻ solution, concentrated H₂SO₄ poured carefully down side → brown ring at interface. Fe²⁺ + NO₃⁻ + H₂SO₄ → Fe³⁺ + NO; [Fe(NO)]²⁺ = brown.

Silver nitrate (AgNO₃) tests:
Cl⁻: white AgCl (insoluble in HNO₃, soluble in NH₃).
Br⁻: pale yellow AgBr (insoluble in HNO₃, sparingly soluble in NH₃).
I⁻: yellow AgI (insoluble in HNO₃ and NH₃).
SO₄²⁻: white BaSO₄ with BaCl₂ (insoluble in HCl — confirmatory).

Barium chloride (BaCl₂) tests:
SO₄²⁻: white BaSO₄ (insoluble in HCl).
SO₃²⁻: white BaSO₃ (soluble in HCl, gives SO₂).
CO₃²⁻: white BaCO₃ (soluble in HCl, gives CO₂).

PART 2: QUANTITATIVE ANALYSIS

GRAVIMETRIC ANALYSIS:
Analyte converted to insoluble precipitate → filter → dry/ignite → weigh.
Precipitation factors: precipitate must be insoluble, filterable, stable.
Common precipitating agents:
BaCl₂ for SO₄²⁻ (as BaSO₄). AgNO₃ for Cl⁻ (as AgCl).
NH₃ for Al³⁺ (as Al₂O₃ after ignition). Dimethylglyoxime for Ni²⁺ (red chelate).
Gravimetric factor = molar mass of analyte / molar mass of precipitate × stoichiometric ratio.

VOLUMETRIC ANALYSIS (TITRATIONS):

Acid-Base Titrations:
Strong acid + strong base: equivalence point at pH 7. Indicators: phenolphthalein (range 8.2-10.0) or methyl orange (3.1-4.4) work.
Strong acid + weak base: equivalence point pH < 7 (acidic hydrolysis). Methyl orange/red.
Weak acid + strong base: equivalence point pH > 7. Phenolphthalein.
Weak acid + weak base: no sharp endpoint. Potentiometric titration.

Indicator theory: HIn ⇌ H⁺ + In⁻ (color change at pKa ± 1).
Choice: equivalence point pH should be within indicator range.

REDOX TITRATIONS:
Permanganometry: KMnO₄ (oxidant). Self-indicating (purple → colorless).
MnO₄⁻ + 8H⁺ + 5e⁻ → Mn²⁺ + 4H₂O (acidic). Titrates: Fe²⁺, C₂O₄²⁻, H₂O₂, Sn²⁺.
Fe²⁺ titration: 5Fe²⁺ + MnO₄⁻ + 8H⁺ → 5Fe³⁺ + Mn²⁺ + 4H₂O.

Dichromatometry: K₂Cr₂O₇. External indicator (diphenylamine).
Cr₂O₇²⁻ + 14H⁺ + 6e⁻ → 2Cr³⁺ + 7H₂O. Titrates Fe²⁺.

Iodimetry (direct): Na₂S₂O₃ (sodium thiosulfate) titrates I₂ (starch indicator).
I₂ + 2S₂O₃²⁻ → 2I⁻ + S₄O₆²⁻.

Iodometry (indirect): analyte reacts with I⁻ to produce I₂, which is titrated with Na₂S₂O₃.
Cu²⁺ + I⁻ → CuI + I₂. Cl₂ + 2KI → 2KCl + I₂.

Cerimetry: Ce⁴⁺ as oxidant. Ce⁴⁺ + e⁻ → Ce³⁺ (E° = +1.72V in H₂SO₄).

COMPLEXOMETRIC TITRATIONS:
EDTA (ethylenediaminetetraacetic acid, H₄Y): hexadentate ligand, forms 1:1 complex with almost all metal ions.
M^n+ + Y⁴⁻ → [MY]^(n-4)
Indicators: Eriochrome Black T (EBT) — wine red with metals, blue free. pH 8-10 (NH₃ buffer).
pH adjustment: low pH → EDTA protonation (less reactive); high pH → metal hydroxide precipitation.

Hardness of water:
Total hardness (Ca²⁺ + Mg²⁺): titrate with EDTA at pH 10 (EBT indicator).
Calcium hardness: titrate with EDTA at pH 12 (murexide indicator, Mg(OH)₂ precipitates).
Mg hardness = total − calcium hardness.
Hardness expressed as mg/L CaCO₃ equivalent.

PRECIPITATION TITRATIONS:
Argentometry: AgNO₃ titrates halides.
Mohr method: Cl⁻ with AgNO₃, K₂CrO₄ indicator. Ag₂CrO₄ (red) precipitates at equivalence point. pH 6.5-9.
Volhard method: excess AgNO₃ added, back-titrate with KSCN. NH₄Fe(SO₄)₂ indicator (red [Fe(SCN)]²⁺ at endpoint). Used for Cl⁻, Br⁻, I⁻.
Fajans method: adsorption indicators (fluorescein for Cl⁻ with AgNO₃).

SPECTROPHOTOMETRIC ANALYSIS:
Beer-Lambert law: A = εcl.
ε (molar absorptivity): characteristic of substance, wavelength-dependent.
Calibration curve: plot A vs c for known concentrations → find c of unknown from A.
λmax: wavelength of maximum absorbance (use for best sensitivity).
Colorimetric reagents: form colored products with analyte.

CHROMATOGRAPHY:
Principle: differential distribution between stationary and mobile phases.
Rf = distance traveled by compound / distance traveled by solvent (TLC).
Resolution: ability to separate two adjacent peaks.

Types:
TLC (Thin Layer Chromatography): silica on glass/aluminum. Polarity-based.
Column chromatography: same principle as TLC, preparative scale.
GC (Gas Chromatography): gas mobile phase, separates volatile compounds.
HPLC (High Performance Liquid Chromatography): liquid mobile phase, high pressure.
Ion exchange: separates ions (cation/anion exchange resins).
Gel filtration (size exclusion): separates by molecular size.
Affinity chromatography: specific binding (enzyme-substrate, antibody-antigen).

PART 3: ENVIRONMENTAL CHEMISTRY

GREEN CHEMISTRY PRINCIPLES (12 Principles):
1. Prevention (waste prevention > treatment).
2. Atom economy: maximize incorporation of starting materials into product.
3. Less hazardous syntheses.
4. Designing safer chemicals.
5. Safer solvents and auxiliaries.
6. Design for energy efficiency.
7. Use of renewable feedstocks.
8. Reduce derivatives (protecting groups waste steps).
9. Catalysis (catalytic > stoichiometric reagents).
10. Design for degradation.
11. Real-time analysis for pollution prevention.
12. Inherently safer chemistry for accident prevention.

Atom economy = (MW of desired product / ΣMW of all products) × 100%.
E-factor = mass of waste / mass of product (lower is greener).

AIR POLLUTION:
Primary pollutants: directly emitted. CO, SO₂, NOₓ, hydrocarbons, particulates.
Secondary pollutants: formed in atmosphere. O₃, peroxyacetyl nitrate (PAN), H₂SO₄, HNO₃.

Photochemical smog: NOₓ + hydrocarbons + sunlight → O₃, PAN, aldehydes.
NO₂ + hν → NO + O (λ < 420 nm). O + O₂ + M → O₃.
O₃ causes: respiratory problems, crop damage, rubber degradation.

Acid rain: SO₂ + H₂O → H₂SO₃ → H₂SO₄. NOₓ + H₂O → HNO₃.
pH of normal rain: 5.6 (CO₂ dissolved). Acid rain: pH < 5.6.
Effects: fish kills, forest damage, corrosion of buildings (limestone: CaCO₃ + H₂SO₄ → CaSO₄ + H₂O + CO₂).

Carbon monoxide (CO): binds hemoglobin 230× stronger than O₂. Prevents O₂ transport.

GREENHOUSE EFFECT:
Greenhouse gases: CO₂, CH₄, N₂O, O₃, CFCs, water vapor.
IR radiation from Earth absorbed by greenhouse gases → atmosphere warms.
Global warming potential (GWP): relative warming effect vs CO₂ (= 1).
CH₄ GWP = 25, N₂O = 298, CFC-11 = 4750.
CO₂ sources: combustion, deforestation. CO₂ sinks: photosynthesis, oceans.
CO₂ in atmosphere: ~420 ppm currently (pre-industrial: 280 ppm).

OZONE DEPLETION:
Stratospheric ozone (15-35 km): protects against UV radiation.
Formation: O₂ + hν → 2O (UV), O + O₂ → O₃.
Destruction: O₃ + hν → O + O₂, O + O₃ → 2O₂ (natural).
CFC mechanism: CFCl₃ + hν → CFCl₂• + Cl•. Cl• + O₃ → ClO + O₂. ClO + O → Cl• + O₂. (Chain reaction!)
One Cl• destroys ~100,000 O₃ molecules.
Montreal Protocol (1987): banned CFCs. Replacements: HFCs, HCFCs.

WATER POLLUTION:
BOD (Biochemical Oxygen Demand): O₂ needed to decompose organic matter. Higher BOD = more polluted.
COD (Chemical Oxygen Demand): using chemical oxidant.
DO (Dissolved Oxygen): minimum 6 ppm for healthy aquatic life.
Eutrophication: excess nutrients (N, P) → algal bloom → O₂ depletion → fish kills.
Sources of nutrients: fertilizer runoff, sewage.

Heavy metal pollution: Pb, Hg, As, Cd, Cr are toxic even at ppb levels.
Mercury: methylation by bacteria → methylmercury (bioaccumulation in food chain).
Minamata disease: mercury poisoning.
Lead: damages nervous system, especially in children. From leaded paint, old plumbing.
Arsenic: natural contamination of groundwater in Bangladesh/India. Causes cancer.

SOIL CHEMISTRY:
pH affects nutrient availability. Optimal for most crops: 6.0-7.0.
Acidic soil: Al³⁺ and Mn²⁺ become soluble → toxic to plants.
Liming: CaCO₃ or Ca(OH)₂ raises pH. Also provides Ca²⁺.
Salinization: excess salt from irrigation → osmotic stress on plants.
Pesticides: chlorinated organics (DDT) persist (bioaccumulate). Organophosphates more biodegradable.

NUCLEAR CHEMISTRY IN ENVIRONMENT:
Radon (Rn-222): radioactive decay product of U-238. Accumulates in basements.
Causes lung cancer.
Half-life: 3.8 days. Comes from soil and rock.

Carbon-14 dating:
¹⁴N + n → ¹⁴C + H (in atmosphere by cosmic rays).
¹⁴C/¹²C ratio constant in living organisms.
After death: ¹⁴C decreases (t₁/₂ = 5730 years).
Age = (t₁/₂/ln2) × ln(A₀/A). Useful up to ~50,000 years.
