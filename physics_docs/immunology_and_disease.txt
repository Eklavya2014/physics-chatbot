1. THE IMMUNE SYSTEM — OVERVIEW
---------------------------------
The immune system defends the body against pathogens, cancerous cells, and foreign
substances. It has two main branches that work together:

Innate Immunity: Non-specific; immediate response (minutes to hours).
Adaptive Immunity: Specific; slower (days); has memory.

1.1 LINES OF DEFENCE
---------------------
First Line (Physical/Chemical barriers):
- Skin          : Mechanical barrier; low pH (acidic sebum); antimicrobial peptides.
- Mucous membranes: Trap pathogens; cilia sweep them out.
- Stomach acid  : pH 1–2 kills most ingested pathogens.
- Saliva, tears : Lysozyme enzyme breaks down bacterial cell walls.
- Normal microbiome: Competes with and suppresses pathogens.

Second Line (Innate Immune Response):
- Phagocytes    : Neutrophils (first responders); macrophages (tissue sentinels).
- Natural Killer cells: Kill virus-infected and tumour cells.
- Complement    : Protein cascade; opsonises bacteria; forms membrane attack complex.
- Inflammation  : Mast cells release histamine → vasodilation, increased permeability
                  → redness, heat, swelling, pain (cardinal signs).
- Fever         : Elevated temp inhibits pathogen growth; enhances immune response.

Third Line (Adaptive Immune Response):
- B cells       : Produce antibodies (humoral immunity).
- T cells       : Cell-mediated immunity; destroy infected cells.
- Memory cells  : Enable rapid response upon re-exposure.

2. INNATE IMMUNITY IN DEPTH
-----------------------------
Pattern Recognition Receptors (PRRs):
  Recognise conserved pathogen structures (PAMPs — pathogen-associated molecular patterns).
- Toll-like receptors (TLRs): On cell surface and endosomes.
  TLR4: Detects LPS (gram-negative bacteria) → NF-kB activation → cytokines.
  TLR3/7/8/9: Detect viral RNA/DNA → interferon production.
- NOD-like receptors (NLRs): Cytosolic; sense bacterial peptidoglycans.
  NLRP3 inflammasome: Senses danger signals → caspase-1 → IL-1beta, IL-18 → pyroptosis.
- RIG-I/MDA5: Cytosolic RNA helicases; detect viral dsRNA → type I interferon.

Complement System (3 pathways):
  Classical: Antibody-antigen complex activates C1q.
  Lectin: MBL binds mannose on pathogens.
  Alternative: Spontaneous C3 hydrolysis on pathogen surfaces.
  All converge: C3 convertase → C3b (opsonin) + C3a/C5a (anaphylatoxins) +
  C5b-9 (Membrane Attack Complex — punches holes in bacteria).

Inflammation — Molecular Mediators:
- Histamine (mast cells)    : Vasodilation, increased permeability.
- Prostaglandins (COX-1/2)  : Pain, fever, vasodilation. NSAIDs block COX.
- TNF-alpha, IL-1, IL-6     : Pro-inflammatory cytokines; systemic fever (pyrogens).
- IL-8 (CXCL8)              : Chemokine; recruits neutrophils to site of infection.
- IL-10                     : Anti-inflammatory; limits immune response.

3. ADAPTIVE IMMUNITY IN DEPTH
-------------------------------
Lymphocyte Development:
  All from haematopoietic stem cells (bone marrow) →
  B cells: mature in Bone marrow.
  T cells: mature in Thymus (positive selection: MHC recognition;
            negative selection: self-antigen deletion → prevents autoimmunity).

T Cell Activation (requires 3 signals):
  Signal 1: TCR binds MHC-peptide complex on APC.
  Signal 2: Co-stimulation — CD28 on T cell binds B7 (CD80/86) on APC.
            Without signal 2 → anergy (T cell inactivated).
  Signal 3: Cytokine environment determines T cell differentiation.

T Helper (CD4+) Subsets:
- Th1  : IFN-gamma → macrophage activation; intracellular pathogens (TB, viruses).
- Th2  : IL-4, IL-5, IL-13 → B cell activation; antibody class switching; parasites/allergy.
- Th17 : IL-17 → neutrophil recruitment; extracellular bacteria/fungi.
- Tregs: Foxp3+; IL-10, TGF-beta; suppress immune response; prevent autoimmunity.

Antibodies (Immunoglobulins):
  Structure: 2 heavy chains + 2 light chains; Fab (antigen binding) + Fc (effector).
  Classes and functions:
    IgM : Pentamer; first antibody in primary response; excellent complement activator.
    IgG : Most abundant; crosses placenta (neonatal protection); opsonisation.
    IgA : Dimer in secretions (gut, mucosa); prevents pathogen attachment.
    IgE : Binds mast cells/basophils; allergic response; antiparasitic.
    IgD : B cell surface receptor; function not fully understood.

  Antibody effector functions:
  - Neutralisation: Block pathogen binding to host cells.
  - Opsonisation: Fc receptors on phagocytes recognise antibody-coated pathogens.
  - Complement activation (IgM, IgG): Classical pathway → MAC formation.
  - ADCC: NK cells kill antibody-coated target cells via Fc receptor.

4. VACCINES
------------
Type                | Mechanism                         | Examples
--------------------|-----------------------------------|---------------------------
Live-attenuated     | Weakened pathogen; strong response | MMR, chickenpox, yellow fever
Inactivated         | Killed pathogen; safe, less potent | Flu shot, polio (Salk), Hep A
Subunit/protein     | Purified antigen; very safe        | Hep B, pertussis, HPV (VLP)
Toxoid              | Inactivated toxin                  | Tetanus, diphtheria
mRNA                | mRNA encodes antigen; novel; rapid | COVID-19 (Pfizer, Moderna)
Viral vector        | Adenovirus delivers antigen gene  | COVID-19 (AstraZeneca, J&J)

Herd immunity threshold: Percentage of population that must be immune to prevent
outbreak spread. Depends on R0 (basic reproduction number).
Measles R0 ~15 → need ~93% immunity. COVID-19 R0 ~2–3 → ~50–67%.

5. IMMUNOLOGICAL TOLERANCE & AUTOIMMUNITY
-------------------------------------------
Central Tolerance:
  T cells in thymus undergo negative selection against self-antigens presented
  by medullary thymic epithelial cells (mTECs) — driven by AIRE (Autoimmune
  Regulator) transcription factor. Failure → APECED syndrome.
  B cells in bone marrow undergo receptor editing or clonal deletion.

Peripheral Tolerance:
  Anergy, regulatory T cell suppression, and activation-induced cell death (AICD)
  prevent self-reactive lymphocytes that escape central tolerance from causing damage.

Autoimmune Diseases — Mechanisms:
  Molecular mimicry: Pathogen antigen resembles self-antigen → cross-reactive immune
  response. Example: Strep pyogenes M protein mimics cardiac myosin → rheumatic fever.
  Bystander activation: Tissue damage releases self-antigens → APC activation
  without infection.
  Epitope spreading: Initial response to one antigen → spreads to others.

Selected Autoimmune Conditions:

------------------------|----------------------------------|----------------------

6. TUMOUR IMMUNOLOGY & CANCER IMMUNOTHERAPY
---------------------------------------------
Cancer Immunoediting (3 phases):
  Elimination → Equilibrium → Escape
  Immune escape mechanisms: MHC I downregulation; PD-L1 upregulation;
  immunosuppressive TME (regulatory T cells, MDSCs, TGF-beta, IL-10).

Checkpoint Inhibitors:
  CTLA-4 (on T cells) binds B7 → inhibits activation. Anti-CTLA-4: ipilimumab.
  PD-1 (on T cells) binds PD-L1/PD-L2 → T cell exhaustion. Anti-PD-1: nivolumab,
  pembrolizumab. Anti-PD-L1: atezolizumab.
  Combination ipilimumab + nivolumab: significantly improves melanoma survival.

CAR-T Cell Therapy:
  Patient's T cells extracted → genetically engineered to express chimeric antigen
  receptor (CAR) specific for tumour antigen (e.g., CD19 in B cell leukaemia) →
  expanded → infused back.
  Approved: tisagenlecleucel (ALL), axicabtagene ciloleucel (DLBCL).
  Challenges: cytokine release syndrome (CRS), neurotoxicity, antigen escape.

Bispecific Antibodies:
  Blinatumomab (anti-CD19 x anti-CD3): Brings T cells into proximity with
  B cell tumours → serial killing. Approved for ALL.

Tumour Microenvironment (TME):
  Immunosuppressive cells: Tumour-associated macrophages (TAMs, M2 phenotype),
  MDSCs, Tregs. Immunostimulatory: CD8+ TILs (tumour-infiltrating lymphocytes),
  NK cells, M1 macrophages. "Hot" (inflamed) vs "cold" (non-inflamed) tumours
  determine checkpoint inhibitor responsiveness.

[CLINICAL LINK] Sepsis is life-threatening immune dysregulation (hyperinflammation
followed by immunosuppression). Pathogenesis: PAMPs/DAMPs → systemic cytokine storm
→ endothelial damage → coagulopathy → multi-organ failure. Treatment: source control,
antibiotics, IV fluids, vasopressors (norepinephrine). Surviving Sepsis guidelines.
Mortality: ~15–30% for sepsis; ~40–60% for septic shock.
