1. BIOLOGICAL CLOCKS
---------------------
Circadian rhythms are internal biological oscillations with a ~24-hour period
that persist without external time cues. Present in virtually all life forms
studied — from cyanobacteria to humans.
Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine 2017: Jeffrey Hall, Michael Rosbash,
Michael Young — for discoveries of the molecular mechanisms of circadian rhythms.

Properties of Circadian Rhythms:
  1. Endogenous: Self-sustaining ~24h rhythm in constant conditions.
  2. Entrainable: Can be synchronised (entrained) by external cues (zeitgebers).
  3. Temperature-compensated: Period remains ~24h across physiological temperatures.

Zeitgebers (time-givers):
  Light: Strongest zeitgeber. Detected by eyes (ipRGCs); resets SCN.
  Food timing: Entrains peripheral clocks (liver, intestine) without resetting SCN.
  Temperature, social cues, exercise: Weaker zeitgebers.

1.1 BIOLOGICAL RHYTHMS
------------------------
Type          | Period       | Examples
--------------|--------------|---------------------------------------------------
Ultradian     | <24 hours    | REM/NREM sleep cycles (~90 min), heartbeat

2. MOLECULAR CLOCK MECHANISM (MAMMALIAN)
------------------------------------------
The molecular clock is a transcription-translation feedback loop (TTFL):

Positive limb:
  CLOCK + BMAL1 heterodimerize → bind E-box elements (CACGTG) in promoters →
  activate transcription of Per1, Per2, Per3, Cry1, Cry2.

Negative limb:
  PER and CRY proteins accumulate → form PER/CRY complex (with CK1-epsilon/delta
  phosphorylation regulating nuclear entry timing) → enter nucleus → inhibit
  CLOCK/BMAL1 → their own transcription suppressed → levels fall → cycle restarts.
  Period: ~24 hours.

Stabilising loop:
  CLOCK/BMAL1 also activates REV-ERB alpha/beta (repressors) and ROR alpha/beta/gamma.
  REV-ERB competes with ROR for RORE elements in Bmal1 promoter:
    REV-ERB: Represses Bmal1 → negative.
    ROR: Activates Bmal1 → positive.
  This reinforces oscillation robustness.

Post-translational regulation:
  CK1-epsilon/delta: Phosphorylate PER → nuclear entry + degradation (SCF-beta-TrCP).
  CK2: Phosphorylates BMAL1 → nuclear retention.
  FBXL3: E3 ligase ubiquitinates CRY → proteasomal degradation.
  Tau mutation (CK1-epsilon): Short period (~20h) in hamsters → discovered period
  length is regulated by PER degradation rate.

3. PHYSIOLOGICAL OUTPUTS OF THE CLOCK
----------------------------------------
The SCN coordinates peripheral clocks in every organ via:
  - Neural signals (sympathetic/parasympathetic nervous system)
  - Hormonal signals (cortisol, melatonin, growth hormone)
  - Temperature rhythms (core body temperature oscillates ~0.5°C over 24h)
  - Feeding-fasting cycles

Melatonin:
  Produced by pineal gland at night (in all light conditions).
  Synthesis: Tryptophan → serotonin → N-acetylserotonin → melatonin.
  Regulated by SCN via NE from superior cervical ganglion → beta-adrenergic receptor.
  Functions: Darkness signal to body; regulates seasonal reproduction in photoperiodic
  animals; antioxidant; mild chronobiotic (can shift circadian phase).
  Melatonin receptor agonists: Ramelteon (insomnia); tasimelteon (non-24h sleep disorder
  in blind).

Cortisol:
  Peaks at dawn (~8am); prepares body for day — gluconeogenesis, alertness.
  Regulated by circadian ACTH release plus glucocorticoid receptor feedback.
  Clock-disruption → cortisol dysrhythmia → metabolic consequences.

Immune Function:
  Many immune processes are circadian: NK cell activity peaks at night;
  adaptive immune responses stronger in afternoon; T cell trafficking peaks
  during rest phase; inflammatory cytokines oscillate.
  Vaccination time may matter: Flu vaccines in morning (AM) → higher Ab titres in some studies.

4. CIRCADIAN CLOCKS IN PERIPHERAL ORGANS
------------------------------------------
Every nucleated cell has a self-sustaining circadian clock. Estimated >40% of
all protein-coding genes are rhythmically expressed in at least one tissue.

Liver clock:
  Controls: Glucose metabolism (gluconeogenesis rhythmic), lipid metabolism
  (bile acid synthesis oscillates), drug metabolism (CYP450 enzymes rhythmic →
  pharmacokinetics of many drugs depend on dosing time — chronopharmacology).
  Entrained primarily by feeding time, not light.

Gut clock:
  Intestinal barrier function circadian: Tight junction proteins (claudins, occludin)
  oscillate → permeability higher at rest; gut microbiome rhythmic (controlled by
  host feeding + clock gene expression in enterocytes).

Cardiac clock:
  Heart rate, blood pressure, stroke volume — all circadian.
  Myocardial infarction peaks in morning (6am–12pm): Heart rate variability,
  platelet aggregability, plaque rupture risk, catecholamine surges all peak AM.
  BMAL1 cardiac KO mice: Dilated cardiomyopathy.

Circadian Clock & Cancer:
  Many tumours show disrupted clock gene expression. Loss of clock oscillation
  associated with worse prognosis in several cancers.
  Clock genes as tumour suppressors: Per2 KO mice develop haematopoietic tumours faster.
  Chronotherapy: Timing chemotherapy to maximise efficacy and minimise toxicity.
  Oxaliplatin: Greater cytotoxicity and lower toxicity at peak DPYD (pyrimidine
  degradation) activity (late afternoon). SWOG trial showed survival benefit for
  optimally timed chronotherapy in mCRC (controversial; ongoing trials).

5. CIRCADIAN DISRUPTION & DISEASE
------------------------------------
Shift Work:
  ~20% of workers in developed nations. Circadian misalignment between body
  clock and external environment → increased risk:
  - Type 2 diabetes (~40% increased risk)
  - Obesity and metabolic syndrome
  - Cardiovascular disease (~20% increased CVD risk)
  - Breast and colorectal cancer (IARC Group 2A: "probably carcinogenic")
  - Depression, anxiety, cognitive impairment
  Mechanism: Uncoupling of SCN (light-entrained) from peripheral clocks
  (food-entrained) → metabolic and inflammatory dysregulation.

Social Jet Lag:
  Discrepancy between biological sleep timing and social/work schedule.
  Average ~1.5–2 hours of social jet lag in working-age adults.
  Associated with obesity, T2DM, cardiovascular risk even in non-shift workers.

Seasonal Affective Disorder (SAD):
  Depressive episodes correlating with short days/long nights (winter).
  Mechanism: Delayed circadian phase + altered melatonin profile.
  Treatment: Light therapy (10,000 lux for 30 min on waking) → phase advance;
  also agomelatine (melatonin agonist + 5-HT2C antagonist), SSRIs.

Familial Advanced Sleep Phase Syndrome (FASPS):
  Extreme early chronotype (sleep 7:30pm–4:30am). Autosomal dominant.
  Mutations: CK1-delta kinase → slower PER2 degradation → short tau →
  earlier circadian phase. PER2 S662G mutation directly (casein kinase I site).

6. COMPARATIVE CIRCADIAN BIOLOGY
-----------------------------------
Cyanobacteria (Synechococcus elongatus):
  Simplest known circadian clock: Three proteins — KaiA, KaiB, KaiC.
  KaiC: Phosphorylated (by itself + KaiA) then dephosphorylated (~24h cycle).
  Remarkable: Reconstituted in test tube with just KaiA + KaiB + KaiC + ATP → maintains
  ~24h oscillation. First circadian clock reconstituted in vitro.

Drosophila melanogaster Clock:
  Key proteins: CLK, CYC (CLOCK/BMAL1 homologs), PER, TIM, DBT (CK1-epsilon homolog).
  Light: Cryptochrome (dCRY) binds TIM in light → rapid TIM degradation → phase reset.
  (Mammalian CRY is not a photoreceptor; Drosophila TIM has no mammalian homolog.)
  Pacemaker neurons: Small LNv (sLNv) neurons express PDF neuropeptide → coordinate
  rest of circadian neuronal network.

[MEDICAL APPLICATION] Circadian medicine:
  Time-of-day-dependent drug efficacy is well documented:
  - ACE inhibitors/ARBs: Better BP control taken at bedtime (Hygia Chronotherapy Trial).
  - Statins: Cholesterol synthesis peaks at night → simvastatin most effective at night.
  - Aspirin: Aspirin given at bedtime → better platelet aggregation inhibition.
  - Corticosteroids: Timed to match cortisol peak to reduce HPA suppression.
  - Immunotherapy: Early clinical evidence that timing PD-1 blockade influences response rates.
