1. WHAT IS BIOPHYSICS?
-----------------------
Biophysics applies the principles of physics and mathematics to understand
biological systems. It bridges biology, chemistry, physics, and mathematics
to explain how living systems work at the molecular, cellular, and organismal level.

Key Areas:
- Structural biology   : How molecular structures determine function.
- Membrane biophysics  : Physics of lipid bilayers and ion transport.
- Motor proteins       : Mechanical work at molecular scales.
- Neurophysics         : Electrical properties of neurons.
- Medical imaging      : MRI, PET, X-ray — physics in diagnostics.
- Biomechanics         : Forces and motion in biological systems.

1.1 PHYSICAL PROPERTIES OF BIOLOGICAL MOLECULES
-------------------------------------------------
Water as a Biological Solvent:
  Polarity (bent molecule; O is electronegative) → hydrogen bonding.
  High specific heat capacity (4.18 J/g/°C) → buffers temperature changes.
  High heat of vaporisation → evaporative cooling (sweating).
  Cohesion and adhesion → capillary action in plants.
  Water is densest at 4°C → ice floats → aquatic life survives under ice.
  Universal solvent: Dissolves polar/ionic molecules; excludes nonpolar (hydrophobic effect).

pH and Buffers:
  pH = -log[H+]. Neutral = 7. Acidic < 7. Basic > 7.
  Buffer: Resists pH changes; consists of weak acid + its conjugate base.
  Henderson-Hasselbalch: pH = pKa + log([A-]/[HA])
  Biological buffers: Bicarbonate (blood), phosphate (intracellular), proteins (histidine).

Diffusion:
  Fick's First Law: J = -D(dC/dx)
  J = flux; D = diffusion coefficient; dC/dx = concentration gradient.
  Diffusion time scales as distance squared: t ~ x²/2D
  Small molecules (O2, CO2) diffuse rapidly over short distances (~µm).
  Limits cell size: Diffusion becomes too slow beyond ~100 µm.

2. MEMBRANE BIOPHYSICS
-----------------------
Lipid Bilayer Physical Properties:
  Thickness: ~5 nm. Capacitance: ~1 µF/cm². Resistance: very high (~10^9 Ω/cm²).
  Fluid mosaic model: Lateral diffusion of lipids (~1 µm²/s); flip-flop rare.
  Phase transition temperature (Tm): Gel phase (ordered) → liquid crystalline (disordered).
  Cholesterol: Broadens transition; maintains fluidity at low temp; reduces at high temp.
  Lipid rafts: Liquid-ordered microdomains enriched in cholesterol + sphingomyelin;
  concentrate signalling proteins (GPI-anchored proteins, src-family kinases).

Goldman-Hodgkin-Katz (GHK) Equation:
  Describes membrane potential when multiple ions are permeable:
  Vm = (RT/F) × ln[(PK[K+]o + PNa[Na+]o + PCl[Cl-]i) /
                    (PK[K+]i + PNa[Na+]i + PCl[Cl-]o)]
  At rest: PK >> PNa, PCl → Vm ≈ -70 mV.
  During AP: PNa >> PK → Vm approaches +55 mV (Na+ Nernst potential).

Nernst Equation (single ion):
  Ex = (RT/zF) × ln([X]o/[X]i)
  For K+ at 37°C: EK = -94 mV; ENa = +60 mV; ECa = +123 mV; ECl = -65 mV.

3. PROTEIN STRUCTURE & BIOPHYSICS
-----------------------------------
Protein Folding:
  Thermodynamics: Folding driven by hydrophobic effect (burial of nonpolar residues)
  + van der Waals, H-bonds, ionic interactions, disulphide bonds.
  Energy landscape: "Funnel" model — native state is global free energy minimum.
  Levinthal's paradox: Random search of conformational space would take >age of universe;
  proteins fold in milliseconds via pathways through the energy landscape.

Molecular Chaperones:
  Assist protein folding; prevent aggregation.
  HSP70/BiP: Bind exposed hydrophobic patches on unfolded proteins.
  GroEL/GroES (Hsp60): Barrel-shaped; provide protected folding chamber.
  HSP90: Stabilises signalling proteins (kinases, steroid receptors).
  Stress response: Heat, heavy metals, oxidative stress → chaperone upregulation.

Protein Misfolding & Aggregation:
  Amyloid fibrils: Cross-beta sheet structure; extremely stable.
  Diseases: Alzheimer's (Abeta42, tau), Parkinson's (alpha-synuclein), Huntington's
  (polyglutamine), Prion diseases (PrPsc), Type 2 diabetes (IAPP), ALS (SOD1, TDP-43).
  Mechanisms: Nucleation-polymerisation → seeding → propagation (prion-like spread).

4. SINGLE MOLECULE BIOPHYSICS
-------------------------------
Optical Tweezers:
  Focused laser beam traps and manipulates individual molecules/particles.
  Measures forces in pico-Newton range (1 pN = 10^-12 N).
  Applications: Measuring force generated by kinesin (~5 pN); RNA polymerase
  translocation; DNA mechanical properties; protein unfolding.

Atomic Force Microscopy (AFM):
  Nanoscale probe scans surface → measures interaction forces.
  Images single proteins, DNA, cell surfaces at nm resolution.
  Force spectroscopy: Stretches single proteins → measures unfolding pathways.

FRET (Förster Resonance Energy Transfer):
  Energy transfer between donor and acceptor fluorophores (1–10 nm range).
  Efficiency: E = 1/(1 + (R/R0)^6) — extremely sensitive to distance.
  Applications: Conformational changes in proteins; RNA folding; protein-protein interactions.

5. STRUCTURAL BIOLOGY TECHNIQUES
----------------------------------
X-ray Crystallography:
  Protein crystals diffract X-rays → diffraction pattern → electron density map
  via Fourier transform (phase problem solved by MAD, SAD, or molecular replacement).
  Resolution: 1–3 Å for most structures. >100,000 structures in PDB (Protein Data Bank).
  Limitations: Requires crystal formation; static snapshot; some proteins resist crystallisation.

Cryo-Electron Microscopy (Cryo-EM):
  Proteins flash-frozen in vitreous ice → imaged by electron beam → thousands of
  2D particle images → computationally reconstructed into 3D map.
  Resolution revolution (2013): Improved detectors + software → near-atomic resolution.
  Advantages: No crystallisation; captures multiple conformational states; large complexes.
  Nobel Prize 2017: Henderson, Frank, Dubochet.
  Current record: ~1.2 Å resolution (apoferritin, 2020).

NMR Spectroscopy:
  Atomic nuclei in magnetic field resonate at characteristic frequencies.
  Chemical shifts, NOEs (nuclear Overhauser effects), J-couplings → distance and
  dihedral angle restraints → structure calculation.
  Advantage: Solution state; protein dynamics; H-bond detection.
  Limitation: Size limit ~50 kDa for standard NMR; TROSY extends to ~1 MDa.

AlphaFold2 (DeepMind, 2021):
  AI system predicts 3D protein structure from amino acid sequence with near-
  experimental accuracy. Uses multiple sequence alignments + attention-based
  neural network (Evoformer + Structure Module). pLDDT confidence score (0–100).
  AlphaFold Database: 200 million+ predicted structures. Revolutionised structural biology.
  AlphaFold3 (2024): Extended to protein-DNA, protein-RNA, protein-ligand interactions.

6. MOLECULAR MOTORS
--------------------
Myosin (Class II):
  Walks along actin filaments. Two-headed; power stroke ~5 nm; force ~3–5 pN.
  ATPase cycle: Detached (ATP bound) → attached-pre-power stroke (ADP+Pi) →
  power stroke (Pi release) → rigor (ADP release) → ATP binds → detaches.
  Processivity: Non-processive (skeletal myosin); processive (myosin V, VI).

Kinesin-1:
  Walks along microtubules toward (+) end (anterograde axonal transport).
  Hand-over-hand mechanism: 8 nm steps. Force: ~5–7 pN. Processivity: ~1 µm before detachment.

Dynein:
  Walks toward (-) end of microtubules (retrograde transport; also cilia/flagella).
  Largest cytoplasmic motor; regulated by dynactin complex.
  Ciliary dynein: Outer and inner arm dyneins power ciliary beat in 9+2 axoneme.

ATP Synthase (F0F1):
  Rotary molecular motor. F0 (membrane): c-ring rotates driven by proton gradient.
  F1 (catalytic): Gamma subunit rotation drives conformational changes in beta subunits
  → ATP synthesis (binding change mechanism; Boyer). 3 ATP per 360° rotation;
  3 H+ per ATP in mitochondria. Rotation speed: ~100–150 revolutions/second.
  Nobel Prize: Boyer and Walker (1997).

[CLINICAL LINK] Kinesin/dynein defects:
  KIF1B mutations → Charcot-Marie-Tooth disease (peripheral neuropathy).
  Dynein/dynactin mutations → ALS, Perry syndrome, motor neuron disease.
  Primary ciliary dyskinesia (PCD): Dynein arm mutations → immotile cilia →
  bronchiectasis, situs inversus (Kartagener syndrome), infertility.
