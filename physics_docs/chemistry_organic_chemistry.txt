1. ORGANIC CHEMISTRY FUNDAMENTALS
-----------------------------------
Organic chemistry: Study of carbon-containing compounds (with exceptions: CO, CO₂, carbonates, carbides treated as inorganic).
~10 million known organic compounds. Carbon's unique properties:
  - Tetravalent (4 bonds); sp³, sp², sp hybridisation.
  - C-C bonds strong (347 kJ/mol) and numerous (chains/rings possible).
  - Can form single, double, triple bonds.
  - Similar EN to H, N, O → forms strong covalent bonds with all.
  - Small size → close approach → effective orbital overlap.

Homologous Series: Compounds with same general formula and functional group, differing by CH₂.
  Alkanes: CₙH₂ₙ₊₂. Alkenes: CₙH₂ₙ. Alkynes: CₙH₂ₙ₋₂. Alcohols: CₙH₂ₙ₊₁OH.
  Each successive member in a series has similar chemical properties but slightly different physical.

IUPAC Nomenclature:
  1. Find longest carbon chain containing principal functional group → root name.
  2. Number chain from end giving principal group LOWEST number.
  3. Add prefixes (substituents): methyl-, ethyl-, bromo-, chloro-, hydroxy-, etc.
  4. Add suffix for principal functional group.
  5. Alphabetical order for multiple substituents; di-, tri-, etc. for identical groups (ignored in alphabetical order).

1.2 ISOMERISM — BASIC
-----------------------
Isomers: Same molecular formula; different structural arrangement.
Constitutional (structural) isomers: Different connectivity.
  Chain isomers: Different carbon skeleton (butane vs isobutane = methylpropane).
  Position isomers: Same functional group at different positions (butan-1-ol vs butan-2-ol).
  Functional group isomers: Different functional groups (ethanol C₂H₅OH vs methoxyethane CH₃OCH₃).

2. STEREOISOMERISM
-------------------
Stereoisomers: Same connectivity; different spatial arrangement.
Geometric (cis-trans) isomerism:
  Requires: Double bond (C=C) OR ring (no free rotation). Two different groups on each carbon.
  cis: Same groups on same side. trans: Same groups on opposite sides.
  E/Z nomenclature (more systematic): Priority rules (CIP) determine E (entgegen = opposite) or Z (zusammen = together) based on atomic number priority.
  Affects physical properties (dipole moments, melting points, boiling points).

Optical Isomerism (Chirality):
  Chiral centre (stereocentre): Carbon bonded to 4 different groups → non-superimposable mirror image.
  Enantiomers: Mirror image isomers. Same physical properties except rotation of plane-polarised light.
  Dextrorotatory (+): Rotates plane right. Levorotatory (-): Rotates plane left.
  Racemic mixture: Equal amounts of both enantiomers → zero net rotation.
  R/S configuration (CIP): Priority order of 4 groups; R = clockwise, S = anticlockwise (lowest priority pointing away).
  Diastereomers: Non-mirror-image stereoisomers (≥2 stereocentres).
  Meso compound: Has stereocentre(s) but is achiral (internal plane of symmetry).

Importance in biology and medicine:
  Thalidomide: R-enantiomer = sedative; S-enantiomer = teratogenic. Racemic mixture → harm.
  Drug chirality: >50% top pharmaceuticals chiral. Single enantiomer often preferred.
  Enzymes are stereospecific: Only D-sugars and L-amino acids found in living organisms.

3. KEY REACTIONS BY FUNCTIONAL GROUP
--------------------------------------
ALKANES (CₙH₂ₙ₊₂):
  Free radical substitution: CH₄ + Cl₂ → CH₃Cl + HCl (UV light initiation).
    Mechanism: Initiation (Cl₂ → 2Cl•); Propagation (Cl• + CH₄ → HCl + CH₃•; CH₃• + Cl₂ → CH₃Cl + Cl•); Termination (radical + radical → product).
  Combustion: CH₄ + 2O₂ → CO₂ + 2H₂O (complete). Incomplete → CO + H₂O.
  Cracking: Long-chain alkanes → shorter alkanes + alkenes (thermal or catalytic; ZSM-5 zeolite at 500°C).

ALKENES (CₙH₂ₙ):
  Electrophilic addition: C=C acts as nucleophile (high electron density) → attacked by electrophiles.
  + HX (Markovnikov's Rule): H adds to C with more H's; X to C with fewer H's (carbocation intermediate; more substituted = more stable = major product).
    Mechanism: H⁺ (electrophile) attacks → carbocation intermediate → X⁻ attacks → product.
    Regiospecific, not stereospecific.
  + Br₂ (halogenation): Both carbons get Br. In CCl₄ — orange bromine decolorises (test for C=C).
    Mechanism: Bromonium ion intermediate → anti addition (trans product).
  + H₂O (acid-catalysed) → alcohol: H₂SO₄/H₃PO₄ + heat → hydration → alcohol.
  + H₂ (hydrogenation): Ni/Pd/Pt catalyst → alkane. Reduces degree of unsaturation.
  Polymerisation: nCH₂=CH₂ → (-CH₂-CH₂-)ₙ (polyethylene/HDPE/LDPE). Addition polymerisation.

ALKYNES (CₙH₂ₙ₋₂):
  Similar to alkenes but 2 additions possible.
  Terminal alkynes: RC≡CH — acidic H (pKa ~25); react with strong bases; form metal acetylides.
  Reduction → alkene (Lindlar's catalyst → cis-alkene) or alkane.

HALOALKANES (R-X):
  Nucleophilic Substitution:
    SN2: Single step; bimolecular; nucleophile attacks from back → Walden inversion; primary R-X preferred.
      Rate = k[R-X][Nu]. Favoured by: Primary substrate; strong nucleophile; polar aprotic solvent (DMSO, DMF, acetone).
    SN1: Two steps; unimolecular; carbocation intermediate; tertiary R-X preferred.
      Rate = k[R-X] only. Favoured by: Tertiary substrate; weak nucleophile; polar protic solvent (water, ethanol). Racemisation (flat carbocation attacked from both sides).
  Elimination:
    E2: Bimolecular; base removes H from β-carbon while X leaves → alkene. Anti-periplanar geometry required (H and X anti). Favoured by: Strong bulky base (KOH in ethanol); tertiary substrate; high temp.
    E1: Carbocation intermediate → H⁺ loss → alkene. Slower than E2; same conditions as SN1 competitive.
    Zaitsev's rule: More substituted alkene = major product (thermodynamic control).

ALCOHOLS (R-OH):
  Oxidation: Primary → aldehyde (with K₂Cr₂O₇/H₂SO₄ limited, or PCC) or carboxylic acid (excess oxidant).
    Secondary → ketone (don't oxidise further easily). Tertiary → no oxidation.
    Test: Dichromate orange → green (Cr³⁺) if primary or secondary alcohol.
  Esterification: Carboxylic acid + alcohol → ester + H₂O (H₂SO₄ catalyst; reversible).
    Fischer esterification: CH₃COOH + C₂H₅OH ⇌ CH₃COOC₂H₅ + H₂O.
  Dehydration: R-OH + H₂SO₄/H₃PO₄, heat → alkene (E2; Zaitsev's rule for regiochemistry).
  Reaction with Na: 2ROH + 2Na → 2RONa + H₂ (gentler than water + Na; alkoxides formed).

CARBONYL COMPOUNDS (RCHO and RCOR'):
  Nucleophilic addition: Nu⁻ attacks electrophilic C of C=O.
  + NaBH₄/LiAlH₄ → alcohol (reduction: aldehyde → 1° alcohol; ketone → 2° alcohol).
  + HCN → cyanohydrin (then → amino acids; hydroxy acids).
  + Grignard (RMgX) → alcohol (1° from HCHO; 2° from RCHO; 3° from ketone).
  Aldehyde-specific:
    Tollens' test (silver mirror): RCHO + [Ag(NH₃)₂]⁺ → Ag↓ + RCOO⁻ (oxidation; silver mirror forms → positive for aldehydes; ketones negative).
    Fehling's test: RCHO + Cu²⁺ (blue) → Cu₂O (red precipitate) → positive for aldehydes.
    Iodoform (triiodomethane) test: Methyl ketones (CH₃CO-) + I₂/NaOH → CHI₃ (yellow) → positive for CH₃CO- group.

CARBOXYLIC ACIDS (RCOOH):
  Weak acid: RCOOH ⇌ RCOO⁻ + H⁺. Ka varies; pKa typically 4-5.
  Esterification (see above). Acid chloride formation: + SOCl₂ or PCl₅ → RCOCl.
  Amide formation: + NH₃ → ammonium salt; then heat → amide -CONH₂.
  Reduction: + LiAlH₄ → 1° alcohol (directly reduces COOH to CH₂OH).
  Decarboxylation: β-keto acids → CO₂ + ketone (biochemistry: metabolic intermediates).

4. POLYMERS
------------
Addition Polymers (free radical or ionic):
  Monomer = alkene. Polymer = long-chain saturated compound.
  Polythene (HDPE/LDPE): Ziegler-Natta catalysts (TiCl₄/AlR₃) → stereoregular polymers.
  Polypropylene (PP): Isotactic (all CH₃ same side) → strong, stiff.
  PVC: Polyvinyl chloride; rigid or plasticised (phthalate plasticisers).
  Polystyrene: C₈H₈ monomer; clear, rigid; expandable (EPS, Styrofoam).
  PTFE: Polytetrafluoroethylene (Teflon); non-stick; inert.
  PMMA: Polymethylmethacrylate (Perspex, Plexiglas); clear; shatter-resistant.

Condensation Polymers:
  Monomer molecules condense → small molecule lost (usually H₂O).
  Nylon-6,6: Diamine + dicarboxylic acid → amide bonds (-CONH-). Nylon rope trick.
  Polyester (Dacron/Terylene, PET): Diol + dicarboxylic acid → ester bonds. Also PET bottles.
  Polylactic acid (PLA): Biodegradable polyester from lactic acid; bioplastic.
  Polyamides, polyurethanes, polycarbonates: All condensation.

5. AROMATIC CHEMISTRY
-----------------------
Electrophilic Aromatic Substitution (EAS): Main reaction of benzene ring.
  Benzene defends aromaticity → prefers substitution (keep ring) over addition (lose ring).
  General mechanism: Electrophile E⁺ attacks ring → sigma complex (arenium ion, Wheland intermediate → cyclohexadienyl cation) → H⁺ lost → re-aromatisation.

EAS Reactions of Benzene:
  Nitration: C₆H₆ + HNO₃/H₂SO₄ → C₆H₅NO₂ + H₂O. (Electrophile = NO₂⁺ nitronium ion; H₂SO₄ protonates HNO₃).
  Sulfonation: C₆H₆ + SO₃/H₂SO₄ → C₆H₅SO₃H (benzenesulphonic acid). Reversible (reflux with steam → desulfonation).
  Halogenation: C₆H₆ + Cl₂/AlCl₃ → C₆H₅Cl + HCl. (AlCl₃ Lewis acid catalyst generates Cl⁺ from Cl₂).
  Friedel-Crafts Alkylation: C₆H₆ + RCl/AlCl₃ → C₆H₅R + HCl. Problem: polyalkylation; carbocation rearrangements.
  Friedel-Crafts Acylation: C₆H₆ + RCOCl/AlCl₃ → C₆H₅COR + HCl. No rearrangement (acylium ion R-C≡O⁺ stable); product deactivated → no polyacylation.

Directing Effects on Substituted Benzene:
  Activating, ortho/para directors: electron-donating groups (OH, NH₂, OR, NR₂, alkyl).
    Donate electrons to ring → ring enriched → faster EAS → prefer ortho and para.
  Deactivating, meta directors: electron-withdrawing groups (NO₂, CN, COOH, CHO, COR, SO₃H).
    Withdraw electrons from ring → ring depleted → slower EAS → prefer meta.
  Deactivating, ortho/para directors: Halogens (F, Cl, Br, I) — withdraw by induction, donate by resonance → net deactivation but ortho/para substitution.

Nucleophilic Aromatic Substitution (NAS):
  Requires: Electron-withdrawing groups on ring (especially at ortho/para to leaving group).
  Meisenheimer complex: Anionic intermediate; rate-determining step = addition (not departure).
  Example: 2,4-dinitrochlorobenzene + OH⁻ → 2,4-dinitrophenol.

7. SPECTROSCOPY FOR STRUCTURE ELUCIDATION
-------------------------------------------
Mass Spectrometry (MS):
  M⁺ (molecular ion): m/z = molecular mass. Fragmentation pattern → structure.
  Base peak: Most abundant fragment ion. High-resolution MS → exact molecular formula.

Infrared Spectroscopy (IR):
  Functional group identification. Characteristic absorption frequencies:
  O-H stretch (alcohol): 3200–3550 cm⁻¹ (broad). O-H stretch (carboxylic acid): 2500–3300 cm⁻¹ (very broad).
  N-H stretch: 3300–3500 cm⁻¹. C-H stretch: 2850–3100 cm⁻¹. C=O stretch (carbonyl): 1680–1750 cm⁻¹.
  C≡N: 2200–2260 cm⁻¹. C=C (alkene): 1620–1680 cm⁻¹. C-O stretch: 1000–1300 cm⁻¹.
  Fingerprint region: 500–1500 cm⁻¹ — unique to each compound; difficult to interpret analytically.

¹H NMR Spectroscopy:
  Chemical shift (δ, ppm): Position of peak; related to electron density around H.
    TMS = 0 ppm reference. High δ = deshielded (electronegative neighbours).
    H environments: Alkyl H ~0–2 ppm; allylic/propargylic ~1.5–2.5; adjacent to O/N ~2–4;
    vinyl H ~4–6; aromatic H ~6–9; aldehyde H ~9–11; carboxylic OH ~11–12.
  Splitting (multiplicity): Due to coupling with adjacent non-equivalent H.
    n+1 rule: n adjacent H atoms → n+1 peaks (for first-order spectra).
    Singlet (s), doublet (d), triplet (t), quartet (q), multiplet (m).
    Coupling constant J (Hz): Measure of coupling strength; vicinal (³J) ~3–12 Hz.
  Integration: Area of peak proportional to number of equivalent H atoms.

¹³C NMR: All C environments. Not routinely split (broadband decoupling). ~0–220 ppm.
  DEPT: Distinguishes CH₃ (methyl), CH₂ (methylene), CH (methine), C (quaternary).
  2D NMR: COSY (H-H connectivity), HSQC (H-C one bond), HMBC (H-C 2-3 bonds), NOESY (space proximity).

UV-Visible Spectroscopy:
  π → π* transitions (alkenes, aromatics): 200–250 nm. n → π* transitions (carbonyl): 270–280 nm.
  Conjugated systems: Bathochromic shift (red shift) with increasing conjugation.
  Beer-Lambert Law: A = εcl (absorbance = molar extinction coefficient × concentration × path length).
