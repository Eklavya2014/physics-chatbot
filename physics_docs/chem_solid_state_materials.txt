SOLID STATE CHEMISTRY AND MATERIALS SCIENCE - COMPREHENSIVE
=============================================================

PART 1: SOLID STATE CHEMISTRY

TYPES OF SOLIDS:
Crystalline: long-range order. Sharp melting point. Anisotropic properties.
Amorphous: short-range order only. Range of softening temperatures. Isotropic.
Polycrystalline: many small crystalline grains.
Quasicrystals: ordered but not periodic (discovered Shechtman 1982, Nobel 2011). Icosahedral symmetry.

CRYSTAL SYSTEMS AND BRAVAIS LATTICES:
14 Bravais lattices in 7 crystal systems:
Cubic: 3 (SC, BCC, FCC). Tetragonal: 2. Orthorhombic: 4. Hexagonal: 1. Rhombohedral: 1. Monoclinic: 2. Triclinic: 1.

CRYSTAL STRUCTURES:
NaCl (rock salt): FCC Cl⁻ + Na⁺ in all octahedral voids. CN = 6:6.
CsCl: SC Cs⁺ in body center of Cl⁻ cube. CN = 8:8. NOT BCC!
ZnS (zinc blende): FCC S²⁻ + Zn²⁺ in half tetrahedral voids. CN = 4:4.
ZnS (wurtzite): HCP S²⁻ + Zn²⁺ in tetrahedral voids. CN = 4:4.
CaF₂ (fluorite): FCC Ca²⁺ + F⁻ in ALL tetrahedral voids. CN = 8:4.
TiO₂ (rutile): HCP-like Ti⁴⁺ + O²⁻ in half octahedral voids.
Perovskite (CaTiO₃): BCC-like Ca²⁺ with Ti⁴⁺ at body center, O²⁻ on face centers. Many functional materials have this structure!
Spinel (MgAl₂O₄): complex. Important magnetic materials.

RADIUS RATIO RULES:
Predict preferred coordination number based on rₐ/r_A (cation/anion radius ratio):
< 0.155: linear (CN 2). 0.155-0.225: triangular (CN 3). 0.225-0.414: tetrahedral (CN 4).
0.414-0.732: octahedral (CN 6). 0.732-1.0: cubic (CN 8). 1.0: CN 12.
Limitations: purely geometric, ignores covalency, directional bonding.

MILLER INDICES:
Notation (hkl) for crystal planes. Direction [hkl].
Find intercepts on a,b,c axes. Take reciprocals. Clear fractions. These are Miller indices.
{hkl}: family of equivalent planes. <hkl>: family of equivalent directions.
Interplanar spacing: 1/d² = h²/a² + k²/b² + l²/c² (orthorhombic).
For cubic: d_hkl = a/√(h²+k²+l²).

POWDER X-RAY DIFFRACTION:
Each crystalline compound has unique diffraction pattern (fingerprint).
d-spacings from Bragg's law: 2d sinθ = nλ.
Intensity depends on: structure factor (atomic positions), temperature factor, multiplicity.
Structure factor: F_hkl = Σⱼ fⱼ exp(2πi(hxⱼ + kyⱼ + lzⱼ)).
Systematic absences: due to lattice centering and glide planes/screw axes.
Rietveld refinement: fit full diffraction pattern to determine crystal structure.

POINT DEFECTS:
Schottky defect: equal number of cation and anion vacancies. Preserves stoichiometry. Density decreases.
Frenkel defect: ion moves from lattice site to interstitial position. Both vacancy and interstitial. Density unchanged.
Schottky more common in NaCl (large ions). Frenkel more common in AgCl, ZnS (small cations or small ions).
Color centers (F-centers): electron trapped at anion vacancy. Gives color to crystal. NaCl in Na vapor → yellow.
Antisite defects: in compounds, cation on anion site or vice versa.
Interstitial: extra atom in interstice. Increases density.

IONIC CONDUCTIVITY:
Ions conduct charge in ionic solids (vs electrons in metals).
Defects enable ion migration.
Nernst-Einstein equation: D = μkT/e (D = diffusion coefficient, μ = mobility).
Superionics/fast ion conductors: very high ionic conductivity. AgI above 147°C. 
β-alumina: 2D ionic conductor. NASICON, LISICON: 3D frameworks.
Applications: solid electrolytes in batteries (Li-ion, Na-S), fuel cells (SOFC), sensors (λ-probe O₂ sensor in cars).

BAND THEORY:
Bloch theorem: ψₖ(r) = uₖ(r) e^(ik·r) where uₖ has periodicity of lattice.
Energy bands: allowed energy ranges for electrons.
Band gap: forbidden energy range. Determines electrical properties.
Brillouin zone: unit cell in reciprocal space. Bragg reflection at zone boundaries → gaps.

Metals: partially filled or overlapping bands. High conductivity (~10⁶-10⁸ S/m).
Insulators: filled valence band, empty conduction band, large gap (> 5 eV).
Semiconductors: gap ~ 1 eV. Si (1.12 eV), Ge (0.67 eV), GaAs (1.42 eV), ZnS (3.6 eV).
Semimetals: tiny band overlap. Bi, graphite. Small density of states at Fermi level.

PHONONS:
Quantized lattice vibrations. Bosons.
Acoustic phonons: atoms in phase (like sound, ω → 0 as k → 0).
Optical phonons: opposite phase oscillations. Higher frequency. IR and Raman active.
Debye model: acoustic phonons only. Debye temperature θ_D. C_v ∝ T³ at low T.
Einstein model: optical phonons at single frequency. C_v ∝ (θ_E/T)² e^(-θ_E/T) at low T.

PART 2: SEMICONDUCTORS (DETAILED)

INTRINSIC SEMICONDUCTORS:
Carrier density: n = p = nᵢ = √(Nc Nv) exp(-Eg/2kT).
Nc = 2(2πmₑ*kT/h²)^(3/2) (effective density of states, conduction band).
Nv = 2(2πmₕ*kT/h²)^(3/2) (valence band).
Fermi level at midgap (for mₑ* = mₕ*). Slightly shifted if masses differ.
Si at 300K: nᵢ = 1.5 × 10¹⁰ cm⁻³. Eg = 1.12 eV.

EXTRINSIC SEMICONDUCTORS:
n-type: donor dopants (Group V in Si: P, As, Sb).
  Each donor contributes one electron to conduction band.
  n ≈ Nd (donor concentration) at room temperature.
  Fermi level moves toward conduction band: Ef = Ec - kT ln(Nc/Nd).
p-type: acceptor dopants (Group III: B, Al, Ga).
  Each acceptor creates one hole in valence band.
  p ≈ Na at room temperature.
  Fermi level moves toward valence band.

Law of mass action: np = nᵢ² (always, at thermal equilibrium).
In n-type: n >> nᵢ >> p. In p-type: p >> nᵢ >> n.

CARRIER TRANSPORT:
Drift: carriers move under electric field.
  Jdrift = (nₑμₑ + pμₕ)E = σE.
  μₑ = eτₑ/mₑ* (electron mobility). μₑ > μₕ (electrons lighter, fewer scattering).
  Si: μₑ = 1400 cm²/V·s, μₕ = 450 cm²/V·s.
Diffusion: carriers move down concentration gradient.
  J_diff = eD_e ∇n - eD_h ∇p.
  Einstein relation: D/μ = kT/e.
Total current: J = J_drift + J_diff.

CONTINUITY EQUATIONS:
∂n/∂t = G - R + (1/e)∇·Jₙ.
∂p/∂t = G - R - (1/e)∇·Jₚ.
G = generation rate, R = recombination rate = (n-n₀)/τ.
Minority carrier lifetime τ: crucial for solar cells, transistors.

p-n JUNCTION (DETAILED):
Built-in potential: V_bi = (kT/e) ln(Na Nd/nᵢ²).
Depletion width: W = √(2ε V_bi/e × (Na+Nd)/(Na×Nd)).
Xn = W × Na/(Na+Nd), Xp = W × Nd/(Na+Nd).

I-V characteristics: I = Is [exp(eV/nkT) - 1].
Is = minority carrier diffusion current. Ideality factor n: 1 (ideal) to 2 (recombination dominated).
Under forward bias: minority carriers injected → diffuse and recombine → current flows.
Under reverse bias: only thermal minority carriers → small reverse current.

SOLAR CELL:
p-n junction illuminated → photon absorption → electron-hole pair generation.
Short-circuit current Isc: proportional to photon flux × quantum efficiency.
Open-circuit voltage Voc = (nkT/e) ln(Isc/Is + 1).
Fill factor FF = P_max/(Voc × Isc).
Efficiency η = P_max/P_incident = Voc × Isc × FF / P_incident.
Shockley-Queisser limit: maximum theoretical efficiency ~33.7% for single-junction solar cell (Eg ≈ 1.1 eV optimal).
Tandem cells: multiple junctions with different Eg → higher efficiency (multi-junction cells >40% in concentrated sunlight).

TRANSISTORS:
BJT (Bipolar Junction Transistor):
npn or pnp. Emitter, base, collector.
Active mode: emitter-base forward biased, collector-base reverse biased.
Current gain: β = Ic/Ib (20-200 typically). Ic = βIb.
Applications: amplifiers.

MOSFET (Metal-Oxide-Semiconductor Field-Effect Transistor):
Gate voltage controls channel between source and drain.
n-channel MOSFET: p-type substrate. Positive gate voltage → invert surface → electrons form channel.
Threshold voltage Vt: gate voltage needed to form channel.
Id = μ Cox W/L (Vgs - Vt)² /2 (saturation region).
Very high input impedance. Used in: digital logic (CMOS), amplifiers, power devices.
Modern transistors: FinFET (3D), gate-all-around. Feature sizes ~3 nm!

PART 3: ADVANCED MATERIALS

SUPERCONDUCTORS (DETAILED):
Type I: single critical field Hc. Complete Meissner effect below Hc.
  Hc(T) = Hc(0)[1-(T/Tc)²]. BCS theory: Tc ∝ exp(-1/N(0)V).
  Pb (Tc = 7.2K), Sn (3.7K), Hg (4.2K). Only metals and simple alloys.

Type II: two critical fields Hc1 and Hc2.
  Below Hc1: Meissner (complete expulsion). Hc1 < H < Hc2: mixed state (Abrikosov vortex lattice).
  Above Hc2: normal metal.
  Vortex: quantized flux tube. Magnetic flux quantum: Φ₀ = h/2e = 2.07 × 10⁻¹⁵ Wb.
  Nb₃Sn: Tc = 18K, Hc2 = 30T. Used in MRI magnets, particle accelerators.

High-Tc superconductors: cuprates (YBCO: Tc = 92K, BSCCO: 110K). Liquid nitrogen temperature!
Mechanism: not BCS. Involves Cu-O planes. Still not fully understood.
Iron-based superconductors: discovered 2008. Various families (FeAs, FeSe based).
Room temperature superconductors: not yet achieved reliably, active area of research.

BCS THEORY BASICS:
Phonon-mediated attraction between electrons → Cooper pairs (spin singlet, k and -k).
Condensation into BCS ground state.
Energy gap: Δ = 2ℏω_D exp(-1/N(0)V). 2Δ = gap in excitation spectrum.
London equations: J = -n_s e²A/m (supercurrent not resistive).
Meissner effect from London equation + Maxwell.

NANOMATERIALS:
Properties change dramatically at nanoscale (1-100 nm):
Quantum confinement: energy levels discrete → size-dependent optical properties.
Quantum dots: semiconductor nanocrystals. Tunable color by size. Used in displays, bioimaging.
Surface effects: high surface area-to-volume ratio → different chemical and physical properties.
Carbon nanomaterials:
- Fullerene C₆₀: soccer ball, molecule, 0D.
- Carbon nanotube (CNT): rolled graphene. 1D. Metallic or semiconducting depending on chirality. Very strong.
- Graphene: single layer of graphite. 2D. Massless Dirac fermions. Nobel Prize 2010 (Geim, Novoselov).
  Extraordinary properties: highest carrier mobility, highest thermal conductivity (~5000 W/m/K), ~100× stronger than steel.
Nanoparticles: gold NP (plasmonic, red color, used in biosensors), iron oxide NP (magnetic, drug delivery), TiO₂ (photocatalysis, sunscreen).

MAGNETIC MATERIALS:
Diamagnetic: χ < 0. Repelled by magnets. All materials (Lenz's law). Very small effect. Superconductors: perfect diamagnets.
Paramagnetic: χ > 0, small. Curie law: χ = C/T. No spontaneous order.
Ferromagnetic: spontaneous magnetization below Tc (Curie temperature). Domains.
Ferrimagnetic: antiparallel spins of different magnitudes → net magnetization. Ferrites, magnetite.
Antiferromagnetic: antiparallel equal spins → no net magnetization. Mn, Cr, MnO.
Exchange interaction: quantum mechanical. Positive J → ferromagnetism. Negative J → antiferromagnetism.
Heisenberg model: H = -J Σ Sᵢ·Sⱼ.

CERAMICS AND GLASS:
Traditional ceramics: clay-based (Al₂Si₂O₅(OH)₄). Fired at high temperature → hard, brittle.
Advanced ceramics: Al₂O₃ (alumina), SiC (silicon carbide), Si₃N₄, ZrO₂, BaTiO₃.
Properties: high hardness, chemical inertness, electrical insulation, high temperature stability.
Piezoelectric ceramics: BaTiO₃, PZT (Pb(Zr,Ti)O₃). Electric field ↔ mechanical deformation.
Applications: ultrasound transducers, actuators, sensors, microphones.
Ferroelectric: permanent electric polarization (like ferromagnets). BaTiO₃ below 120°C.

Glass: amorphous solid. Network former (SiO₂, B₂O₃, P₂O₅) + modifier (Na₂O, CaO) + intermediate.
Glass transition temperature Tg: not a phase transition. Viscosity changes rapidly.
Optical glass: precise refractive index, low dispersion.
Optical fiber: SiO₂ core with fluorinated SiO₂ cladding. Loss ~0.2 dB/km at 1550 nm.
Photochromic glass: AgCl nanoparticles. Darkens in UV, clears in dark.
Metallic glass: amorphous metal (fast quenching). No grain boundaries → high strength.
