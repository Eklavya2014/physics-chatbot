1. BODY PLANS & SYMMETRY
--------------------------
Body Symmetry:
  Asymmetry    : No plane of symmetry (most sponges).
  Radial       : Multiple planes through central axis (jellyfish, sea anemones, sea stars).
  Bilateral    : One plane divides into mirror halves; defines anterior/posterior,
                 dorsal/ventral. All vertebrates; most invertebrates.
  Biradial     : Combination (comb jellies — ctenophores).

Body Cavity (Coelom):
  Acoelomate   : No body cavity (flatworms — Platyhelminthes).
  Pseudocoelomate: False coelom (not lined by mesoderm); roundworms (Nematoda).
  Eucoelomate  : True coelom lined by mesoderm; annelids, molluscs, echinoderms, chordates.

Protostome vs Deuterostome:
  Protostomes: Mouth develops from blastopore first. Includes: Annelida, Mollusca,
    Arthropoda, Nematoda. Spiral cleavage (mostly); determinate.
  Deuterostomes: Anus develops from blastopore; mouth second. Includes: Echinodermata,
    Chordata, Hemichordata. Radial cleavage; indeterminate.

1.1 BODY ORGANISATION ACROSS ANIMAL GROUPS
--------------------------------------------
Invertebrate Body Plans:
  Porifera (sponges): No true tissues. Filter feeders. Choanocytes (collar cells) create
    water currents. Spicules (silica or CaCO3) for support. Totipotent archaeocytes.
  Cnidaria: Two tissue layers (diploblastic). Polyp (sessile) ↔ medusa (free-swimming).
    Nematocysts: Stinging cells for prey capture. Nerve net (no brain).
  Annelida: Segmented (metamerism). Closed circulatory system. Nephridia (excretion).
    Chaetae (bristles). Classes: Polychaeta (marine worms), Oligochaeta (earthworms),
    Hirudinea (leeches).
  Mollusca: Mantle, foot, visceral mass. Radula (rasping tongue, absent bivalves).
    Classes: Gastropoda (snails/slugs), Bivalvia (clams/oysters), Cephalopoda (squid/octopus).
    Cephalopods: Largest invertebrate brain; complex behaviour; chromatophores.

2. VERTEBRATE COMPARATIVE ANATOMY
------------------------------------
Skeletal Systems:
  Fish        : Cartilage (sharks) or bone (teleosts). Fins supported by fin rays.
  Amphibia    : Lightweight; pectoral + pelvic girdles for limb support. Hyoid for tongue.
  Reptilia    : Stronger skeleton; ribs for breathing (no diaphragm). Kinetic skull.
  Aves        : Hollow (pneumatised) bones; fused clavicles (furcula/wishbone);
                keeled sternum (flight muscle attachment); no teeth (beak).
  Mammalia    : Secondary palate (eat and breathe simultaneously); dentary mandible;
                3 middle ear ossicles (malleus, incus, stapes — evolved from reptile jaw bones).

Cardiovascular Comparison:
  Fish        : 2-chambered heart (1 atrium + 1 ventricle). Single circulation.
                Gill circulation → body.
  Amphibia    : 3-chambered heart (2 atria + 1 ventricle). Double but mixed.
  Reptilia    : 3-chambered (crocodilians: 4). Partial double circulation.
  Birds/Mammals: 4-chambered heart. Complete double circulation. Fully separated.

Respiratory Systems:
  Fish        : Gills; countercurrent exchange maximises O2 extraction.
  Amphibia    : Lungs + cutaneous respiration (moist skin). Buccal pumping (positive pressure).
  Reptilia    : Lungs only; aspiration breathing (negative pressure); no diaphragm.
  Birds       : Most efficient tetrapod lung. Parabronchial flow-through lung + air sacs
                (9 air sacs). Unidirectional airflow (crosscurrent gas exchange).
  Mammals     : Tidal breathing; diaphragm; alveolar lung.

Excretory Systems:
  Fish (marine): Produce very little urine; salt excreted via gills.
  Fish (freshwater): Dilute urine; ion absorption by gills.
  Amphibia    : Urea (uricotelic if terrestrial). Kidney; reabsorption via bladder.
  Reptilia/Birds: Uric acid (white paste — conserves water; does not require dilution).
  Mammals     : Urea in urine; loop of Henle concentrates urine (up to ~28x in desert rodents).

3. MAMMALIAN DIVERSITY
------------------------
Monotremata (egg-laying):
  Platypus (Ornithorhynchus anatinus): Electroreception in bill; venomous spur (male);
  echidnas: Spiny; no nipples (milk oozes through skin). Only ~5 living species.

Marsupialia (pouched):
  Embryo born at very early stage; completes development in pouch.
  ~335 species; mostly Australian/Americas.
  Examples: Kangaroo, wallaby, wombat, Tasmanian devil, koala, opossum.
  Koala: Diet exclusively eucalyptus leaves (toxic); detoxifying liver enzymes.

4. EVO-DEVO (EVOLUTIONARY DEVELOPMENTAL BIOLOGY)
--------------------------------------------------
Evo-Devo studies how changes in developmental processes drive morphological evolution.

Toolkit Genes:
  The same genes control development in radically different organisms.
  Hox genes: Determine A-P body axis segment identity. Conserved from flies to humans.
    Fruit fly: 8 Hox genes. Mammals: 39 Hox genes in 4 clusters (A, B, C, D).
    Homeotic mutations (e.g., Antennapedia — legs in place of antennae in fly) reveal
    Hox gene function.
  Pax6: Master regulator of eye development. Same gene in mice, flies, and humans.
    Misexpression → ectopic eye formation in flies (legs, wings, antennae).
  Tinman/Nkx2.5: Heart development across all bilaterians.
  Dll/Distalless: Limb development from annelids to vertebrates.

Developmental Constraints:
  Phylotypic stage: Period of maximum similarity across vertebrate embryos (pharyngula stage).
  Von Baer's Laws: Embryos of different species are more similar early in development.
  Developmental constraint: Some mutations are never observed because they disrupt
  essential developmental pathways.

Modularity:
  Body organised into semi-independent modules (segments, organs, limbs) that can
  evolve independently. Limb module: Same genetic programme in all tetrapod limbs
  but with different expression patterns → forelimb vs hindlimb differences.

Heterochrony: Changes in timing of developmental events.
  Neoteny: Retention of juvenile features in adult (paedomorphosis).
    Axolotl: Neotenous salamander — retains larval features (external gills) as adult.
    Humans: Compared to chimpanzees, humans show neoteny (large brain, flat face,
    delayed maturation).
  Peramorphosis: Extension of ancestral development (more development than ancestor).

5. INSECT BIOLOGY — MOST DIVERSE ANIMALS
------------------------------------------
~1 million described insect species (~5–10 million estimated total).
~75% of all animal species are insects.

Key Insect Features:
  3 body segments: Head, thorax, abdomen.
  3 pairs of legs; 2 pairs of wings (most); compound eyes; antennae.
  Exoskeleton (chitin + proteins): Growth by moulting (ecdysis) — controlled by ecdysone.
  Open circulatory system: Haemolymph in haemocoel; dorsal vessel (heart).
  Tracheal system: Spiracles → tracheoles → direct O2 delivery to cells (no RBCs).
  Malpighian tubules: Nitrogenous waste excretion (uric acid).

Metamorphosis:
  Hemimetabolous (incomplete): Egg → nymph (resembles adult) → adult.
    Examples: Grasshoppers, cockroaches, dragonflies.
  Holometabolous (complete): Egg → larva → pupa → adult (imago).
    Examples: Butterflies, beetles, flies, bees.
    Larva: Specialised for feeding/growth. Pupa: Metamorphosis; histolysis + histogenesis.
    Imaginal discs: Groups of cells in larva that give rise to adult structures.

Insect Communication:
  Chemical: Pheromones (trail, alarm, sex). Bombykol (silk moth sex pheromone) active at
  femtomolar concentrations; detected by olfactory sensilla on antennae.
  Acoustic: Crickets (stridulation — file + scraper); cicadas (tymbals — sound organs).
  Visual: Firefly flash patterns (species-specific); butterfly wing patterns.
  Tactile: Honeybee waggle dance; antennal contact.

[ECOLOGICAL IMPORTANCE] Insects:
  Pollination: Bees, butterflies, moths, flies → ~$235 billion/year economic value.
  Decomposition: Dung beetles, carrion beetles, blow flies.
  Biological control: Parasitoid wasps control pest populations.
  Food source: For birds, bats, amphibians, fish. Entomophagy (humans eating insects).
  Decline: ~40% insect species declining; biomass down ~75% in 27 years (Germany study).
  Causes: Habitat loss, pesticides (neonicotinoids → bee colony collapse disorder),
  light pollution, climate change.
