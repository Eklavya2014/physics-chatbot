1. WHAT IS TAXONOMY?
---------------------
Taxonomy is the science of naming, describing, and classifying organisms.
Systematics is the broader discipline studying evolutionary relationships among
organisms. Founded by Carl Linnaeus (1707–1778), who formalised binomial nomenclature.

Binomial Nomenclature:
  Every species has a two-part Latin name: Genus + species epithet.
  Written in italics; genus capitalised; species lowercase.
  Examples: Homo sapiens, Panthera leo, Escherichia coli, Quercus robur.
  Author citation: Homo sapiens Linnaeus, 1758.

Taxonomic Hierarchy (from broadest to most specific):
  Domain → Kingdom → Phylum → Class → Order → Family → Genus → Species
  Mnemonic: "Dear King Philip Came Over For Good Soup"
  Additional ranks: Subphylum, Superclass, Suborder, Subfamily, Subspecies.

Example — Human Classification:
  Domain    : Eukarya
  Kingdom   : Animalia
  Phylum    : Chordata
  Subphylum : Vertebrata
  Class     : Mammalia
  Order     : Primates
  Family    : Hominidae
  Genus     : Homo
  Species   : Homo sapiens

The Three Domains of Life (Carl Woese, 1977 — based on 16S rRNA):
  Bacteria  : Prokaryotes; diverse metabolism; cell wall (peptidoglycan); ester-linked lipids.
  Archaea   : Prokaryotes; extremophiles and ubiquitous; ether-linked lipids; no peptidoglycan.
  Eukarya   : Eukaryotes; membrane-bound nucleus; 80S ribosomes.
  Archaea and Eukarya are sister groups — more closely related to each other than either is to Bacteria.

2. CLADISTICS & PHYLOGENETIC CLASSIFICATION
---------------------------------------------
Cladistics (phylogenetic systematics — Hennig, 1950):
  Classification should reflect evolutionary history (monophyletic groups only).

Key Terms:
  Clade (monophyletic group): Common ancestor + ALL its descendants.
  Paraphyletic group        : Common ancestor + SOME descendants (not natural).
  Polyphyletic group        : No common ancestor — artificial grouping.
  Plesiomorphy              : Ancestral (primitive) character state.
  Apomorphy                 : Derived character state.
  Synapomorphy              : Shared derived character → defines a clade.
  Homoplasy                 : Similar characters evolved independently (convergence).

Homology vs Analogy:
  Homologous structures: Same evolutionary origin; may have different functions.
    Example: Human arm, bat wing, whale flipper, bird wing — all modified tetrapod limbs.
  Analogous structures (homoplasy): Similar function, different origin.
    Example: Bird wing and insect wing — convergent evolution of flight.
  Molecular homology: Shared gene sequences (e.g., Hox genes across all bilaterians).

Building Phylogenetic Trees:
  Parsimony: Tree requiring fewest evolutionary changes (most parsimonious).
  Maximum Likelihood: Tree most likely given a model of molecular evolution.
  Bayesian inference: Uses prior probabilities + likelihood → posterior probability.
  Bootstrap support: Resampling to assess node confidence (>70% = acceptable).
  Molecular clock: Neutral mutations accumulate at roughly constant rate → estimate divergence times.

Arthropoda — Largest animal phylum (~85% of all animal species):
  Chelicerata (Arachnida): Spiders, scorpions, mites, ticks. Chelicerae (fangs/pincers).
  Myriapoda: Centipedes, millipedes. Many leg pairs.
  Crustacea: Crabs, shrimps, lobsters, barnacles, copepods. Mostly aquatic.
  Insecta: ~1 million described species. Wings; 3 body segments; 6 legs.
    Orders: Coleoptera (beetles ~25% of all animal species); Lepidoptera (butterflies/moths);
    Diptera (flies); Hymenoptera (ants/bees/wasps); Orthoptera (grasshoppers).

4. VERTEBRATE CLASSIFICATION
------------------------------
Subphylum Vertebrata:
  Agnatha (jawless fish)    : Lamprey, hagfish. Oldest vertebrate lineage.
  Chondrichthyes           : Cartilaginous fish. Sharks, rays, chimaeras.
  Actinopterygii           : Ray-finned fish. ~30,000 species; most diverse vertebrates.
  Sarcopterygii            : Lobe-finned fish + tetrapods. Coelacanth, lungfish (closest fish relatives of tetrapods).
  Amphibia                 : Frogs, salamanders, caecilians. Dual life (water + land).
  Reptilia (paraphyletic!) : Lizards, snakes, turtles, crocodilians. Ectothermic.
  Aves (birds)             : Derived theropod dinosaurs. Feathers; endothermic; hollow bones.
  Mammalia                 : Hair; mammary glands; endothermic.
    Monotremata: Egg-laying (platypus, echidna).
    Marsupialia: Pouched (kangaroo, koala, opossum).
    Placentalia: Internal gestation; most mammals.

5. MOLECULAR SYSTEMATICS & THE TREE OF LIFE
---------------------------------------------
Universal Phylogenetic Marker — 16S/18S rRNA:
  Ribosomal RNA genes are present in all life, evolve slowly enough to compare
  across deep branches. Carl Woese used 16S rRNA sequences to discover Archaea (1977)
  and establish the three-domain tree of life. 16S = prokaryotes; 18S = eukaryotes.

Multi-gene & Whole-genome Phylogenetics:
  Concatenated gene trees; coalescent methods; horizontal gene transfer complicates
  deep phylogenetics (especially among prokaryotes). ~15–30% of bacterial genes
  involved in HGT. Network vs tree representation for prokaryote evolution.

Current Consensus — Eukaryote Supergroups:
  Opisthokonta : Animals + Fungi + choanoflagellates (closest relatives of animals).
  Amoebozoa    : Amoebae, slime moulds.
  Archaeplastida: Plants, green algae, red algae, glaucophytes.
  SAR           : Stramenopiles (diatoms, kelp) + Alveolates (ciliates, dinoflagellates,
                  Plasmodium) + Rhizaria (foraminifera, radiolaria).
  Excavata      : Flagellates; Giardia, Trichomonas, trypanosomes, Euglena.
  TSAR/CRuMs    : Several emerging groups still being resolved.

Species Concepts:
  Biological Species Concept (Mayr): Groups that actually or potentially interbreed
  and are reproductively isolated from others. Problem: Asexuals; ring species.
  Phylogenetic Species Concept: Smallest monophyletic group with unique shared
  derived characters. Problem: Over-splitting; depends on data.
  Ecological Species Concept: Lineage occupying distinct adaptive zone.
  Cohesion Species Concept: Most inclusive group with genetic/demographic cohesion.
  Morphological Species Concept: Defined by morphological characters. Oldest; still practical.

6. BARCODING & METAGENOMICS
-----------------------------
DNA Barcoding:
  Short standardised DNA region used to identify species.
  Animals: COI (cytochrome c oxidase I — mitochondrial).
  Plants: rbcL + matK (chloroplast genes).
  Fungi: ITS (Internal Transcribed Spacer — nuclear ribosomal).
  BOLD (Barcode of Life Data System): >8 million barcodes; 250,000+ species.
  Applications: Identifying cryptic species, detecting mislabelled food, customs
  enforcement (CITES), invasive species detection, diet analysis.

Environmental DNA (eDNA):
  Collect water/soil sample → extract DNA → sequence → identify organisms present.
  Does not require direct observation. Detects rare/cryptic species (great crested newt,
  whale shark, giant panda). Used for biodiversity surveys, invasive species monitoring.

Metagenomics:
  Sequence ALL DNA in an environmental sample → profile entire community.
  Revealed: ~99% of microbes cannot be cultured (Great Plate Count Anomaly).
  Earth Microbiome Project: Cataloguing microbial diversity globally.
  Discovered: Giant viruses (Mimivirus, Pandoravirus) larger than some bacteria.
  Revealed vast diversity in ocean (Tara Oceans), soil, deep subsurface biosphere.

[NOMENCLATURE RULES] International Codes govern naming:
  ICZoN: International Code of Zoological Nomenclature (animals).
  ICN: International Code of Nomenclature for algae, fungi, plants.
  ICNB: International Code of Nomenclature of Bacteria.
  Priority principle: First valid description takes precedence.
  Type specimen: Physical specimen defining the species (holotype).
  Synonyms: Multiple names for the same species — valid name = oldest valid publication.
