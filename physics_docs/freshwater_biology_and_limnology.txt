1. FRESHWATER ECOSYSTEMS
--------------------------
Limnology: Scientific study of freshwater bodies (lakes, rivers, ponds, wetlands).
Freshwater covers <1% of Earth's surface but is home to ~10% of all species,
including ~33% of all vertebrate species.

Types of Freshwater Habitats:
  Lentic systems (standing water): Lakes, ponds, reservoirs, swamps, bogs, marshes.
  Lotic systems (flowing water): Rivers, streams, springs, brooks.
  Wetlands: Transitional; seasonally or permanently flooded; extremely productive.
    Types: Marshes (emergent plants); swamps (woody plants); bogs (peat, Sphagnum moss,
    acidic, low nutrients); fens (calcareous, higher pH, fed by groundwater).

1.1 LAKE ZONATION
------------------
Littoral zone:  Near shore; shallow; sunlit to bottom; most diverse; rooted plants.
Limnetic zone:  Open water; photosynthesis by phytoplankton; fish, zooplankton.
Profundal zone: Deep, dark, cold; detritivores; bacteria; decomposition.
Benthic zone:   Lake bottom; detritus; invertebrates (chironomids, oligochaetes).

Thermal Stratification (temperate lakes):
  Summer: Warm epilimnion (surface) / thermocline (rapid temp change) / cold hypolimnion.
  Autumn turnover: Surface cools → density equalises → wind mixes entire lake → nutrients
  from bottom brought to surface → phytoplankton bloom.
  Winter: Ice layer; inverse stratification; hypolimnion warmer (4°C).
  Spring turnover: Ice melts; surface warms to 4°C → density maximum → mixing again.
  Dimictic lakes: Mix twice yearly. Meromictic: Never fully mix (saline bottom layer).

Water Quality Parameters:
  Dissolved oxygen (DO): Critical for aquatic life. Cold water holds more O2. Depleted by
  decomposition. Stratified lakes → anoxic hypolimnion in summer.
  pH: Most freshwater organisms thrive pH 6.5–8.5. Acid rain → pH decline → Al3+ toxicity.
  Turbidity: Suspended particles reduce light penetration (Secchi depth measurement).
  Conductivity: Indicator of dissolved ion concentration.
  Nutrients (N, P): Primary productivity limited by P in freshwater; N in marine.

2. FRESHWATER ECOLOGY
----------------------
Trophic Status of Lakes:
  Oligotrophic: Low nutrients; clear, high DO, deep; few algae; diverse fish.
    Example: Lake Tahoe, Lake Baikal.
  Mesotrophic: Moderate nutrients; intermediate clarity; mixed fish community.
  Eutrophic: High nutrients; algal blooms; low DO; cyanobacteria; bottom anoxia.
  Hypereutrophic: Extreme; algal scum; fish kills; strong odour.
  Cultural eutrophication: Human-accelerated nutrient loading (agriculture runoff,
  sewage, urban stormwater) → massive water quality degradation globally.

Eutrophication Cascade:
  Excess N + P → phytoplankton bloom → light blocked to submerged plants →
  plants die → decomposition → O2 depletion → fish kills → anoxia →
  release of P from sediments (internal loading) → positive feedback loop.
  Harmful algal blooms (HABs): Cyanobacteria (Microcystis, Anabaena) → microcystin
  toxin → liver damage; dog deaths; human recreational illness.

River Ecology — Longitudinal Zonation:
  Headwaters (rhithral): Cold, fast, high O2, shaded; insects (stoneflies, mayflies,
  caddisflies — EPT index for water quality); stream metabolism dominated by allochthonous
  (terrestrial) organic matter input.
  Mid-reaches (potamal): Warmer, slower, more sunlight; algae; more phytoplankton.
  Lower reaches: Turbid; nutrient-rich; detritus; invertebrate shredder → collector → 
  scraper community shifts.
  River Continuum Concept (Vannote 1980): Describes gradient of community structure from
  headwaters to mouth; P:R ratio shifts from heterotrophic (shaded) to autotrophic (mid) back.

3. FRESHWATER ORGANISMS
------------------------
Phytoplankton:
  Diatoms (Bacillariophyceae): Silica frustules; dominant in spring/autumn (cold, mixed).
  Green algae (Chlorophyta): Summer; nutrient-rich; diverse forms.
  Cyanobacteria (blue-green algae): Summer; warm; N-fixing; forms surface scums in eutrophic.
  Chrysophytes (golden algae): Cold, oligotrophic; Dinobryon.
  Dinoflagellates: Some freshwater species; Peridinium common in stratified lakes.

Zooplankton:
  Cladocera (water fleas): Daphnia — keystone grazer; filter phytoplankton; indicator species.
  Copepoda: Cyclops — predatory/herbivorous; important secondary producers.
  Rotifera: Microscopic; diverse; important in oligotrophic systems.
  Daphnia magna: Model organism in ecotoxicology; sensitive to pollutants.

Macroinvertebrates (Benthic):
  Insect larvae: Chironomids (midges — most abundant), EPT insects (Ephemeroptera, Plecoptera,
  Trichoptera), Odonata (dragonfly/damselfly) larvae.
  Non-insect: Oligochaetes (worms — tolerate low O2); molluscs (freshwater mussels, snails);
  crustaceans (crayfish, freshwater shrimp, amphipods).
  Biotic index: Community composition used to assess water quality.
    BMWP: British Macroinvertebrate Wealth Prediction score — tolerant vs sensitive taxa.

Fish — Major Freshwater Groups:
  Salmon (Salmonidae): Anadromous; cold, oxygenated water; high ecological/economic value.
  Cyprinidae (carp family): Most diverse freshwater family; carp, minnows, roach, tench.
  Cichlidae: Tropical; Lake Victoria (>500 species — rapid adaptive radiation); Lake Malawi, Tanganyika.
  Percidae: Perch, pike-perch; important predators in European/North American lakes.

4. WETLAND ECOLOGY
-------------------
Wetlands: Among most productive ecosystems on Earth. 40% of all species use wetlands.
  Carbon sinks: Peatlands store ~30% of all terrestrial carbon despite covering only 3% of land.
    Sphagnum moss: Creates acid conditions; inhibits decomposition; builds peat over millennia.
  Water purification: Sediment trapping; nutrient uptake by plants; denitrification by bacteria.
  Flood regulation: Absorb excess water; slow release (green infrastructure).
  Biodiversity hotspots: Breeding grounds for waterfowl; nursery for fish; amphibian habitat.

Loss of Wetlands:
  ~35% of wetlands lost since 1970. Faster than forests.
  Causes: Drainage for agriculture, urban development, peat extraction, pollution.
  Ramsar Convention (1971): First international environmental treaty; protects wetlands of
  international importance. ~170 countries; 2,400+ sites.

5. ADAPTIVE RADIATION IN FRESHWATER
-------------------------------------
Cichlid Adaptive Radiation (Great Lakes of Africa):
  Lake Victoria: ~500+ cichlid species evolved in <15,000 years (post-desiccation refilling).
  Fastest adaptive radiation in vertebrates. Driven by: Sexual selection (male colour);
  ecological opportunity; stable lake conditions.
  Trophic diversity: Piscivores, zooplanktivores, insectivores, molluscivores, algae scrapers,
  scale eaters (lepidophages), paedophages, detritivores — all from common ancestor.
  Pharyngeal jaw innovation: Decoupled from oral jaws → allows specialised prey processing.
  Nile perch introduction (1950s): Eliminated >200 endemic cichlid species in Lake Victoria
  → one of largest recorded vertebrate extinctions caused by a single species.

Daphnia as Model System:
  Cyclical parthenogenesis: Asexual (clonal) reproduction normally; sexual when conditions
  deteriorate → produce dormant eggs (ephippia) → genetic diversity + dispersal.
  Ecological stoichiometry: Daphnia body C:N:P ratio relatively fixed → track algal stoichiometry.
  Eutrophication: Daphnia bloom on algae → clear-water phase; then algae recover when Daphnia
  food quality drops (cyanobacteria inedible) → trophic cascade dynamics.
  Paleolimnology (Cladocera): Daphnia ephippia preserved in lake sediments → reconstruct past
  zooplankton communities and lake history.

6. FRESHWATER CONSERVATION
----------------------------
Threats to Freshwater Biodiversity:
  Overextraction: Water diversion (Colorado River no longer reaches sea seasonally).
  Dams: Fragment rivers; alter thermal/flow regimes; block fish migration.
    ~800,000 dams globally; 60% of major river systems fragmented.
  Invasive species: Signal crayfish (destroying native crayfish + vegetation); Asian carp
    (outcompeting native fish in Mississippi); water hyacinth (blocks light/O2).
  Pollution: Agricultural runoff (nutrients, pesticides), industrial effluent, pharmaceuticals,
    microplastics, heavy metals, endocrine disruptors (e17-alpha ethinylestradiol feminising fish).
  Climate change: Altered precipitation; increased drought; warming (cold-water fish retreat);
    glacier loss; intensified eutrophication.

Freshwater Protected Areas:
  Only ~0.4% of freshwater area is strictly protected (vs ~14% terrestrial).
  EU Water Framework Directive: Achieve "good ecological status" across all water bodies.
  Salmon river restoration: Dam removal (e.g., Elwha River dams removed 2011–2014 →
  salmon recolonised within months; ecosystem recovery progressing rapidly).
  Beaver reintroduction: Keystone species; builds dams → creates wetlands, pools → increases
  biodiversity. Reintroduced in UK, Germany, Netherlands → transformative landscape effects.

[EMERGING ISSUE] Freshwater microplastics:
  All major rivers studied contain microplastics. Rhine carries >190 million particles/day.
  Effects on aquatic organisms: Reduced feeding, reproduction, survival; carry persistent
  organic pollutants and pathogens. Nanoplastics (<1 µm): Cross cell membranes; accumulate
  in tissues. No effective river-scale remediation exists. Prevention essential.
