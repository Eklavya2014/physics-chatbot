1. BIOMOLECULES — THE FOUR MACROMOLECULES
------------------------------------------
All living things are built from four classes of large biological molecules:

1.1 CARBOHYDRATES
  Monomer: Monosaccharides (glucose, fructose, galactose)
  Polymer: Polysaccharides (starch, glycogen, cellulose, chitin)
  Bond: Glycosidic bond (formed by dehydration synthesis)
  Functions: Energy source (glucose), energy storage (glycogen/starch),
             structural support (cellulose in plants, chitin in insects/fungi).

  Key examples:
  - Glucose (C6H12O6)   : Primary fuel for cellular respiration.
  - Sucrose             : Glucose + Fructose (table sugar).
  - Lactose             : Glucose + Galactose (milk sugar).
  - Starch              : Alpha-1,4 and alpha-1,6 glycosidic bonds; energy storage in plants.
  - Glycogen            : Highly branched; energy storage in animals (liver, muscle).
  - Cellulose           : Beta-1,4 bonds; structural; cannot be digested by humans.

1.2 LIPIDS
  Not polymers — diverse group joined by hydrophobic properties.
  Types:
  - Triglycerides       : 3 fatty acids + glycerol; long-term energy storage.
  - Phospholipids       : 2 fatty acids + glycerol + phosphate head; membrane bilayer.
  - Steroids            : 4-ring carbon structure; includes cholesterol, hormones.
  - Waxes               : Long-chain fatty acid + alcohol; waterproofing.
  Saturated fatty acids : No double bonds; solid at room temp; animal fats.
  Unsaturated           : One or more double bonds; liquid at room temp; plant oils.
  Trans fats            : Artificially hydrogenated; raise LDL, lower HDL.

1.3 PROTEINS
  Monomer: Amino acids (20 standard amino acids)
  Bond: Peptide bond (formed between amino group and carboxyl group)
  Structure levels:
  - Primary   : Sequence of amino acids (determined by DNA).
  - Secondary  : Alpha helices and beta sheets (H-bonds between backbone).
  - Tertiary   : 3D folding; stabilised by R-group interactions, disulphide bridges.
  - Quaternary : Multiple polypeptide subunits (e.g., haemoglobin: 4 subunits).
  Functions: Enzymes, structural (collagen, keratin), transport (haemoglobin),
             signalling (hormones), immune (antibodies), motor (actin, myosin).

1.4 NUCLEIC ACIDS
  Monomer: Nucleotides (phosphate + sugar + nitrogenous base)
  Polymers: DNA and RNA
  Bond: Phosphodiester bond
  Functions: Genetic information storage (DNA), protein synthesis (mRNA, tRNA, rRNA).

2. ENZYMES
-----------
Enzymes are biological catalysts — proteins (or sometimes RNA = ribozymes) that
speed up reactions by lowering activation energy without being consumed.

Key Concepts:
- Active site       : Specific region where substrate binds.
- Induced fit model : Enzyme changes shape slightly upon substrate binding.
- Km (Michaelis constant): Substrate concentration at half-maximum velocity.
                           Low Km = high affinity for substrate.
- Vmax              : Maximum reaction velocity when enzyme is saturated.
- Turnover number   : Reactions catalysed per enzyme per second.

Enzyme Inhibition:
- Competitive       : Inhibitor resembles substrate; competes for active site.
                      Reversible; overcome by increasing substrate.
                      Effect: increased Km, unchanged Vmax.
- Non-competitive   : Inhibitor binds allosteric site; changes enzyme shape.
                      Cannot be overcome by more substrate.
                      Effect: decreased Vmax, unchanged Km.
- Uncompetitive     : Binds enzyme-substrate complex only.
                      Effect: decreased Km AND Vmax.
- Irreversible      : Covalently modifies active site (e.g., aspirin → COX enzyme;
                      nerve agents → acetylcholinesterase).

Factors affecting enzyme activity:
  Temperature: Increases rate up to optimum (~37°C for human enzymes); above
               optimum → denaturation (loss of 3D structure).
  pH: Each enzyme has optimal pH; extremes disrupt ionic/H-bonds (pepsin pH 2;
      trypsin pH 8).
  Cofactors: Non-protein helpers (metal ions: Mg2+, Zn2+, Fe2+).
  Coenzymes: Organic cofactors (NAD+, FAD, CoA — derived from vitamins).

3. METABOLISM — CATABOLISM & ANABOLISM
----------------------------------------
Metabolism = all chemical reactions in a cell.
Catabolism = breakdown reactions; release energy (exergonic; produce ATP).
Anabolism  = synthesis reactions; require energy (endergonic; consume ATP).

ATP (Adenosine Triphosphate): Universal energy currency.
  ATP → ADP + Pi releases ~30.5 kJ/mol; drives endergonic reactions.
  Regenerated by: cellular respiration, photosynthesis, substrate-level phosphorylation.

NAD+/NADH and FAD/FADH2: Electron carriers.
  NAD+ + 2H+ + 2e- → NADH (carries high-energy electrons to ETC).
  FAD + 2H+ + 2e- → FADH2.

4. CARBOHYDRATE METABOLISM
----------------------------
Glycolysis (cytoplasm):
  Glucose (6C) → 2 Pyruvate (3C)
  Net yield: 2 ATP, 2 NADH, 2 Pyruvate
  Key enzymes: Hexokinase, PFK-1 (rate-limiting, allosterically regulated),
               Pyruvate kinase.

Pyruvate Decarboxylation (mitochondrial matrix):
  Pyruvate + CoA + NAD+ → Acetyl-CoA + CO2 + NADH
  Enzyme: Pyruvate dehydrogenase complex (PDC).

Krebs Cycle / TCA Cycle (mitochondrial matrix):
  Acetyl-CoA (2C) + Oxaloacetate (4C) → Citrate (6C) → ... → Oxaloacetate
  Per turn: 3 NADH, 1 FADH2, 1 GTP, 2 CO2
  Two turns per glucose: 6 NADH, 2 FADH2, 2 GTP, 4 CO2

Electron Transport Chain (inner mitochondrial membrane):
  NADH and FADH2 donate electrons to protein complexes (I→III→IV).
  Electrons passed to O2 (final electron acceptor) → H2O.
  H+ pumped across membrane → proton gradient.
  ATP synthase (Complex V) uses proton flow (chemiosmosis) → ~32–34 ATP.

Gluconeogenesis:
  Synthesis of glucose from non-carbohydrate precursors (pyruvate, lactate,
  glycerol, amino acids). Occurs mainly in liver. Activated during fasting.
  Uses 6 ATP equivalents (energy costly).

Glycogenesis / Glycogenolysis:
  Glycogenesis: Glucose → Glycogen (storage; insulin stimulated).
  Glycogenolysis: Glycogen → Glucose-1-phosphate → Glucose (glucagon/epinephrine).

5. LIPID & PROTEIN METABOLISM
-------------------------------
Beta-Oxidation (fatty acid catabolism):
  Fatty acids activated to fatty acyl-CoA → transported into mitochondria via
  carnitine shuttle (rate-limiting step regulated by malonyl-CoA inhibition).
  Each cycle removes 2C as Acetyl-CoA; yields 1 FADH2 + 1 NADH.
  Palmitate (16C): 7 cycles → 8 Acetyl-CoA + 7 FADH2 + 7 NADH → ~106 ATP total.

Ketogenesis:
  During prolonged fasting/starvation, excess Acetyl-CoA in liver converted
  to ketone bodies (acetoacetate, beta-hydroxybutyrate, acetone).
  Exported to peripheral tissues (brain, muscle) as alternative fuel.
  Diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA): Uncontrolled ketogenesis → blood acidosis.

Amino Acid Catabolism:
  Amino acids → carbon skeleton + NH3.
  NH3 is toxic; converted to urea via the UREA CYCLE (liver):
    NH3 + CO2 → Carbamoyl phosphate → Citrulline → Argininosuccinate
    → Arginine → Urea (excreted by kidneys) + Ornithine (cycle continues).
  Carbon skeletons: glucogenic (→ glucose) or ketogenic (→ ketone bodies).

6. METABOLIC REGULATION & INTEGRATION
---------------------------------------
Allosteric Regulation:
  Key regulatory enzymes: PFK-1 (glycolysis), pyruvate kinase, isocitrate
  dehydrogenase (TCA), acetyl-CoA carboxylase (fatty acid synthesis).
  Energy charge = (ATP + 0.5ADP) / (ATP + ADP + AMP)
  High energy charge → inhibit catabolism; stimulate anabolism.

Hormonal Integration:
  Insulin (fed state):
    Stimulates: glucose uptake (GLUT4), glycolysis, glycogenesis, lipogenesis.
    Inhibits: gluconeogenesis, glycogenolysis, lipolysis, beta-oxidation.
  Glucagon/Epinephrine (fasted/stress):
    Stimulates: glycogenolysis, gluconeogenesis, lipolysis, ketogenesis.
    Inhibits: glycolysis, glycogenesis, lipogenesis.

mTOR Signalling:
  mTOR complex 1 (mTORC1) integrates nutrient, energy, and growth signals.
  Active when amino acids (sensed by GATOR/Ragulator complex on lysosome),
  insulin (PI3K-Akt pathway), and energy (AMPK inactive) are abundant.
  Activates: protein synthesis (S6K1 → ribosome biogenesis; 4E-BP1 release → cap-
  dependent translation), lipid synthesis (SREBP activation), nucleotide synthesis.
  Inhibited by: rapamycin, metformin (via AMPK), caloric restriction.

AMPK (AMP-activated protein kinase):
  Energy sensor; activated when AMP:ATP ratio rises (low energy).
  Stimulates: beta-oxidation, glycolysis, autophagy, mitochondrial biogenesis.
  Inhibits: fatty acid synthesis, gluconeogenesis, mTORC1.
  Target of metformin (type 2 diabetes drug).

Warburg Effect (aerobic glycolysis):
  Cancer cells preferentially use glycolysis even with O2 present.
  Generates lactate (not entering TCA) despite lower ATP yield.
  Advantage: rapid ATP production; generates biosynthetic precursors;
  avoids ROS from mitochondria; supports rapid proliferation.
  Basis for FDG-PET imaging of tumours (high glucose uptake).

[CLINICAL LINK] Inborn errors of metabolism (IEMs) arise from enzyme deficiencies:
  PKU (phenylketonuria): Phenylalanine hydroxylase deficiency → Phe accumulates
  → neurological damage. Treated with low-Phe diet + BH4 supplementation.
  Gaucher disease: Glucocerebrosidase deficiency → lipid accumulation in macrophages.
  MCAD deficiency: Medium-chain acyl-CoA dehydrogenase → impaired beta-oxidation
  → hypoglycaemia during fasting; most common fatty acid oxidation disorder.
