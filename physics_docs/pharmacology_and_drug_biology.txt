1. WHAT IS PHARMACOLOGY?
--------------------------
Pharmacology is the study of how drugs interact with biological systems to produce
their effects. It encompasses drug mechanisms, effects, uses, and safety.

Pharmacodynamics (PD): What the drug does to the body.
Pharmacokinetics (PK): What the body does to the drug (ADME).

Drug Targets:
  Receptors       : Proteins that drugs bind to produce a response.
  Enzymes         : Drugs inhibit or activate (e.g., aspirin inhibits COX).
  Ion channels    : Drugs block or open channels (e.g., local anaesthetics block Na+ channels).
  Transporters    : Drugs block uptake proteins (e.g., SSRIs block serotonin transporter).
  Nucleic acids   : Some antibiotics/anticancer drugs bind DNA.

Drug-Receptor Relationships:
  Agonist      : Binds receptor and activates it (mimics endogenous ligand).
  Antagonist   : Binds receptor but does NOT activate; blocks agonist binding.
  Partial agonist: Activates receptor but max response lower than full agonist.
  Inverse agonist: Binds constitutively active receptor → reduces baseline activity.
  Allosteric modulator: Binds different site; changes receptor conformation.

Affinity: How strongly drug binds receptor. Ki (inhibitor constant) or Kd.
Efficacy: Maximum response drug can produce.
Potency: Concentration to produce 50% max effect. ED50 (effective dose 50%).

1.1 PHARMACOKINETICS — ADME
-----------------------------
Absorption:
  Routes: Oral (PO), intravenous (IV), intramuscular (IM), subcutaneous (SC),
  transdermal, inhaled, sublingual, rectal.
  Bioavailability (F): Fraction of dose reaching systemic circulation.
  IV: F = 100%. Oral: Usually <100% (first-pass effect, poor absorption).
  First-pass effect: Drug absorbed from gut → portal vein → liver → metabolised
  before reaching systemic circulation. High first-pass: Morphine (F~25%), propranolol.

Distribution:
  Volume of distribution (Vd): Apparent volume drug distributes into.
  High Vd: Drug penetrates tissues/fat (e.g., chloroquine Vd = 600 L/kg).
  Low Vd: Drug stays in plasma (e.g., warfarin Vd = 0.14 L/kg).
  Blood-brain barrier (BBB): Tight junctions + P-glycoprotein efflux → limits CNS entry.
  Protein binding: Drugs bind albumin/AAG → only free drug is active.

Metabolism:
  Mainly liver. Phase I: CYP450 enzymes (oxidation, reduction, hydrolysis) → often
  more polar metabolites.
  Phase II: Conjugation (glucuronidation, sulphation, acetylation, methylation) → water-
  soluble; ready for excretion.
  CYP450 enzymes: CYP3A4 metabolises ~50% of drugs. CYP2D6, CYP2C9, CYP2C19.
  Genetic polymorphisms: Poor, intermediate, extensive, ultrarapid metabolisers.

Excretion:
  Kidneys: Glomerular filtration + tubular secretion - tubular reabsorption.
  Bile/faeces: Enterohepatic circulation (some drugs recycled).
  Half-life (t½): Time for plasma concentration to halve. t½ = 0.693 × Vd/CL.
  Steady state: Achieved after ~5 half-lives of regular dosing.

2. AUTONOMIC PHARMACOLOGY
---------------------------
Sympathetic NS (adrenergic):
  Neurotransmitter: Norepinephrine (NE) at target organ; epinephrine from adrenal medulla.
  Receptors:
    Alpha-1 (Gq): Vascular smooth muscle contraction → vasoconstriction; pupil dilation.
    Alpha-2 (Gi): Presynaptic autoreceptor → inhibit NE release; central BP regulation.
    Beta-1 (Gs): Heart → ↑ HR (chronotropy), ↑ contractility (inotropy), ↑ conduction.
    Beta-2 (Gs): Bronchodilation; vasodilation; glycogenolysis; uterine relaxation.
    Beta-3 (Gs): Lipolysis in adipose; thermogenesis.

  Key drugs:
    Beta-blockers (propranolol, metoprolol): Block beta receptors → ↓ HR, ↓ BP, anti-arrhythmic.
    Alpha-1 blockers (prazosin, doxazosin): Vasodilation → ↓ BP; BPH relief.
    Alpha-2 agonists (clonidine): ↓ central sympathetic outflow → ↓ BP.
    Adrenaline (epinephrine): Alpha + beta agonist; anaphylaxis, cardiac arrest, local anaesthesia.

Parasympathetic NS (cholinergic):
  Neurotransmitter: Acetylcholine (ACh).
  Receptors:
    Muscarinic (G-protein coupled): M1 (CNS; gastric acid), M2 (heart — ↓HR), M3 (smooth muscle/glands).
    Nicotinic (ionotropic): Skeletal NMJ (NM); autonomic ganglia (NN).

  Key drugs:
    Atropine (muscarinic antagonist): ↑ HR, mydriasis, dry mouth, bronchodilation; antidote to
      organophosphate poisoning.
    Neostigmine (AChE inhibitor): ↑ ACh at NMJ → reverses neuromuscular blockade; myasthenia gravis.
    Pilocarpine (M3 agonist): Glaucoma (reduces IOP via ciliary muscle contraction).
    Botulinum toxin: Blocks ACh vesicle fusion at NMJ → flaccid paralysis. Cosmetic (Botox),
      hyperhidrosis, cervical dystonia, migraine prevention.

3. CARDIOVASCULAR PHARMACOLOGY
--------------------------------
Antihypertensives:
  ACE inhibitors (ramipril, lisinopril): Block conversion Ang I → Ang II → vasodilation +
    ↓ aldosterone → ↓ BP. Cardioprotective post-MI. SE: Dry cough (bradykinin accumulation).
  ARBs (losartan, valsartan): Block AT1 receptor → vasodilation. No cough. Renoprotective.
  CCBs — dihydropyridines (amlodipine): Block L-type Ca2+ channels in vascular smooth muscle
    → vasodilation → ↓ BP. Also non-DHP (verapamil, diltiazem) → cardiac effects.
  Thiazide diuretics (bendroflumethiazide): Block NCC in DCT → ↓ Na+/water retention → ↓ BP.
  Spironolactone: Aldosterone antagonist → ↑ Na+ excretion + ↑ K+ retention. Heart failure.
  Beta-blockers: ↓ HR + contractility → ↓ cardiac output → ↓ BP.

Antiarrhythmics (Vaughan Williams):
  Class I: Na+ channel blockers (flecainide → AF; lidocaine → ventricular arrhythmia).
  Class II: Beta-blockers (metoprolol → rate control in AF, SVT).
  Class III: K+ channel blockers → prolong action potential (amiodarone, sotalol → AF).
  Class IV: CCBs (verapamil, diltiazem → rate control in AF).

Statins (HMG-CoA reductase inhibitors):
  Atorvastatin, rosuvastatin, simvastatin. Block cholesterol synthesis → upregulate LDL
  receptors → ↓ LDL. 30–50% LDL reduction. ~25% ↓ CV events per 1 mmol/L LDL reduction.
  SE: Myopathy (↑ CK); rhabdomyolysis (rare); hepatotoxicity.

4. CNS PHARMACOLOGY
--------------------
Antidepressants:
  SSRIs (fluoxetine, sertraline, citalopram): Block SERT → ↑ synaptic serotonin.
    First-line; 4–6 weeks onset. SE: GI, sexual dysfunction, initial anxiety.
  SNRIs (venlafaxine, duloxetine): Block SERT + NET → ↑ 5-HT + NE.
  TCAs (amitriptyline): Block multiple transporters; more SE; used in neuropathic pain/migraine.
  MAOIs (phenelzine): Block MAO → ↑ monoamines; tyramine cheese reaction (hypertensive crisis).
  Ketamine (esketamine): NMDA antagonist; rapid antidepressant (hours); treatment-resistant depression.

Antipsychotics:
  Typical (haloperidol, chlorpromazine): D2 receptor antagonist. Positive symptoms.
    SE: EPS (extrapyramidal symptoms — Parkinsonism, tardive dyskinesia, akathisia), hyperprolactinaemia.
  Atypical (clozapine, olanzapine, risperidone, quetiapine): D2 + 5-HT2A antagonism.
    Fewer EPS; more metabolic SE (weight gain, diabetes). Clozapine: Most effective; agranulocytosis.
  Dopamine hypothesis: Excess D2 activity → positive symptoms of schizophrenia.

Analgesics:
  NSAIDs (ibuprofen, naproxen, diclofenac): COX-1/2 inhibition → ↓ PGE2 → analgesia, antipyretic.
    SE: Peptic ulcer (↓ protective PGI2/mucus), renal impairment, platelet dysfunction.
  Opioids (morphine, codeine, oxycodone, fentanyl): Mu-opioid receptor agonists.
    Analgesia + euphoria + respiratory depression + constipation. Tolerance; dependence.
    Naloxone: Mu-antagonist; reverses opioid overdose.
  Paracetamol/Acetaminophen: Mechanism unclear; central COX-3? Cannabinoid pathway?
    Overdose → NAPQI accumulates → hepatotoxicity → liver failure; antidote = N-acetylcysteine.

5. PHARMACOGENOMICS
--------------------
Pharmacogenomics studies how genetic variants affect drug response.

Key Examples:
  CYP2D6: Codeine → morphine (by CYP2D6). Poor metabolisers → no analgesia.
    Ultrarapid metabolisers → toxic morphine levels. Used to guide opioid prescribing.
  CYP2C19: Clopidogrel → active metabolite. Poor metabolisers (20% Asians) → inadequate
    antiplatelet effect → increased stent thrombosis risk. Test before PCI.
  HLA-B*57:01: Predicts abacavir (HIV drug) hypersensitivity. Test mandatory before prescribing.
  HLA-B*15:02: Carbamazepine → Stevens-Johnson syndrome in Asians. Screen before use.
  TPMT (thiopurine methyltransferase): Metabolises azathioprine/6-MP. Low activity
    → drug accumulation → severe myelosuppression. Test before starting.
  VKORC1 + CYP2C9: Warfarin dose calculation algorithms use both variants.
    VKORC1 (warfarin target) variant → lower dose needed.
  DPYD: Dihydropyrimidine dehydrogenase; metabolises 5-FU and capecitabine.
    Deficiency → severe neutropenia/mucositis. Screen before fluoropyrimidine therapy.

6. BIOLOGICS & ADVANCED THERAPEUTICS
---------------------------------------
Monoclonal Antibodies (mAbs):
  Murine (-omab) → Chimeric (-ximab) → Humanised (-zumab) → Fully human (-umab).
  IgG1 backbone: Fc-mediated ADCC + CDC. IgG4: No ADCC (for receptor blocking only).
  Bispecific antibodies: Two antigen-binding sites (blinatumomab: CD19×CD3).
  ADCs (Antibody-Drug Conjugates): mAb + cytotoxic payload. Trastuzumab emtansine (T-DM1);
    sacituzumab govitecan.

Therapeutic Proteins:
  Insulin, EPO, G-CSF, Factor VIII, GLP-1 agonists, anti-VEGF (bevacizumab).
  Made in CHO cells (most), E. coli (insulin), yeast (EPO, Hep B vaccine antigen).

Gene Therapy:
  In vivo: AAV vectors delivering functional gene (Zolgensma for SMA; Luxturna for RPE65).
  Ex vivo: Cells removed, modified, reinfused (CAR-T, HSCT with gene correction).
  Sickle cell disease: Casgevy (exagamglogene autotemcel) — first CRISPR therapy; BCL11A
    erythroid enhancer editing → fetal haemoglobin reactivation. FDA approved December 2023.

mRNA Therapeutics:
  Modified mRNA + lipid nanoparticle (LNP) delivery → intracellular protein expression.
  COVID-19 vaccines demonstrated platform safety/efficacy. Expanding to: cancer vaccines
  (personalised neoantigen), infectious disease (flu, RSV, HIV), rare diseases,
  protein replacement therapies.
  Pseudouridine modification (Karikó & Weissman, Nobel 2023): Prevents mRNA from
  triggering innate immune response → enables therapeutic use.

[DRUG DISCOVERY] Modern pipeline:
  Target identification → Target validation → Hit finding (HTS, fragment screening,
  AI-based design) → Lead optimisation → Candidate selection → Preclinical
  (in vitro, in vivo safety/efficacy) → Phase I (safety, dose-finding in healthy
  volunteers ~20–100) → Phase II (efficacy/safety in patients ~100–500) →
  Phase III (large RCT ~1,000–10,000) → Regulatory submission (FDA/EMA) → Approval.
  Average time: ~12–15 years. Average cost: ~$2–3 billion per approved drug.
  Success rate: ~1 in 5,000–10,000 compounds screened reach approval.
