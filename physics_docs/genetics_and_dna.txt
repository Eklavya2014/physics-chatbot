1. DNA STRUCTURE
----------------
DNA (Deoxyribonucleic acid) is the molecule carrying genetic information in all
living organisms. Watson and Crick described its double helix structure in 1953,
building on Rosalind Franklin's X-ray diffraction data.

Structure:
- Two antiparallel strands wound in a double helix.
- Backbone: alternating deoxyribose sugar + phosphate groups.
- Bases pair via hydrogen bonds: Adenine–Thymine (2 bonds), Guanine–Cytosine (3 bonds).
- DNA is read 5' to 3'.

DNA vs RNA:
Feature        | DNA                        | RNA
---------------|----------------------------|---------------------------
Sugar          | Deoxyribose                | Ribose
Strands        | Double-stranded            | Usually single-stranded
Bases          | A, T, G, C                 | A, U, G, C
Function       | Stores genetic information  | Carries/translates genetic info
Location       | Nucleus (mainly)           | Nucleus and cytoplasm

1.1 KEY TERMS
--------------
- Gene         : Segment of DNA coding for a protein or functional RNA.
- Allele       : Alternative forms of a gene (e.g., brown vs blue eye alleles).
- Genotype     : An organism's genetic makeup (e.g., BB, Bb, bb).
- Phenotype    : Observable traits (e.g., brown eyes).
- Homozygous   : Two identical alleles (BB or bb).
- Heterozygous : Two different alleles (Bb).
- Dominant     : Allele that masks the other when present (B).
- Recessive    : Allele masked by the dominant (b).

1.2 MENDELIAN GENETICS
-----------------------
Gregor Mendel's laws (derived from pea plant experiments):

Law of Segregation:
  Each organism has two alleles per trait. During gamete formation, alleles
  separate so each gamete carries only one allele.

Law of Independent Assortment:
  Genes for different traits are inherited independently (when on different
  chromosomes). Basis for the 9:3:3:1 dihybrid ratio.

Monohybrid Cross Example (Tt x Tt):
  Offspring: 1 TT : 2 Tt : 1 tt → 3 tall : 1 short (3:1 phenotype ratio)

2. DNA REPLICATION
------------------
Semi-conservative: each new helix = one original strand + one new strand.
Direction: always 5' → 3'. Occurs in S phase of the cell cycle.

Key Enzymes:
- Helicase          : Unwinds/separates the double helix.
- Primase           : Synthesizes short RNA primers as starting points.
- DNA Polymerase III : Adds complementary nucleotides (5'→3' only).
- DNA Polymerase I  : Removes RNA primers; replaces with DNA.
- DNA Ligase        : Seals nicks between Okazaki fragments.
- Topoisomerase     : Relieves torsional stress ahead of replication fork.

Leading Strand: Synthesized continuously toward the fork.
Lagging Strand : Synthesized discontinuously as Okazaki fragments.

3. TRANSCRIPTION & TRANSLATION (THE CENTRAL DOGMA)
----------------------------------------------------
Information flow: DNA → mRNA → Protein

Transcription (nucleus):
  RNA polymerase binds the promoter region, unwinds DNA, and synthesizes
  pre-mRNA complementary to the template strand.
  Eukaryotic mRNA processing:
    - 5' cap added (7-methylguanosine)
    - 3' poly-A tail added
    - Splicing: introns removed by spliceosomes; exons joined.

Translation (ribosomes):
  mRNA codons (3-nucleotide sequences) are read by ribosomes.
  tRNA molecules carry amino acids with matching anticodons.

  Stages:
    Initiation   : Ribosome assembles at start codon AUG (Met).
    Elongation   : tRNA brings amino acids; peptide bonds form via
                   peptidyl transferase activity of the large ribosome subunit.
    Termination  : Stop codon (UAA, UAG, or UGA) reached; release factor
                   causes polypeptide release and ribosome disassembly.

4. INHERITANCE PATTERNS BEYOND MENDEL
---------------------------------------
- Incomplete Dominance : Heterozygote shows intermediate phenotype.
                         Example: red x white flowers = pink offspring.
- Codominance          : Both alleles expressed fully in heterozygote.
                         Example: AB blood type (IA and IB both expressed).
- Sex-Linked (X-linked): Gene on X chromosome. Males (XY) only have one copy.
                         Example: haemophilia, red-green colour blindness.
- Polygenic Inheritance: Multiple genes control one trait → continuous variation.
                         Example: skin colour, height, IQ.
- Epistasis            : One gene masks/modifies expression of another gene.

5. GENE REGULATION
-------------------
Gene expression is regulated at multiple levels:
  Transcriptional → Post-transcriptional → Translational → Post-translational

Transcriptional Regulation (Eukaryotes):
- Enhancers/Silencers: Cis-regulatory elements acting over large distances via
  chromatin looping. Can be thousands of base pairs from the promoter.
- Transcription Factors (TFs): Bind specific DNA motifs; recruit coactivators
  or the Mediator complex to stimulate RNA Pol II.
- Chromatin Remodeling: SWI/SNF complexes reposition nucleosomes to expose DNA.
- Histone Modifications:
    Acetylation (HATs) → open chromatin → active transcription
    Deacetylation (HDACs) → closed chromatin → gene silencing
    Methylation (HMTs): context-dependent (H3K4me3 = active; H3K27me3 = silent)
- DNA Methylation: CpG methylation by DNMTs silences genes. Key in development,
  imprinting, and cancer (hypermethylation of tumour suppressor promoters).

Post-Transcriptional Regulation:
- microRNA (miRNA)  : ~22 nt ncRNAs loaded into RISC complex. Bind mRNA 3'UTR;
                      cause mRNA degradation or translational repression.
- siRNA             : Small interfering RNA triggers sequence-specific mRNA
                      cleavage. Basis of RNAi technology.
- lncRNA            : >200 nt non-coding RNAs regulate chromatin architecture.
                      Example: Xist mediates X-chromosome inactivation.
- Alternative Splicing: ~95% of human multi-exon genes alternatively spliced,
                      producing multiple protein isoforms from one gene.

6. CRISPR-Cas9 GENOME EDITING
-------------------------------
Cas9 nuclease is guided to a specific DNA sequence by a single guide RNA (sgRNA).
It creates a double-strand break (DSB) repaired by:

- NHEJ (Non-Homologous End Joining):
    Error-prone; introduces indels (insertions/deletions) → disrupts gene.
    Used for gene knockouts.

- HDR (Homology-Directed Repair):
    Uses a DNA template for precise edits → gene correction or insertion.
    Less efficient; requires template DNA.

Advanced CRISPR Tools:
- Base Editing         : Cytosine base editors (CBEs) or adenine base editors
                         (ABEs) make single nucleotide changes without DSBs.
- Prime Editing        : "Search and replace" genome editing via pegRNA +
                         reverse transcriptase. No DSB required.
- CRISPRi/CRISPRa      : Catalytically dead dCas9 fused to repressors or
                         activators for transcriptional control without cutting.
- CRISPR Screens       : Genome-wide sgRNA libraries identify essential genes
                         or drug targets at scale.

7. MUTATIONS & DNA REPAIR
--------------------------
Types of Mutations:
- Point Mutation      : Single base change.
    Silent            : No amino acid change (redundancy of genetic code).
    Missense          : Different amino acid (e.g., sickle-cell: Glu→Val in HbB).
    Nonsense          : Premature stop codon → truncated protein.
- Frameshift          : Insertion/deletion shifts reading frame → usually
                         non-functional protein.
- Chromosomal         : Deletions, duplications, inversions, translocations.

DNA Repair Mechanisms:
- Base Excision Repair (BER)    : Fixes small base lesions (oxidation, alkylation).
- Nucleotide Excision Repair (NER): Fixes bulky lesions (UV-induced thymine dimers).
  Defect causes Xeroderma Pigmentosum (extreme UV sensitivity).
- Mismatch Repair (MMR)         : Corrects replication errors; defect linked to
  hereditary non-polyposis colorectal cancer (Lynch syndrome).
- Homologous Recombination (HR) : Error-free DSB repair using sister chromatid.
  BRCA1/BRCA2 are key HR factors; mutations raise breast/ovarian cancer risk.
