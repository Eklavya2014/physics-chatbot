CHAPTER 3: ELECTRICITY
=======================

ELECTRIC CHARGE AND CURRENT

Electric Charge:
- Matter is made of atoms. Atoms have protons (+) and electrons (-).
- Like charges repel; unlike charges attract.
- SI unit of charge: Coulomb (C)
- Charge on one electron = -1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ C

Electric Current:
Electric current is the rate of flow of electric charges through a conductor.
I = Q/t
Where I = current (Ampere), Q = charge (Coulomb), t = time (second)
SI unit: Ampere (A)

Conventional current flows from positive to negative terminal (outside battery).
Electron flow is from negative to positive terminal.

ELECTRIC POTENTIAL AND POTENTIAL DIFFERENCE

Electric Potential:
The electric potential at a point is the work done in bringing a unit positive charge from infinity to that point.

Potential Difference (Voltage):
The potential difference between two points is the work done in moving a unit positive charge from one point to the other.
V = W/Q
SI unit: Volt (V) = J/C

OHM'S LAW
Statement: The current flowing through a conductor is directly proportional to the potential difference across its ends, provided temperature and other physical conditions remain constant.
V ∝ I
V = IR
Where R = resistance of the conductor

A graph of V vs I is a straight line passing through the origin for an ohmic conductor.

RESISTANCE
Resistance is the property of a conductor to oppose the flow of electric current.
R = V/I
SI unit: Ohm (Ω)

Factors affecting resistance:
1. Length (l): R ∝ l (resistance increases with length)
2. Area of cross-section (A): R ∝ 1/A (resistance decreases with larger area)
3. Material (resistivity ρ): R = ρl/A
4. Temperature: Resistance increases with temperature for metals.

Resistivity (ρ):
R = ρl/A
SI unit of resistivity: Ohm-meter (Ω·m)
- Good conductors (copper, silver): very low resistivity (~10⁻⁸ Ω·m)
- Insulators (rubber, glass): very high resistivity (~10¹² Ω·m)
- Semiconductors (silicon): intermediate resistivity

COMBINATION OF RESISTANCES

Series Combination:
- Same current flows through each resistor.
- Voltage is divided among resistors.
- R_total = R₁ + R₂ + R₃ + ...
- Total resistance is always greater than any individual resistance.

Parallel Combination:
- Same voltage across each resistor.
- Current is divided among resistors.
- 1/R_total = 1/R₁ + 1/R₂ + 1/R₃ + ...
- Total resistance is always less than the smallest individual resistance.

Advantages of Parallel Connection (household wiring):
- Each appliance gets the same voltage.
- If one device fails, others continue to work.
- Each device can be switched on/off independently.
- Total resistance is lower, so more current can be drawn.

ELECTRIC POWER AND ENERGY

Electric Power:
The rate at which electrical energy is consumed or dissipated.
P = VI = I²R = V²/R
SI unit: Watt (W)

Commercial unit of electrical energy: kilowatt-hour (kWh)
1 kWh = 3.6 × 10⁶ J = 1 unit of electricity

Calculation of electricity bill:
Energy (in kWh) = Power (in kW) × Time (in hours)
Cost = Energy (kWh) × Rate per unit

HEATING EFFECT OF ELECTRIC CURRENT

Joule's Law of Heating:
The heat produced in a conductor is:
H = I²Rt
Where H = heat (Joules), I = current, R = resistance, t = time

This heat is produced because electrons collide with atoms in the conductor, transferring energy.

Applications of Heating Effect:
- Electric bulb (tungsten filament, melting point 3380°C)
- Electric iron, toaster, oven, heater
- Electric fuse

ELECTRIC FUSE
A safety device that protects circuits from overloading and short circuits.
- Made of low melting point alloy (tin-lead alloy).
- When current exceeds safe limit, fuse wire melts and breaks the circuit.
- Fuse is always connected in series.
- Fuse rating: 1A, 2A, 5A, 10A, 15A, etc.

MCB (Miniature Circuit Breaker):
Modern alternative to fuse. Can be reset after tripping (fuse must be replaced).

SHORT CIRCUIT
When live wire and neutral wire come in direct contact, resistance becomes almost zero and very high current flows. This can cause fires.

OVERLOADING
When too many appliances are connected to a single circuit, drawing more current than the wire can safely handle. This can melt insulation and cause fires.

EARTHING (GROUNDING)
A safety measure where the body of electrical appliances is connected to the earth (ground). If a live wire accidentally touches the appliance body, current flows to earth instead of through a person.

ELECTRIC CIRCUIT COMPONENTS AND SYMBOLS
- Cell: provides EMF (electromotive force)
- Battery: combination of cells
- Switch: opens/closes circuit
- Resistor: opposes current flow
- Variable resistor (Rheostat): adjustable resistance
- Ammeter: measures current (connected in series)
- Voltmeter: measures voltage (connected in parallel)
- Galvanometer: detects small currents

IMPORTANT FORMULAS SUMMARY
- Current: I = Q/t
- Ohm's Law: V = IR
- Resistance: R = ρl/A
- Series resistance: R_s = R₁ + R₂ + R₃
- Parallel resistance: 1/R_p = 1/R₁ + 1/R₂ + 1/R₃
- Power: P = VI = I²R = V²/R
- Heat: H = I²Rt = VIt = V²t/R
- Energy: E = Pt (in kWh: E = P(kW) × t(h))

NUMERICAL EXAMPLES

Example 1:
A bulb has resistance 100 Ω. If a voltage of 220 V is applied, find the current and power.
Solution:
I = V/R = 220/100 = 2.2 A
P = VI = 220 × 2.2 = 484 W

Example 2:
Two resistors of 6 Ω and 12 Ω are connected in parallel. Find equivalent resistance.
Solution:
1/R = 1/6 + 1/12 = 2/12 + 1/12 = 3/12
R = 12/3 = 4 Ω

Example 3:
A family uses 250 W TV for 5 hours, 100 W bulb for 10 hours, and 1000 W heater for 2 hours per day. Find daily energy consumption in kWh.
Solution:
TV: 250 × 5 = 1250 Wh = 1.25 kWh
Bulb: 100 × 10 = 1000 Wh = 1.0 kWh
Heater: 1000 × 2 = 2000 Wh = 2.0 kWh
Total = 4.25 kWh per day

IMPORTANT QUESTIONS AND ANSWERS

Q: Why are copper and aluminum used as electric wires?
A: They are good conductors (very low resistivity), malleable, and relatively cheap. Copper has resistivity of about 1.7 × 10⁻⁸ Ω·m.

Q: Why is tungsten used in electric bulbs?
A: Tungsten has a very high melting point (3380°C), so it doesn't melt even when it glows white hot. It also has high resistivity, generating enough heat and light.

Q: Why does the resistance of a wire increase with temperature?
A: As temperature increases, atoms vibrate more vigorously, increasing the frequency of collisions with electrons. This makes it harder for electrons to flow, increasing resistance.

Q: What is the difference between EMF and terminal voltage?
A: EMF (electromotive force) is the total voltage a cell can provide. Terminal voltage is the actual voltage across the cell terminals when current is flowing, which is less than EMF due to the internal resistance of the cell.
