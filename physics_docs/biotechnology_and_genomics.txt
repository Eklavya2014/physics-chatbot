1. BIOTECHNOLOGY — WHAT IS IT?
--------------------------------
Biotechnology uses living organisms, cells, or biological systems to develop
products and technologies that benefit humans. Modern biotechnology began with
recombinant DNA technology in the 1970s.

Key Applications:
- Medicine     : Insulin production, vaccines, gene therapy, cancer biologics.
- Agriculture  : GMO crops, disease-resistant plants, improved yields.
- Forensics    : DNA fingerprinting, paternity testing, crime scene analysis.
- Environment  : Bioremediation (bacteria clean up oil spills); biofuels.
- Industry     : Enzymes in detergents, food production, bioplastics.

1.1 BASIC TOOLS OF BIOTECHNOLOGY
----------------------------------
Restriction Enzymes:
  "Molecular scissors" — cut DNA at specific recognition sequences (4–8 bp).
  Produce sticky ends (most common) or blunt ends.
  Example: EcoRI recognises GAATTC → cuts between G and A on both strands.
  Source: Bacterial immune defence against foreign DNA.
  Application: Creating recombinant DNA by cutting + ligating DNA fragments.

DNA Ligase:
  "Molecular glue" — joins DNA fragments by forming phosphodiester bonds.
  Used together with restriction enzymes in cloning.

Vectors:
  DNA molecules used to carry foreign DNA into host cells.
  Types:
  - Plasmids         : Circular bacterial DNA; most common for small inserts.
  - Bacteriophages   : Viruses that infect bacteria; larger inserts.
  - BACs/YACs        : Bacterial/Yeast Artificial Chromosomes; very large inserts.
  - Viral vectors    : Adenovirus, AAV, lentivirus — used in gene therapy.

Gel Electrophoresis:
  Separates DNA, RNA, or proteins by size using an electric field.
  DNA (negatively charged) migrates toward positive electrode.
  Smaller fragments migrate faster → further from wells.
  Ethidium bromide or SYBR Green stains DNA → visible under UV light.
  Used for: visualising PCR products, restriction digests, DNA quality.

PCR (Polymerase Chain Reaction):
  Amplifies specific DNA sequences exponentially in vitro.
  Components: Template DNA, two primers, Taq polymerase, dNTPs, buffer.
  Cycle: Denaturation (94°C) → Annealing (50–65°C) → Extension (72°C)
  30 cycles → ~1 billion copies from one template molecule.
  Applications: Diagnostics (COVID-19 PCR test), forensics, cloning, sequencing.

2. DNA SEQUENCING
------------------
Sanger Sequencing (First Generation):
  Chain termination method (Sanger, 1977). Uses ddNTPs (dideoxy-nucleotides) that
  lack 3'-OH → terminate chain extension at different points → fragments of all
  lengths → separated by electrophoresis → sequence read.
  Accuracy: Very high (~99.99%). Throughput: Low (one read at a time).
  Still used for: targeted sequencing, validating Sanger clones, clinical confirmation.

Next-Generation Sequencing (NGS / Short-Read):
  Massively parallel sequencing — millions of fragments simultaneously.
  Illumina platform: Sequence by synthesis; fluorescent reversible terminators.
  Applications:
  - Whole Genome Sequencing (WGS): Entire ~3.2 Gb human genome.
  - Whole Exome Sequencing (WES): Coding regions only (~1.5% of genome).
  - RNA-Seq: Transcriptomics; gene expression profiling.
  - ChIP-Seq: Maps protein-DNA interactions (TF binding, histone marks).
  - ATAC-Seq: Open chromatin regions; identifies regulatory elements.

Third-Generation Sequencing (Long-Read):
  Oxford Nanopore: DNA passes through protein nanopore; ionic current changes
  identify bases. Reads >100 kb; real-time; no amplification needed.
  PacBio SMRT: Single-molecule real-time; ~20–30 kb reads; high accuracy HiFi mode.
  Advantage: Resolve repetitive regions, structural variants, full-length transcripts.

3. GENOMICS
------------
The Human Genome Project (HGP):
  International collaboration (1990–2003). First ~complete human genome sequence.
  Findings: ~3.2 billion bp; ~20,000–25,000 protein-coding genes; only ~1.5%
  of genome is protein-coding; ~50% is repetitive sequences (transposons, SINEs, LINEs).

Comparative Genomics:
  Human-chimp: ~98.7% DNA identity. Human-mouse: ~85% coding region similarity.
  Synteny: Conservation of gene order between species.
  Model organisms: E. coli, S. cerevisiae (yeast), C. elegans, Drosophila, zebrafish,
  mouse — all sequenced; invaluable for understanding human gene function.

Functional Genomics:
- Transcriptomics : Study of all RNA in a cell (RNA-Seq).
- Proteomics      : Study of all proteins (mass spectrometry).
- Metabolomics    : Study of all metabolites.
- Epigenomics     : Genome-wide epigenetic modifications (ENCODE project).
- Interactomics   : Protein-protein interaction networks.

GWAS (Genome-Wide Association Studies):
  Compare SNP frequencies across genomes of affected vs unaffected individuals.
  Identifies genetic loci associated with complex traits/diseases.
  Identified variants for: type 2 diabetes (TCF7L2), coronary artery disease,
  breast cancer (BRCA regions), schizophrenia, Alzheimer's (APOE-e4, CLU, CR1).
  Limitation: Most GWAS hits are in non-coding regions; missing heritability problem.

4. RECOMBINANT DNA TECHNOLOGY & CLONING
-----------------------------------------
Molecular Cloning Workflow:
  1. Isolate gene of interest (via PCR or restriction digestion).
  2. Cut vector + insert with compatible restriction enzymes.
  3. Ligate insert into vector → recombinant plasmid.
  4. Transform into E. coli (heat shock or electroporation).
  5. Select transformants (antibiotic resistance marker on vector).
  6. Screen clones (colony PCR, restriction mapping, sequencing).
  7. Express protein (IPTG induction if under lac promoter control).

Expression Systems:
  E. coli      : Fast, cheap; no post-translational modifications.
  Yeast        : Glycosylation; used for Hep B vaccine antigen, insulin.
  Insect cells : Baculovirus system; complex proteins.
  Mammalian    : CHO cells; proper folding/glycosylation; biologics (monoclonal Ab).

Recombinant Products:
  Insulin (Humulin): First recombinant protein (1982); expressed in E. coli.
  Erythropoietin (EPO): Treats anaemia in CKD; expressed in CHO cells.
  Monoclonal antibodies: Adalimumab (anti-TNF); trastuzumab (anti-HER2).
  Factor VIII/IX: Haemophilia treatment.
  Vaccines: Hepatitis B surface antigen (yeast); HPV VLPs (yeast).

5. ADVANCED GENOME EDITING
----------------------------
CRISPR-Cas9 (review and advanced):
  Type II CRISPR system from Streptococcus pyogenes. Cas9 + sgRNA.
  PAM sequence (NGG): Required adjacent to target for Cas9 cleavage.
  Delivery: Plasmid, RNP (ribonucleoprotein), LNP (lipid nanoparticle) for in vivo.
  Off-target effects: Detected by GUIDE-Seq, CIRCLE-Seq, Digenome-Seq.
  Reduced by: High-fidelity Cas9 variants (eSpCas9, HiFi Cas9), truncated guides.

Prime Editing:
  PE2: Cas9 nickase (H840A) + reverse transcriptase (M-MLT RT).
  pegRNA: Extended sgRNA with primer binding site + RT template.
  No DSB; no donor template required. Can install all 12 types of base substitution
  plus small indels. PE3: Additional nick on non-edited strand → increases efficiency.

Epigenome Editing:
  dCas9 (D10A + H840A mutations → catalytically dead) fused to:
  - DNMT3A          : CpG methylation → gene silencing.
  - TET1            : Demethylation → gene activation.
  - p300 (HAT)      : H3K27ac → activate enhancers.
  - KRAB (repressor) : H3K9me3 → gene silencing.
  - VPR (activator)  : VP64-p65-Rta → strong gene activation (CRISPRa).

6. SINGLE-CELL GENOMICS
-------------------------
scRNA-Seq (Single-cell RNA sequencing):
  Captures transcriptome of individual cells → reveals cell heterogeneity,
  rare cell types, cell state transitions.
  Workflow: Cell isolation (droplet-based: 10x Genomics; plate-based: Smart-Seq2)
  → cell barcoding → library prep → sequencing → dimensionality reduction
  (PCA, UMAP/tSNE) → clustering → cell type annotation.

Applications:
  - Atlas projects: Human Cell Atlas (all cell types in human body).
  - Cancer: Identify tumour subclones, drug-resistant subpopulations.
  - Development: Lineage tracing; reconstruct cell fate trajectories (pseudotime).
  - Immunology: TCR/BCR clonotype tracking; immune cell subset characterisation.

Spatial Transcriptomics:
  RNA sequencing with spatial coordinates preserved (Visium, MERFISH, seqFISH+).
  Reveals gene expression within tissue architecture. Nobel Prize 2020 cited
  related smFISH technology (Seurat + STARmap integration).

7. SYNTHETIC BIOLOGY
---------------------
Design principles: Standardised biological parts (BioBricks); rational circuit design.

Gene Circuits:
  Toggle switches, oscillators (repressilator), logic gates using genetic
  components (promoters, RBS, coding sequences, terminators).
  Applications: Biosensors, drug delivery switches, metabolic engineering.

Metabolic Engineering:
  Redesign microbial metabolism to produce valuable compounds.
  Artemisinin (anti-malarial): Yeast engineered with plant CYP450 genes.
  1,3-Propanediol: E. coli engineered to produce from glycerol → bioplastics.
  Lycopene, resveratrol, opioids: All produced in engineered microbes.

Whole Genome Synthesis:
  JCVI Syn 1.0 (2010): First synthetic bacterial genome (Mycoplasma mycoides).
  Sc2.0 project: Synthesising all 16 yeast chromosomes (redesigned; SCRaMbLE
  recombination system for rapid genome shuffling).
  Implications for understanding genome design principles.

[CLINICAL LINK] Gene therapy using AAV vectors:
  Luxturna (AAV2-RPE65): First FDA-approved in vivo gene therapy; treats
  inherited retinal dystrophy.
  Zolgensma (AAV9-SMN1): Spinal muscular atrophy (SMA) in infants; single IV dose.
  Hemgenix (AAV5-FIX): Haemophilia B; first approved for adults.
  Challenge: Manufacturing scale, immunogenicity, re-dosing limitations.
  mRNA therapeutics: BNT111 (melanoma mRNA vaccine); personalised neoantigen vaccines.
