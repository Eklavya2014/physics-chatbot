1. WHAT IS ETHOLOGY?
---------------------
Ethology is the scientific study of animal behaviour, especially in natural
environments. Founded by Konrad Lorenz, Nikolaas Tinbergen, and Karl von Frisch
(Nobel Prize 1973).

Tinbergen's Four Questions (levels of analysis for any behaviour):
  1. Causation   : What stimuli trigger the behaviour? (proximate mechanism)
  2. Development : How does behaviour change as animal develops?
  3. Function    : What is the adaptive value? (survival/reproduction benefit)
  4. Evolution   : How did the behaviour evolve? (phylogenetic history)

1.1 TYPES OF BEHAVIOUR
------------------------
Innate (Instinctive) Behaviour:
  Genetically programmed; does not require learning.
- Fixed Action Patterns (FAP): Stereotyped, invariant response triggered by a
  specific sign stimulus (releaser). Example: Male stickleback attacks anything
  red (red belly of rival); greylag goose egg-rolling.
- Kinesis: Non-directional movement in response to stimulus intensity.
  (Woodlice move faster in dry conditions — kinesis, not directed)
- Taxis: Directed movement toward (+) or away (-) from stimulus.
  Phototaxis, chemotaxis, gravitaxis.
- Reflexes: Simple, rapid involuntary responses (knee-jerk, pupil constriction).

Learned Behaviour:
- Habituation  : Decreased response to repeated harmless stimuli. Simplest form.
- Sensitisation: Increased response after intense/harmful stimulus.
- Classical Conditioning (Pavlov): Neutral stimulus paired with unconditioned
  stimulus → neutral stimulus alone elicits conditioned response.
- Operant Conditioning (Skinner): Behaviour strengthened (reinforcement) or
  weakened (punishment) by its consequences.
- Imprinting   : Learning during critical/sensitive period; irreversible.
  Filial imprinting: Young follow first moving object seen (Lorenz and geese).
- Insight Learning: Solving novel problems without trial and error.
  Example: Chimpanzees using tools; crows bending wire into hooks.
- Social/Observational Learning: Learning by watching others.

2. COMMUNICATION IN ANIMALS
-----------------------------
Modalities of Communication:
- Visual     : Body postures, colour displays, facial expressions, bioluminescence.
               Example: Peacock tail; threat display in gorillas; firefly flash patterns.
- Acoustic   : Calls, songs, ultrasound, substrate vibration.
               Birdsong: Learned; dialects exist; used for mate attraction + territory.
               Whale song: Humpback males; complex, evolving patterns; long-range (1000s km).
               Bat echolocation: 20–100 kHz; insectivorous bats; Doppler shift detection.
- Chemical   : Pheromones, trail markers, alarm substances, territorial scent marks.
               Ant trail pheromones (formic acid derivatives); moth sex pheromones
               detectable at femtomolar concentrations.
- Tactile    : Grooming, antennation, dance communication (honeybee waggle dance).
               Waggle dance (von Frisch): Direction = angle relative to sun;
               duration = distance; vigour = food quality.

3. MATING SYSTEMS & SEXUAL SELECTION
--------------------------------------
Darwin's Sexual Selection:
  Intrasexual selection: Competition within same sex (male-male combat).
  Intersexual selection (mate choice): Choosy sex (usually female) selects based
  on indicators of genetic quality or resources.

Mating Systems:
- Monogamy    : One male + one female (socially common in birds; ~90%; genetic
                monogamy rarer due to extra-pair copulations).
- Polygyny     : One male + multiple females (most mammals; harems; lek mating).
- Polyandry    : One female + multiple males (jacanas, pipefish).
- Promiscuity  : Multiple partners for both sexes.

Honest Signalling & Handicap Principle (Zahavi):
  Costly signals are honest because only high-quality individuals can afford them.
  Example: Peacock tail — large tail is a handicap; surviving with it signals fitness.
  Parasite resistance hypothesis (Hamilton & Zuk): Bright colouration signals
  resistance to parasites → female choice drives coevolution with parasites.

4. SOCIAL BEHAVIOUR
--------------------
Altruism & Kin Selection (Hamilton, 1964):
  Hamilton's Rule: Altruistic behaviour evolves when rB > C
  (r = relatedness coefficient; B = benefit to recipient; C = cost to actor).
  Inclusive fitness = direct fitness + indirect fitness (via shared genes in relatives).
  Eusociality: Highest level of social organisation. Reproductive division of labour,
  overlapping generations, cooperative brood care.
  Examples: Honeybees (r = 0.75 in haplodiploid system), ants, termites, naked mole-rats.

Reciprocal Altruism (Trivers, 1971):
  Helping non-relatives when help will be reciprocated in future.
  Requires: Recognition; memory; repeated interactions; cheating detection.
  Example: Vampire bat food sharing; cleaner fish; human cooperation.

Game Theory in Biology:
  Hawk-Dove game: Evolutionarily Stable Strategy (ESS) where mixed population
  resists invasion by alternative strategies.
  Prisoner's Dilemma: Cooperate vs defect; tit-for-tat is robust ESS strategy
  in iterated games (Axelrod tournaments).

5. NAVIGATION & MIGRATION
---------------------------
Bird Navigation Systems:
- Magnetic compass  : Iron-containing cells (magnetite) in beak; cryptochrome
                      proteins in retina detect Earth's magnetic field.
- Sun compass       : Time-compensated; uses circadian clock.
- Star compass      : Learned during sensitive period by observing stellar rotation
                      around Polaris.
- Olfactory maps    : Homing pigeons use odour maps in familiar areas.
- Landmark memory   : Spatial memory of terrain features.

Long-Distance Migration:
  Arctic Tern: 70,000–90,000 km/year; Arctic to Antarctic and back.
  Monarch Butterfly: Eastern North America to Mexican oyamel fir forests.
    Multi-generational journey; navigation uses sun compass + magnetic field.
    Overwintering site discovered 1975.
  Salmon: Pacific salmon use olfactory imprinting to return to natal stream.

6. BEHAVIOURAL GENETICS & NEUROBIOLOGY
----------------------------------------
Genetic Basis of Behaviour:
  Period (per) gene in Drosophila: Controls circadian rhythm period length.
  fruitless (fru) gene: Controls male courtship behaviour; sexually dimorphic
  splicing produces different protein isoforms in males and females.
  foraging (for) gene: Encodes cGMP-dependent protein kinase. sitter vs rover
  alleles affect larval foraging behaviour; also influences bee division of labour.
  Vole vasopressin receptor (avpr1a): Distribution difference between monogamous
  prairie voles and promiscuous meadow voles. V1aR in nucleus accumbens →
  reward-bonding after mating → monogamy.

Neurogenetics of Learning (Aplysia model):
  Kandel (Nobel Prize 2000): Sea slug Aplysia; simple gill-withdrawal reflex.
  Short-term sensitisation: PKA phosphorylates K+ channels and synapsin →
  increased transmitter release.
  Long-term sensitisation: Repeated stimuli → PKA → CREB activation →
  new synapse formation → structural changes.
  Directly analogous to LTP mechanisms in vertebrates.

7. COGNITION & ANIMAL INTELLIGENCE
------------------------------------
Theory of Mind:
  Ability to attribute mental states to others ("mentalising").
  Great apes pass false-belief tasks (updated paradigm with eye-tracking).
  Ravens pass some false-belief tasks; corvids are remarkably intelligent.

Mirror Self-Recognition:
  Indicates some form of self-awareness. Pass: Great apes, dolphins, elephants,
  magpies, some fish (cleaner wrasse). Fail: Monkeys (generally).

Tool Use:
  New Caledonian crows: Manufacture and use hooked tools from memory; understand
  causal relationships; plan for future.
  Chimpanzees: Nut cracking, termite fishing, spear hunting (Senegal).
  Octopuses: Carry coconut shells as portable shelter; open jars.

Language Research:
  Honeybee waggle dance: Symbolic; conveys information about absent objects.
  Ape language: ASL-trained chimps/gorillas learn hundreds of signs; combine them;
  limited to ~2-unit combinations; no recursive grammar.
  Alex (African grey parrot, Pepperberg): Demonstrated counting, colour/shape
  concepts, understanding of zero concept.

[CONSERVATION LINK] Behavioural ecology informs conservation:
  Corridors designed based on dispersal behaviour. Anti-poaching: Training elephants
  to fear human scent/voices. Reintroduction: Teaching captive-bred wolves to hunt.
  Acoustic deterrents: Using recorded killer whale calls to deter belugas from ship
  strikes. Understanding animal cognition drives debates on welfare legislation.
