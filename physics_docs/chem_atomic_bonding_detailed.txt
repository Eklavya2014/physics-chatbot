ATOMIC STRUCTURE AND CHEMICAL BONDING - COMPREHENSIVE GUIDE
=============================================================

PART 1: ATOMIC STRUCTURE

DISCOVERY OF SUBATOMIC PARTICLES:
Electron: J.J. Thomson (1897) — cathode ray tube experiment. Charge/mass ratio = 1.76 × 10¹¹ C/kg.
Proton: Goldstein (1886) — canal rays (anode rays). Charge = +1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ C.
Neutron: James Chadwick (1932) — bombarded Be with alpha particles. Be + He → C + n.
Nucleus: Rutherford (1911) — gold foil experiment.

ATOMIC MODELS IN DETAIL:

Thomson's Model (Plum Pudding, 1904):
- Atom = uniform sphere of positive charge with electrons embedded like plums in pudding.
- Failure: Could not explain Rutherford's scattering experiment results.

Rutherford's Model (Nuclear Model, 1911):
- Gold foil experiment: thin gold foil bombarded with alpha particles.
- Observations: Most passed through (atom mostly empty), some deflected (nucleus), very few bounced back (direct hit on nucleus).
- Conclusions: (1) Atom mostly empty space, (2) Dense positive nucleus at centre, (3) Electrons in orbit outside.
- Failure: Accelerating electrons should emit radiation → spiral into nucleus → atom collapse in 10⁻⁸ s.

Bohr's Model (1913):
Postulates:
1. Electrons revolve in fixed circular orbits (stationary states) without emitting radiation.
2. Only certain orbits are allowed: mvr = nℏ = nh/2π (angular momentum quantized).
3. Energy emitted/absorbed when electron jumps: hν = E₂ - E₁.
Results for hydrogen-like atoms:
- Radius: rₙ = n²a₀/Z, a₀ = 0.529 Å (Bohr radius)
- Velocity: vₙ = Ze²/(2ε₀hn) = 2.18 × 10⁶ × Z/n m/s
- Energy: Eₙ = -13.6 Z²/n² eV = -2.18 × 10⁻¹⁸ Z²/n² J
- Frequency: ν = RHz²(1/n₁² - 1/n₂²), R_H = 1.097 × 10⁷ m⁻¹

Limitations of Bohr's Model:
- Only works for one-electron (hydrogen-like) species.
- Cannot explain splitting of spectral lines in magnetic field (Zeeman effect).
- Cannot explain splitting in electric field (Stark effect).
- Cannot explain fine structure of spectral lines.
- Violates Heisenberg's uncertainty principle.

QUANTUM MECHANICAL MODEL (Modern):
Based on: de Broglie wavelength + Heisenberg uncertainty + Schrödinger equation.
Orbital: 3D region where probability of finding electron is maximum (90% probability).
NOT a fixed orbit — just a probability distribution.

QUANTUM NUMBERS IN DETAIL:

1. Principal Quantum Number (n):
- Values: 1, 2, 3, 4, ... (positive integers)
- Determines: energy of shell, size of orbital, shell designation (K, L, M, N...)
- Maximum electrons in shell: 2n²

2. Azimuthal/Orbital Angular Momentum Quantum Number (l):
- Values: 0, 1, 2, ..., (n-1)
- Determines: shape of orbital, subshell
- l=0: s (sharp), l=1: p (principal), l=2: d (diffuse), l=3: f (fundamental)
- Number of orbitals in subshell: 2l+1
- Orbital angular momentum: L = √(l(l+1))ℏ

3. Magnetic Quantum Number (mₗ):
- Values: -l, -(l-1), ..., 0, ..., (l-1), l [total 2l+1 values]
- Determines: orientation of orbital in space, behavior in magnetic field
- s: 1 orbital (mₗ=0)
- p: 3 orbitals (mₗ = -1, 0, +1) → px, py, pz
- d: 5 orbitals (mₗ = -2, -1, 0, +1, +2) → dxy, dyz, dxz, dx²-y², dz²
- f: 7 orbitals

4. Spin Quantum Number (mₛ):
- Values: +½ or -½
- Intrinsic angular momentum of electron.
- Spin angular momentum: S = √(s(s+1))ℏ = (√3/2)ℏ
- Two electrons in same orbital must have opposite spins (Pauli principle).

ELECTRONIC CONFIGURATION RULES:
Aufbau Principle: Fill lowest energy orbitals first.
Energy order: 1s < 2s < 2p < 3s < 3p < 4s < 3d < 4p < 5s < 4d < 5p < 6s < 4f < 5d < 6p < 7s < 5f < 6d

Pauli Exclusion Principle: No two electrons can have identical set of all four quantum numbers. Maximum 2 electrons per orbital (with opposite spins).

Hund's Rule of Maximum Multiplicity: Electrons fill orbitals of same energy (degenerate) singly before pairing. All unpaired electrons have same spin direction.

EXCEPTIONS IN ELECTRON CONFIGURATIONS:
Cr: [Ar] 3d⁵ 4s¹ (NOT [Ar] 3d⁴ 4s²) — half-filled 3d is extra stable
Cu: [Ar] 3d¹⁰ 4s¹ (NOT [Ar] 3d⁹ 4s²) — fully-filled 3d is extra stable
Mo: [Kr] 4d⁵ 5s¹, Ag: [Kr] 4d¹⁰ 5s¹, Au: [Xe] 4f¹⁴ 5d¹⁰ 6s¹

STABILITY OF COMPLETELY AND HALF-FILLED ORBITALS:
Half-filled (d⁵, f⁷) and fully-filled (d¹⁰, f¹⁴) subshells are extra stable because:
- Symmetrical electron distribution
- Maximization of exchange energy

IONIZATION ENERGY (IE):
Energy needed to remove electron from gaseous atom.
First IE < Second IE < Third IE (always increases).
Trends: Increases across period (left to right), decreases down group.
Exceptions: IE(B) < IE(Be), IE(O) < IE(N).
Why? B: 2p electron easier to remove than 2s. O: electron pairing repulsion in 2px.

ELECTRON AFFINITY (EA):
Energy released when neutral gaseous atom gains electron.
Cl has highest EA (-349 kJ/mol), NOT fluorine (-333 kJ/mol).
Why? F is too small — high electron-electron repulsion in very compact 2p subshell.
Noble gases: EA ≈ 0 (stable configuration). N, Be, Mg also have nearly zero EA.
Second EA always positive (endothermic) — adding to already negative ion.

ELECTRONEGATIVITY:
Pauling scale: F (3.98) > O (3.44) > Cl (3.16) > N (3.04) > Br (2.96) > C (2.55).
Bond type based on electronegativity difference (Δχ):
Δχ < 0.5: non-polar covalent. 0.5-1.7: polar covalent. > 1.7: ionic.

PART 2: CHEMICAL BONDING

IONIC BONDING:
Formation: metal transfers electron(s) to non-metal.
Conditions: large electronegativity difference (>1.7), usually metal + nonmetal.
Lattice energy (U): Energy released when gaseous ions form 1 mole of solid crystal.
U = -N_A × M × Z+ × Z- × e² / (4πε₀ × r₀) × (1 - 1/n)
[M = Madelung constant, n = Born exponent]
Higher lattice energy → more stable ionic compound → higher melting point.
Madelung constants: NaCl = 1.748, CsCl = 1.763, ZnS = 1.638.

BORN-HABER CYCLE:
Thermodynamic cycle for ionic compounds.
ΔH°f = ΔH°sub + IE + ½ΔH°diss + EA + U (lattice energy)
For NaCl: ΔH°f = +108 + 496 + 121 + (-349) + (-787) = -411 kJ/mol

FAJAN'S RULES (Polarization):
Covalent character increases when:
1. High charge on cation (more polarizing): Al³⁺ > Mg²⁺ > Na⁺
2. Small size of cation (more polarizing power)
3. High charge on anion (more polarizable): I⁻ > Br⁻ > Cl⁻ > F⁻
4. Large size of anion (more polarizable)
Polarizing power = charge/size = charge density
AlCl₃ has more covalent character than NaCl.

COVALENT BONDING:
Lewis theory: shared electron pairs.
Octet rule: atoms share electrons to achieve 8 electrons in outer shell.
Octet rule exceptions:
- Incomplete octet: BF₃ (6e around B), BeCl₂ (4e around Be)
- Expanded octet: PCl₅ (10e), SF₆ (12e) — only for n ≥ 3
- Odd electron molecules: NO (11e), NO₂ (17e), ClO₂ (19e)

BOND PARAMETERS:
Bond length: Distance between nuclei of bonded atoms. Decreases with increasing bond order. Single > double > triple.
Bond energy (enthalpy): Energy to break 1 mole of bonds in gaseous state. Increases with bond order.
Bond order: (bonding electrons - antibonding electrons)/2 in MO theory.
Bond angle: Angle between two bonds at central atom.

MOLECULAR GEOMETRY — VSEPR DETAILED:
Steric number (SN) = bonding pairs + lone pairs on central atom.

SN=2: Linear (180°) — BeCl₂, CO₂, HCN, CS₂
SN=3: Trigonal planar (120°) — BF₃, SO₃, NO₃⁻
       With 1 LP: Bent (< 120°) — SO₂ (119°), O₃
SN=4: Tetrahedral (109.5°) — CH₄, SiCl₄, SO₄²⁻, PO₄³⁻
       With 1 LP: Trigonal pyramidal (107°) — NH₃, PH₃, PCl₃
       With 2 LP: Bent (104.5°) — H₂O, H₂S, OF₂
SN=5: Trigonal bipyramidal — PCl₅, AsF₅
       With 1 LP: See-saw (SF₄)
       With 2 LP: T-shaped (ClF₃)
       With 3 LP: Linear (XeF₂, I₃⁻)
SN=6: Octahedral (90°) — SF₆, [Co(NH₃)₆]³⁺
       With 1 LP: Square pyramidal (IF₅, BrF₅)
       With 2 LP: Square planar (XeF₄, [PtCl₄]²⁻)

Lone pair vs bond pair: LP-LP > LP-BP > BP-BP repulsion.
That's why NH₃ (107°) < CH₄ (109.5°), H₂O (104.5°) < NH₃ (107°).

HYBRIDIZATION IN DETAIL:
sp hybridization: 1s + 1p → 2 sp orbitals (linear, 180°)
Examples: BeCl₂, C₂H₂ (acetylene), CO₂, HCN, [Ag(NH₃)₂]⁺
Remaining 2 p orbitals → 2 π bonds in triple bonds.

sp² hybridization: 1s + 2p → 3 sp² orbitals (trigonal planar, 120°)
Examples: BF₃, C₂H₄ (ethylene), benzene, graphite, SO₃, NO₃⁻
Remaining 1 p orbital → 1 π bond in double bonds.

sp³ hybridization: 1s + 3p → 4 sp³ orbitals (tetrahedral, 109.5°)
Examples: CH₄, NH₃, H₂O, diamond, SiO₂, CCl₄

sp³d hybridization: trigonal bipyramidal — PCl₅
sp³d² hybridization: octahedral — SF₆, [Co(NH₃)₆]³⁺

SIGMA AND PI BONDS:
Sigma (σ): Head-on overlap, cylindrical symmetry, free rotation. Every single bond is σ.
Pi (π): Lateral (side) overlap of p orbitals, no rotation (causes cis-trans isomerism).
Single bond: 1σ. Double bond: 1σ + 1π. Triple bond: 1σ + 2π.
Pi bonds are weaker than sigma bonds.

RESONANCE:
When a molecule cannot be accurately described by a single Lewis structure.
Resonance hybrid is more stable than any contributing structure.
Resonance energy = stability gained.
Examples: benzene (150 kJ/mol), O₃, CO₃²⁻, NO₃⁻, SO₄²⁻, carboxylate ion.
Conditions: Same arrangement of atoms, same number of electrons, contributing structures differ only in electron positions.

INTERMOLECULAR FORCES (detailed):
1. Ion-ion (ionic bonds): 40-400 kJ/mol. Strongest.
2. Hydrogen bonds: 10-40 kJ/mol. X-H···Y (X,Y = F, O, N).
   Intramolecular H-bond: within molecule, forms ring → lowers bp (o-nitrophenol vs p-nitrophenol).
   Intermolecular H-bond: between molecules → raises bp (HF, H₂O, NH₃).
3. Dipole-dipole: 3-4 kJ/mol. Between polar molecules.
4. Dipole-induced dipole (Debye forces): between polar and non-polar.
5. London dispersion (van der Waals, instantaneous dipole): 0.05-40 kJ/mol. All molecules.
   Increases with: larger molecule, more electrons, higher polarizability, larger surface area.
   Explains: why alkanes become liquids/solids as chain length increases.

BOND POLARITY AND MOLECULAR POLARITY:
Bond polarity: due to electronegativity difference.
Molecular polarity: vector sum of bond dipoles + lone pair contributions.
Symmetric molecules are non-polar even with polar bonds:
CO₂ (O=C=O, linear): bond dipoles cancel.
CCl₄ (tetrahedral): bond dipoles cancel.
BF₃ (trigonal planar): bond dipoles cancel.
SF₆ (octahedral): bond dipoles cancel.
Unsymmetric molecules are polar: H₂O, NH₃, CHCl₃, HCl.
