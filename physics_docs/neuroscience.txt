1. THE NERVOUS SYSTEM — OVERVIEW
----------------------------------
Neuroscience is the study of the brain, spinal cord, and nervous system and how
they control behaviour, thought, emotion, sensation, and bodily functions.

The human brain contains ~86 billion neurons and ~85 billion non-neuronal cells.
It consumes ~20% of total body energy despite being only ~2% of body weight.

Divisions of the Nervous System:
  Central Nervous System (CNS)    : Brain + Spinal cord
  Peripheral Nervous System (PNS) :
    Somatic NS      : Voluntary; controls skeletal muscles; sensory input.
    Autonomic NS    : Involuntary; controls organs.
      Sympathetic   : Fight-or-flight (adrenergic; norepinephrine).
      Parasympathetic: Rest-and-digest (cholinergic; acetylcholine).
      Enteric NS    : "Second brain"; gut nervous system (~500 million neurons).

1.1 NEURON TYPES
-----------------
- Sensory (afferent) neurons : Carry signals FROM body TO CNS.
- Motor (efferent) neurons   : Carry signals FROM CNS TO muscles/glands.
- Interneurons               : Connect neurons within CNS; process information.

Neuron anatomy:
  Dendrites → Cell body (soma) → Axon hillock → Axon (myelinated segments +
  nodes of Ranvier) → Axon terminals → Synaptic vesicles → Synapse

Glial Cells (support cells):
- Astrocytes       : Blood-brain barrier; nutrient support; synaptic modulation.
- Oligodendrocytes : Myelinate axons in CNS (each cell myelinates ~50 axons).
- Schwann cells    : Myelinate axons in PNS (each cell myelinates 1 axon).
- Microglia        : Immune cells of CNS; phagocytosis of debris.
- Ependymal cells  : Line ventricles; produce cerebrospinal fluid (CSF).

2. NEURONAL SIGNALLING IN DEPTH
---------------------------------
Resting Membrane Potential (RMP): –70 mV
Maintained by:
  - Na+/K+ ATPase pump: 3 Na+ out, 2 K+ in (electrogenic).
  - K+ leak channels: K+ diffuses out, leaving negative charge inside.

Action Potential Steps:
  1. Stimulus reaches threshold (–55 mV).
  2. Depolarisation: Voltage-gated Na+ channels open → Na+ rushes in → +30 mV.
  3. Repolarisation: Na+ channels inactivate; voltage-gated K+ channels open
     → K+ flows out → membrane returns to negative.
  4. Hyperpolarisation (after-potential): K+ channels slow to close → brief
     overshoot below –70 mV.
  5. Refractory period: Absolute (no AP possible); Relative (larger stimulus needed).
  6. Na+/K+ pump restores ion distribution.

Propagation:
  Unmyelinated axons: AP spreads continuously (slow, ~0.5–2 m/s).
  Myelinated axons: Saltatory conduction — AP jumps between nodes of Ranvier
  (fast, up to 120 m/s). Myelin acts as electrical insulator.

Synaptic Transmission:
  AP reaches terminal → Ca2+ enters via voltage-gated Ca2+ channels →
  vesicle fusion (SNARE proteins: synaptobrevin, SNAP-25, syntaxin) →
  neurotransmitter released into synaptic cleft →
  binds postsynaptic receptors → EPSP or IPSP →
  NT removed by: reuptake (transporters), enzymatic degradation, diffusion.

Summation:
  Temporal: Multiple APs from one neuron in rapid succession.
  Spatial: Simultaneous input from multiple synapses.
  If EPSPs summate to threshold at axon hillock → AP generated.

4. SENSORY SYSTEMS
-------------------
Vision:
  Light → photoreceptors (rods: dim light; cones: colour [R, G, B]) →
  bipolar cells → retinal ganglion cells → optic nerve →
  lateral geniculate nucleus (thalamus) → primary visual cortex (V1, occipital).
  Rhodopsin (rod photopigment): retinal + opsin → light → all-trans-retinal →
  hyperpolarisation → reduced glutamate release → signal to bipolar cell.

Hearing:
  Sound waves → outer ear → tympanic membrane → ossicles (malleus, incus, stapes)
  → oval window → cochlea → basilar membrane vibrates →
  hair cells (stereocilia) deflect → K+ influx → depolarisation →
  auditory nerve → cochlear nucleus → inferior colliculus →
  medial geniculate nucleus → primary auditory cortex (A1, temporal lobe).
  Tonotopy: High frequencies at base; low at apex of cochlea.

5. SYNAPTIC PLASTICITY & LEARNING
-----------------------------------
Long-Term Potentiation (LTP):
  Molecular basis of learning and memory (Bliss & Lomo, 1973).
  High-frequency stimulation → AMPA receptor activation → depolarises postsynaptic
  membrane → Mg2+ block removed from NMDA receptor → Ca2+ enters via NMDA →
  CaMKII activation → AMPA receptor phosphorylation + insertion into membrane →
  enhanced synaptic strength.
  LTP maintenance: CREB activation → new protein synthesis → structural changes
  (dendritic spine growth; new synapses formed).

Long-Term Depression (LTD):
  Low-frequency stimulation → modest Ca2+ rise → calcineurin/PP2B activation →
  AMPA receptor dephosphorylation + internalisation → reduced synaptic strength.
  Role in motor learning (cerebellum) and memory erasure.

Spike-Timing Dependent Plasticity (STDP):
  Pre-before-post (within ~20ms): LTP. Post-before-pre: LTD.
  Hebbian learning: "Neurons that fire together, wire together."

6. NEUROLOGICAL & PSYCHIATRIC DISORDERS
-----------------------------------------
Parkinson's Disease:
  Loss of dopaminergic neurons in substantia nigra pars compacta → reduced
  striatal dopamine → basal ganglia circuit imbalance → rigidity, tremor,
  bradykinesia, postural instability.
  Pathology: Lewy bodies (alpha-synuclein aggregates).
  Treatment: L-DOPA + carbidopa; dopamine agonists; deep brain stimulation (STN).

Alzheimer's Disease:
  Two hallmarks: Amyloid-beta plaques (extracellular) + neurofibrillary tangles
  (intracellular tau hyperphosphorylation). Progressive loss of cholinergic
  neurons (basal forebrain) → memory/cognitive decline.
  Amyloid cascade hypothesis: APP cleavage by beta + gamma secretase →
  Abeta42 aggregation → synaptic dysfunction → neuronal death.
  Treatments: AChE inhibitors (donepezil); memantine (NMDA antagonist);
  new: lecanemab/donanemab (anti-amyloid antibodies).

Epilepsy:
  Recurrent seizures from abnormal, synchronous neuronal firing.
  Mechanism: Imbalance between excitation (glutamate) and inhibition (GABA).
  Channelopathies: Mutations in Na+ (SCN1A in Dravet syndrome), K+, Ca2+ channels.
  Treatment: Sodium channel blockers (valproate, carbamazepine); GABA enhancers
  (benzodiazepines, vigabatrin); ketogenic diet.

Depression:
  Monoamine hypothesis: Deficiency of serotonin, norepinephrine, dopamine.
  Modern view: Impaired neuroplasticity; reduced BDNF; HPA axis dysregulation;
  neuroinflammation; glutamate excess (ketamine → rapid antidepressant via NMDA
  antagonism + AMPA upregulation + BDNF release).
  Treatment: SSRIs, SNRIs, MAOIs, ketamine, electroconvulsive therapy (ECT),
  transcranial magnetic stimulation (TMS).

7. CONSCIOUSNESS & HIGHER BRAIN FUNCTION
-----------------------------------------
Global Workspace Theory (Baars/Dehaene):
  Consciousness arises when information is broadcast widely across frontoparietal
  networks. "Ignition" pattern: late, widespread cortical activation correlates
  with conscious perception.

Default Mode Network (DMN):
  Active during rest, self-referential thought, mind-wandering. Includes medial
  prefrontal cortex, posterior cingulate, angular gyrus. Deactivates during
  focused tasks. Disrupted in Alzheimer's, schizophrenia, depression.

Sleep & Memory Consolidation:
  Slow-wave sleep (NREM3): Memory replay; hippocampal-cortical dialogue;
  synaptic downscaling (synaptic homeostasis hypothesis).
  REM sleep: Procedural memory consolidation; emotional memory processing;
  theta oscillations; acetylcholine dominance.

[CLINICAL LINK] Psychedelic compounds (psilocybin, LSD) act on 5-HT2A receptors
in cortical pyramidal neurons → increased glutamate → disrupts DMN → increased
neural entropy and connectivity. Clinical trials show efficacy in treatment-
resistant depression and PTSD. Mechanism may involve BDNF-dependent neuroplasticity.
