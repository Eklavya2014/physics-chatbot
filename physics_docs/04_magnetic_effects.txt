CHAPTER 4: MAGNETIC EFFECTS OF ELECTRIC CURRENT
=================================================

MAGNETS AND MAGNETIC FIELDS

Properties of Magnets:
- Every magnet has two poles: North (N) and South (S).
- Like poles repel; unlike poles attract.
- Magnetic poles always exist in pairs (no magnetic monopole).
- A freely suspended magnet always aligns in the North-South direction.

Magnetic Field:
The region around a magnet where its magnetic force can be experienced.
- Represented by magnetic field lines (imaginary lines).
- Field lines emerge from the North pole and enter the South pole outside the magnet.
- Field lines are closed loops (inside the magnet they go from S to N).
- Field lines never intersect.
- Closer lines → stronger field; farther apart → weaker field.

OERSTED'S EXPERIMENT (1820)
Hans Christian Oersted discovered that a current-carrying conductor produces a magnetic field around it.

Observation: When current flows through a wire, a compass needle placed near it deflects. Reversing the current reverses the deflection.

This showed that electricity and magnetism are related (electromagnetism).

MAGNETIC FIELD DUE TO A STRAIGHT CURRENT-CARRYING CONDUCTOR
The field lines are concentric circles centered on the wire.

Right-Hand Thumb Rule (Maxwell's Corkscrew Rule):
If you hold the wire in your right hand with the thumb pointing in the direction of current, the curled fingers give the direction of the magnetic field.

Factors affecting magnetic field strength:
1. Magnitude of current (more current → stronger field)
2. Distance from wire (closer → stronger field)

MAGNETIC FIELD DUE TO A CIRCULAR LOOP
- Field lines near the wire are concentric circles.
- At the center of the loop, field lines are straight.
- The field at the center is perpendicular to the plane of the loop.

Direction: Use right-hand rule for each small segment of the loop.

Increasing field strength:
- Increase current
- Reduce radius of loop
- Increase number of turns

SOLENOID
A solenoid is a long coil of wire consisting of many circular turns.

Magnetic field of a solenoid:
- Inside the solenoid: Uniform, strong magnetic field parallel to the axis.
- Outside: Similar to that of a bar magnet.
- One end acts as a North pole, other as South pole.

The strength of the magnetic field increases with:
- More current
- More turns per unit length

ELECTROMAGNET
A solenoid with a soft iron core inside it acts as an electromagnet.
- The iron core gets strongly magnetized, greatly increasing the field strength.
- The magnetism can be switched on/off with the current.

Properties of Electromagnet:
- Can be switched on and off.
- Strength can be varied by changing current.
- Polarity can be reversed by reversing current.
- Much stronger than a permanent magnet of the same size.

Uses of Electromagnets:
- Electric bells
- Cranes for lifting heavy iron objects
- Loudspeakers
- MRI machines
- Electric motors

FORCE ON A CURRENT-CARRYING CONDUCTOR IN A MAGNETIC FIELD
A current-carrying conductor placed in an external magnetic field experiences a mechanical force (motor effect).

Fleming's Left-Hand Rule:
Stretch the thumb, index finger, and middle finger of the left hand mutually perpendicular.
- Index finger (forefinger): Direction of magnetic field (B)
- Middle finger: Direction of current (I)
- Thumb: Direction of force/motion (F)

This rule is used in electric motors.

Factors affecting the force:
- Magnitude of current (F ∝ I)
- Strength of magnetic field (F ∝ B)
- Length of conductor in the field (F ∝ l)
- Angle between current and field (F = BIl sin θ)

ELECTRIC MOTOR
A device that converts electrical energy into mechanical energy.

Principle: A current-carrying conductor in a magnetic field experiences a force (motor effect).

Parts of a DC Motor:
1. Armature (Coil): Rectangular coil of wire that rotates.
2. Magnet (Field Magnet): Provides the magnetic field (permanent or electromagnet).
3. Commutator (Split Ring): Reverses the direction of current after every half rotation to maintain rotation in the same direction.
4. Brushes: Carbon brushes in contact with the commutator, connecting to the external circuit.

Working:
- Current flows through the coil in the magnetic field.
- One side experiences upward force, the other downward → coil rotates.
- After half rotation, commutator reverses current → forces continue in same rotational direction.
- Continuous rotation is maintained.

Uses of Electric Motor:
- Fans, mixers, washing machines
- Water pumps
- Electric trains and cars
- Lifts and elevators

ELECTROMAGNETIC INDUCTION
When the magnetic flux (number of field lines) through a coil changes, an EMF (voltage) is induced in the coil. This is electromagnetic induction.

Discovered by Michael Faraday (1831).

Faraday's Law: The magnitude of induced EMF is proportional to the rate of change of magnetic flux.

Fleming's Right-Hand Rule:
Stretch thumb, index finger, and middle finger of the right hand mutually perpendicular.
- Index finger: Direction of magnetic field (B)
- Thumb: Direction of motion of conductor
- Middle finger: Direction of induced current

This rule is used in generators.

Ways to induce EMF:
1. Move the magnet into/out of the coil.
2. Move the coil into/out of the magnetic field.
3. Change current in a nearby coil (mutual induction).
4. Change the strength of the magnetic field.

ELECTRIC GENERATOR (DYNAMO)
A device that converts mechanical energy into electrical energy.

Principle: Electromagnetic induction.

Parts of an AC Generator:
1. Armature (Coil): Rectangular coil that rotates in the magnetic field.
2. Field Magnet: Provides the magnetic field.
3. Slip Rings: Two separate rings attached to the coil ends.
4. Brushes: Make contact with slip rings.

Working:
- The coil is rotated mechanically (by steam, water, wind, etc.).
- As the coil rotates, the flux through it changes → EMF is induced.
- The induced EMF alternates (changes direction) → AC is produced.

AC vs DC Generator:
- AC Generator: Uses slip rings → produces alternating current.
- DC Generator: Uses split ring commutator → produces direct current.

ALTERNATING CURRENT (AC) vs DIRECT CURRENT (DC)
Direct Current (DC):
- Current flows in one direction only.
- Produced by cells, batteries, DC generators.
- Cannot be transmitted over long distances efficiently.

Alternating Current (AC):
- Current direction alternates periodically.
- In India: frequency = 50 Hz (reverses 100 times/second), Voltage = 220 V.
- Can be stepped up/down using transformers.
- Used for household supply (more efficient for long-distance transmission).

DOMESTIC ELECTRIC CIRCUITS

Three wires in household wiring:
1. Live Wire (Phase wire): Red insulation, at 220 V.
2. Neutral Wire: Black insulation, at 0 V.
3. Earth Wire: Green insulation, connected to earth, for safety.

The potential difference between live and neutral is 220 V in India.

Household Circuit:
- Main fuse and main switch in the main supply box.
- Separate circuits for lights (5A) and power (15A).
- All appliances connected in parallel.
- Each circuit protected by its own fuse or MCB.

IMPORTANT FORMULAS
- Force on conductor: F = BIl sin θ
- For perpendicular field: F = BIl

KEY CONCEPTS SUMMARY
- Oersted: Current produces magnetic field.
- Faraday: Changing magnetic field produces current (EMF).
- Motor: Electrical → Mechanical energy (Left-hand rule).
- Generator: Mechanical → Electrical energy (Right-hand rule).

IMPORTANT QUESTIONS AND ANSWERS

Q: Why do we use AC for power transmission and not DC?
A: AC can be easily stepped up to high voltages using transformers. High voltage transmission reduces current, which reduces energy loss (P = I²R). DC cannot be easily transformed.

Q: Why is the core of a transformer made of laminated iron sheets?
A: Laminated sheets reduce eddy currents (unwanted induced currents in the core) that would waste energy as heat.

Q: What would happen if a DC supply is used for the primary of a transformer?
A: A transformer works only with AC. DC produces a constant flux (no change), so no EMF is induced in the secondary coil. The transformer would not work.

Q: What is back EMF in a motor?
A: As the motor armature rotates, it also acts as a generator and produces an EMF opposing the applied EMF. This opposing EMF is called back EMF. It increases as the motor speeds up, limiting the current.
