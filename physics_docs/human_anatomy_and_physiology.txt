1. ORGANIZATION OF THE HUMAN BODY
-----------------------------------
Levels of structural organization (smallest to largest):
  Chemical → Cellular → Tissue → Organ → Organ System → Organism

Four Tissue Types:
- Epithelial   : Covers body surfaces and lines cavities; forms glands.
- Connective   : Supports and connects structures (bone, blood, cartilage, fat).
- Muscle       : Produces movement (skeletal, cardiac, smooth).
- Nervous      : Detects stimuli and transmits signals (neurons + glia).

The 11 Organ Systems:
System              | Key Organs                  | Primary Function
--------------------|-----------------------------|---------------------------------
Integumentary       | Skin, hair, nails           | Protection, thermoregulation
Skeletal            | Bones, cartilage, joints    | Support, movement, mineral storage
Muscular            | Skeletal/cardiac/smooth m.  | Movement, posture, heat production
Nervous             | Brain, spinal cord, nerves  | Control, sensation, communication
Endocrine           | Hypothalamus, thyroid, etc. | Hormonal regulation
Cardiovascular      | Heart, arteries, veins      | Transport O2, nutrients, hormones
Lymphatic/Immune    | Lymph nodes, spleen, thymus | Immunity, fluid balance
Respiratory         | Lungs, trachea, bronchi     | Gas exchange (O2/CO2)
Digestive           | Stomach, intestines, liver  | Nutrient digestion & absorption
Urinary             | Kidneys, bladder, ureters   | Waste excretion, fluid regulation
Reproductive        | Gonads, reproductive organs | Reproduction

Homeostasis:
  Maintenance of a stable internal environment. Key regulated variables:
  - Body temperature  : 37°C (thermoregulation via hypothalamus)
  - Blood pH          : 7.35–7.45 (buffer systems + respiratory control)
  - Blood glucose     : 70–100 mg/dL (insulin/glucagon from pancreas)
  - Blood pressure    : ~120/80 mmHg (baroreceptors, kidneys, ANS)
  Mechanism: Negative feedback loops (most common) and positive feedback loops.

2. THE CARDIOVASCULAR SYSTEM
------------------------------
The heart is a dual pump:
  Right side → Pulmonary circuit (heart → lungs → heart)
  Left side  → Systemic circuit  (heart → body → heart)

Cardiac Cycle:
  Systole (contraction) → Diastole (relaxation) → repeat (~70 times/min)

Cardiac Conduction System:
  SA Node (pacemaker, 60–100 bpm) → AV Node (0.1s delay) →
  Bundle of His → Left & Right Bundle Branches → Purkinje Fibers

Blood Vessels:
- Arteries   : Carry blood AWAY from heart; thick muscular walls; high pressure.
- Veins      : Carry blood TO heart; thinner walls; have valves to prevent backflow.
- Capillaries: Site of gas and nutrient exchange; one cell thick.

Blood Pressure Regulation:
  BP = Cardiac Output × Peripheral Resistance
  Regulated by: RAAS system (kidneys), baroreceptors, sympathetic/parasympathetic NS.

3. THE RESPIRATORY SYSTEM
--------------------------
Gas exchange occurs in the alveoli of the lungs.
Mechanics of Breathing:
  Inhalation: Diaphragm contracts (flattens) → thoracic volume increases →
              pressure drops → air flows in.
  Exhalation: Diaphragm relaxes → thoracic volume decreases → air flows out.

Gas Exchange (Fick's Law of Diffusion):
  O2 diffuses from alveoli (high pO2) into blood (low pO2).
  CO2 diffuses from blood (high pCO2) into alveoli (low pCO2).

Oxygen Transport in Blood:
  98.5% bound to haemoglobin (Hb) in red blood cells.
  1.5% dissolved in plasma.
  Haemoglobin-O2 relationship: S-shaped (sigmoidal) dissociation curve.
  Bohr effect: increased CO2, decreased pH, increased temperature → Hb releases O2.

4. THE NERVOUS SYSTEM
----------------------
Divisions:
  CNS  : Brain + Spinal cord (integration and processing)
  PNS  : All nerves outside CNS
    Somatic NS     : Voluntary control of skeletal muscles
    Autonomic NS   : Involuntary control of organs
      Sympathetic  : Fight-or-flight (adrenaline, increased HR, dilated pupils)
      Parasympathetic: Rest-and-digest (acetylcholine, decreased HR)

Neuron Structure:
  Dendrites → Cell body (soma) → Axon hillock → Axon → Synaptic terminals

Action Potential:
  Resting potential : –70 mV (Na+/K+ pump maintains)
  Depolarization    : Na+ rushes in → membrane potential rises to +30 mV
  Repolarization    : K+ flows out → membrane returns to negative
  Hyperpolarization : Brief overshoot below –70 mV (refractory period)
  Saltatory conduction: AP jumps between nodes of Ranvier (myelinated axons)
                        — speeds transmission up to 120 m/s.

Synapse:
  Action potential → Ca2+ influx → vesicles release neurotransmitter →
  binds postsynaptic receptors → EPSP or IPSP.

Key Neurotransmitters:
- Acetylcholine (ACh)    : NMJ, parasympathetic, memory
- Dopamine               : Reward, motor control (deficient in Parkinson's)
- Serotonin              : Mood, appetite, sleep
- GABA                   : Main inhibitory NT in brain
- Glutamate              : Main excitatory NT in brain
- Norepinephrine         : Sympathetic NS, alertness

6. RENAL PHYSIOLOGY — KIDNEY FUNCTION
---------------------------------------
Functional unit: Nephron (~1 million per kidney)
Nephron regions and functions:
  Glomerulus            : Filtration — blood pressure forces fluid into Bowman's capsule.
  Proximal Convoluted   : Reabsorbs ~65% Na+, glucose, amino acids, water.
  Tubule (PCT)
  Loop of Henle         : Creates osmotic gradient in medulla.
    Descending limb     : Permeable to water; water leaves → concentrated filtrate.
    Ascending limb      : Impermeable to water; Na+/K+/Cl- pumped out → dilute filtrate.
  Distal Convoluted     : Fine-tuning of Na+/K+ under aldosterone control.
  Tubule (DCT)
  Collecting Duct       : Water reabsorption regulated by ADH (aquaporin-2 insertion).

GFR (Glomerular Filtration Rate): Normal ~125 mL/min (~180 L/day).
Most is reabsorbed; ~1.5 L excreted as urine.

RAAS Axis:
  Low BP → kidney releases Renin → cleaves Angiotensinogen → Angiotensin I →
  ACE (lung) converts → Angiotensin II → vasoconstriction + aldosterone release
  → Na+ & water retention → BP rises.

7. MUSCLE PHYSIOLOGY — SLIDING FILAMENT THEORY
-------------------------------------------------
Skeletal muscle contraction at the molecular level:

1. Action potential arrives at neuromuscular junction.
2. ACh released; binds nicotinic receptors on motor end plate.
3. AP propagates into T-tubules → triggers Ca2+ release from SR (via RyR1).
4. Ca2+ binds troponin C → conformational change exposes actin binding sites.
5. Myosin heads (with ATP hydrolysed to ADP+Pi) bind actin → power stroke.
6. ADP+Pi released → myosin pulls actin toward M-line.
7. New ATP binds myosin → cross-bridge detaches → cycle repeats.
8. Ca2+ pumped back into SR (SERCA pump) → relaxation.

Key proteins: Actin, Myosin (thick filaments), Tropomyosin, Troponin (TnI, TnT, TnC)

Muscle fibre types:
  Type I   (slow oxidative)   : Fatigue resistant; aerobic; rich in mitochondria.
  Type IIa (fast oxidative)   : Intermediate; mix of aerobic + anaerobic capacity.
  Type IIx (fast glycolytic)  : Fast, powerful, fatigue quickly; few mitochondria.

8. IMMUNOLOGY
--------------
Innate Immunity (non-specific, immediate):
- Physical barriers (skin, mucus, cilia)
- Phagocytes: Neutrophils, macrophages engulf pathogens.
- NK cells: Kill virus-infected and tumour cells.
- Complement system: Cascade of proteins → opsonisation, MAC pore formation.
- Inflammation: Mast cells release histamine → vasodilation, increased permeability.

Adaptive Immunity (specific, has memory):
- Humoral (B cells): Activated B cells → plasma cells → secrete antibodies.
    Antibody classes: IgM (first response), IgG (most abundant), IgA (mucosal),
    IgE (allergy/parasites), IgD (B cell surface receptor).
- Cell-mediated (T cells):
    CD4+ Helper T cells: Orchestrate immune response via cytokines.
    CD8+ Cytotoxic T cells: Kill infected/tumour cells via perforin and granzymes.
    Regulatory T cells (Tregs): Suppress immune response to prevent autoimmunity.

MHC (Major Histocompatibility Complex):
  MHC I : On ALL nucleated cells; presents intracellular antigens to CD8+ T cells.
  MHC II: On antigen-presenting cells (APCs); presents extracellular antigens to CD4+.

[CLINICAL LINK] Autoimmune diseases arise when tolerance breaks down and the
immune system attacks self (e.g., Type 1 diabetes — beta cells; Rheumatoid
arthritis — joints; MS — myelin). Immunosuppressants (corticosteroids, biologics
like anti-TNF) are key treatments.
