PART 1: STEREOCHEMISTRY

ISOMERISM OVERVIEW:
Constitutional (structural) isomers: same molecular formula, different connectivity.
Stereoisomers: same connectivity, different spatial arrangement.
  - Conformational isomers (conformers): interconvert by rotation about single bonds.
  - Configurational isomers: require bond breaking to interconvert.
    - Geometric (cis-trans) isomers.
    - Optical isomers (enantiomers, diastereomers).

CONFORMATIONAL ANALYSIS:
Newman projection: looking down C-C bond.
Eclipsed: dihedral angle = 0¬∞. Maximum energy (steric strain, torsional strain).
Staggered: dihedral angle = 60¬∞. Lower energy.
Anti: dihedral angle = 180¬∞. Most stable for butane.
Gauche: dihedral angle = 60¬∞. Less stable than anti (methyl groups close).

For cyclohexane:
Chair conformation: most stable. Axial and equatorial positions.
Boat conformation: less stable.
Ring flip: axial ‚Üî equatorial (10 kJ/mol energy barrier).
Large groups prefer equatorial position (less 1,3-diaxial interactions).

GEOMETRIC ISOMERISM (CIS-TRANS):
Occurs when: restricted rotation (double bond or ring) + two different groups on each carbon.
Cis: same groups on same side. Trans: same groups on opposite sides.
E/Z notation (when 4 different groups): E (entgegen = opposite), Z (zusammen = same side).
Priority rules (CIP): higher atomic number = higher priority. 

Physical properties differ: boiling points, melting points, dipole moments.
Example: cis-2-butene bp = 3.7¬∞C, trans-2-butene bp = 0.9¬∞C.
Maleic acid (cis) vs fumaric acid (trans): different melting points, one can form anhydride.

OPTICAL ISOMERISM:
Chiral carbon (stereocentre): carbon bonded to 4 different groups.
Enantiomers: non-superimposable mirror images. Identical in all physical properties EXCEPT:
- Rotate plane-polarized light in opposite directions.
- React differently with other chiral molecules.
Racemic mixture (racemate): equal mixture of enantiomers. Optically inactive (rotations cancel).
Specific rotation [Œ±]: [Œ±] = Œ±/(l √ó c). Each enantiomer rotates light equally but in opposite direction.
(+) or (d): dextrorotatory (clockwise). (-) or (l): levorotatory (anticlockwise).
R/S configuration (CIP rules):
1. Assign priorities 1-4 (highest atomic number = highest priority).
2. Orient with lowest priority (4) pointing away.
3. If 1‚Üí2‚Üí3 is clockwise: R (rectus). Anticlockwise: S (sinister).

Diastereomers: stereoisomers that are NOT mirror images.
Multiple stereocentres: maximum stereoisomers = 2‚Åø (n = chiral centres).
Meso compounds: has chiral centres but is achiral due to internal plane of symmetry.

SN1 MECHANISM (DETAILED):
Step 1: Slow ionization of leaving group ‚Üí carbocation (rate-determining step).
Step 2: Rapid nucleophile attack from either face ‚Üí racemization.
Rate = k[substrate]. First order. Rate independent of nucleophile.
Favored by:
- 3¬∞ > 2¬∞ (never 1¬∞ unless SN1 intermediate is stabilized).
- Good leaving groups (I‚Åª > Br‚Åª > Cl‚Åª >> F‚Åª >> OH‚Åª).
- Polar protic solvents (water, alcohols) ‚Äî stabilize carbocation by solvation.
- Weak nucleophiles.
Stereochemistry: racemization (some inversion due to ion pair shielding).
Rearrangements: hydride shift or methide shift to form more stable carbocation.

SN2 MECHANISM (DETAILED):
One step: nucleophile attacks BACKSIDE of C-LG bond simultaneously with LG departure.
Rate = k[substrate][nucleophile]. Second order. Bimolecular.
Favored by:
- Primary > secondary >> tertiary (steric access required).
- Strong nucleophiles: I‚Åª, RS‚Åª, CN‚Åª, N‚ÇÉ‚Åª, RO‚Åª, HO‚Åª, R‚ÇÇNH.
- Polar aprotic solvents (DMSO, DMF, acetone) ‚Äî don't solvate nucleophile.
- Good leaving groups.
Stereochemistry: INVERSION (Walden inversion) ‚Äî backside attack.
Nucleophilicity order: I‚Åª > Br‚Åª > Cl‚Åª > F‚Åª (in protic solvent, polarizability dominates).
In aprotic solvent: F‚Åª > Cl‚Åª > Br‚Åª > I‚Åª (less solvated = more reactive).

LEAVING GROUP ABILITY:
Good leaving groups = weak bases = stable anions.
Order: I‚Åª > Br‚Åª > Cl‚Åª > TsO‚Åª (tosylate) > F‚Åª >> OH‚Åª
Tosylation: converts OH to OTs (better leaving group) to enable SN2.
OH‚Åª is a POOR leaving group. Must be protonated first (H‚ÇÉO‚Å∫ environment).

E1 vs E2 ELIMINATION:
E1: Carbocation intermediate (same as SN1). Saytzeff product (more substituted alkene).
E2: Concerted one-step. Anti-periplanar arrangement required (Hb 180¬∞ from LG).
    Strong bulky base (t-BuOK) ‚Üí Hofmann product (less substituted, less hindered).
Competition: SN2 vs E2 ‚Äî temperature, base strength, substrate.
Higher temperature favors elimination over substitution.

ELECTROPHILIC ADDITION TO ALKENES:
Mechanism for HX addition:
Step 1: H‚Å∫ attacks œÄ electrons ‚Üí more stable carbocation (Markovnikov: H to C with more H).
Step 2: X‚Åª attacks carbocation.

Halogenation (Br‚ÇÇ, Cl‚ÇÇ) ‚Äî Anti addition:
Step 1: Br‚ÇÇ attacks alkene ‚Üí bromonium ion (cyclic intermediate, blocks one face).
Step 2: Br‚Åª attacks from other face (back side).

Hydroboration-Oxidation:
BH‚ÇÉ adds across double bond (syn, anti-Markovnikov). Then H‚ÇÇO‚ÇÇ/NaOH oxidizes B to OH.

ELECTROPHILIC AROMATIC SUBSTITUTION MECHANISM (DETAILED):

Step 1: Electrophile (E‚Å∫) attacks aromatic ring œÄ electrons ‚Üí arenium ion (Wheland intermediate/œÉ complex). The ring loses aromaticity ‚Äî this is the slow step.
Step 2: Loss of H‚Å∫ (proton) regenerates aromatic ring ‚Äî fast step.
Rate-determining step: formation of arenium ion.
Activating groups: stabilize arenium ion ‚Üí faster reaction.
Deactivating groups: destabilize arenium ion ‚Üí slower reaction.

DIRECTING EFFECTS:
Ortho-para directors (activating): -OH, -OR, -NH‚ÇÇ, -NR‚ÇÇ, -NHCOR, -R, -Ar.
Mechanism: lone pair donation (resonance) ‚Üí places electrons at o and p positions of arenium ion.
Ortho-para directors (deactivating): -F, -Cl, -Br, -I.
Mechanism: -I effect (deactivating) dominates overall, but +M lone pair donation ‚Üí o/p selectivity.
Meta directors (deactivating): -NO‚ÇÇ, -CN, -COOH, -CHO, -COR, -SO‚ÇÉH, -CF‚ÇÉ.
Mechanism: electron withdrawal by resonance/induction ‚Üí destabilizes o/p arenium ‚Üí meta product.

NUCLEOPHILIC ADDITION TO CARBONYLS:

Reactivity order: HCHO > RCHO > RCOR' > RCOCl > RCOOCOR' > RCOOR' > RCONH‚ÇÇ.
Aldehydes more reactive than ketones (less steric, less electron donation).
Acyl chlorides most reactive (Cl withdraws electrons, great leaving group).

Grignard reagent is a strong base ‚Äî react with water, alcohols, carboxylic acids.

PART 3: SPECTROSCOPY AND STRUCTURE DETERMINATION

MASS SPECTROMETRY:
Molecular ion M‚Å∫: highest mass peak (gives molecular weight).
Base peak: 100% relative abundance (most stable fragment).
M+1 peak: due to ¬π¬≥C (~1.1% natural abundance). Ratio (M+1)/M = 1.1% √ó number of carbons.
M+2 peak: if Cl (M:M+2 = 3:1) or Br (M:M+2 = 1:1) present.

Common fragmentations:
Alpha cleavage: bond next to heteroatom or carbonyl breaks (most common).
McLafferty rearrangement: gamma hydrogen transfer to carbonyl ‚Üí alkene + enol fragment.
