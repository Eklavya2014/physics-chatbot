1. STATES OF MATTER & GAS LAWS
--------------------------------
States of Matter:
  Solid   : Fixed shape; fixed volume; particles vibrate in fixed positions; strong forces.
  Liquid  : Fixed volume; no fixed shape; particles move freely but close together.
  Gas     : No fixed shape; no fixed volume; particles move rapidly, far apart; negligible forces.
  Plasma  : Ionised gas; 4th state; stars, lightning, neon signs.
  Supercritical fluid: Above critical temperature AND pressure → properties between liquid and gas.

Kinetic Molecular Theory of Gases:
  1. Gas molecules in constant, random motion. 2. Molecules have negligible volume vs container.
  3. Collisions elastic (no net energy loss). 4. No intermolecular forces between molecules.
  5. Average kinetic energy proportional to absolute temperature: KE_avg = (3/2)kT.
  Root Mean Square Speed: v_rms = √(3RT/M). Faster for lighter molecules at same T.

Ideal Gas Law: PV = nRT
  P = pressure (Pa or atm); V = volume (m³ or L); n = moles; R = 8.314 J/mol·K; T = temperature (K).
  Derived from: Boyle's Law (PV = const at constant T,n); Charles's Law (V/T = const at constant P,n);
  Avogadro's Law (V ∝ n at constant T,P).

Dalton's Law of Partial Pressures: P_total = P₁ + P₂ + P₃ + ... (each gas independent).
Graham's Law of Effusion: Rate ∝ 1/√M. Lighter gases effuse faster.
Standard Conditions: STP = 0°C, 100 kPa (new IUPAC); old STP = 0°C, 101.325 kPa. Molar volume = 22.4 L/mol (old STP); 22.7 L/mol (new STP).

van der Waals Equation (Real Gases): (P + a/V²)(V - b) = nRT
  a = attraction correction (reduces effective pressure); b = excluded volume correction.
  Deviations from ideal: High pressure (volume correction); low temperature (attraction correction).

1.1 ENERGY & THERMOCHEMISTRY
------------------------------
Heat (q) and Work (w):
  First Law of Thermodynamics: ΔU = q + w (or ΔU = q - w depending on sign convention).
    ΔU = change in internal energy; q = heat absorbed by system; w = work done on system.
  Endothermic reaction: q > 0; absorbs heat from surroundings (temperature of surroundings drops).
  Exothermic reaction: q < 0; releases heat to surroundings (temperature of surroundings rises).

Enthalpy (H):
  H = U + PV. At constant pressure: ΔH = q_p (heat at constant pressure).
  ΔH < 0: Exothermic. ΔH > 0: Endothermic.
  Standard enthalpy of formation ΔHf°: Enthalpy change to form 1 mole of compound from elements in standard states. ΔHf°(elements in standard state) = 0 by definition.
  Hess's Law: ΔH_reaction = ΣΔHf°(products) - ΣΔHf°(reactants). Enthalpy is a state function.

Bond Enthalpies (Mean Bond Enthalpies):
  ΔH_reaction ≈ ΣBE(bonds broken) - ΣBE(bonds formed). Bonds broken = endothermic; bonds formed = exothermic.
  Approximate only (uses average values; ΔHf° method more accurate).

2. THERMODYNAMICS — THE THREE LAWS
------------------------------------
First Law (Conservation of Energy):
  ΔU = q + w. Energy cannot be created or destroyed, only converted.
  Work done by gas at constant pressure: w = -PΔV (expansion = negative work by system = positive work on surroundings).

Second Law (Entropy and Spontaneity):
  Entropy (S): Measure of disorder/randomness/number of microstates.
    ΔS_universe = ΔS_system + ΔS_surroundings ≥ 0 (always; = 0 for reversible; >0 for irreversible/spontaneous).
  Entropy increases with: Higher temperature; larger volume; more particles; more complex molecules; phase change from solid → liquid → gas.
  ΔS° = ΣS°(products) - ΣS°(reactants). S° = absolute entropy (J/mol·K). S° > 0 always (Boltzmann, third law).
  Boltzmann's equation: S = k_B × ln(W). W = number of microstates; k_B = 1.381×10⁻²³ J/K.

Third Law (Absolute Zero and Entropy):
  At absolute zero (0 K), entropy of perfect crystal = 0. S = 0 at T = 0 K (for perfectly ordered crystal).
  Therefore, absolute entropies can be calculated.

Gibbs Free Energy (G):
  G = H - TS. At constant T and P: ΔG = ΔH - TΔS.
  ΔG < 0: Spontaneous (thermodynamically favourable).
  ΔG = 0: Equilibrium. ΔG > 0: Non-spontaneous (reverse process spontaneous).

Predicting Spontaneity:
  ΔH    | ΔS    | ΔG = ΔH - TΔS   | Spontaneity
  ------|-------|-----------------|-------------------------
  -     | +     | Always -        | Spontaneous at all temps
  -     | -     | - at low T      | Spontaneous at low temps only
  +     | +     | - at high T     | Spontaneous at high temps only
  +     | -     | Always +        | Never spontaneous

Standard Gibbs Energy and Equilibrium:
  ΔG° = -RT ln(K). K = equilibrium constant.
  ΔG = ΔG° + RT ln(Q). Q = reaction quotient.
  If Q < K: ΔG < 0 → forward reaction spontaneous.
  If Q > K: ΔG > 0 → reverse reaction spontaneous.
  If Q = K: ΔG = 0 → equilibrium.

3. CHEMICAL EQUILIBRIUM
------------------------
Le Chatelier's Principle: System at equilibrium disturbed → shifts to partially oppose the change.

Equilibrium Constant Expressions:
  aA + bB ⇌ cC + dD
  Kc = [C]ᶜ[D]ᵈ / [A]ᵃ[B]ᵇ  (in terms of concentrations; mol/L)
  Kp = (pC)ᶜ(pD)ᵈ / (pA)ᵃ(pB)ᵇ  (in terms of partial pressures; atm or bar)
  Relationship: Kp = Kc(RT)^Δn  where Δn = moles of gas (products - reactants).

  Rules for K:
  - Pure solids and pure liquids NOT included in K expression.
  - K >> 1: Equilibrium lies far to right (products favoured).
  - K << 1: Equilibrium lies far to left (reactants favoured).
  - K changes ONLY with temperature.

Effect of Changes on Equilibrium:
  Concentration increase of reactant: Shifts right (increases Q → reaction proceeds until Q=K).
  Pressure increase (at constant T): Shifts toward fewer moles of gas.
  Temperature increase (endothermic reaction): Shifts right (K increases).
  Temperature increase (exothermic reaction): Shifts left (K decreases).
  Catalyst: No effect on K or equilibrium position; only speeds up reaching equilibrium.
  Inert gas at constant volume: No effect. Inert gas at constant pressure: Volume increases → shifts toward more moles of gas.

Reaction Quotient (Q): Same form as K but using current (not equilibrium) concentrations.
  Q < K → reaction proceeds forward.
  Q > K → reaction proceeds backward.
  Q = K → at equilibrium.

4. ACIDS AND BASES
-------------------
Arrhenius: Acid → H⁺; Base → OH⁻ (in water only).
Brønsted-Lowry: Acid = proton donor; Base = proton acceptor.
  Conjugate acid-base pair: Differ by one proton.
  Stronger acid → weaker conjugate base. Strong acid has negligible conjugate base strength.
Lewis: Acid = electron pair acceptor; Base = electron pair donor.
  More general; includes reactions without proton transfer (BF₃ + NH₃ → F₃B-NH₃).

Strong Acids (essentially 100% dissociated in water):
  HCl, HBr, HI, HNO₃, H₂SO₄ (1st dissociation), HClO₄, HClO₃.
Strong Bases: Group 1 + Group 2 hydroxides (NaOH, KOH, Ca(OH)₂, Ba(OH)₂).

Weak Acids and Bases:
  Ka = [H⁺][A⁻]/[HA]. pKa = -log(Ka). Smaller pKa → stronger acid.
  Kb = [BH⁺][OH⁻]/[B]. pKb = -log(Kb).
  Relationship: Ka × Kb = Kw. pKa + pKb = 14 (at 25°C).

pH Scale:
  pH = -log[H⁺]. pOH = -log[OH⁻]. pH + pOH = 14 (at 25°C).
  Kw = [H⁺][OH⁻] = 10⁻¹⁴ at 25°C.
  Neutral pH = 7 (at 25°C). Acidic < 7. Basic > 7.

Buffer Solutions:
  Resist pH changes on addition of small amounts of acid or base.
  Components: Weak acid + its conjugate base (or weak base + conjugate acid).
  Henderson-Hasselbalch: pH = pKa + log([A⁻]/[HA]).
  Maximum buffer capacity at pH = pKa (equal concentrations of acid and base).
  Examples: Ethanoic acid/sodium ethanoate (pH ~4.75); NH₃/NH₄⁺ (pH ~9.25); H₂CO₃/HCO₃⁻ (blood buffer, pH 7.4).

5. CHEMICAL KINETICS
---------------------
Reaction Rate: Change in concentration per unit time.
  Rate = -Δ[A]/Δt = +Δ[B]/Δt (for A → B).

Rate Laws:
  Rate = k[A]ᵐ[B]ⁿ  (k = rate constant; m, n = partial orders; determined experimentally, NOT from stoichiometry of overall equation).
  Overall order = m + n.
  First order (n=1): Rate = k[A]. [A] = [A]₀e^(-kt). t½ = ln2/k (constant, independent of [A]).
  Second order (n=2): Rate = k[A]². 1/[A] = 1/[A]₀ + kt. t½ = 1/(k[A]₀) (depends on [A]).
  Zero order: Rate = k. [A] = [A]₀ - kt. t½ = [A]₀/2k.

Integrated Rate Laws (summary):
  Zero order: [A] = [A]₀ - kt. Linear plot: [A] vs t.
  First order: ln[A] = ln[A]₀ - kt. Linear plot: ln[A] vs t.
  Second order: 1/[A] = 1/[A]₀ + kt. Linear plot: 1/[A] vs t.

Activation Energy and Arrhenius Equation:
  Arrhenius equation: k = Ae^(-Ea/RT)
  A = pre-exponential factor (frequency factor); Ea = activation energy.
  ln(k) = ln(A) - Ea/RT. Linear plot: ln(k) vs 1/T → slope = -Ea/R.
  Two-temperature form: ln(k₂/k₁) = -Ea/R × (1/T₂ - 1/T₁).
  Rule of thumb: Rate approximately doubles per 10°C increase at room temperature (Q₁₀ rule).

Reaction Mechanisms:
  Elementary steps: Individual molecular events. Molecularity: Unimolecular, bimolecular, termolecular.
  Rate law for elementary step: Written directly from stoichiometry (unlike overall reactions).
  Rate-determining step (RDS): Slowest step; controls overall rate. Rate law for overall reaction = rate law of RDS.
  Steady-state approximation: d[intermediate]/dt ≈ 0 (intermediates consumed as fast as formed).
  Pre-equilibrium approximation: Fast equilibrium before slow step.

Catalysis:
  Catalyst: Provides alternative pathway with lower Ea; increases rate; not consumed.
  Homogeneous catalysis: Same phase as reactants. V₂O₅ in Contact process; acid/base catalysis.
  Heterogeneous catalysis: Different phase. Fe in Haber process; Ni in hydrogenation; Pt in catalytic converter.
    Mechanism: Adsorption → surface reaction → desorption.
    Langmuir-Hinshelwood: Both reactants adsorb first. Eley-Rideal: One adsorbs, other reacts from gas phase.
  Enzyme catalysis: Michaelis-Menten kinetics: v = Vmax[S]/(Km + [S]).
    Km = substrate concentration at half-maximum rate. Vmax = maximum rate.
    Turnover number (kcat): Reactions per enzyme per second (Vmax/[E_total]).

6. ELECTROCHEMISTRY
--------------------
Oxidation-Reduction (Redox):
  Oxidation: Loss of electrons; increase in oxidation state. (OIL: Oxidation Is Loss).
  Reduction: Gain of electrons; decrease in oxidation state. (RIG: Reduction Is Gain).
  Oxidising agent: Causes oxidation; is itself reduced. Reducing agent: Causes reduction; is itself oxidised.

Electrochemical Cells:
  Galvanic (voltaic) cell: Spontaneous redox → electrical energy (ΔG < 0 → positive EMF).
    Anode: Oxidation occurs; negative electrode in galvanic cell.
    Cathode: Reduction occurs; positive electrode in galvanic cell.
    Salt bridge: Maintains electrical neutrality; allows ion flow.
    Standard EMF: E°_cell = E°_cathode - E°_anode.
    Daniel cell: Zn(s)/Zn²⁺(aq) || Cu²⁺(aq)/Cu(s). E°_cell = +1.10 V.

Standard Reduction Potentials (E°, vs Standard Hydrogen Electrode SHE = 0.00 V):
  Strong oxidising agents: F₂/F⁻ = +2.87V; MnO₄⁻/Mn²⁺ = +1.51V; Cl₂/Cl⁻ = +1.36V.
  Moderate: Cu²⁺/Cu = +0.34V; H⁺/H₂ = 0.00V (reference).
  Strong reducing agents: Zn²⁺/Zn = -0.76V; Al³⁺/Al = -1.66V; Na⁺/Na = -2.71V; Li⁺/Li = -3.04V.
  More positive E°: More easily reduced; stronger oxidising agent.
  More negative E°: More easily oxidised; stronger reducing agent.

Nernst Equation (non-standard conditions):
  E = E° - (RT/nF) × ln(Q) = E° - (0.0257/n) × ln(Q) at 25°C.
  Or: E = E° - (0.0592/n) × log(Q) at 25°C.
  n = number of electrons; F = Faraday's constant (96,485 C/mol); Q = reaction quotient.
  At equilibrium: E = 0, Q = K → ΔG° = -RT ln(K) = -nFE°.

Relationship: ΔG° = -nFE°. If E° > 0: ΔG° < 0 → spontaneous.

Electrolytic Cells (non-spontaneous redox driven by external electricity; ΔG > 0):
  Anode: Oxidation (positive electrode in electrolytic cell — connected to + terminal of battery).
  Cathode: Reduction (negative electrode).
  Faraday's Laws: 1. Mass deposited ∝ charge passed. 2. Mass ∝ molar mass / charge of ion.
    m = (Q × M) / (n × F) where Q = charge (C), M = molar mass, n = ionic charge, F = 96,485 C/mol.

Applications of Electrolysis:
  Electroplating: Coat object with thin metal layer (Cu, Ni, Cr, Ag, Au).
  Anodising: Anodic oxidation of Al → thicker Al₂O₃ layer → better corrosion resistance.
  Hall-Héroult (Al production): Al₂O₃ in molten cryolite electrolysed at 950°C → Al (cathode) + O₂ (anode).
  Chloralkali process: NaCl(aq) electrolysis → Cl₂ (anode) + H₂ + NaOH (cathode).
  Electrorefining: Impure Cu anode → pure Cu cathode. Cu²⁺ transferral; impurities fall to bottom.

Concentration Cells:
  Two identical electrodes; different ion concentrations. E > 0 because reaction drives concentrations to equal.
  E = (RT/nF) × ln(c₂/c₁) where c₁ < c₂.

7. PHASE EQUILIBRIA
--------------------
Phase Diagrams: Show stable phase at given T and P.
  Triple point: Unique T,P where all three phases coexist (water: 273.16 K, 611.7 Pa).
  Critical point: Above Tc and Pc → supercritical fluid (no liquid-gas distinction).
    Water: Tc = 374°C, Pc = 218 atm. CO₂: Tc = 31.1°C, Pc = 73.8 atm.
  Normal bp and mp: At 1 atm.
  Water anomaly: Solid-liquid line has negative slope (ice less dense than liquid → pressure melts ice).

Clausius-Clapeyron Equation:
  d(ln P)/dT = ΔH_vap / RT²  or  ln(P₂/P₁) = -(ΔH_vap/R) × (1/T₂ - 1/T₁).
  Relates vapour pressure to temperature; use to determine ΔH_vap from vapour pressure data.

Colligative Properties: Depend on number of solute particles, not their identity.
  Vapour pressure lowering (Raoult's Law): P_solution = χ_solvent × P°_solvent.
    ΔP = χ_solute × P°_solvent. More solute → less vapour pressure → higher bp, lower mp.
  Boiling point elevation: ΔTb = Kb × m × i.  Kb = ebullioscopic constant; m = molality; i = van't Hoff factor.
  Freezing point depression: ΔTf = Kf × m × i.  Road salt (NaCl/CaCl₂); antifreeze (ethylene glycol).
  Osmotic pressure: Π = iMRT. M = molarity; important in biology (cell membranes, dialysis, RO).

[APPLIED CHEMISTRY] Important equilibrium constants at 25°C:
  Water autoionisation: Kw = 1.0 × 10⁻¹⁴.
  Acetic acid: Ka = 1.8 × 10⁻⁵ (pKa = 4.74).
  Ammonia: Kb = 1.8 × 10⁻⁵ (pKb = 4.74).
  Solubility products (Ksp): AgCl = 1.8×10⁻¹⁰; BaSO₄ = 1.1×10⁻¹⁰; Ca₃(PO₄)₂ = 2.1×10⁻³³.
  Haber process equilibrium: K ≈ 6.2 × 10⁻² at 500°C (low K → low conversion → must run continuously + recycle).
  Contact process: K varies with T; high T needed for kinetics but K decreases; 450°C compromise.
