1. WHY DO WE AGE?
------------------
Ageing is the progressive decline in physiological function that increases vulnerability
to disease and death. Maximum human lifespan ~120–125 years (Jeanne Calment: 122 years).
Life expectancy (global average 2024): ~73 years.

Evolutionary Theories of Ageing:
  Disposable Soma Theory (Kirkwood): Body invests limited energy in reproduction vs.
    repair. After reproductive success, repair becomes less worthwhile → ageing.
  Antagonistic Pleiotropy (Williams): Genes beneficial early in life (reproduction) may be
    harmful later. Selection cannot remove them because benefits outweigh later costs.
    Example: Testosterone → reproduction early; prostate cancer and CVD later.
  Mutation Accumulation (Medawar): Late-acting harmful mutations accumulate because
    selection pressure is weak after reproductive age.

1.1 CHANGES WITH AGEING
-------------------------
System           | Changes with Ageing
-----------------|----------------------------------------------------------
Cardiovascular   | Arterial stiffening; ↓ max HR; diastolic dysfunction
Respiratory      | ↓ lung elasticity; ↓ FEV1/FVC; reduced gas exchange
Musculoskeletal  | Sarcopenia (muscle loss); osteoporosis; joint degeneration
Neurological     | Neuronal loss; synaptic changes; slower processing
Immune           | Immunosenescence; inflammageing; reduced vaccine response
Metabolic        | Insulin resistance; ↓ BMR; adipose redistribution
Renal            | ↓ GFR by ~1 mL/min/year after age 40; reduced concentrating ability
Skin             | Collagen loss; reduced elastin; thinner epidermis; slower healing
Reproductive     | Menopause (women ~51); declining testosterone (men, gradual)
Sensory          | Presbyopia; presbycusis; reduced taste/smell sensitivity

2. CELLULAR & MOLECULAR HALLMARKS OF AGEING
---------------------------------------------
(Based on Lopez-Otin et al., Cell 2023 — 12 Hallmarks)

1. Genomic Instability:
   Accumulation of DNA damage over time: DSBs, point mutations, insertions, deletions.
   Reduced DNA repair capacity (decreased NER, NHEJ efficiency with age).
   Somatic mutation burden increases exponentially with age.

2. Telomere Attrition:
   Telomeres: Repetitive sequences (TTAGGG)n + shelterin complex; protect chromosome ends.
   ~50–200 bp lost per cell division (end-replication problem).
   Critical shortening → p53-mediated replicative senescence or apoptosis.
   Telomerase (hTERT + hTERC): Reverse transcriptase; maintains telomeres in stem cells + cancer.
   Dyskeratosis congenita: Telomerase mutations → premature ageing syndrome.

3. Epigenetic Alterations:
   Global DNA hypomethylation + focal hypermethylation at CpG islands with age.
   Epigenetic clocks (Horvath 2013; GrimAge): DNA methylation patterns predict biological age.
   Histone modifications shift: H3K9me3 and H3K27me3 distribution changes.
   Chromatin becomes less compact; heterochromatin erosion → retrotransposon activation.

4. Loss of Proteostasis:
   Protein homeostasis maintained by: Chaperones (HSP70, HSP90, GroEL); UPS (ubiquitin-
   proteasome system); autophagy (macroautophagy, CMA, mitophagy).
   With ageing: All three decline → protein aggregation (hallmark of neurodegenerative disease).
   Heat shock response (HSR) declines with age → less upregulation of chaperones after stress.

5. Disabled Macroautophagy:
   Autophagy clears damaged organelles, protein aggregates, pathogens.
   mTORC1 activation (common with ageing/obesity) suppresses autophagy.
   ATG gene expression declines with age. Beclin-1 levels fall.
   Caloric restriction → mTOR inhibition → autophagy activation → lifespan extension.

6. Deregulated Nutrient Sensing:
   IIS (Insulin/IGF-1 signalling): Hyperactivation → accelerated ageing.
     FOXO transcription factors: Downstream of insulin pathway; when dephosphorylated
     (low IIS) → nuclear → activate stress resistance + longevity genes.
     DAF-2 (C. elegans IGF-1R) mutants: 100% lifespan extension. daf-16 (FOXO) required.
   mTOR: Nutrient sensor. Rapamycin (mTORC1 inhibitor) extends lifespan in multiple organisms
     including mice. Clinical trials ongoing.
   AMPK: Activated by low energy → catabolic; promotes autophagy; extends lifespan.
   Sirtuins (SIRT1–7): NAD+-dependent deacylases. SIRT1 activated by CR/NAD+ →
     deacetylates FOXO, p53, NF-kB → stress resistance. SIRT3: Mitochondrial; reduces ROS.

7. Mitochondrial Dysfunction:
   Mitochondrial quality declines with age: Fewer, less efficient, accumulate mtDNA mutations.
   Electron transport chain → reactive oxygen species (ROS) → oxidative damage (Harman, 1956).
   Modern view: ROS are also signalling molecules (mitohormesis — low ROS → adaptive response).
   Mitophagy (PINK1-Parkin pathway): Targets damaged mitochondria for autophagy. Declines with age.

8. Cellular Senescence:
   Permanent cell cycle arrest in response to telomere shortening, DNA damage, oncogene activation.
   SASP: IL-6, IL-8, MMPs, VEGF → chronic sterile inflammation (inflammageing).
   p16INK4a + p21 markers increase with age in tissues.
   Senolytics: Selectively kill senescent cells → extend healthspan in mice.
     Dasatinib + quercetin; navitoclax (BCL-2/BCL-xL inhibitor); fisetin; ABT-263.

9. Stem Cell Exhaustion:
   Haematopoietic stem cell (HSC) output declines; clonal haematopoiesis increases (DNMT3A,
   TET2, ASXL1 mutations → clonal expansion; MDS/leukaemia risk; CVD risk).
   Intestinal stem cell turnover maintained but niche support declines.
   Muscle satellite cells: Reduced number and activation in old muscle → impaired regeneration.

10. Altered Intercellular Communication:
    Inflammageing: Low-grade chronic inflammation driven by NF-kB, SASP, senescent cells,
    gut dysbiosis, accumulated damage signals, mitochondrial DNA leakage → cGAS-STING pathway.
    Parabiosis experiments: Connecting old + young mouse blood → old tissue rejuvenation.
    Young blood factors: GDF11 (controversial), GPLD1, klotho.
    Old blood factors (geronegative): TGF-beta, eotaxin/CCL11, beta-2-microglobulin.

3. MODEL ORGANISMS IN AGEING RESEARCH
---------------------------------------
C. elegans (Nematode):
  Lifespan: 2–3 weeks. Transparent. All 959 cells mapped. Genetic tools superb.
  daf-2 (IGF-1R) mutants: 2x lifespan. daf-16 (FOXO): Required for longevity.
  age-1 (PI3K): Null → 10x lifespan extension.
  clk-1 (CoQ biosynthesis): Mitochondrial function and ageing.
  eat-2 (CR model): Reduced feeding → 20% lifespan extension.
  isp-1 (Complex III): ETC reduction → extended lifespan via ROS signalling.

Drosophila melanogaster:
  Lifespan: 60–80 days. Powerful genetics; >20,000 characterised genes.
  dFOXO overexpression in fat body → 20–50% lifespan extension.
  Methuselah (mth) receptor mutants: ~35% longer-lived; stress resistant.
  Sir2 overexpression → lifespan extension (controversial).
  Dietary restriction in Drosophila: Extends lifespan but optimum nutrient:lifespan curve.

Mice:
  Lifespan: 2–3 years. Mammalian; closest model to humans.
  Rapamycin (mTOR inhibitor): ~10–15% median lifespan extension even started at 20 months!
  Growth hormone receptor KO (Laron mice): ~40% lifespan extension; small body; low IGF-1.
  Ames + Snell dwarf mice: GH/IGF-1 deficient → 40–65% lifespan extension.
  p16INK4a-ATTAC mice: Selective depletion of senescent cells → extended healthspan.

Naked Mole Rat (Heterocephalus glaber):
  Lives up to 37 years (vs. ~3 years for similar-sized mice). Cancer essentially absent.
  Mechanisms: Superior protein quality control; low metabolic rate; low ROS production;
  high molecular chaperone levels; high poly-unsaturated fatty acids in membranes;
  unique RNA ribosomal modifications → more accurate protein translation.
  High hyaluronic acid (HMM-HA) → anti-cancer mechanism.

4. LONGEVITY INTERVENTIONS
-----------------------------
Caloric Restriction (CR):
  Most robust lifespan-extending intervention across many species. ~20–40% CR.
  Mechanisms: ↓ mTOR, ↓ IIS, ↑ AMPK, ↑ autophagy, ↑ sirtuins, ↑ FOXO.
  CALERIE trial (humans, 2-year 25% CR): Improved metabolic markers; reduced biological age.
  CR mimetics: Metformin (AMPK), rapamycin (mTOR), resveratrol (SIRT1), NAD+ precursors.

Rapamycin:
  Most consistently life-extending drug in mice. Clinical trials in humans (PEARL trial
  for disease prevention; INVOKE for immunosenescence in elderly).
  Allosteric mTORC1 inhibitor. Concerns: Immunosuppression; wound healing; metabolic effects
  (hyperglycaemia, hyperlipidaemia at high doses). Intermittent dosing may reduce SE.

NAD+ Precursors (NR, NMN):
  NAD+ declines with age → reduced sirtuin/PARP activity.
  NR (nicotinamide riboside) and NMN (nicotinamide mononucleotide) → raise NAD+.
  Mice: Extended healthspan, improved muscle/cognition/vascular function. Human trials ongoing.

Yamanaka Factor Partial Reprogramming:
  Transient expression of OSKM (Oct4, Sox2, Klf4, c-Myc) in aged cells → erasure of
  epigenetic ageing marks → restored function WITHOUT loss of cell identity.
  Juan Carlos Izpisua Belmonte lab: Partial reprogramming of mice → 50% lifespan extension.
  Epigenetic age reversal: Specific factors (OSK without c-Myc) can reverse ageing of
  retinal ganglion cells → restore vision in old mice (Sinclair lab, 2020).
  Clinical translation: Retinal disease (Leber's hereditary optic neuropathy clinical trial).

5. EPIGENETIC CLOCKS & BIOLOGICAL AGE
----------------------------------------
Horvath Clock (2013): 353 CpG sites → predict chronological age ±3.6 years from DNA methylation.
GrimAge Clock: Predicts mortality risk; includes DNAm-based surrogate biomarkers.
DunedinPACE: Pace of ageing clock; captures rate of biological ageing (tracks longitudinally).
PhenoAge (Levine): Composite of clinical + methylation data.
Protein clock (Argentieri et al., 2024): ~3,000 plasma proteins → strong age predictor.

Accelerated epigenetic ageing associated with: Smoking, obesity, depression, low SES,
childhood adversity, HIV infection, many cancers. Decelerated with: Caloric restriction,
exercise, Mediterranean diet, HRT (in women), education.

[FUTURE OUTLOOK] The first drugs to modulate biological ageing may reach clinical use
in the 2030s. Current leading candidates: Rapamycin/rapalogs; senolytics (Unity Biotechnology
trials for osteoarthritis, AMD); NAD+ precursors; GLP-1 agonists (reduce inflammageing).
The goal is extending HEALTHSPAN (healthy years) rather than just lifespan.
The TAME trial (Targeting Aging with Metformin): Landmark clinical trial testing whether
metformin can delay onset of age-related diseases in 3,000+ non-diabetic older adults.
