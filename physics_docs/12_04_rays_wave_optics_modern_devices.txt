CHAPTER: RAY OPTICS AND OPTICAL INSTRUMENTS

REFLECTION AT SPHERICAL SURFACES:
- Mirror formula: 1/f = 1/v + 1/u
- Magnification for mirrors: m = h_i/h_o = -v/u
- Focal length and radius relation: f = R/2

REFRACTION:
- Snell's law: n1 sin(theta1) = n2 sin(theta2)
- Refractive index: n = c/v

REFRACTION AT SPHERICAL SURFACES:
- n2/v - n1/u = (n2 - n1)/R

LENSES:
- Lens formula: 1/f = 1/v - 1/u
- Magnification: m = v/u
- Power of lens: P = 1/f(in metre)
- Unit of power: dioptre

LENS MAKER'S FORMULA:
- 1/f = (mu - 1)(1/R1 - 1/R2)

COMBINATION OF LENSES:
- In contact: P = P1 + P2 + P3
- Equivalent focal length: 1/f = 1/f1 + 1/f2 + ...

TOTAL INTERNAL REFLECTION:
Conditions:
- Light must travel from denser to rarer medium.
- Angle of incidence > critical angle.
- sin C = 1/mu

OPTICAL INSTRUMENTS:
Simple microscope:
- Magnifying power = 1 + D/f  (final image at near point)
- = D/f (final image at infinity)

Astronomical telescope:
- Magnifying power in normal adjustment = fo/fe
- Length in normal adjustment = fo + fe

Compound microscope:
- Magnifying power approx = (L/fo)(1 + D/fe)

PRISM:
- Angle of deviation: delta = i + e - A
- At minimum deviation:
  mu = sin[(A + delta_m)/2] / sin(A/2)

COMMON RAY OPTICS MISTAKES:
- Follow sign convention carefully.
- Mirror and lens magnification signs are often confused.
- Power of convex lens is positive; concave lens negative.
- Do not mix real depth and apparent depth.
- Formula units must be consistent, especially focal length in metre for power.

CHAPTER: WAVE OPTICS

Huygens principle:
- Every point on a wavefront acts as source of secondary wavelets.

INTERFERENCE:
For coherent sources:
- Path difference = n lambda -> constructive interference
- Path difference = (2n+1)lambda/2 -> destructive interference

Young's double slit experiment:
- Fringe width: beta = lambda D/d
  lambda = wavelength, D = distance of screen, d = slit separation

INTENSITY:
- Resultant intensity:
  I = I1 + I2 + 2sqrt(I1 I2) cos(phi)

DIFFRACTION:
Single slit diffraction:
- Central maximum is widest and brightest.
- Angular width of central maximum approx = 2lambda/a

POLARISATION:
- Proves transverse nature of light.
- Brewster's law:
  mu = tan(ip)
  where ip = polarising angle

COMMON WAVE OPTICS MISTAKES:
- Interference needs coherent sources.
- Diffraction and interference patterns are not same.
- Polarisation is not shown by sound waves because sound is longitudinal.
- In YDSE, fringe width increases with lambda and D, decreases with d.

CHAPTER: DUAL NATURE OF RADIATION AND MATTER

PHOTOELECTRIC EFFECT:
- Emission of electrons from metal surface when light of suitable frequency falls on it.

Einstein photoelectric equation:
- h nu = phi + Kmax
- Kmax = h nu - phi
- Also, Kmax = eV0
  where V0 = stopping potential

IMPORTANT POINTS:
- Photoelectric effect is instantaneous.
- Number of emitted electrons depends on intensity.
- Maximum kinetic energy depends on frequency, not intensity.
- Threshold frequency nu0: minimum frequency for emission.
- Work function: phi = h nu0

DE BROGLIE WAVELENGTH:
- lambda = h/p = h/mv
For electron accelerated by V volts:
- lambda = h/sqrt(2meV)

COMMON MISTAKES:
- Intensity changes photoelectric current, not stopping potential.
- If frequency is below threshold, no emission even at high intensity.
- Do not confuse wave nature of light with matter waves; both are important.

CHAPTER: ATOMS AND NUCLEI

BOHR MODEL:
- Electrons revolve only in certain allowed orbits.
- Angular momentum quantisation:
  mvr = nh/2pi

Radius of nth orbit:
- rn = n^2 a0
  where a0 = 0.53 A for hydrogen

Energy of nth orbit:
- En = -13.6/n^2 eV

Frequency of emitted photon:
- h nu = E2 - E1

HYDROGEN SPECTRUM:
- 1/lambda = R(1/n1^2 - 1/n2^2), n2 > n1
Series:
- Lyman: n1 = 1
- Balmer: n1 = 2
- Paschen: n1 = 3

NUCLEAR TERMS:
- Mass number A = protons + neutrons
- Atomic number Z = protons
- Isotopes: same Z, different A
- Isobars: same A, different Z
- Isotones: same neutrons

RADIOACTIVE DECAY:
- N = N0 e^(-lambda t)
- Activity: A = lambda N
- Half-life: T1/2 = 0.693/lambda
- Mean life: tau = 1/lambda

BINDING ENERGY:
- BE = Delta m c^2
- Greater binding energy per nucleon means more stable nucleus.

COMMON NUCLEAR REACTIONS:
- Alpha decay: A decreases by 4, Z decreases by 2
- Beta minus decay: neutron -> proton + electron + antineutrino
- Gamma decay: no change in A or Z, only de-excitation

COMMON MISTAKES:
- Do not write Bohr model for multi-electron atoms as exact theory.
- Half-life is independent of temperature and pressure.
- In gamma decay, nucleus loses energy, not particles changing A and Z.

CHAPTER: SEMICONDUCTOR ELECTRONICS

SEMICONDUCTORS:
- Conductivity lies between conductors and insulators.
- Examples: Si, Ge

TYPES:
- Intrinsic semiconductor: pure semiconductor
- Extrinsic semiconductor: doped semiconductor

DOPING:
- n-type: pentavalent impurity, electrons are majority carriers
- p-type: trivalent impurity, holes are majority carriers

p-n JUNCTION:
- Depletion region formed near junction.
- Barrier potential opposes further diffusion.

BIASING:
- Forward bias: current increases strongly, junction resistance low
- Reverse bias: very small current, high resistance

DIODE AS RECTIFIER:
- Converts AC to DC.
- Half-wave and full-wave rectifiers are important applications.

LOGIC GATES:
- OR gate: output 1 if any input is 1
- AND gate: output 1 only if all inputs are 1
- NOT gate: output opposite of input
- NAND and NOR are universal gates

TRANSISTOR BASICS:
- Used as amplifier and switch.
- Current gains:
  alpha = Ic/Ie
  beta = Ic/Ib
  beta = alpha/(1-alpha)

COMMON SEMICONDUCTOR MISTAKES:
- In n-type semiconductor, material is still electrically neutral.
- Majority carriers in n-type are electrons, in p-type are holes.
- Forward bias reduces barrier potential; reverse bias increases it.
- Do not confuse rectification with amplification.

MUST-KNOW FORMULAS

- 1/f = 1/v + 1/u  (mirror)
- 1/f = 1/v - 1/u  (lens)
- m = -v/u (mirror)
- m = v/u (lens)
- P = 1/f
- beta = lambda D/d
- h nu = phi + Kmax
- lambda = h/p
- En = -13.6/n^2 eV
- 1/lambda = R(1/n1^2 - 1/n2^2)
- N = N0 e^(-lambda t)
- T1/2 = 0.693/lambda
- BE = Delta m c^2

VERY IMPORTANT CONCEPTUAL CHECKS:
- Ray optics is sign-convention sensitive.
- Wave optics asks pattern logic: interference, diffraction, polarisation.
- Modern physics is direct and scoring if formulas are remembered properly.
- Semiconductor questions often test carrier type, biasing effect, and logic truth tables.

ONE-LINE REVISION:
- Optics chapters require diagrams and sign convention discipline.
- Modern physics is theory plus short formulas; extremely high-yield for exams.
- Semiconductor electronics is easy scoring if you remember definitions, biasing, and gates.
- Most mistakes happen by mixing formulas from mirrors and lenses, or intensity with frequency in photoelectric effect.
