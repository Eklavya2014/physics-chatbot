BIOCHEMISTRY AND MEDICINAL CHEMISTRY - COMPREHENSIVE GUIDE
===========================================================

PART 1: AMINO ACIDS AND PROTEINS

AMINO ACIDS (20 standard):
General structure: H₂N-CHR-COOH (alpha-amino acid).
In solution at pH 7: zwitterion form NH₃⁺-CHR-COO⁻.
Isoelectric point (pI): pH at which amino acid has zero net charge.
pI = (pKa1 + pKa2)/2.

CLASSIFICATION:
Nonpolar/hydrophobic: Glycine (G), Alanine (A), Valine (V), Leucine (L), Isoleucine (I), Proline (P), Phenylalanine (F), Methionine (M), Tryptophan (W).
Polar/uncharged: Serine (S), Threonine (T), Cysteine (C), Tyrosine (Y), Asparagine (N), Glutamine (Q).
Positively charged (basic): Lysine (K) pI ~9.7, Arginine (R) pI ~10.8, Histidine (H) pI ~7.6.
Negatively charged (acidic): Aspartate (D) pI ~2.8, Glutamate (E) pI ~3.2.

ESSENTIAL AMINO ACIDS (cannot be synthesized — must be in diet):
Mnemonic PVT TIM HaLL: Phenylalanine, Valine, Threonine, Tryptophan, Isoleucine, Methionine, Histidine, Leucine, Lysine.
(Arginine semi-essential for adults, essential for children.)

PEPTIDE BOND:
Formed between α-carboxyl of one amino acid and α-amino of next, releasing H₂O.
Resonance: partial double bond character. Planar, trans configuration preferred.
N-terminus (free NH₂) and C-terminus (free COOH) in peptide chain.
Peptide bonds resistant to hydrolysis (kinetically stable but thermodynamically unstable).
Hydrolysis: H₂O + peptide → amino acids. Catalyzed by HCl (100°C) or proteolytic enzymes.

PROTEIN STRUCTURE:
Primary structure: amino acid sequence. Determined by DNA. Held by covalent peptide bonds.
Secondary structure: local folding.
α-helix: right-handed helix, 3.6 residues/turn, i to i+4 H-bonding (NH···O=C). Found in globular and fibrous proteins.
β-pleated sheet: extended chains H-bond laterally. Parallel or antiparallel.
Tertiary structure: overall 3D fold of single polypeptide.
Interactions: hydrophobic, H-bonds, electrostatic (salt bridges), disulfide bonds (Cys-Cys).
Quaternary structure: assembly of multiple polypeptide subunits.

PROTEIN FOLDING:
Driven by thermodynamics (ΔG < 0).
Key force: hydrophobic effect — nonpolar residues bury inside, polar on surface.
Chaperones: proteins that help other proteins fold correctly.
Denaturation: disruption of 3D structure. Causes: heat, pH extremes, urea, detergents. Usually irreversible.
Amyloid fibers: misfolded proteins forming β-sheet aggregates. Alzheimer's, Parkinson's, prions.

ENZYMES:
Proteins (RNA enzymes = ribozymes).
Active site: specific region where substrate binds and reaction occurs.
Cofactors: metal ions (Zn²⁺, Mg²⁺, Fe²⁺) or coenzymes (NAD⁺, FAD, CoA, vitamins).
Holoenzyme = apoenzyme (protein) + cofactor.

Enzyme kinetics:
Michaelis-Menten: v = Vmax[S]/(Km + [S]).
Km = [S] at half Vmax. Low Km = high affinity.
kcat = Vmax/[E]total = turnover number (reactions per second per enzyme).
kcat/Km = catalytic efficiency. Diffusion limit ~10⁸-10⁹ M⁻¹s⁻¹.
Lineweaver-Burk plot: 1/v = (Km/Vmax)(1/[S]) + 1/Vmax. Straight line, useful for Ki, inhibition type.

Enzyme inhibition:
Competitive: inhibitor looks like substrate, binds active site. Increases apparent Km, Vmax unchanged. Reversed by increasing [S].
Noncompetitive: inhibitor binds elsewhere (allosteric). Decreases Vmax, Km unchanged.
Uncompetitive: inhibitor binds only ES complex. Decreases both Km and Vmax.
Irreversible: covalent modification. Organophosphates inhibit acetylcholinesterase (nerve agents, some pesticides).
Suicide inhibitors: look like substrate, become reactive inside active site (penicillin → β-lactamase).

PART 2: CARBOHYDRATES

MONOSACCHARIDES:
Aldoses: CHO at C1 (glyceraldehyde, glucose, galactose).
Ketoses: C=O at C2 (dihydroxyacetone, fructose).
Trioses, tetroses, pentoses, hexoses (based on carbon number).
D/L configuration: based on C farthest from carbonyl (reference to D/L glyceraldehyde).
Most biological sugars are D-form.

GLUCOSE (D-glucopyranose):
Open chain (Fischer projection): aldohexose. C1 aldehyde, C2-C5 CHOH, C6 CH₂OH.
Cyclic form (Haworth): hemiacetal formation between C1 and C5-OH.
α-D-glucopyranose: C1-OH (anomeric -OH) axial/below plane.
β-D-glucopyranose: C1-OH equatorial/above plane.
β form more stable (anomeric effect minimized, all substituents equatorial).
Mutarotation: α ⇌ β in solution (through open-chain form). Equilibrium: ~64% β, 36% α.
[α]D = +112° (α), +18.7° (β), +52.7° (equilibrium).

Important reactions of glucose:
Reducing agent (open chain form): reduces Tollens', Fehling's, Cu²⁺.
Oxidation: COOH (gluconic acid with mild oxidant), COOH at both ends (glucaric acid with HNO₃).
Reduction: sorbitol (glucose + NaBH₄ or H₂/Ni).
Osazone: glucose + excess phenylhydrazine → osazone (yellow crystals, same as fructose → identical osazones → share same C3-C6).

DISACCHARIDES:
Maltose: α-D-glucose-1,4-α-D-glucose. Reducing sugar (free C1 on second glucose). From starch hydrolysis.
Cellobiose: β-D-glucose-1,4-β-D-glucose. From cellulose hydrolysis.
Lactose: β-D-galactose-1,4-β-D-glucose. Reducing sugar. Milk sugar. Lactase deficiency → lactose intolerance.
Sucrose: α-D-glucose-1,2-β-D-fructose. NON-reducing (no free anomeric OH). Invert sugar (hydrolysis gives glucose + fructose).

POLYSACCHARIDES:
Starch: storage in plants. Two components:
Amylose: linear α-1,4 glucose chains. Helical structure. Blue-black with I₂.
Amylopectin: branched, α-1,4 (main chain) and α-1,6 (branch points every ~30 units).
Glycogen: animal starch. More branched than amylopectin (~10 units between branches). Stored in liver and muscle.
Cellulose: structural in plants. β-1,4 glucose units. Humans cannot digest (no cellulase).
Chitin: N-acetylglucosamine β-1,4. Found in insect exoskeletons, fungi cell walls.
Hyaluronic acid: alternating N-acetylglucosamine and glucuronate. Joint fluid, extracellular matrix.

PART 3: LIPIDS

FATTY ACIDS:
Saturated: no double bonds. Solid at room temperature (animal fats).
Unsaturated: one or more double bonds. Usually cis configuration. Liquid at room temperature (vegetable oils).
Monounsaturated: one double bond (oleic acid, 18:1, Δ9 — olive oil).
Polyunsaturated: >1 double bond (linoleic acid 18:2, linolenic acid 18:3, EPA 20:5, DHA 22:6 — fish oil).
Essential fatty acids (can't synthesize): linoleic (ω-6), linolenic (ω-3).
Trans fats: unsaturated but trans configuration. Solid at room T. Formed by partial hydrogenation.

NOMENCLATURE:
18:2 Δ9,12 means 18 carbons, 2 double bonds, at positions 9 and 12.
ω-3 means double bond 3 carbons from methyl end.

TRIGLYCERIDES (Triacylglycerols):
Glycerol + 3 fatty acids (ester bonds).
Hydrolysis: triglyceride + 3NaOH → soap (sodium salts of fatty acids) + glycerol (saponification).
Soap: micelle formation, sodium salt of fatty acid. R-COO⁻Na⁺.
Hydrophobic tail (fatty acid chain) and hydrophilic head (carboxylate).
Hard water: Ca²⁺, Mg²⁺ precipitate soap. Detergents don't have this problem.
Rancidity: oxidative (O₂ attacks double bonds) or hydrolytic (hydrolysis of ester bonds).
Antioxidants (vitamin E, BHA, BHT) prevent oxidative rancidity.

PHOSPHOLIPIDS:
Glycerol + 2 fatty acids + phosphate group + alcohol.
Phosphatidylcholine (lecithin): most common. Choline head group.
Forms bilayer membrane: hydrophobic tails inside, hydrophilic heads outside.
Fluid mosaic model: membrane is fluid (lipids can move laterally), embedded with proteins.
Cholesterol: regulates membrane fluidity. Steroid.
Sphingomyelin: phospholipid in nerve myelin sheath.

STEROIDS:
Four-ring carbon skeleton. Hydrophobic.
Cholesterol: membrane component, precursor of bile acids, steroid hormones, vitamin D.
Bile acids: emulsify fats in digestion.
Steroid hormones: testosterone (male), estrogen (female), progesterone (pregnancy), cortisol (stress).
Vitamin D: synthesized from cholesterol in skin + sunlight.
Anabolic steroids: testosterone analogs → muscle building.

PART 4: NUCLEIC ACIDS

NUCLEOTIDES:
Nucleotide = nitrogenous base + pentose sugar + 1-3 phosphate groups.
DNA: deoxyribose sugar (no 2'-OH). RNA: ribose sugar (has 2'-OH).
Purines: Adenine (A), Guanine (G). Two-ring structure.
Pyrimidines: Cytosine (C), Thymine (T, DNA only), Uracil (U, RNA only). One-ring structure.
Base pairing (Watson-Crick): A=T (2 H-bonds), G≡C (3 H-bonds) in DNA.
A=U (2 H-bonds) in RNA.

DNA STRUCTURE:
Double helix (Watson & Crick, 1953). Right-handed B-DNA most common.
Two antiparallel strands. Sugar-phosphate backbone outside. Bases inside.
Major groove and minor groove (accessible to proteins).
Diameter: ~2 nm. Pitch: 3.4 nm per 10 base pairs.
Base stacking: van der Waals forces + hydrophobic effect stabilize helix.
Hydrogen bonds between base pairs.

DNA REPLICATION:
Semi-conservative: each new DNA has one old + one new strand.
Helicase: unwinds double helix at replication fork.
Primase: makes short RNA primer.
DNA polymerase III: synthesizes new strand (5'→3' direction only).
Leading strand: continuous synthesis. Lagging strand: Okazaki fragments (discontinuous).
DNA polymerase I: removes RNA primer, fills gap.
DNA ligase: seals nicks.
Proofreading: DNA polymerase has 3'→5' exonuclease activity → removes mismatched bases.

RNA AND PROTEIN SYNTHESIS:
Central dogma: DNA → RNA (transcription) → Protein (translation).
Transcription: RNA polymerase synthesizes mRNA from DNA template (5'→3').
mRNA: carries genetic code from nucleus to ribosomes.
tRNA: carries specific amino acid to ribosome. Anticodon matches codon.
rRNA: structural and catalytic component of ribosome.
Genetic code: 64 triplet codons → 20 amino acids + 3 stop codons.
Codon: mRNA triplet. Anticodon: tRNA triplet (complementary, antiparallel to codon).
Start codon: AUG (methionine). Stop codons: UAA, UAG, UGA.
Degenerate code: most amino acids encoded by multiple codons (wobble).

Translation: ribosome reads mRNA 5'→3', aminoacyl-tRNA enters A site, peptide bond forms, ribosome translocates.
Post-translational modifications: glycosylation, phosphorylation, acetylation, cleavage of signal peptide.

MUTATIONS:
Point mutation: change in single base.
Silent: no change in amino acid (degenerate code).
Missense: different amino acid → may change protein function.
Nonsense: creates stop codon → truncated protein.
Frameshift: insertion/deletion → shifts reading frame → garbled sequence downstream.
Sickle cell disease: A→T mutation in β-globin gene → Glu→Val at position 6 → HbS polymerizes when deoxygenated.

PART 5: MEDICINAL CHEMISTRY

DRUG-RECEPTOR INTERACTIONS:
Receptor: macromolecule (usually protein) that binds drug.
Agonist: binds receptor and activates it.
Antagonist: binds receptor, blocks but does not activate.
Partial agonist: activates to less than maximum.

PHARMACOKINETICS (ADME):
Absorption: drug enters bloodstream. Routes: oral, IV, sublingual, transdermal, inhalation.
Distribution: drug distributes to tissues. Blood-brain barrier limits CNS access.
Metabolism: liver cytochrome P450 enzymes (CYP). First-pass effect (oral drugs metabolized before systemic circulation).
Excretion: mainly kidneys (urine) or bile (feces).
Half-life (t₁/₂): time for plasma concentration to decrease by half. t₁/₂ = 0.693/k_el.

STRUCTURE-ACTIVITY RELATIONSHIPS (SAR):
Pharmacophore: minimum structural features required for activity.
Bioisosteres: groups with similar size, shape, electronic properties (F for H, NH for O, etc.).
Prodrug: inactive compound converted to active drug by metabolism.
Lead compound: starting point for drug development.

IMPORTANT DRUG CLASSES:
Antibiotics:
Penicillins: β-lactam ring inhibits bacterial cell wall synthesis (transpeptidase).
β-lactamase: bacterial enzyme that hydrolyzes β-lactam → resistance.
Cephalosporins: broader spectrum β-lactams.
Tetracyclines: inhibit 30S ribosome → block aminoacyl-tRNA binding.
Aminoglycosides (gentamicin): inhibit 30S ribosome → misread mRNA.
Macrolides (erythromycin): inhibit 50S ribosome → block translocation.
Quinolones (ciprofloxacin): inhibit DNA gyrase (topoisomerase II).
Sulfonamides: inhibit dihydropteroate synthase → block folate synthesis.

Anticancer drugs:
Cisplatin: forms cross-links in DNA → blocks replication.
Taxol (paclitaxel): stabilizes microtubules → prevents cell division.
Methotrexate: DHFR inhibitor → blocks folate metabolism → prevents DNA synthesis.
Alkylating agents (cyclophosphamide): cross-link DNA strands.
Topoisomerase inhibitors: prevent DNA unwinding.

Cardiovascular:
Statins (simvastatin): HMG-CoA reductase inhibitors → lower cholesterol.
Beta-blockers (propranolol): block β-adrenergic receptors → lower heart rate, BP.
ACE inhibitors (enalapril): block angiotensin-converting enzyme → lower BP.
Aspirin: irreversible COX inhibitor → reduces prostaglandins → anti-inflammatory, antiplatelet.

CNS drugs:
Morphine/opioids: bind opioid receptors → pain relief, euphoria, addiction.
Benzodiazepines (diazepam): enhance GABA → anxiolytic, sedative.
SSRIs (fluoxetine): block serotonin reuptake → antidepressant.
L-DOPA: dopamine precursor, crosses BBB → Parkinson's treatment.

Antiviral:
Acyclovir: guanosine analog → inhibits viral DNA polymerase.
HIV antiretrovirals: NRTIs (AZT), NNRTIs, protease inhibitors, integrase inhibitors.
Neuraminidase inhibitors (oseltamivir/Tamiflu): block influenza virus release from cells.
