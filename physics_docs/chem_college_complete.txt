COLLEGE CHEMISTRY - ADVANCED GUIDE
=====================================

CHAPTER 1: QUANTUM CHEMISTRY

MOLECULAR ORBITAL THEORY (DETAILED):
Linear Combination of Atomic Orbitals (LCAO):
ψ_bonding = ψ_A + ψ_B (constructive interference, lower energy)
ψ_antibonding = ψ_A - ψ_B (destructive interference, higher energy, denoted *)

MO DIAGRAMS FOR DIATOMIC MOLECULES:
For Li₂ to N₂: σ1s, σ*1s, σ2s, σ*2s, π2p, π2p, σ2p, π*2p, π*2p, σ*2p
For O₂ to Ne₂: σ1s, σ*1s, σ2s, σ*2s, σ2p, π2p, π2p, π*2p, π*2p, σ*2p

Bond order = (bonding - antibonding)/2
O₂: bond order 2, has 2 unpaired electrons (paramagnetic) — explained by MO, not VB theory!
N₂: bond order 3 (triple bond, very stable)
F₂: bond order 1, He₂: bond order 0 (doesn't exist)

VALENCE BOND THEORY:
Bond formed by overlap of atomic orbitals.
σ bond: head-on overlap, free rotation.
π bond: lateral overlap, no rotation (causes cis-trans isomerism).
Resonance: when single Lewis structure inadequate (benzene, O₃, CO₃²⁻).
Resonance stabilization energy: difference between calculated and actual energy.

MOLECULAR SPECTROSCOPY:
ΔE = hν = hc/λ = hcν̃ (ν̃ = wavenumber in cm⁻¹)

Rotational spectroscopy (microwave): E_J = BJ(J+1), B = h/(8π²Ic)
Gives bond lengths and moments of inertia.
Selection rule: ΔJ = ±1, molecule must have permanent dipole.

Vibrational spectroscopy (IR): E_v = (v + ½)hν, ν = (1/2π)√(k/μ)
k = force constant (bond strength), μ = reduced mass.
Selection rule: Δv = ±1, dipole must change during vibration.
IR active: heteronuclear bonds (C=O, O-H, N-H, C-H, etc.)
IR inactive: homonuclear bonds (H₂, N₂, O₂).
Fingerprint region: 600-1500 cm⁻¹ (unique to each molecule).

Raman spectroscopy: complement to IR. Polarizability must change.
Mutual exclusion rule: for centrosymmetric molecules, IR and Raman bands are mutually exclusive.

ELECTRONIC SPECTROSCOPY (UV-Vis):
Beer-Lambert law: A = εcl (A = absorbance, ε = molar absorptivity, c = concentration, l = path length)
A = log(I₀/I) = -log T (T = transmittance)
Chromophore: group responsible for absorption (C=O, C=C, benzene ring, etc.)
Auxochrome: shifts absorption to longer wavelength.

NMR SPECTROSCOPY:
Nuclear Magnetic Resonance: nuclei in magnetic field absorb at specific frequencies.
¹H NMR: chemical shift (δ, ppm), integration, multiplicity (n+1 rule).
Chemical shift: δ = (ν_sample - ν_reference)/(ν_spectrometer) × 10⁶
TMS (tetramethylsilane): reference standard, δ = 0.
Shielded protons: appear at lower δ (upfield). Deshielded: higher δ (downfield).
Coupling constant J: splitting due to neighboring H atoms. J in Hz.

MASS SPECTROMETRY:
Ionize molecule → fragment. M⁺ = molecular ion (gives molecular weight).
Base peak: most abundant fragment.
McLafferty rearrangement, α-cleavage, retro Diels-Alder.

CHAPTER 2: THERMODYNAMICS (ADVANCED)

CHEMICAL POTENTIAL:
μ = (∂G/∂n)_{T,P} = μ° + RT ln a (a = activity)
For ideal solution: a = x (mole fraction)
For ideal gas: a = P/P° (fugacity = effective pressure for real gases)
Equilibrium: μ same in all phases/components.

PHASE EQUILIBRIA:
Gibbs phase rule: F = C - P + 2
F = degrees of freedom, C = components, P = phases.
Phase diagram: plot of T vs P showing phases.
Triple point: solid, liquid, gas coexist.
Critical point: above which liquid-gas distinction disappears.
Clausius-Clapeyron equation: dP/dT = ΔH_vap/(TΔV) ≈ P ΔH_vap/(RT²)
ln(P₂/P₁) = -ΔH_vap/R (1/T₂ - 1/T₁)

ACTIVITY AND FUGACITY:
For real gases: f = φP (φ = fugacity coefficient)
For real solutions: a = γx (γ = activity coefficient)
Raoult's law: ideal solution, γ = 1 for all compositions.
Henry's law: dilute solution, γ → constant for solute.
Positive deviation: weaker A-B interactions than A-A, B-B (ethanol-water).
Negative deviation: stronger A-B (acetone-chloroform).

CHAPTER 3: ADVANCED ORGANIC CHEMISTRY

REACTION MECHANISMS IN DETAIL:

SN1 MECHANISM:
Step 1: Ionization → carbocation (rate-determining step)
Step 2: Nucleophile attack (fast)
Favored by: tertiary alkyl halides, polar protic solvents (water, alcohol), weak nucleophiles.
Racemization: flat carbocation attacked from both sides.
Rearrangements possible: hydride/methide shift to more stable carbocation.

SN2 MECHANISM:
Single step: backside attack while leaving group departs simultaneously.
Favored by: primary alkyl halides, polar aprotic solvents (acetone, DMF, DMSO), strong nucleophiles.
Inversion of configuration (Walden inversion): like umbrella flipping.
Second-order kinetics: rate = k[RX][Nu⁻].

E1 vs E2:
E1: carbocation intermediate, favored by tertiary, protic solvents, weak base.
E2: concerted, favored by strong base, anti-periplanar arrangement required.
Zaitsev's rule: major alkene product is more substituted.
Hofmann's rule: bulky base gives less substituted alkene.

PERICYCLIC REACTIONS (Woodward-Hoffmann rules):
Orbital symmetry controls these reactions.
Thermally allowed ≡ photochemically forbidden (and vice versa).
Electrocyclic reactions: ring opening/closing. Thermal: conrotatory (4n), disrotatory (4n+2).
Cycloadditions: [4+2] Diels-Alder thermally allowed. [2+2] thermally forbidden.
Sigmatropic rearrangements: [1,3]-H thermal forbidden; [1,5]-H thermal allowed.

DIELS-ALDER REACTION:
Diene (4π) + dienophile (2π) → cyclohexene.
Requires s-cis diene conformation.
Electron-rich diene + electron-poor dienophile (most favorable).
Endo rule: kinetic product usually endo (secondary orbital interactions).
Stereospecific: syn addition, retains stereochemistry of dienophile.

AROMATIC CHEMISTRY:
Hückel's rule: aromatic if (4n+2) π electrons in continuous cyclic π system.
Benzene: 6 π electrons (n=1), perfectly stable.
Naphthalene: 10 π electrons (n=2).
Annulenes: [4n+2] annulenes are aromatic.
Anti-aromatic: 4n π electrons (cyclobutadiene: 4 π, very unstable).

EAS reactions and directing effects:
Activating/ortho-para directors: -OH, -OR, -NH₂, -NR₂, -NHCOR, alkyl groups (donate electrons).
Deactivating/meta directors: -NO₂, -CN, -COOH, -CHO, -COR, -SO₃H (withdraw electrons).
Halogens: deactivating but ortho-para directors (lone pair donation overrides induction).

CARBONYL CHEMISTRY:
Nucleophilic addition to C=O:
Aldehydes > ketones (steric and electronic).
Hard nucleophiles add to C=O. Soft nucleophiles prefer conjugate addition (1,4) to α,β-unsaturated carbonyls.

Enolate chemistry: α-carbon deprotonation → enolate → alkylation, aldol, Claisen.
Aldol: enolate attacks another carbonyl → β-hydroxy carbonyl (aldol product) → dehydration → α,β-unsaturated carbonyl.
Claisen condensation: ester enolate + ester → β-ketoester.
Michael addition: nucleophile adds to β-carbon of α,β-unsaturated carbonyl.

HETEROCYCLIC CHEMISTRY:
Pyridine: 6-membered, N replaces CH. Aromatic, 6π electrons. N lone pair not in aromatic system. Basic (pKa ~5). EAS deactivated, especially at 2,4 positions.
Pyrrole: 5-membered, NH. Aromatic (N lone pair part of ring). Acidic NH (pKa ~17). EAS activated.
Furan: O, 5-membered, aromatic. Less aromatic than benzene.
Thiophene: S, 5-membered, most aromatic of five-membered heterocycles.
Imidazole: two N atoms, 5-membered. One basic N, one NH. Component of histidine.
Purine, pyrimidine: components of nucleic acids.

POLYMER CHEMISTRY:
Addition polymerization: monomers add without byproduct.
Free radical: initiation, propagation, termination.
Cationic: carbocation intermediate.
Anionic: carbanion intermediate.
Coordination (Ziegler-Natta): stereoregular polymers.
Condensation polymerization: with byproduct (usually water).
Nylon 6,6: diamine + diacid. Polyester: diol + diacid. Proteins, polysaccharides.

Degree of polymerization: n = M_polymer/M_monomer
Number average: M_n = ΣN_iM_i/ΣN_i
Weight average: M_w = ΣN_iM_i²/ΣN_iM_i
Polydispersity index: PDI = M_w/M_n (1 for monodisperse, >1 for real polymers)

Physical constants:

Nₐ = 6.022 × 10²³ mol⁻¹
F = 96485 C/mol
h = 6.626 × 10⁻³⁴ J·s
k_B = 1.38 × 10⁻²³ J/K

Key equations:
ΔG° = -RT ln K = -nFE°
pH = pKa + log([A⁻]/[HA]) (Henderson-Hasselbalch)
Arrhenius: k = Ae^(-Ea/RT)
Nernst: E = E° - (0.0592/n) log Q (25°C)
Beer-Lambert: A = εcl
van't Hoff: ln K₂/K₁ = -ΔH°/R (1/T₂ - 1/T₁)
