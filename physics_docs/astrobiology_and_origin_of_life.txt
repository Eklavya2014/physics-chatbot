1. WHAT IS ASTROBIOLOGY?
--------------------------
Astrobiology studies the origin, evolution, distribution, and future of life in
the universe. It addresses three central questions:
  1. How did life begin and evolve on Earth?
  2. Is there life elsewhere in the universe?
  3. What is the future of life on Earth and beyond?

Life's Requirements:
  Liquid solvent: Water (on Earth); possibly ammonia, methanol, or formamide elsewhere.
  Carbon source: CO2 or organic compounds.
  Energy source: Sunlight (photosynthesis), chemical redox reactions (chemolithotrophy),
  heat (geothermal).
  Elements: C, H, N, O, P, S — CHNOPS. Trace elements: Fe, Mg, Zn, Cu, Mo, etc.

1.1 TIMELINE OF LIFE ON EARTH
-------------------------------
~4.6 billion years ago (Gya): Earth forms.
~4.4 Gya          : Liquid water present (zircon evidence).
~4.1–3.8 Gya      : Late Heavy Bombardment.
~3.8 Gya          : Earliest chemical evidence of life (carbon isotope fractionation).
~3.5 Gya          : Oldest microbial fossils (Apex Chert, Australia); stromatolites.
~3.0–2.7 Gya      : Oxygen-producing cyanobacteria evolving.
~2.4 Gya          : Great Oxidation Event (GOE) — O2 accumulates in atmosphere.
~2.0 Gya          : Eukaryotic cells first appear.
~1.2 Gya          : Sexual reproduction evolves.
~600–700 Mya      : First multicellular animals.
~541 Mya          : Cambrian explosion — rapid diversification.
~500 Mya          : Plants colonise land.
~360 Mya          : Amphibians onto land.
~230 Mya          : First dinosaurs.
~200 Mya          : First mammals.
~65 Mya           : K-Pg mass extinction; mammal diversification.
~7 Mya            : Human-chimp divergence.
~300,000 years ago: Homo sapiens appear.

2. ORIGIN OF LIFE HYPOTHESES
------------------------------
Prebiotic Chemistry:
  Miller-Urey Experiment (1953): Simulated early Earth atmosphere (H2, CH4, NH3, H2O)
  + lightning → amino acids (glycine, alanine, etc.). Proved organic molecules can
  form abiotically. Modern revision: Early atmosphere may have been CO2/N2 dominated
  (less reducing) → but amino acid synthesis still occurs in localized niches.

  RNA World Hypothesis:
  RNA can both store information (like DNA) and catalyse reactions (ribozymes).
  Proposed that first life used RNA alone, before proteins evolved.
  Evidence: Ribosomes are ribozymes (peptidyl transferase activity = RNA); group I/II
  self-splicing introns; HDV ribozyme; in vitro evolution (SELEX) generates new ribozymes.
  Problem: How did RNA form abiotically? RNA nucleotide synthesis is chemically challenging.
  Sutherland synthesis (2009): Showed cyanosulfidic chemistry can generate pyrimidine
  ribonucleotides under plausible prebiotic conditions.

Hydrothermal Vent Hypothesis:
  Lost City vents (alkaline, warm, ~70–90°C) provide: Mineral catalysts (Fe-S, Ni-S
  clusters), H2 + CO2 → organic molecules (like an ancient acetyl-CoA pathway),
  proton gradients (natural chemiosmosis), protected microcompartments.
  Championed by Mike Russell and Nick Lane.
  Earliest life may have been a chemolithoautotroph.

Panspermia:
  Life or organic molecules delivered to Earth from space.
  Evidence: Amino acids in carbonaceous meteorites (Murchison meteorite ~70 amino acids);
  PAHs in meteorites; phosphorus in Enceladus plumes.
  Lithopanspermia: Rocks ejected from one planet land on another (physically plausible).
  Directed panspermia (Crick & Orgel, 1973): Life deliberately seeded by intelligence
  (speculative/controversial).

Compartmentalisation:
  Life requires membranes. Fatty acids spontaneously form vesicles under aqueous conditions
  (Szostak lab). Fatty acid vesicles can grow, divide, and take up nucleotides.
  Protocell: Lipid vesicle + replicating RNA → simplest ancestor of life.

3. EXTREMOPHILES & HABITABILITY
---------------------------------
Extremophiles demonstrate the remarkable range of conditions compatible with life:

Type              | Condition              | Organism Example           | Temp./Conditions
------------------|------------------------|----------------------------|-----------------
Thermophile       | High temperature       | Thermus aquaticus          | 50–80°C
Hyperthermophile  | Very high temp         | Pyrolobus fumarii          | up to 121°C
Psychrophile      | Low temperature        | Chroococcidiopsis (algae)  | -20 to 10°C
Acidophile        | Low pH                 | Acidithiobacillus          | pH 0–2
Alkaliphile       | High pH                | Natronobacterium           | pH 10–12
Halophile         | High salt              | Halobacterium salinarum    | 20–30% NaCl
Piezophile        | High pressure          | Marianas Trench bacteria   | >100 MPa
Radioresistant    | Ionising radiation     | Deinococcus radiodurans    | 1.5 million Gy
Xerophile         | Desiccation            | Tardigrades (water bears)  | cryptobiosis
Endolithic        | Inside rock            | Cyanobacteria in Antarctic | inside rock crevices

Tardigrades (Water Bears):
  Microscopic (~0.5mm); most stress-resistant animals known.
  Survive: -272°C to +150°C, 6,000 atm pressure, hard radiation, vacuum of space,
  desiccation for decades (cryptobiosis — anhydrobiosis with trehalose production).
  Unique proteins: IDPs (intrinsically disordered proteins) form protective glass on drying.

4. SEARCH FOR EXTRATERRESTRIAL LIFE
--------------------------------------
Mars:
  Evidence for ancient habitability: Liquid water flowed ~3.5 Gya (valley networks,
  delta deposits in Jezero Crater, mineral evidence — phyllosilicates, sulphates).
  Current environment: -60°C average; 0.6% atm pressure (mostly CO2); intense UV;
  perchlorate soil (toxic). Subsurface: possible briny liquid water (MARSIS radar).
  Mars 2020 (Perseverance): Collecting samples from Jezero; astrobiology mission;
  MOXIE (O2 production from CO2 demonstrated). Sample return mission planned ~2033.

Europa (Jupiter's moon):
  Subsurface liquid water ocean (~100 km deep; 2x Earth's ocean volume) under ~10 km ice shell.
  Heat from tidal flexing. Water-rock interface → hydrothermal activity possible.
  Plumes of water vapour detected. Europa Clipper mission (launched 2024): Fly-bys.
  ESA's JUICE: Orbits Ganymede; flybys of Europa and Callisto.

Enceladus (Saturn's moon):
  Active cryovolcanism: Plumes from south polar region contain water, H2, CO2, NH3,
  organics, silica nanoparticles (evidence of hydrothermal activity at seafloor).
  Cassini mission detected molecular hydrogen → indicates serpentinisation → energy
  source compatible with life.
  Dragonfly mission: Titan (Saturn's moon) — methane lakes; organic chemistry.

Exoplanets:
  Habitable Zone (HZ): Region around star where liquid water possible.
  Kepler/TESS: Thousands of exoplanets discovered. Proxima Centauri b: Nearest star;
  in HZ. TRAPPIST-1: 7 Earth-sized planets; 3 in HZ; 40 light-years away.
  Biosignatures: O2, O3, H2O, CH4 in atmosphere (James Webb Space Telescope —
  first atmospheric detections). Technosignatures: Radio signals, industrial chemicals.

5. UNIVERSAL FEATURES OF LIFE & LAST UNIVERSAL COMMON ANCESTOR (LUCA)
------------------------------------------------------------------------
All known life shares:
  - Genetic code (universal, with minor variations)
  - DNA double helix
  - Ribosomal RNA (16S/18S rRNA — basis of all phylogenetics)
  - ATP as energy currency
  - Lipid bilayer membranes
  - Same 20 amino acids (L-form)
  - Same nucleotides

LUCA Reconstruction:
  Phylogenetic analysis of universally conserved genes → reconstruct LUCA.
  ~355 gene families likely present in LUCA (Weiss et al., 2016).
  LUCA was: Thermophilic (heat-loving); anaerobic; used H2 + CO2 → acetate (Wood-Ljungdahl pathway);
  had ion pumps; nitrogen-fixing ability; lived in hydrothermal vents environment.
  LUCA was NOT the first life — it was the last common ancestor of Bacteria and Archaea.

6. MAJOR EVOLUTIONARY TRANSITIONS
------------------------------------
Major Transitions in Evolution (Maynard Smith & Szathmáry, 1995):
  1. Replicating molecules → Compartmentalised replicators (protocells)
  2. RNA-based genome → DNA + protein-based genome
  3. Prokaryote → Eukaryote (endosymbiosis of mitochondria; then plastids)
  4. Unicellular → Multicellular organisms
  5. Asexual → Sexual reproduction
  6. Solitary individuals → Social colonies (eusociality)
  7. Primate societies → Language-based human societies

Endosymbiotic Theory (Lynn Margulis, 1967):
  Mitochondria: Descended from alpha-proteobacteria (closest relative: Rickettsiales).
  Plastids/Chloroplasts: Descended from cyanobacteria.
  Evidence: Own circular DNA; 70S ribosomes (not 80S); divide by binary fission;
  double membrane; gene transfer to nucleus over time.
  Secondary endosymbiosis: Eukaryote engulfs photosynthetic eukaryote → cryptomonads,
  diatoms (3 or 4 membrane chloroplasts).

[PHILOSOPHICAL NOTE] Fermi Paradox: If life is common, why don't we see evidence
of other intelligences? Proposed resolutions: Rare Earth (complex life is unusual);
Great Filter (most civilisations self-destruct or go extinct); Zoo hypothesis (they
choose not to contact us); simulation argument. The search for life — even microbial
life on Mars or Enceladus — would be among the most profound discoveries in history.
Finding that life arose independently multiple times would suggest life is a near-
inevitable outcome of planetary chemistry under the right conditions.
