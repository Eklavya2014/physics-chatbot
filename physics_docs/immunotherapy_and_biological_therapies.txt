1. WHAT IS IMMUNOTHERAPY?
--------------------------
Immunotherapy uses the immune system (or immune-based agents) to prevent, detect,
or treat disease. Represents a revolution in oncology and treatment of autoimmune/
inflammatory diseases. Nobel Prize 2018: James Allison & Tasuku Honjo — immune checkpoint blockade.

Types of Immunotherapy:
  Cancer immunotherapy      : Checkpoint inhibitors, CAR-T, cancer vaccines, cytokines, bispecific antibodies.
  Autoimmune biologics      : Anti-cytokine antibodies (anti-TNF, anti-IL-6, anti-IL-17).
  Allergy immunotherapy     : Subcutaneous or sublingual allergen desensitisation.
  Transplant immunosuppression: Prevent rejection.
  Passive immunisation      : Monoclonal antibodies (anti-infection, anti-toxin, anti-cancer).
  Active immunisation (vaccines): Stimulate immune memory.

1.1 MONOCLONAL ANTIBODIES (mAbs) — FUNDAMENTALS
-------------------------------------------------
Generated by immortalising antibody-producing B cells (hybridoma technology, Milstein & Köhler, Nobel 1984).
Modern methods: Phage display, transgenic mice, single B cell cloning.

Naming convention:
  -omab = murine; -ximab = chimeric (~65% human); -zumab = humanised (~95% human); -umab = fully human.

IgG subclasses and effector functions:
  IgG1: ADCC (via FcgammaRIII/CD16 on NK cells) + CDC (complement C1q binding). Most common for cancer therapy.
  IgG2: Weak ADCC/CDC; good for receptor antagonism (no FcR engagement wanted).
  IgG4: No ADCC/CDC; switches arms; silent Fc; used when you want blocking only (pembrolizumab).

Half-life: ~21 days for IgG1 (FcRn recycling). Extended half-life variants: YTE/LS mutations → 6–9 weeks.

2. CHECKPOINT INHIBITORS IN CANCER
------------------------------------
Immune checkpoints are "brakes" on T cells to prevent autoimmunity. Tumours exploit them.

CTLA-4 (Cytotoxic T-Lymphocyte-Associated Protein 4):
  Expressed on activated T cells; competes with CD28 for B7 (CD80/86) binding; higher affinity → outcompetes → inhibits T activation.
  Also expressed on Tregs → suppression.
  Ipilimumab (anti-CTLA-4, IgG1): First approved checkpoint inhibitor (melanoma, 2011).
    Tremelimumab (anti-CTLA-4, IgG2): Combination with durvalumab → NSCLC, HCC.
  Mechanism: Removes brake during priming phase → increases T cell response in lymph node.
  irAEs (immune-related adverse events): Colitis, hepatitis, hypophysitis, thyroiditis, skin rash. Due to breaking self-tolerance.

PD-1 / PD-L1 Axis:
  PD-1 (Programmed Death-1): Expressed on activated T cells → chronic stimulation → T cell exhaustion.
    Binds PD-L1 (B7-H1, CD274) and PD-L2.
  PD-L1: Expressed on tumour cells + tumour-infiltrating immune cells (induced by IFN-gamma).
    Interaction → T cell exhaustion → immune evasion.
  Anti-PD-1: Nivolumab (IgG4, BMS); pembrolizumab (IgG4, Merck/MSD).
    Approved: Melanoma, NSCLC, MSI-H cancers (pan-tumour), Hodgkin lymphoma, HCC, RCC, bladder, cervical, HNSCC, oesophageal, TNBC, colorectal (dMMR), endometrial, TMB-H.
  Anti-PD-L1: Atezolizumab (IgG1 Fc-modified, Genentech); durvalumab (IgG1, AZ); avelumab (IgG1).
  Mechanism: Works in tumour microenvironment (effector phase) + reinvigorate exhausted T cells.
  Predictive biomarkers: PD-L1 expression (imperfect); MSI-H/dMMR (strong predictor); TMB-H; TIL infiltrate ("hot" vs "cold" tumour).

LAG-3, TIM-3, TIGIT (Next-generation checkpoints):
  LAG-3 (Lymphocyte-Activation Gene 3): Expressed on exhausted T cells. Binds MHC II + fibrinogen-like protein 1.
    Relatlimab (anti-LAG-3) + nivolumab = Opdualag → melanoma (FDA 2022); improved PFS vs nivo alone.
  TIM-3: Expressed on exhausted T cells; ligands: Galectin-9, CEACAM-1, HMGB1, phosphatidylserine.
    Several anti-TIM-3 in clinical trials.
  TIGIT: Competes with CD226 (activating) for CD155 (PVR) and CD112. Anti-TIGIT + PD-1 → combination trials.
    Vibostolimab (anti-TIGIT, MK-7684): Phase III in combination with pembrolizumab.

3. CYTOKINE THERAPIES
-----------------------
IL-2:
  Promotes T cell proliferation and survival. High-dose IL-2 → durable responses in melanoma + RCC (~5–10%).
  Toxicity: Vascular leak syndrome (capillary leak, hypotension). Limited to fit patients.
  Engineered IL-2: Bempegaldesleukin (NKTR-358): PEGylated; biased toward CD8+ T cells vs Tregs.

Interferons:
  IFN-alpha: Approved for hairy cell leukaemia, CML (pre-imatinib era), hepatitis B/C, Kaposi sarcoma, melanoma.
  IFN-beta: MS treatment (reduces relapse rate ~30%); antiviral.
  IFN-gamma: Activates macrophages; used in chronic granulomatous disease.

TNF-alpha blocking agents (used in autoimmune disease):
  Biologics that block TNF-alpha: Most widely used biologics globally.
  Etanercept (TNF receptor fusion); Infliximab, adalimumab, golimumab, certolizumab (anti-TNF mAbs).
  Indications: Rheumatoid arthritis, psoriasis, psoriatic arthritis, ankylosing spondylitis, IBD (Crohn's + UC).
  Risks: Reactivation of latent TB (screen with IGRA/TST before starting); invasive fungal infection; lymphoma; demyelination.

4. ALLERGY IMMUNOTHERAPY
--------------------------
Allergen Immunotherapy (AIT):
  SCIT (subcutaneous): Injections with gradually increasing allergen dose → tolerance.
    Mechanism: Shift Th2 → Th1 response; Tregs induced; IgG4 (blocking antibody) replaces IgE;
    reduced mast cell/basophil sensitivity; mucosal regulatory responses.
    Duration: 3–5 years. Effective for: Rhinitis, allergic asthma, bee venom (life-saving).
  SLIT (sublingual): Drops or tablets; safer (low systemic absorption); OAT (oral allergen tablets).
    Grazax (grass pollen), Acarizax (house dust mite) — licensed European products.
  OIT (Oral Immunotherapy): Food allergies (peanut — Palforzia approved 2020; milk; egg).
    Desensitises but sustained unresponsiveness less common.
  Omalizumab (anti-IgE): Binds free IgE → prevents mast cell/basophil activation. Severe allergic asthma; chronic urticaria; food allergy adjunct (OUTMATCH trial — omalizumab enables OIT in multi-food allergy).

5. CAR-T CELL THERAPY IN DEPTH
---------------------------------
CAR (Chimeric Antigen Receptor) Structure:
  scFv (single-chain variable fragment from antibody) → transmembrane domain → co-stimulatory domain(s) → CD3zeta signalling domain.
  Generations:
    1st gen: scFv + CD3zeta only. Poor persistence.
    2nd gen: scFv + one co-stim (CD28 or 4-1BB) + CD3zeta. Current standard.
    3rd gen: scFv + two co-stim + CD3zeta. Investigated.
    4th gen (TRUCKs): Armoured CAR-T; also secrete cytokines (IL-12, IL-18) → recruit innate immunity.
    5th gen: Co-stimulatory signalling domain that activates JAK/STAT. Universal CAR-T (allogeneic).

CD28 vs 4-1BB co-stimulatory domains:
  CD28: Faster expansion; more central memory? More prone to exhaustion long-term.
  4-1BB (CD137): Better persistence; more memory phenotype; slower expansion.
  KymRiah (tisa-cel): 4-1BB domain. Yescarta (axi-cel): CD28 domain. Both approved for DLBCL.

Manufacturing:
  Leukapheresis → T cell isolation → activation (anti-CD3/CD28) → viral transduction (lentivirus or retrovirus encodes CAR) → expansion → cryopreservation → QC → infusion.
  ~3–4 weeks to manufacture. Lymphodepleting chemo (flu/cy) before infusion → remove host immune cells → facilitate CAR-T expansion.

Toxicities:
  CRS (Cytokine Release Syndrome): IL-6, IFN-gamma, IL-1 storm → fever, hypotension, hypoxia.
    Tocilizumab (anti-IL-6R): First-line for severe CRS. Corticosteroids if refractory.
    Graded 1–4; managed with supportive care, tocilizumab, steroids, anakinra (anti-IL-1).
  ICANS (Immune effector Cell-Associated Neurotoxicity Syndrome): Cerebral oedema, confusion, aphasia, seizures. Treated with dexamethasone.
  Prolonged cytopenia; infection risk; B cell aplasia (CD19-targeting → hypogammaglobulinaemia → IVIG supplementation).

Allogeneic CAR-T (Off-the-shelf):
  Donor T cells edited to remove: TCR (TRAC gene edited → prevents GvHD); HLA class I (B2M edited → avoids host T cell rejection; but exposes to NK cells → add HLA-E or CD47).
  Tools: CRISPR, TALEN, transposons (Sleeping Beauty).
  Advantages: Immediately available; cheaper; standardised manufacturing. Clinical trials early/mid-stage.

6. BISPECIFIC ANTIBODIES & NEXT-GEN IMMUNOTHERAPY
---------------------------------------------------
Bispecific Antibodies:
  Simultaneously bind two antigens → bring cells into proximity or block two pathways.
  Blinatumomab (CD19×CD3, BITE): T cell + B cell leukaemia → serial killing. Approved ALL.
  Mosunetuzumab (CD20×CD3): Follicular lymphoma → step-in treatment.
  Glofitamab (CD20×CD3): Approved DLBCL 2023.
  Epcoritamab (CD20×CD3): Subcutaneous; DLBCL.
  Teclistamab (BCMA×CD3): Myeloma; monthly SC. Talquetamab (GPRC5D×CD3): Myeloma.
  IBI363 (PD-1×IL-2m): PD-1 antibody arm anchors to T cell; IL-2 mutein arm selectively activates. Phase I promising results.

Cancer Vaccines:
  Prophylactic: HPV vaccine (Gardasil 4/9 → HPV 6, 11, 16, 18, 31, 33, 45, 52, 58) → prevents cervical, anal, oropharyngeal cancer. HBV vaccine → prevents HBV → prevents HCC.
  Therapeutic cancer vaccines: Stimulate immune response against existing tumour.
    Sipuleucel-T (Provenge): DC-based; PAP-GM-CSF loaded DCs; prostate cancer; modest benefit.
    Personalised neoantigen vaccines: Tumour WGS/RNA-seq → identify private neoantigens (mutations creating new peptides) → peptide vaccine + adjuvant (Poly-ICLC) or mRNA.
      mRNA-4157/V940 (Moderna/MSD) + pembrolizumab: Phase IIb melanoma → 49% reduction recurrence vs pembrolizumab alone. Phase III ongoing.
      BNT111 (BioNTech): Fixed melanoma antigen mRNA → Phase II.

[RESISTANCE TO IMMUNOTHERAPY]
  Primary resistance: PD-L1 negative, low TMB, microsatellite stable, cold (non-inflamed) tumour.
    No TILs → T cells can't enter (exclusion pattern) or absent entirely (desert pattern).
  Secondary (acquired) resistance: Beta-2-microglobulin (B2M) loss → MHC class I loss → CD8+ T cells can't recognise tumour. JAK1/2 mutations → IFN-gamma signalling impaired → PD-L1 not induced. Antigen loss.
  Strategies to overcome: Combination approaches (checkpoint + VEGF; checkpoint + targeted therapy; checkpoint + radiation — abscopal effect; checkpoint + vaccine); oncolytic viruses (T-VEC); adoptive cell therapy in checkpoint-resistant tumours.
