ENGLISH - LITERATURE ANALYSIS, COMPREHENSION AND FUNCTIONAL WRITING
=====================================================================

PART 1: LITERATURE ANALYSIS

HOW TO ANALYSE A POEM (SMILE method):
S — Structure: Form, stanza structure, line lengths, rhyme scheme, enjambment, caesura.
M — Meaning: What is the poem literally about? What is the deeper meaning/theme?
I — Imagery: What images does the poet use? What do they represent?
L — Language: Specific words and phrases. Why were they chosen? What effect do they create?
E — Effect: Overall impact on the reader. How does the poem make you feel? Why?

ANALYSING LANGUAGE — P.E.E. method:
P — Point: Make your analytical claim.
E — Evidence: Quote directly from the text.
E — Explanation: Analyse the language. How/why does this create the effect?

Example:
"Tennyson presents grief as an unending cycle through his use of structure. The repetition of the phrase 'In Memoriam' throughout the poem mimics the obsessive nature of mourning, suggesting that grief does not progress but spirals, returning the griever to the same point of loss."

LITERARY DEVICES TO IDENTIFY:
Allegory: Extended symbolic narrative (Animal Farm = Soviet Russia).
Allusion: Reference to another text/event without explanation.
Anachronism: Something out of its time period.
Bildungsroman: Coming-of-age narrative (David Copperfield, Jane Eyre).
Catharsis: Emotional release experienced by audience (Greek drama).
Deus ex machina: Unlikely rescue of characters by improbable means.
Dramatic irony: Audience knows something characters don't.
Foil: Character who contrasts with and highlights qualities of another.
Hubris: Excessive pride leading to downfall (Greek tragedy).
In media res: Beginning in the middle of action.
Leitmotif: Recurring theme, image, or phrase with symbolic significance.
Stream of consciousness: Interior monologue, unfiltered thoughts (Woolf, Joyce).
Tragic flaw (hamartia): Character flaw leading to protagonist's downfall.

THEMES IN LITERATURE:
Love and loss: Romeo and Juliet, A Midsummer Night's Dream, Wuthering Heights.
Identity and belonging: To Kill a Mockingbird, The Kite Runner, The Catcher in the Rye.
Power and corruption: Macbeth, 1984, Lord of the Flies, Animal Farm.
Good vs Evil: Hamlet, Crime and Punishment, Paradise Lost.
Coming of age: Great Expectations, Jane Eyre, To Kill a Mockingbird.
War and its consequences: All Quiet on the Western Front, A Farewell to Arms.
Social class and inequality: Pride and Prejudice, Oliver Twist, The Great Gatsby.
Freedom and oppression: 1984, Brave New World, The Handmaid's Tale.
The individual vs society: Antigone, Walden, The Scarlet Letter.

SHAKESPEARE:
Tragedies: Hamlet, Othello, King Lear, Macbeth, Romeo and Juliet.
Tragic elements: Noble protagonist, tragic flaw (hamartia), reversal of fortune (peripeteia), recognition (anagnorisis), catharsis.
Comedies: A Midsummer Night's Dream, Much Ado About Nothing, Twelfth Night, The Merchant of Venice.
Comic elements: mistaken identity, disguise, romantic confusion, resolution through marriage.
Histories: Richard II, Henry IV, Henry V, Richard III. Based on real monarchs.
Sonnets: 154 sonnets. Themes: beauty, time, love, mortality, the rival poet.

Key Shakespearean language:
Soliloquy: Character alone on stage speaks thoughts to audience ("To be or not to be...")
Aside: Character speaks to audience, not heard by other characters.
Dramatic irony: Common in Shakespeare (we know Juliet isn't dead, Romeo doesn't).
Iambic pentameter: The metre of Shakespeare. 10 syllables per line, unstressed-stressed pattern.
Prose vs verse: High-status characters usually speak in verse; lower status in prose. Madness often breaks the verse.

PART 2: COMPREHENSION SKILLS

READING COMPREHENSION STRATEGIES:
1. Skim first: Get overall idea of what the passage is about.
2. Read the questions BEFORE reading in detail.
3. Read carefully: Pay attention to tone, purpose, audience.
4. Locate evidence: Find specific lines that answer questions.
5. Use context clues for unfamiliar vocabulary.

TYPES OF COMPREHENSION QUESTIONS:
Literal: Answer directly stated in the text. "What did the character do when..."
Inferential: Answer implied but not stated. "Why do you think..." Requires reading between the lines.
Vocabulary in context: "What does the word X mean in paragraph 2?"
Language effect: "What is the effect of the writer's use of X?"
Structure/form: "Why does the writer structure the passage in this way?"
Summary: "In your own words, summarise..."
Evaluation: "How effectively does the writer achieve their purpose?"

HOW TO ANSWER COMPREHENSION QUESTIONS:
Refer to the question. Provide evidence. Explain (don't just quote).
Short answer: Direct answer + quote + brief explanation.
Extended answer: PEEL structure — Point + Evidence + Explanation + Link.
"In your own words": Paraphrase, do not copy the text directly. Show you understand meaning.
Effect questions: "The writer uses X to create an effect of Y because..."

IDENTIFYING PURPOSE AND AUDIENCE:
Purpose: To inform, persuade, entertain, advise, argue, describe, instruct, review.
Audience: Consider age, expertise level, relationship to writer, cultural context.
Tone clues: Word choice, sentence structure, use of humour/sarcasm/formality, first/second/third person.
Bias: Is the writer presenting a balanced view? What perspective are they coming from?

PART 3: FUNCTIONAL WRITING

REPORT WRITING:
Purpose: Present factual information clearly, often following an investigation or event.
Structure:
- Title: "Report on..."
- Introduction: Purpose of the report, what was investigated.
- Findings/Body: Subheadings organise sections. Factual, objective.
- Conclusion: Summary of findings.
- Recommendations: (if required) Suggested actions.

Format features: Subheadings, numbered sections, bullet points acceptable, no personal pronouns (generally), passive voice often used, formal language.
Key phrases: "The purpose of this report is to...", "The investigation revealed that...", "It is recommended that...", "In conclusion, the findings suggest..."

NOTICE WRITING:
Purpose: Brief announcement displayed publicly in school/office/community.
Format:
[Organisation Name]
NOTICE
[Date]
[Title of Notice in caps or bold]
[Body: Who/What/When/Where/How. Brief and clear.]
[Action required]
[Name and Designation of issuing authority]

Rules: Use capital letters for "NOTICE". Write in a box. Keep it brief (50-80 words). Include all relevant details. No personal opinions. Formal language.

NEWSPAPER REPORT:
Features: Headline (grabs attention, short, active), Byline (journalist's name), Dateline (place + date), Lead paragraph (5 W's: Who, What, When, Where, Why), Body (inverted pyramid — most important first), Quotes from witnesses/officials, Neutral, factual tone, Past tense, Third person.
Headline techniques: Alliteration, Pun, Shock, Question.

ARTICLE WRITING:
For magazines, newspapers, school publications.
Features: Catchy title, engaging opening (hook), subheadings (optional), conversational or formal tone depending on audience, opinion + evidence, ending that rounds off the piece.
Types: Opinion article, informative article, feature article, review article.

REVIEW WRITING (Book/Film/Restaurant):
Structure: Introduction (title, author/director, genre, brief overview — NO major spoilers) → Plot summary (brief) → Analysis (characters, themes, writing style, direction, strengths) → Evaluation (what worked, what didn't, comparison to similar works) → Recommendation (who would enjoy this, rating).
Features: First person acceptable, evaluative language, balanced (strengths AND weaknesses), specific examples.
Key phrases: "The author skilfully...", "The narrative is undermined by...", "A compelling performance...", "Highly recommended for...", "Falls short of...", "One of the most...", "This thought-provoking work..."

EMAIL WRITING:
Formal email:
Subject line: Brief, specific. "Application for Marketing Intern Position" not "Job"
Opening: Dear [Name/Sir/Madam],
Body: Clear purpose → details → action required.
Closing: Yours sincerely/faithfully, Full name.
No contractions. Professional tone. Short paragraphs. Proofread.

Informal email:
Subject: Casual.
Opening: Hi/Hey [Name],
Body: Conversational, can use contractions, emojis acceptable in very informal contexts.
Closing: Cheers/Best/Take care/Love, First name.

MINUTES OF A MEETING:
Format: Date, time, venue. Present: [names and designations]. Apologies: [names of absent members].
Agenda: Points to be discussed.
Minutes body: Record of what was said, decisions made, actions agreed.
Action items: Who does what by when.
Signed by: Secretary and Chair.
Language: Past tense ("The chairperson welcomed..."), reported speech, impersonal.

CURRICULUM VITAE (CV)/RESUME:
Sections: Personal details, Career objective, Education, Work experience, Skills, Achievements, Interests, References.
Rules: 1-2 pages. Chronological (most recent first). Use action verbs: Led, Managed, Developed, Created, Improved, Achieved, Organised. Quantify achievements: "Increased sales by 20%". No photo in some countries (UK, USA). Tailor for each job.

PART 4: ENGLISH GRAMMAR — ADVANCED

SENTENCE TYPES:
Simple: One independent clause. "The dog barked."
Compound: Two independent clauses joined by a conjunction. "The dog barked, and the cat fled."
Complex: One independent + one dependent clause. "The dog barked when it heard the noise."
Compound-complex: Two independent + one dependent. "The dog barked when it heard the noise, and the cat fled immediately."

CLAUSES:
Independent (main) clause: Can stand alone as a sentence.
Dependent (subordinate) clause: Cannot stand alone. Begins with subordinating conjunction (because, although, when, if, since, unless, until, while, after, before, as).
Relative clause: Modifies a noun. Introduced by who, which, that, whose, whom.
  Defining relative clause: Identifies which one. No commas. "The man who called is waiting."
  Non-defining relative clause: Adds extra info. Commas essential. "My brother, who lives in London, called."

TENSES (overview):
Simple present: "I write." General truths, habits, states.
Present continuous: "I am writing." Actions happening now, temporary actions.
Present perfect: "I have written." Actions connected to present, experiences.
Present perfect continuous: "I have been writing." Ongoing actions started in past.
Past simple: "I wrote." Completed actions at specific time in past.
Past continuous: "I was writing." Ongoing past action, interrupted action.
Past perfect: "I had written." Action before another past action.
Future simple: "I will write." Predictions, decisions made at moment of speaking.
Future continuous: "I will be writing." Ongoing action at specific future time.
Future perfect: "I will have written." Action completed before a future point.

CONDITIONALS:
Zero: "If you heat ice, it melts." (general truths, both present tense)
First: "If it rains, I will stay home." (real/possible future condition)
Second: "If I were rich, I would travel." (hypothetical/unlikely present/future)
Third: "If she had studied, she would have passed." (impossible past condition)
Mixed: "If she had taken the job, she would be rich now." (past condition → present result)

PASSIVE VOICE:
Formed: be + past participle. "The cake was eaten by the children."
Use when: Agent unknown, unimportant, or deliberately withheld. Scientific writing. To vary sentence structure.
"It is believed that...", "The experiment was conducted...", "Mistakes were made."
Convert active to passive: Object becomes subject. Verb becomes be+pp. Subject becomes "by..." phrase.
