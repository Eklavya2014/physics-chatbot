CLASS 11 PHYSICS ESSENTIALS: ROTATION, PROPERTIES OF MATTER, FLUIDS
=====================================================================

CHAPTER: SYSTEM OF PARTICLES AND ROTATIONAL MOTION

ANGULAR VARIABLES:
- Angular displacement = theta
- Angular velocity: omega = d(theta)/dt
- Angular acceleration: alpha = d(omega)/dt

For constant angular acceleration:
- omega = omega0 + alpha t
- theta = omega0 t + (1/2)alpha t^2
- omega^2 = omega0^2 + 2alpha theta

RELATION BETWEEN LINEAR AND ANGULAR QUANTITIES:
- v = r omega
- a_t = r alpha
- a_c = v^2/r = r omega^2

TORQUE:
- tau = r x F
- Magnitude: tau = rF sin(theta)
- SI unit: N m

ANGULAR MOMENTUM:
- L = r x p
- For rigid body about fixed axis: L = I omega

ROTATIONAL DYNAMICS:
- Net torque = I alpha
- Rotational kinetic energy: K = (1/2)I omega^2

MOMENT OF INERTIA:
- Depends on mass and distribution about axis.
- Radius of gyration: I = Mk^2

Standard values:
- Ring about central axis: I = MR^2
- Disc/cylinder about central axis: I = (1/2)MR^2
- Hollow cylinder: I = MR^2
- Rod about center perpendicular to length: I = (1/12)ML^2
- Rod about end perpendicular to length: I = (1/3)ML^2
- Solid sphere about diameter: I = (2/5)MR^2
- Hollow sphere about diameter: I = (2/3)MR^2

THEOREMS:
- Parallel axis theorem: I = Icm + Md^2
- Perpendicular axis theorem: Iz = Ix + Iy

ROLLING MOTION:
- For pure rolling: v = R omega
- Total kinetic energy = (1/2)Mv^2 + (1/2)Icm omega^2

EQUILIBRIUM OF RIGID BODY:
For equilibrium:
- Net force = 0
- Net torque = 0

Types:
- Stable equilibrium: PE minimum
- Unstable equilibrium: PE maximum
- Neutral equilibrium: PE constant

CENTER OF MASS:
- Motion of COM depends on external force.
- If external torque = 0, angular momentum is conserved.

COMMON MISTAKES IN ROTATION:
- Do not confuse force with torque.
- tau = I alpha is rotational analog of F = ma, but use about correct axis.
- In rolling without slipping, friction is usually static.
- Angular momentum is conserved only when external torque is zero.
- Pure rolling means translational and rotational motions are linked by v = R omega.

CHAPTER: MECHANICAL PROPERTIES OF SOLIDS

STRESS:
- Normal stress = Force/Area
- Shear stress = Tangential force/Area

STRAIN:
- Longitudinal strain = Delta L/L
- Volumetric strain = Delta V/V
- Shear strain = angular deformation

HOOKE'S LAW:
- Within elastic limit, stress is proportional to strain.

ELASTIC MODULI:
- Young's modulus: Y = (F L)/(A Delta L)
- Bulk modulus: K = -p/(Delta V/V)
- Shear modulus: eta = shear stress/shear strain

ELASTIC ENERGY PER UNIT VOLUME:
- u = (1/2) x stress x strain

IMPORTANT POINTS:
- Elastic limit: maximum stress up to which body regains original shape.
- Plastic deformation: permanent deformation.
- Steel is more elastic than rubber because Young's modulus of steel is larger.
- Ductile materials can be drawn into wires.
- Brittle materials fracture with small deformation.

CHAPTER: MECHANICAL PROPERTIES OF FLUIDS

PRESSURE:
- P = Force/Area
- SI unit: pascal

HYDROSTATIC PRESSURE:
- P = P0 + rho g h

PASCAL'S LAW:
- Pressure applied to enclosed fluid is transmitted equally in all directions.

HYDRAULIC LIFT:
- F2/F1 = A2/A1

ARCHIMEDES' PRINCIPLE:
- Buoyant force = weight of displaced fluid

LAW OF FLOATATION:
- Weight of floating body = weight of displaced liquid

STREAMLINE FLOW:
- In steady streamline flow, velocity at a point remains constant with time.

EQUATION OF CONTINUITY:
- A v = constant

BERNOULLI'S PRINCIPLE:
- P + (1/2)rho v^2 + rho g h = constant

Applications:
- Venturimeter
- Atomizer
- Carburetor
- Lift on airplane wing

VISCOSITY:
- Viscous force: F = eta A (dv/dx)

STOKES' LAW:
- Drag force on sphere: F = 6 pi eta r v

TERMINAL VELOCITY:
- v_t = 2r^2(rho - sigma)g / 9eta
  rho = density of body, sigma = density of fluid

POISEUILLE'S EQUATION:
- Q = (pi P r^4)/(8 eta l)
  Q = volume flow rate

SURFACE TENSION:
- Surface tension = force/length
- Surface energy per unit area = surface tension

EXCESS PRESSURE:
- Liquid drop: Delta P = 2T/r
- Soap bubble: Delta P = 4T/r

CAPILLARY RISE:
- h = 2T cos(theta)/(r rho g)

IMPORTANT CONCEPTS:
- Pressure in liquid depends on depth, density, g; not on container shape.
- Buoyant force depends on volume of displaced fluid.
- Bernoulli's theorem applies to ideal fluid in streamline flow.
- Surface tension decreases with temperature.
- Soap bubble has two liquid surfaces, hence excess pressure is double that of a drop.

COMMON MISTAKES IN FLUIDS:
- Do not apply Bernoulli's theorem to viscous/turbulent flow blindly.
- Do not forget atmospheric pressure when absolute pressure is asked.
- Terminal velocity occurs when net force becomes zero.
- In floating body problems, equate weight of body with buoyant force.
- Remember factor 2 and 4 in drop and soap bubble pressure formulas.
