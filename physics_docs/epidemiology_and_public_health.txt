1. WHAT IS EPIDEMIOLOGY?
--------------------------
Epidemiology studies the distribution, determinants, and control of health-related
events in populations. It is the foundation of public health.

Core Concepts:
  Epidemic    : Unusual increase in disease cases in a region/time period.
  Pandemic    : Epidemic spanning multiple countries or continents.
  Endemic     : Disease constantly present at baseline level in a population.
  Outbreak    : Sudden increase in disease cases in a specific area.
  Incidence   : Number of NEW cases in a population over a time period.
  Prevalence  : Total existing cases at a given point/period.
  Mortality   : Deaths from a disease. Case fatality rate (CFR) = deaths/cases.
  Morbidity   : Illness/disability caused by a disease.

1.1 DISEASE TRANSMISSION
--------------------------
Routes of Transmission:
  Direct contact  : Person-to-person (STIs, MRSA).
  Droplet          : Large particles (>5µm); short range (<1m). Influenza, RSV.
  Airborne         : Small particles (<5µm); long range; linger in air. TB, measles, SARS-CoV-2.
  Fomite           : Contaminated surfaces → contact. Norovirus, C. diff spores.
  Foodborne/waterborne: Salmonella, E. coli O157, Vibrio cholerae.
  Vector-borne     : Via arthropod vector. Malaria (Anopheles), dengue (Aedes), Lyme (Ixodes).
  Vertical         : Mother to child (HIV, CMV, rubella, syphilis, HBV).
  Zoonotic         : Animal to human. COVID-19, rabies, Ebola, influenza, Lyme disease.

R0 (Basic Reproduction Number):
  Average number of secondary cases generated by one case in fully susceptible population.
  R0 > 1: Epidemic growth. R0 = 1: Endemic steady state. R0 < 1: Disease dies out.

Disease              | R0 estimate
---------------------|------------
Measles              | 12–18
Chickenpox           | 8–10
COVID-19 (Omicron)   | 8–15
COVID-19 (original)  | 2–3
Influenza (seasonal) | 1.2–1.4
HIV                  | 2–5
Ebola                | 1.5–2.5
SARS                 | 2–5
Polio                | 5–7

2. EPIDEMIOLOGICAL STUDY DESIGNS
----------------------------------
Observational Studies:
  Case-Control: Compare PAST exposures between cases (disease+) and controls (disease-).
    Measure: Odds Ratio (OR). Good for rare diseases and multiple exposure analysis.
    Bias: Recall bias; selection bias.
  Cohort: Follow exposed and unexposed groups forward in time → compare disease rates.
    Prospective or retrospective. Measure: Relative Risk (RR) / Incidence Rate Ratio.
    Framingham Heart Study: ~70-year cohort; identified major CVD risk factors.
    UK Biobank: 500,000 participants; genetics + health outcomes + lifestyle.
  Cross-sectional: Snapshot of exposure AND disease at same time → prevalence.
    Cheap/quick but cannot establish causality (chicken-and-egg problem).
  Ecological: Compare disease rates across populations vs aggregate exposures.
    Risk of ecological fallacy (group-level associations ≠ individual-level).

Experimental Studies:
  RCT (Randomised Controlled Trial): Gold standard. Random allocation → controls
  confounding. Double-blind: Neither participant nor researcher knows allocation.
  Cluster RCT: Randomise groups (villages, schools) rather than individuals.
  Crossover RCT: Same participant receives both treatments (in sequence). Needs washout period.

Measures of Association:
  Relative Risk (RR): Incidence in exposed / Incidence in unexposed.
    RR = 1: No association. RR > 1: Increased risk. RR < 1: Protective.
  Odds Ratio (OR): Odds of exposure in cases / Odds of exposure in controls.
    Approximates RR when disease is rare (<10%).
  Attributable Risk (AR): Incidence in exposed - Incidence in unexposed.
    Population AR (PAR): Disease burden attributable to a risk factor → policy relevance.
  Number Needed to Treat (NNT): 1 / Absolute Risk Reduction → clinical significance.

Confounding & Bias:
  Confounding: Third variable associated with both exposure and outcome → spurious association.
    Control: Randomisation; restriction; matching; stratification; multivariate adjustment.
  Selection bias: Systematic difference in how study participants are selected.
  Information bias: Systematic error in data collection (recall bias, observer bias).
  Bradford Hill Criteria: Strength, Consistency, Specificity, Temporality, Biological
    gradient, Plausibility, Coherence, Experiment, Analogy → assessing causality.

3. OUTBREAK INVESTIGATION
--------------------------
Steps of Outbreak Investigation:
  1. Verify diagnosis and existence of outbreak.
  2. Establish case definition (confirmed, probable, suspected).
  3. Find cases; construct epidemic curve (epi curve).
  4. Characterise by person, place, time.
  5. Form hypotheses about source and transmission.
  6. Test hypotheses (case-control or cohort study).
  7. Implement control measures.
  8. Communicate findings; write report.

Epidemic Curves:
  Point-source: Sudden rise then fall (single exposure event — food poisoning at a party).
  Propagated: Multiple peaks; person-to-person spread (measles outbreak).
  Continuous: Prolonged exposure from ongoing source (contaminated water supply).

Reproductive Number During Outbreak:
  Effective Rt: R0 × proportion susceptible.
  As immunity builds (natural or vaccine): Rt falls → outbreak controlled when Rt < 1.
  Contact tracing: Identify and quarantine contacts to reduce Rt.

4. GLOBAL BURDEN OF DISEASE & SDOH
-------------------------------------
Global Burden of Disease (GBD):
  DALY: Disability-Adjusted Life Year = YLL (years of life lost) + YLD (years lived with disability).
  Leading causes of DALYs globally: Ischaemic heart disease, stroke, COPD, lower respiratory
  infections, neonatal disorders, diarrhoeal diseases, road injuries.
  Epidemiological transition: Shift from infectious to non-communicable diseases as countries develop.

Social Determinants of Health (SDOH):
  Income, education, housing, employment, social support, food security, early childhood
  development → ~80% of health outcomes determined by social/economic factors; 20% by healthcare.
  Gradient: Each step up the social ladder → better health (Marmot Review, UK).
  Health inequalities: Life expectancy gap between richest and poorest areas >10 years in many countries.

5. MOLECULAR EPIDEMIOLOGY
--------------------------
Pathogen Genomic Epidemiology:
  Whole-genome sequencing (WGS) of pathogens → high-resolution outbreak tracking.
  COVID-19: SARS-CoV-2 was sequenced within 2 weeks of identification (Wuhan, Dec 2019).
    GISAID database: >15 million SARS-CoV-2 sequences → tracked variant emergence
    (Alpha, Delta, Omicron) and transmission chains in real time.
  TB genomics: WGS replaces RFLP typing → transmission clusters; resistance mutations
    identified within days (rpoB → rifampicin; katG → isoniazid).
  Salmonella outbreak investigations: WGS identifies source faster than traditional typing.
    FDA GenomeTrakr: WGS database of foodborne pathogen isolates → routine use in USA.

Metagenomics in Public Health:
  Wastewater epidemiology: Sewage contains RNA/DNA of viruses, bacteria shed by community.
  SARS-CoV-2 in sewage → 4–7 days EARLIER signal than clinical testing.
  Poliovirus detected in London sewage 2022 before any clinical cases.
  Antibiotic resistance genes: AMR surveillance via metagenomics of wastewater.

6. MATHEMATICAL MODELLING OF INFECTIOUS DISEASES
--------------------------------------------------
SIR Model:
  Population divided into Susceptible (S), Infected (I), Recovered/Removed (R).
  dS/dt = -β × S × I / N
  dI/dt = β × S × I / N - γ × I
  dR/dt = γ × I
  β = transmission rate; γ = recovery rate; R0 = β/γ.
  Threshold: Epidemic occurs when S × R0 > 1.

SEIR: Adds Exposed (E) compartment (latent period).
SEIRD: Adds Deaths (D) compartment.
Agent-based models: Simulate individual interactions; incorporates heterogeneity (age,
  contact patterns, geography). Used in COVID-19 policy modelling (Ferguson et al., Imperial).
Network models: Transmission on contact networks; identifies super-spreaders and hubs.

Herd Immunity Threshold (HIT):
  HIT = 1 - 1/R0.
  Measles (R0~15): HIT = 93–95%. Polio (R0~6): HIT = ~83%. SARS-CoV-2 (original R0~3): ~67%.
  Vaccination shifts S population directly → accelerates reaching HIT without infection.

One Health Framework:
  Human health, animal health, and ecosystem health are interconnected.
  ~75% of emerging infectious diseases are zoonotic.
  EID risk drivers: land use change, wildlife trade, antimicrobial use, climate change,
  globalisation of food/travel.
  AMR: ~1.27 million deaths directly attributable to AMR in 2019 (Lancet). Linked to
  antibiotic use in livestock (~70% global antibiotic use is in agriculture).

[HISTORICAL PERSPECTIVE] John Snow (1854): Cholera in Soho, London.
  Identified contaminated Broad Street pump as source of cholera outbreak before germ
  theory was established. Removed pump handle → outbreak declined.
  Considered the father of epidemiology. Demonstrated that disease can be understood
  and controlled through systematic observation and analysis of population-level data.
  This approach remains the cornerstone of modern public health practice.
